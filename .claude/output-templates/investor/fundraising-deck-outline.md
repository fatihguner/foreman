# Fundraising Deck Outline

A pitch deck is a persuasion architecture — twelve slides building one compounding argument: this team, this market, this moment, this model, outsized returns. Every slide advances that thesis or weakens it. There is no neutral ground. Replace all bracketed placeholders. Delete HTML comments before presenting.

---

### SLIDE 1: Title
- Company name and one-line descriptor (max 8 words) | Logo | Founder name(s) | Round: [Seed / A / B]
- **Investor Lens:** The one-liner sorts. Partners read 50 decks weekly. "[Specific outcome] for [specific buyer]" works. "AI-powered platform for X" does not.
- **Mistake:** Clever taglines that sacrifice clarity. Buzzwords that fit 200 companies.

### SLIDE 2: Problem
- [Problem in one sentence from the customer's perspective]
- [Quantify the pain: cost, time, failure rate, or missed revenue]
- [Why existing solutions fail — the structural gap]
- **Investor Lens:** Investors fund problems, not products. "42% of mid-market CFOs manually reconcile invoices, consuming 11 hours weekly" outperforms "reconciliation is painful."
- **Mistake:** Describing the problem as the absence of your solution.

### SLIDE 3: Solution
- [What you built in plain language — two sentences maximum]
- [The primary insight or mechanism that makes it work]
- [One screenshot, product image, or diagram — visual proof it exists]
- **Investor Lens:** One clear mechanism beats a feature tour. The deck earns a meeting, not a term sheet.
- **Mistake:** Feature dumping. Eight capabilities when one sharp insight suffices.

### SLIDE 4: Market Opportunity
- TAM: $[amount] — [source] | SAM: $[amount] — [filter] | SOM: $[amount] — [3-year assumptions]
- [One sentence on the macro tailwind: regulatory shift, tech inflection, behavioral change]
- **Investor Lens:** Seasoned investors ignore TAM. They scrutinize SAM-to-SOM logic. Bottom-up sizing is credible; "the global market is $80B, we capture 1%" is not.
- **Mistake:** Analyst reports as proof without a plausible capture path.

### SLIDE 5: Business Model
- Revenue model: [Subscription / Transaction / Marketplace / Licensing] | Pricing: $[amount] per [unit]
- Unit economics: LTV $[amount] / CAC $[amount] / Ratio [X:1] | Gross margin: [X%]
- Expansion mechanism: [Upsell / land-and-expand / usage-based growth]
- **Investor Lens:** Does this model improve or degrade with volume? LTV:CAC >3:1, payback <18 months, expansion structural not aspirational.
- **Mistake:** Unit economics based on projected-future costs. Show actuals, even if early.

### SLIDE 6: Traction
- [Primary metric: ARR, MRR, GMV, or active users — with timeline]
- [Growth rate: MoM or YoY] | [Customer logos or count] | [Cohort retention data]
- **Investor Lens:** The most influential slide at Series A. Growth rate matters more than absolute revenue. Cohort curves that flatten or bend upward are the most powerful data a founder can present.
- **Mistake:** Showing only acquisition. Investors ask: "What happens after sign-up?" Answer it on the slide.

### SLIDE 7: Competitive Landscape
- [2x2 matrix with axes relevant to your differentiation]
- [3-5 competitors plotted honestly] | [One sentence on your value innovation via ERRC grid]
- **Investor Lens:** "We have no competitors" is the most disqualifying sentence in fundraising. Blue Ocean positioning — competing on dimensions others ignore — outperforms incremental superiority on the same axes.
- **Mistake:** Feature comparison where you win every row. Acknowledge strength elsewhere; explain why it does not matter for your segment.

### SLIDE 8: Go-to-Market
- Channel: [Sales / PLG / Content / Partnerships] | CAC by channel: $[amount] | Cycle: [X days]
- Near-term motion (12 months): [Specific plan with milestones]
- **Investor Lens:** GTM reveals whether you understand the customer's buying process. The strongest slides show a flywheel — each customer makes the next one cheaper to acquire.
- **Mistake:** Listing every channel instead of demonstrating mastery of one.

### SLIDE 9: Team
- [2-4 founders: name, title, one line connecting experience to this specific problem]
- [Key hires made + planned] | [Advisors with domain credibility]
- **Investor Lens:** Founder-market fit outweighs credentials. "Led Google's identity infrastructure, the core problem we solve" beats "Ex-Google."
- **Mistake:** Credentials without connection to the company's challenge.

### SLIDE 10: Financials
- [Revenue projection: current year + 2 years, monthly granularity for current year]
- [Key assumptions stated explicitly] | [Burn: $[amount]/mo, runway: [X] months]
- **Investor Lens:** Nobody believes three-year projections. Investors use them to test reasoning. "We project $5M ARR in Y2, contingent on ACV expansion from $12K to $25K" is credible. A hockey stick with no mechanism is not.
- **Mistake:** Projections without named assumptions. The model is the argument.

### SLIDE 11: The Ask
- Raising: $[amount] | Instrument: [SAFE / Priced / Note]
- Use of funds: [Specific — e.g., "50% engineering, 30% GTM, 20% ops"]
- Target milestone: [What this capital achieves — the milestone that de-risks the next round]
- **Investor Lens:** "$5M to grow the business" is a red flag. "$5M to reach $3M ARR by Q3 2027" demonstrates a modeled path between capital and outcome.
- **Mistake:** Vague allocation. Missing milestone. Amount disconnected from any stated goal.

### SLIDE 12: Closing
- Restate the one-line value proposition | [Single most compelling data point from the deck]
- Contact: email, phone | "Data room, financials, and customer references available"
- **Investor Lens:** This slide stays on screen during Q&A — the longest part of the meeting. Leave one number in the room.
- **Mistake:** "Thank you" and nothing else. Wasting the most persistent visual real estate in the presentation.

## Narrative Arc Checklist

Before presenting, verify the throughline: Problem (urgency) leads to Solution (insight) leads to Market (scale) leads to Model (durability) leads to Traction (proof) leads to Competition (defensibility) leads to GTM (path) leads to Team (credibility) leads to Financials (reasoning) leads to Ask (milestone). Each slide should make the next more powerful. If removing a slide does not weaken the arc, remove it.
