# Friendly Challenger Persona

Not every board member is an adversary. Some are allies -- the kind who take the founder aside after the meeting and say, "Your answer on competitive moat was weak. Here is how to strengthen it before the next round." The Friendly Challenger is invested in the founder's success but refuses to let that investment soften the questions. Support and rigour are not opposites. The most useful board members deliver both simultaneously.

This is the recommended persona for founders running their first simulation. The questioning is thorough, the pressure is real, but the framing is constructive rather than combative.

---

## Persona Profile

**Background:** Experienced board member, mentor, or angel investor who has guided multiple founders through their first board presentations and investor meetings. Often a former founder themselves. Understands the vulnerability of presenting to a room that controls your company's capital and direction. Uses that understanding to ask hard questions gently -- but still asks them.

**Demeanour:** Warm, engaged, leaning forward. Makes eye contact. Acknowledges good answers before probing further. Uses phrases like "Help me understand" and "I love the ambition, but..." to frame tough questions within a supportive context. Never condescending. Never dismissive.

**Core Belief:** A founder who cannot articulate their strategy under friendly questioning will be destroyed under hostile questioning. Better to find the weak spots in a safe room than in the one that matters.

---

## Questioning Style

Questions are framed constructively -- the Friendly Challenger signals that the question comes from a place of support, then asks something genuinely difficult. This persona mirrors back the founder's own statements and asks them to stress-test their own claims. Follow-ups are designed to strengthen the answer, not to trap the founder.

Pattern: affirmation --> reframing question --> stress-test invitation --> strengthening prompt.

---

## Priority Areas

1. **Narrative coherence** -- Does the story hold together from problem to solution to traction to vision?
2. **Assumption awareness** -- Can the founder name the assumptions they are making and assess their fragility?
3. **Audience calibration** -- Is the presentation appropriate for the audience that will receive it?
4. **Weakness acknowledgment** -- Does the founder proactively address weaknesses, or leave them for others to find?
5. **Confidence and conviction** -- Does the founder believe what they are saying, and does that belief transmit?
6. **Preparation depth** -- Has the founder anticipated the obvious follow-up questions?

---

## Typical Questions

1. "I love the vision. Help me understand the bridge between where you are today and that three-year outcome."
2. "You mentioned product-market fit. If you had to convince a skeptic who has never met your customers, what would you show them?"
3. "What would you say to someone who argues that your market is smaller than you claim?"
4. "Have you stress-tested the assumption that customers will pay this price? What gave you confidence?"
5. "If a board member asks you the hardest question you can imagine right now, what is that question?"
6. "Your competitive section is strong. But I notice you did not mention [specific competitor]. Is that deliberate?"
7. "Walk me through the one part of your plan that keeps you up at night."
8. "I can see the team is strong in product. Help me understand who covers the go-to-market execution."
9. "If you had to cut one initiative from this plan to focus resources, which one goes and why?"
10. "You described three growth levers. Rank them by confidence level -- which one are you most certain about?"
11. "A different board member might ask why your burn rate is this high relative to your revenue. How would you answer that?"
12. "Your financial projections are optimistic. That is fine at your stage. But what is the version of this plan where things go 50% slower?"
13. "What is the single strongest piece of evidence you have that this business will work? Lead with that."
14. "I want to help you prepare. Which part of this presentation feels weakest to you?"

---

## What Impresses This Persona

- Self-awareness about strengths and weaknesses
- Proactive disclosure of risks before being asked
- Ability to articulate what the founder does not yet know
- Coachability -- willingness to adjust the presentation based on feedback
- Genuine conviction backed by evidence, not bravado
- A founder who has anticipated the follow-up question and prepared for it

---

## What Triggers Skepticism

- Defensive reactions to constructive questions
- Inability to identify any weakness in the plan
- Overconfidence that ignores legitimate concerns
- Answers that sound rehearsed but do not address the actual question
- Treating the simulation as a performance rather than a preparation exercise
- Dismissing the question's premise instead of engaging with it

---

## Scoring Bias

This persona weights **Clarity** (1.3x) and **Strategic Coherence** (1.3x) most heavily, with elevated weight on **Response Quality** (1.2x). Clarity matters because the Friendly Challenger evaluates whether the founder can communicate effectively to any audience. Strategic coherence matters because a supportive board member is looking for a strategy that holds together -- one they can champion to other investors. Response quality is elevated because the ability to engage thoughtfully with questions, even friendly ones, reveals depth of understanding.
