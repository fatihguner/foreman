# Cofounder Conflict Diagnosis

## Entry Symptoms

Cofounder conflict is the leading non-market cause of startup death, yet founders almost always describe it in vague relational terms: "We just don't see eye to eye anymore." The ambiguity itself is diagnostic. Cofounders who can articulate the precise nature of their disagreement are already halfway to resolving it. Those who cannot — who speak in general frustration and mutual disappointment — are typically dealing with something structural they have not yet named.

## Triage Questions

### Q1: What is the conflict actually about? (Force specificity.)

- **Where the company is going (strategy, market, product direction, ambition level)** — likely diagnosis: **vision-divergence**. Proceed to Q2 for depth.
- **Who does what and who decides what (roles, authority, day-to-day operations)** — likely diagnosis: **role-ambiguity**. Proceed to Q3.
- **Who is contributing more (effort, hours, sacrifice, results)** — likely diagnosis: **contribution-asymmetry**. Proceed to Q4.
- **How the company should operate (ethics, speed, culture, risk tolerance)** — likely diagnosis: **values-clash**. Proceed to Q5.
- **Equity, compensation, or financial fairness** — likely diagnosis: **equity-resentment**. Proceed to Q4 and Q5.
- **"I can't really articulate it — everything just feels wrong"** — likely diagnosis: **communication-avoidance**. The inability to name the conflict is the conflict. Proceed to Q6.

### Q2: When you imagine the company in three years, do your pictures match?

- **Fundamentally different visions — one wants to build a lifestyle business, the other wants to raise Series B** — likely diagnosis: **vision-divergence** at the most fundamental level. This is existential; frameworks can clarify but may not reconcile.
- **Same destination, different routes — both want growth but disagree on how** — this is strategic disagreement, not vision misalignment. Proceed to Q3; the real issue may be decision authority.
- **One founder's vision has evolved while the other's has not** — likely diagnosis: **founder-market-fit-split**. One cofounder has grown with the company's demands; the other remains anchored to an earlier stage.

### Q3: If a contentious decision needed to be made tomorrow, is it clear who has final authority?

- **No — decisions require consensus, and consensus has become impossible** — likely diagnosis: **role-ambiguity**. Without clear decision rights, every disagreement becomes existential.
- **Yes — one founder has authority, but the other undermines or resents it** — likely diagnosis: **contribution-asymmetry** or **equity-resentment**. The authority structure exists but lacks legitimacy.
- **Authority shifts depending on the topic, with no explicit agreement about domains** — likely diagnosis: **role-ambiguity** compounded by **communication-avoidance**. The founders never formalised what was obvious when the team was two people.

### Q4: Would each founder say the other is contributing fairly?

- **No — one founder perceives a significant imbalance** — likely diagnosis: **contribution-asymmetry**. But contribution is subjective. Is the perceived imbalance about hours, impact, sacrifice, or visibility?
- **Both feel they contribute more (each believes they carry the heavier load)** — likely diagnosis: **contribution-asymmetry** driven by different definitions of value. A technical cofounder counts code; a business cofounder counts deals. Neither sees the other's work.
- **One founder's contribution has genuinely declined — they're checked out, distracted, or pursuing other interests** — likely diagnosis: **founder-market-fit-split**. This founder may have outgrown the company or lost belief in it.

### Q5: Do you trust your cofounder's judgment, even when you disagree with their conclusions?

- **Yes — I trust their intent and capability but not their decisions lately** — the relationship foundation is intact. The conflict is circumstantial. Proceed to Q6 for communication diagnosis.
- **No — I question their competence in their domain** — likely diagnosis: **founder-market-fit-split**. The company's complexity has outgrown one founder's capabilities.
- **No — I question their motives or ethics** — likely diagnosis: **values-clash**. This is the most dangerous form of cofounder conflict because it corrodes the basis for collaboration itself.

### Q6: When did you last have an honest, uncomfortable conversation about the state of your partnership?

- **Never — we talk about the business but never about us** — likely diagnosis: **communication-avoidance**. Every other diagnosis festers when this one is present.
- **We tried once but it went badly — accusations, defensiveness, withdrawal** — likely diagnosis: **communication-avoidance** compounded by the underlying issue from Q1.
- **Recently, but nothing changes afterward** — the communication channel works; implementation is broken. Consider external facilitation.

## Diagnosis Map

| Diagnosis | Key Signals | Confirmation Path |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Vision divergence** | Different endpoints (lifestyle vs. scale), incompatible ambition levels | Q1 shows directional conflict + Q2 shows fundamentally different pictures |
| **Role ambiguity** | Decision gridlock, territory disputes, consensus paralysis | Q1 shows role-based friction + Q3 shows unclear authority |
| **Contribution asymmetry** | Perceived imbalance in effort/sacrifice, mutual resentment about workload | Q1 shows effort-based tension + Q4 shows divergent contribution beliefs |
| **Values clash** | Disagreement on ethics, culture, risk tolerance, how to treat people | Q1 shows operating-philosophy tension + Q5 shows motive distrust |
| **Equity resentment** | Financial fairness grievances, comp or equity split feels wrong | Q1 shows economic tension + Q4 shows contribution disparity fuelling it |
| **Communication avoidance** | Inability to name the conflict, no meta-conversations, growing distance | Q1 shows vague frustration + Q6 shows no honest dialogue |
| **Founder-market fit split** | One founder evolved, the other didn't; competence gap widened with scale | Q2 shows divergent evolution + Q4 shows genuine contribution decline + Q5 shows competence doubt |

**Vision divergence:**
1. Apply `cooperative-negotiation` — structure the conversation as interest-based, not position-based

3. Output with `negotiation-prep-sheet`

**Role ambiguity:**
1. Apply `thomas-kilmann-conflict` — identify conflict styles, create explicit decision-rights framework
2. Apply `radical-candor` — establish direct feedback norms between founders
3. Output with `one-on-one-template`

**Contribution asymmetry:**
1. Apply `trust-equation` — diagnose which trust element has eroded
2. Apply `cooperative-negotiation` — negotiate explicit contribution expectations
3. Output with `negotiation-prep-sheet`

**Values clash:**
1. Apply `thomas-kilmann-conflict` — determine if the clash is resolvable or terminal
2. Apply `resolving-people-conflict` — systematic resolution before escalating to breakup
3. Output with `negotiation-prep-sheet`

**Equity resentment:**
1. Apply `coalition-and-partnership-games` — model equity against current and future contribution
2. Apply `cooperative-negotiation` — structure a renegotiation conversation
3. Output with `negotiation-prep-sheet`

**Communication avoidance:**
1. Apply `radical-candor` — build the capability for direct, caring confrontation
2. Apply `psychological-safety` — create conditions where honest conversation becomes possible
3. Output with `one-on-one-template`

**Founder-market fit split:**
1. Apply `trust-equation` — assess whether the relationship can accommodate the gap
2. Apply `coalition-and-partnership-games` — model graceful exit or role restructuring
3. Output with `negotiation-prep-sheet`

## Red Flags

- **One cofounder has already consulted a lawyer without telling the other.** Once legal counsel is engaged unilaterally, the partnership has shifted from conflict to dissolution. Framework-based mediation can still help structure the exit, but reconciliation probability drops sharply.
- **The conflict involves allegations of financial misconduct, fraud, or breach of fiduciary duty.** This is a legal matter, not a coaching matter. Engage legal counsel and a forensic accountant before any strategic conversation.
- **The cofounders have stopped communicating entirely — updates happen through team members or Slack messages only.** Total communication breakdown in a two-person leadership team is an organisational emergency. The company cannot function while its leaders operate in parallel silos. External mediation is required before any diagnostic framework applies.
