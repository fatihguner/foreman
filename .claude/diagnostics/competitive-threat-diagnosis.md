# Competitive Threat Diagnosis

Sun Tzu wrote that every battle is won before it is fought. The corollary, less quoted but more useful, is that most battles are lost because the general misidentified the enemy. A founder screaming "competitors are crushing us" may be fighting a price war when the real threat is a substitute technology. Another, panicking about a big-tech entrant, may be overlooking the fact that the entrant's strategy validates the market rather than destroys it. Competitive anxiety is universal among entrepreneurs. Accurate competitive diagnosis is not.

---

## Entry Symptoms

Founders experiencing competitive pressure typically present with one of these complaints:

- **"Competitors are crushing us"** -- Revenue or growth declining relative to known rivals
- **"Price war"** -- Margins compressing as competitors undercut on price
- **"We're losing market share"** -- Customer acquisition slowing while the market grows
- **"A big player just entered our space"** -- An established company (often with asymmetric resources) has launched a competing product
- **"We're being commoditized"** -- Customers treat the product as interchangeable with alternatives

The instinct is to react tactically: cut prices, add features, increase ad spend. The diagnostic below determines whether the problem is tactical, structural, or existential.

---

## Triage Questions

### Level 1: Threat Classification

**Q1: Who exactly is the threat -- a direct competitor, an adjacent player expanding into your space, or a new entrant with a fundamentally different model?**
- Direct competitor (same product, same market) --> Proceed to Q2.
- Adjacent player (different product, overlapping market) --> Proceed to Q4.
- New entrant with a different model (lower price point, different distribution, new technology) --> **Diagnosis: Disruptive Entrant.** Proceed to Q5 for severity assessment.

### Level 2: Direct Competition Dynamics

**Q2: Is the competition primarily on price or on value (features, brand, experience, switching costs)?**
- Price --> **Diagnosis: Price Commoditization.** When the only differentiator is the number on the invoice, the industry has a structural problem, not a sales problem.
- Value, but you are losing --> Proceed to Q3.

**Q3: Can you articulate -- in one sentence -- why a customer should choose you over the closest alternative?**
- No / the answer sounds generic --> **Diagnosis: Differentiation Failure.** If you cannot articulate the difference, customers certainly cannot perceive it.
- Yes, but customers still leave --> **Diagnosis: Moat Erosion.** The differentiator exists but is not defensible. Competitors have replicated or neutralized it.

### Level 3: Adjacent and Structural Threats

**Q4: Is the adjacent player entering your market because it is growing, or are they cannibalizing a shrinking market?**
- Growing market --> **Diagnosis: Wrong Competitive Arena.** You may be competing on the wrong dimension. The adjacent player sees an opportunity you have not framed correctly.
- Shrinking or consolidating market --> **Diagnosis: Market Category Collapse.** The category itself is contracting, and the adjacent player is absorbing what remains.

**Q5: Does the disruptive entrant serve the same customers or different (typically lower-end or non-consumers)?**
- Same customers, lower price --> **Diagnosis: Disruptive Entrant** (classic low-end disruption). The threat is existential if unaddressed.
- Different customers, expanding the market --> The threat is real but slower. You have time to respond strategically, not reactively.

### Level 4: Moat Assessment

**Q6: What is your defensible advantage -- network effects, proprietary data, switching costs, regulatory barriers, brand, or cost structure?**
- Cannot identify one --> **Diagnosis: Moat Erosion** (or never had a moat). This is the most urgent finding.
- Has one but it is weakening --> **Diagnosis: Moat Erosion.** Identify the specific mechanism of erosion.
- Has a strong moat but still losing share --> Revisit Q2-Q3; the problem may be execution, not strategy.

## Diagnosis Map

| Diagnosis | Core Problem | Typical Pattern |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Moat Erosion** | Defensible advantage is weakening or was illusory | Competitors replicate features within months; customer loyalty declining |
| **Price Commoditization** | Market treats offerings as interchangeable | Margins compressing quarter over quarter; RFPs decided on price alone |
| **Disruptive Entrant** | New player with structurally different cost or delivery model | Losing low-end customers first; incumbent advantages becoming liabilities |
| **Wrong Competitive Arena** | Competing on the wrong dimension or in the wrong frame | Adjacent player grows faster by serving the same need differently |
| **Differentiation Failure** | No clear, communicable reason to choose this product | Win rates dropping; customer feedback cites "similar to alternatives" |
| **Market Category Collapse** | The category itself is contracting or being absorbed | Total addressable market shrinking; multiple players exiting simultaneously |

## Red Flags

Stop triage and escalate if any of these conditions emerge:

- **The founder cannot name their top three competitors.** Competitive ignorance is itself the diagnosis. No framework helps until market awareness exists.
- **Gross margins have been negative for two consecutive quarters with no structural path to profitability.** This is not a competitive threat problem; this is a survival problem.
- **The "competitor" is actually a substitute that eliminates the need for the category entirely.** Kodak's competitor was not Fuji; it was the smartphone camera. Category substitution requires a fundamentally different strategic response than competitive rivalry.
- **The founder's response to every competitive threat is "we'll just add that feature."** Feature parity is not strategy. It is a treadmill that accelerates until someone falls off.
- **Customer churn has exceeded 10% monthly for three consecutive months.** The competitive threat has already metastasized into a retention crisis. Address churn before strategy.
