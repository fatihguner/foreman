# Negotiation Stuck Diagnosis

A stalled negotiation feels like a wall. Both sides sit across the table, repeat their positions with increasing frustration, and mistake stubbornness for strategy. The founder concludes that the other party is unreasonable. The other party reaches the identical conclusion. Meanwhile, the deal that could benefit both sides rots in the space between them -- not because agreement is impossible, but because neither side has diagnosed why they are actually stuck.

## Entry Symptoms

- **"The deal is stuck."** Neither party has made a concession in weeks; conversations are repetitive or have ceased entirely.
- **"We can't agree on terms."** Specific deal points remain unresolved despite multiple rounds of discussion.
- **"The other side won't budge."** The counterparty appears rigid; every proposal is rejected; no visible movement.
- **"Negotiation keeps going in circles."** The same arguments recur each meeting; apparent progress unravels by the next session.
- **"I think I'm getting a bad deal."** The founder suspects the terms are unfavourable but cannot assess whether this is true or merely feels true.

## Triage Questions

1. **Nature of negotiation**: Is this fundamentally competitive (one party's gain is the other's loss) or is there potential for cooperative value creation?
2. **Interests vs. positions**: Can you articulate the other side's underlying interests, or do you only know their stated positions?
3. **Power assessment**: Is the power balance roughly equal, or does one side hold significantly more leverage?
4. **BATNA clarity**: Have you defined your Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement? Can you quantify what happens if this deal falls through?
5. **Relationship stakes**: Is preserving the relationship important beyond this deal, or is this a one-time transaction?
6. **Third-party dynamics**: Is a third party influencing or blocking progress? Are there decision-makers absent from the room?
7. **Good faith assessment**: Do you believe the other side genuinely wants to reach agreement, or are there signs of stalling?

## Diagnosis Map

| Diagnosis | Key Indicators | Core Problem |
|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| **Positional Deadlock** | Both sides repeat fixed demands; concessions are seen as weakness; negotiation has become a contest of wills | Parties are arguing positions rather than exploring interests; the negotiation framework is purely distributive when it need not be |
| **BATNA Weakness** | The founder feels pressured to accept unfavourable terms; walking away feels impossible; the other side senses desperation | The founder lacks a credible alternative, and this weakness -- whether real or merely perceived -- undermines negotiating power |
| **Power Imbalance** | One side controls the timeline, the information, or the market access; terms are dictated rather than negotiated | Structural asymmetry means standard negotiation tactics are insufficient; the weaker party must change the game rather than play it better |
| **Hidden Interests** | Proposals are rejected without clear explanation; the counterparty's objections seem irrational or disproportionate; stated reasons do not match behaviour | Unstated needs, fears, or constraints are driving the other side's resistance; surface-level negotiation cannot resolve subsurface problems |
| **Relationship Damage** | Personal tension has entered the negotiation; past grievances are referenced; trust has broken down; emotional reactions override rational analysis | The negotiation has shifted from substance to sentiment; relational repair must precede deal-making |
| **Bad Faith Counterpart** | Repeated broken commitments; ever-moving goalposts; information withheld or misrepresented; the process itself is being exploited | The other side is not negotiating to agree but to extract information, stall, or gain leverage for use elsewhere |
| **Internal Misalignment** | The founder's own team disagrees on acceptable terms; different stakeholders send conflicting signals to the counterparty; the mandate is unclear | The negotiation is stuck not because of the counterparty but because the founder's side has not achieved internal consensus on goals and limits |

## Red Flags

- **The founder has no BATNA and does not realise it.** A negotiator without alternatives is not negotiating; they are accepting terms. Establish whether a walkaway option exists before any tactical advice. If none exists, creating one is the first intervention.
- **"The other side won't budge" but the founder has not asked about their interests.** Perceived rigidity is often unexplored interests. Route to cooperative-negotiation before escalating competitive tactics.
- **Emotional investment exceeds economic rationality.** When the founder frames the negotiation in personal terms ("they disrespected us"), the deal has become an identity contest. Route to negotiation-mindset-and-self.
- **A third party is blocking progress from the shadows.** Silent stakeholders -- an investor's spouse, a board member who was not consulted -- can veto deals invisibly. Surface these actors before optimising tactics.
- **The founder is negotiating against themselves.** Some founders concede preemptively, offering terms the counterparty never requested. Route to negotiation-mindset-and-self.
