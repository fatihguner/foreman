# Partnership Diagnosis

## Entry Symptoms

Partnerships arrive wrapped in optimism. The entrepreneur describes: "We got an offer to partner with a major player," "Our current partnership isn't delivering," "Should we acquire them or just partner?," or "There's a joint venture opportunity on the table." The allure of combining forces is powerful — two companies, complementary strengths, shared upside. The reality is that most partnerships fail, and the failure modes are identifiable in advance.

## Triage Questions

### Q1: What type of partnership is this?

- **Strategic partnership (capability access — technology, expertise, IP)** — the core question is whether the partner actually delivers the capability you need. Proceed to Q2.
- **Distribution partnership (market access — channels, customers, geography)** — the core question is whether incentives stay aligned as volumes grow. Proceed to Q3.
- **Financial partnership (capital — investment, revenue share, co-funding)** — the core question is governance and control. Proceed to Q4.

### Q2: What does each side bring that the other cannot build alone?

- **Both sides contribute something genuinely irreplaceable** — strong foundation. Proceed to Q3 to test incentive alignment.
- **One side's contribution could be built internally within 12-18 months** — likely diagnosis: **better-to-build**. The partnership buys speed, but at what cost in dependency?
- **One side's contribution is vague or aspirational ("access to their network," "brand association")** — likely diagnosis: **capability-illusion**. The promised value may never materialise.

### Q3: Are the incentives aligned, and will they remain so over time?

- **Yes — both parties benefit proportionally from the same outcomes** — proceed to Q4 to assess power dynamics.
- **Aligned now, but they diverge as the partnership scales** — likely diagnosis: **incentive-misalignment**. Model the scenarios: what happens when one side outgrows the other, or when market conditions shift?
- **Each side has a different definition of success** — likely diagnosis: **incentive-misalignment**. If success metrics do not overlap, the partnership will pull in two directions.

### Q4: Is there a power imbalance?

- **Roughly equal — similar size, similar dependency, similar alternatives** — proceed to Q5.
- **One side is significantly larger, better funded, or less dependent** — likely diagnosis: **power-asymmetry**. The smaller party will find that "partnership" gradually becomes "supplier relationship." Proceed to Q5.
- **One side has no alternatives; the other has many** — likely diagnosis: **power-asymmetry** compounded by **exit-trap**. The dependent party's negotiating position will erode over time.

### Q5: What happens if the partnership fails — can you walk away cleanly?

- **Yes — assets remain separate, customers are not shared, IP is clearly owned** — the exit cost is low, which paradoxically makes the partnership healthier.
- **Partially — some shared customers or co-developed IP would need to be divided** — likely diagnosis: **exit-trap** if no pre-negotiated separation terms exist. Define them now.
- **No — core product depends on partner's technology, or customers were acquired through partner's channels** — likely diagnosis: **exit-trap**. The partnership has created a dependency that makes departure destructive.

### Q6: Has the partnership's scope been clearly defined with success metrics?

- **Yes — written agreement with KPIs, milestones, and review cadence** — proceed to confirm that metrics are being tracked and meetings are happening.
- **Broadly defined — "we'll work together on X" without specifics** — likely diagnosis: **scope-creep**. Without boundaries, the partnership will expand into areas neither side intended, generating friction.
- **No metrics, no milestones, no review process** — likely diagnosis: **scope-creep** compounded by **accountability vacuum**. The partnership is a handshake with no mechanism for course correction.

## Diagnosis Map

| Diagnosis | Key Signals | Confirmation Path |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Capability illusion** | Partner's contribution is vague, promised value not yet demonstrated, references are thin | Q2 shows aspirational value + Q1 reveals strategic rationale is weak |
| **Incentive misalignment** | Different definitions of success, revenue-share disputes, diverging strategies | Q3 shows incentives diverge at scale + Q6 shows no shared metrics |
| **Power asymmetry** | One side is 10x larger, one side has alternatives the other lacks, terms favour the stronger party | Q4 confirms size or dependency imbalance + Q5 shows unequal exit costs |
| **Scope creep** | Partnership expanded beyond original intent, new obligations emerged, original metrics abandoned | Q6 shows vague scope + Q3 shows incentives shifting as scope broadened |
| **Exit trap** | Core dependencies on partner, shared customers or IP, no separation agreement | Q5 confirms entanglement + Q4 shows power imbalance makes renegotiation difficult |
| **Better to build** | Partner's contribution is replicable, 12-18 month internal build is feasible, partnership cost exceeds build cost | Q2 shows buildable capability + Q4 shows dependency risk exceeds speed benefit |

## Routing

**Capability illusion:**
1. Apply `trust-equation` — assess credibility, reliability, intimacy, and self-orientation of the partner before committing
2. Apply `porters-five-forces` — evaluate whether the partner's "capability" is actually available from multiple sources
3. Output with `partnership-memo` — document the due diligence findings
**Incentive misalignment:**
1. Apply `negotiation-game-theory` — model the payoff structure for both parties across multiple scenarios
2. Apply `cooperative-negotiation` — redesign the agreement to align incentives at scale, not just at launch
3. Output with `negotiation-prep-sheet`
**Power asymmetry:**
1. Apply `coalition-and-partnership-games` — understand the value each party contributes and how to maintain leverage
2. Apply `win-win-negotiation` — negotiate terms that protect the smaller party's position as the partnership evolves
3. Output with `negotiation-prep-sheet`
**Scope creep:**
1. Apply `cooperative-negotiation` — renegotiate boundaries with clear deliverables and exclusions
2. Apply `negotiation-game-theory` — model the cost of continued scope expansion versus the benefit of refocusing
3. Output with `partnership-memo` — redraft the partnership scope with explicit boundaries
**Exit trap:**
1. Apply `negotiation-game-theory` — map the cost of exit versus the cost of continued dependency
2. Apply `porters-five-forces` — identify alternative partners or internal capabilities that reduce dependency
3. Output with `negotiation-prep-sheet` — prepare for renegotiation or separation
**Better to build:**
1. Apply `porters-five-forces` — confirm that the capability can be developed internally without prohibitive barriers
2. Apply `coalition-and-partnership-games` — compare the total cost of partnership (financial, strategic, dependency) against the total cost of building
3. Output with `partnership-memo` — document the build-versus-partner analysis

## Red Flags

- **The partner insists on exclusivity without reciprocal commitments.** Exclusivity without symmetry is a power grab. The stronger party locks in optionality while restricting yours.
- **The partnership is driven by a single personal relationship.** When the executive champion departs, the foundation evaporates. Ensure the alliance is institutionally grounded.
- **Economics have not been discussed after three meetings.** Partnerships that avoid the money conversation are built on assumptions. The longer economics remain undefined, the more painful the reckoning.
- **The entrepreneur's primary motivation is the partner's brand name.** Brand halo is not a business model. Demand specific, quantifiable commitments.
