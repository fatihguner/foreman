# Market Entry Diagnosis

## Entry Symptoms

A new market gleams on the horizon. The entrepreneur arrives with some variation of: "We want to expand internationally," "There's an adjacent segment we should go after," "We got inbound interest from a new market," or "We've maxed out our current market — where next?" The appetite for expansion is healthy; the danger lies in confusing appetite with readiness.

## Triage Questions

### Q1: What type of market entry is this?

- **Geographic expansion (same product, new country or region)** — the core challenge is localisation, regulation, and distribution. Proceed to Q3.
- **Segment expansion (same geography, new customer type)** — the core challenge is value proposition adaptation and channel discovery. Proceed to Q2.
- **Product expansion (new product into an adjacent market)** — the core challenge is capability and competitive positioning. Proceed to Q4.

### Q2: What is driving the entry — pull or push?

- **Pull (customers in the new market are asking for it)** — this is the stronger signal. Proceed to Q3 to validate depth of demand.
- **Push (current market feels saturated or threatened)** — expansion as escape carries higher risk. Likely diagnosis: **premature-expansion** if the core is not truly saturated. Confirm with Q5.
- **Opportunistic (a partnership or event opened a door)** — proceed with caution. Opportunity is not strategy. Proceed to Q4 to assess capability fit.

### Q3: How different is the new market from your current one?

- **Very similar (same language, similar regulations, adjacent culture)** — entry friction is low. Focus on competitive dynamics. Proceed to Q6.
- **Moderately different (different regulations, some cultural distance, new competitors)** — proceed to Q4 to identify capability gaps.
- **Fundamentally different (new language, unfamiliar regulations, no existing brand awareness)** — high entry cost. Likely diagnosis: **resource-underestimation** or **wrong-entry-mode**. Proceed to Q4 and Q5.

### Q4: Do you have the capabilities to serve the new market?

- **Yes — the product works as-is, and we have relevant operational capacity** — proceed to Q6 for competitive and timing assessment.
- **Partially — the product needs modification, or we lack local expertise** — likely diagnosis: **capability-gap**. Quantify what must be built, hired, or acquired.
- **No — serving this market requires fundamentally new capabilities** — likely diagnosis: **capability-gap** compounded by **resource-underestimation**. Consider whether partnership is the right entry mode (Q5).

### Q5: Can you test the market before committing fully?

- **Yes — a pilot, a partnership, or a limited launch is feasible** — this reduces risk substantially. Validate demand with minimal capital exposure before scaling commitment.
- **No — the market requires significant upfront investment (regulatory approval, physical infrastructure, local team)** — likely diagnosis: **resource-underestimation** if the entrepreneur has not modelled the full cost. Proceed to Q6.
- **We already committed significant resources** — the question is no longer whether to enter but how to succeed. Shift from entry diagnosis to execution diagnosis.

### Q6: What does the competitive landscape look like in the target market?

- **Fragmented — no dominant player, many small competitors** — favourable entry conditions. Apply competitive analysis to identify positioning.
- **Consolidated — one or two dominant incumbents** — entry will require a differentiated value proposition or a niche strategy. Likely diagnosis: **market-misread** if the entrepreneur expects to compete head-on without differentiation.
- **A well-funded competitor just entered the same market** — likely diagnosis: **timing-error**. The window may be closing, or the cost of entry just increased materially.
- **No competitors (virgin market)** — either the opportunity is genuinely untapped or the market does not exist. Likely diagnosis: **market-misread** if demand is assumed rather than validated.

## Diagnosis Map

| Diagnosis | Key Signals | Confirmation Path |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Premature expansion** | Core market not fully captured, push-driven entry, core operations still unstable | Q2 shows push motivation + Q5 reveals core market has room |
| **Market misread** | Assumed demand not validated, no competitor presence (possible absent market), surface-level research | Q6 shows empty or impenetrable market + Q2 shows no pull signal |
| **Capability gap** | Product needs modification, local expertise missing, operational capacity insufficient | Q4 confirms partial or absent capability + Q3 shows market distance |
| **Wrong entry mode** | Should partner but planning to build, or building when acquisition is cheaper | Q4 shows gaps + Q5 shows testing is possible but entrepreneur chose full commitment |
| **Resource underestimation** | Entry costs modelled optimistically, timeline aggressive, hidden costs not accounted for | Q3 shows high market distance + Q5 shows significant upfront investment required |
| **Timing error** | Competitor just entered, regulatory window closing, market not yet ready | Q6 shows recent competitive entry or absent demand + Q2 shows opportunistic trigger |

## Routing

**Premature expansion:**
1. Apply `business-model-canvas` — stress-test whether the core business model is robust enough to fund and survive a second front
2. Apply `swot-pest-analysis` — honestly assess internal weaknesses that expansion would amplify
3. Output with `strategic-analysis-one-pager`
**Market misread:**
1. Apply `porters-five-forces` — rigorously analyse the target market's structure, not the entrepreneur's assumptions about it
2. Apply `scenario-planning` — model multiple demand scenarios to pressure-test the opportunity thesis
3. Output with `market-opportunity-summary`
**Capability gap:**
1. Apply `swot-pest-analysis` — catalogue what the organisation has versus what the new market demands
2. Apply `business-model-canvas` — redesign the value proposition for the new market's requirements
3. Output with `competitive-landscape-brief`
**Wrong entry mode:**
1. Apply `first-mover-strategy` — determine whether speed (partnering) or control (building) matters more
2. Apply `porters-five-forces` — assess bargaining power dynamics with potential partners
3. Output with `strategic-analysis-one-pager`
**Resource underestimation:**
1. Apply `scenario-planning` — model best-case, base-case, and worst-case resource requirements
2. Apply `business-model-canvas` — map all cost structures and revenue assumptions for the new market
3. Output with `market-opportunity-summary`
**Timing error:**
1. Apply `first-mover-strategy` — evaluate whether first-mover advantage exists or whether fast-follower is preferable
2. Apply `blue-ocean-strategy` — identify whether the market can be entered from an uncontested angle
3. Output with `competitive-landscape-brief`

## Red Flags

- **The entrepreneur cannot articulate why the new market will buy.** "It's a big market" is not a thesis. Without a validated demand signal, expansion is speculation funded with operational capital.
- **Core unit economics are negative, and expansion is positioned as the fix.** Scaling a broken model into a new market multiplies losses. Fix unit economics at home first.
- **The expansion plan has no exit criteria.** Every market entry should define what failure looks like and when to pull back. Without these, sunk cost bias extends losing commitments indefinitely.
- **The entire case rests on a single partnership or customer request.** One data point is an anecdote. Market entry requires pattern recognition across multiple signals.
