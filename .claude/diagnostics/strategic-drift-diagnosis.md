# Strategic Drift Diagnosis

Strategy dies quietly. It rarely announces its departure with a dramatic pivot or a board revolt. Instead, it erodes through a thousand small decisions -- each individually reasonable, collectively fatal. The founder adds one more product line. The team chases a tangential partnership. A competitor's move triggers a reactive sprint. Six months later, no one can articulate what the company actually does, and every Monday feels like starting over.

---

## Entry Symptoms

The entrepreneur typically presents with one or more of these complaints:

- **"We've lost focus."** The company is working on many things but excelling at none; energy is dispersed across too many fronts.
- **"I don't know our strategy anymore."** The founder cannot state the company's strategic direction in a single clear sentence -- or gives a different answer each time asked.
- **"We're doing too many things."** The product roadmap resembles a wish list; the team is spread thin; nothing ships with conviction.
- **"The team doesn't know what matters."** Employees prioritise based on whoever shouted last; there is no shared understanding of strategic priorities.
- **"We keep changing direction."** Quarterly plans are abandoned by month two; the team experiences whiplash from frequent course corrections.

---

## Triage Questions

Work through these sequentially. Each answer narrows the diagnosis.

1. **Historical clarity**: Was there ever a clear, articulated strategy that the team understood and could repeat? Or has the company always operated without one?
2. **Erosion pattern**: Did focus erode gradually over months, or did a specific event trigger the drift (new competitor, failed launch, new investor pressure)?
3. **Source of distraction**: Is the founder the primary source of new directions and tangential ideas? Or is the drift coming from the team, the market, or external stakeholders?
4. **Priority count**: How many "top priorities" does the company currently have? Fewer than three suggests zero clarity; more than five suggests priority proliferation.
5. **Market signal**: Has the market genuinely shifted in ways that demand strategic adaptation? Or is the company changing direction while the market remains stable?
6. **Decision architecture**: Is there a regular cadence for strategic review (quarterly, monthly)? Or do strategic decisions happen reactively, in the moment?

## Diagnosis Map

| Diagnosis | Key Indicators | Core Problem |
|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| **Vision Erosion** | Original vision was clear but has faded; no one references it; daily decisions have disconnected from founding purpose | The strategy existed but was never reinforced, documented, or tied to operational decisions -- it withered from neglect |
| **Founder-Driven Distraction** | New ideas arrive weekly from the founder; team cannot finish projects before the next pivot; the founder's enthusiasm outpaces organisational capacity | The founder confuses ideation with strategy; their creative energy, unfiltered, destabilises the team |
| **Priority Proliferation** | Everything is priority one; resource allocation resembles peanut butter spread evenly; no initiative receives sufficient investment to succeed | The organisation cannot say no; each new opportunity is added without removing an existing one |
| **Market Shift Unacknowledged** | Customer behaviour has changed, competitor landscape has shifted, but the company's strategy still reflects the old reality | The environment has moved and the company has not; the drift is not internal chaos but external denial |
| **Strategic Misalignment** | Different departments or co-founders hold different understandings of the strategy; execution diverges despite apparent agreement at the top | Alignment was assumed but never verified; the strategy was communicated once and interpreted differently by each listener |
| **Shiny Object Syndrome** | Pattern of chasing trends (AI, crypto, new markets) without connection to core competence; excitement-driven rather than analysis-driven decisions | The company pursues novelty over depth; each new trend feels urgent but produces no lasting strategic value |

## Red Flags

- **The founder describes constant pivoting as "agility."** Genuine agility is deliberate adaptation with clear reasoning. If direction changes lack documented rationale, the company is drifting, not adapting. The founder may resist this diagnosis.
- **Strategic drift is actually strategic absence.** Some companies never had a strategy to lose. If triage question one reveals there was never a clear strategy, the problem is not drift but omission. Route to strategic-thinking-and-alignment as a foundational exercise, not a corrective one.
- **Multiple co-founders pulling in different directions.** Strategic misalignment among co-founders is a governance crisis, not a planning problem. Surface this explicitly; frameworks alone will not resolve it without direct conversation and decision-rights clarity.
- **The team has stopped caring about strategy announcements.** Repeated direction changes create learned helplessness. If the team ignores new priorities because they expect them to change, the founder must rebuild credibility through consistent follow-through before any strategic exercise will be taken seriously.
- **"We keep changing direction" but revenue is growing.** Examine whether the perceived drift is actually healthy experimentation that the founder has not yet recognised as strategic search. Early-stage companies exploring product-market fit may exhibit symptoms of drift that are, in reality, appropriate discovery.
