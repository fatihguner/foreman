# The Acquisition Evaluation Playbook

## When to Use This Playbook

Most acquisitions destroy value. The research is unambiguous: between 70% and 90% of acquisitions fail to deliver their projected synergies, and a substantial fraction leave the acquirer worse off than before. The acquirer overpays. The target's talent departs. The cultures collide. The integration stalls. Yet acquisitions remain among the most consequential decisions a company can make -- and among the most time-pressured. Offers arrive with deadlines. Competitive bidders create urgency. The emotional weight of "this could change everything" clouds judgment precisely when clarity matters most.

This playbook applies on both sides of the table: whether the company has received an acquisition offer it must evaluate or is considering acquiring another company. The analytical steps are symmetric; the orientation shifts. It does not cover the legal mechanics of deal execution (those require M&A counsel) but rather the strategic evaluation, human dynamics, and negotiation preparation that determine whether the deal should happen and on what terms.

## Prerequisites

- A clear understanding of the company's standalone trajectory (growth rate, runway, strategic options)
- Preliminary financial data on the other party (revenue, margins, growth, key metrics)
- Access to legal counsel with M&A experience (this playbook complements, not replaces, legal advice)
- A defined decision-making group: who ultimately says yes or no, and what is their process?
- Emotional honesty about the motivations driving the deal -- fatigue, ego, fear, greed, and genuine strategic logic all present differently but feel the same under pressure

## The Sequence

### Step 1: Evaluating and Deciding
- **Purpose:** Before analysing the specifics, establish the decision framework. Is this acquisition a one-way door -- irreversible once closed -- or can it be unwound? What is the cost of saying no? What is the cost of saying yes to the wrong terms? This step prevents the common failure of optimising deal terms on a deal that should not happen at all.
- **Key questions:** What is the company's trajectory without this deal? What specific strategic gap does the acquisition fill? Is this the only path to that objective, or are there alternatives? What would a sceptical board member ask?
- **Input:** Company strategic plan, current performance data, the acquisition proposal or target profile
- **Output:** Decision framework with go/no-go criteria, reversibility assessment, and alternative paths analysis
- **Time:** 1.5-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** If the primary motivation is fear (competitor acquiring the target, market window closing, runway shrinking), acknowledge it explicitly. Fear-driven acquisitions have the highest failure rate. The framework must separate strategic logic from emotional pressure.

## Decision Point

After completing Step 1, the fundamental orientation:

**Are you the buyer or the target?**

- **If you are the buyer:** The remaining steps orient toward evaluating the target's strategic fit, assessing integration complexity, and preparing to negotiate from a position of optionality. Emphasis falls on Steps 2-3 (competitive and strategic assessment) and Step 4 (post-acquisition dynamics). The negotiation steps (5-6) focus on price discipline, earn-out structures, and retention mechanisms.
- **If you are the target:** The remaining steps orient toward evaluating the offer's fairness, understanding the acquirer's true motivations, and preparing to negotiate from a position of maximum leverage. Emphasis falls on Step 1 (the standalone alternative, which defines your BATNA), Step 4 (what your life looks like inside the acquirer), and Steps 5-6 (protecting founder interests, team welfare, and product vision in the terms).

Both paths follow the same step sequence. The questions asked at each step, the evidence weighted, and the negotiation posture differ fundamentally.

### Step 2: Porter's Five Forces
- **Purpose:** An acquisition reshapes the competitive landscape -- not just for the two parties but for every player in the market. This step maps the before-and-after: how the combined entity changes buyer power, supplier dynamics, competitive rivalry, threat of new entrants, and substitute availability. The analysis reveals whether the deal creates durable competitive advantage or merely creates a larger target.
- **Key questions:** Does the combination increase pricing power or simply add revenue? Does it raise barriers to entry for competitors? Does it create a substitute threat for adjacent markets? How will competitors respond -- and is that response factored into the synergy projections?
- **Input:** Decision framework from Step 1, market data, competitive intelligence on both entities
- **Output:** Comparative five forces analysis: current state versus post-acquisition state with competitive response scenarios
- **Time:** 1.5-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** If the five forces analysis shows that the combined entity's competitive position is only marginally better than the standalone position, the strategic rationale for the deal is weak. Financial engineering alone (revenue addition without competitive improvement) is not a sufficient reason to accept the complexity of integration.

### Step 3: SWOT-PEST Analysis
- **Purpose:** Due diligence on the numbers is necessary but insufficient. This step conducts strategic due diligence: an honest assessment of both entities' strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats, plus the macro environment (political, economic, social, technological) that will shape the combined entity's future.
- **Key questions:** Which weaknesses of one entity are strengths of the other (genuine synergy)? Which weaknesses compound (compounding risk)? What macro trends support or undermine the combined thesis? Where do the cultures diverge on values, speed, or risk tolerance?
- **Input:** Five forces analysis from Step 2, financial data on both entities, culture and team assessments
- **Output:** Dual SWOT matrix with synergy mapping (where strengths complement) and conflict mapping (where weaknesses compound), plus PEST overlay
- **Time:** 1.5-2.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** The combined SWOT must include at least three honest vulnerabilities of the deal itself -- not just the entities. Integration risk, culture clash, and customer confusion are real threats that deserve the same analytical rigour as market opportunities.

### Step 4: Coalition and Partnership Games
- **Purpose:** The deal closes in weeks. The integration takes years. This step models the human dynamics of the post-acquisition reality: who holds power, who loses it, where coalitions form, where resistance emerges, and which key people leave (taking institutional knowledge with them). More acquisitions fail in integration than in negotiation.
- **Key questions:** Which leaders on both sides gain or lose power? Where will cultural norms clash (decision speed, risk tolerance, communication style)? What retention mechanisms keep critical talent? How do you prevent the "us versus them" dynamic from calcifying?
- **Input:** SWOT analysis from Step 3, organisational structures of both entities, key-person dependencies
- **Output:** Post-acquisition dynamics model with coalition map, key-person risk matrix, and integration scenario analysis
- **Time:** 1.5-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** If the model shows that more than 30% of key personnel are flight risks post-close, the integration plan must address retention before the deal is signed, not after. Earnouts and retention bonuses are table stakes -- the deeper question is whether the acquiring culture gives those people a reason to stay beyond the vesting period.

### Step 5: Negotiation Mindset and Self
- **Purpose:** Acquisition negotiations are emotionally charged in ways that fundraising and sales negotiations are not. The founder's identity, legacy, team, and financial future are all on the table simultaneously. This step prepares the psychological ground: identifying biases, mapping emotional triggers, and building the mental resilience to negotiate clearly when the stakes are personal.
- **Key questions:** Where are you most vulnerable to anchoring (the first number named)? What emotional triggers might cause you to accept terms you would reject in a calmer state? Do you have a trusted advisor who will challenge your rationalisation? Have you defined your walk-away point before emotions intensify?
- **Input:** Decision framework from Step 1, personal financial situation, advisor network
- **Output:** Negotiation self-assessment with identified biases, emotional trigger plan, and walk-away criteria defined in advance
- **Time:** 1-1.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** The walk-away point must be written down and shared with at least one trusted advisor before the negotiation begins. A walk-away point that exists only in the founder's mind will shift under pressure. A written one, witnessed by an advisor, holds.

### Step 6: Delivering the Deal
- **Purpose:** The final step structures the terms, anticipates obstacles, and designs the closing sequence. Good deals die in the last mile -- over working-capital adjustments, indemnification clauses, non-compete scope, or integration timeline disagreements. This step maps the terrain between handshake and signature.
- **Key questions:** What are the three to five most important terms beyond price? Where should you concede to preserve goodwill, and where must you hold firm? What are the likely last-minute obstacles (board approval, regulatory review, financing conditions)? What does success look like 12 months post-close?
- **Input:** All previous outputs, legal counsel input, term sheet or offer letter
- **Output:** Deal structure outline with term prioritisation, concession strategy, obstacle contingency plan, and post-close success criteria
- **Time:** 2-3 hours
- **Checkpoint:** The deal structure must satisfy the interests identified in Step 1, not just the positions adopted during negotiation. If the final terms achieve the price target but sacrifice the strategic objectives that justified the deal, the negotiation has succeeded tactically and failed strategically.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Falling in love with the deal.** Once a founder emotionally commits to an acquisition -- as buyer or target -- objectivity evaporates. The decision framework in Step 1 exists to anchor analysis before emotions engage. Return to it whenever the conversation shifts from "should we?" to "how do we make this work?"
2. **Neglecting the integration model.** The synergy spreadsheet is fiction until the integration plan is reality. Step 4 forces confrontation with the human complexity that synergy projections ignore: culture clash, power dynamics, and talent flight.
3. **Anchoring on the headline number.** A $50M acquisition at 2x revenue with onerous earn-out terms, aggressive non-competes, and a hostile integration plan may be worth less than a $35M acquisition with clean terms and genuine autonomy. Price is one variable among many.
4. **Rushing due diligence under artificial time pressure.** Acquirers create urgency. Targets create competitive tension. Both are negotiation tactics, not immutable deadlines. The cost of an additional two weeks of diligence is trivial compared to the cost of discovering a material problem post-close.
5. **Ignoring the standalone alternative.** The most important number in any acquisition evaluation is the value of the company if the deal does not happen. If the standalone trajectory is strong, the walk-away position is strong. If the standalone trajectory is deteriorating, the negotiation leverage weakens -- and the founder must account for that honestly.

## Adaptation Notes

- **Growth stage:** Acquisitions at this stage are typically acqui-hires (buying talent), tuck-ins (buying product capabilities), or consolidation plays. The emphasis falls on Steps 3-4 (strategic fit and integration dynamics) because the acquirer is still building its own culture and processes. Adding a poorly fitting acquisition to an immature organisation compounds complexity rather than resolving it.
- **Scale stage:** Acquisitions become more sophisticated: market expansion, vertical integration, competitive elimination, or platform plays. All six steps carry full weight. The five forces analysis (Step 2) becomes critical because the competitive response to a large acquisition can be aggressive -- competitors may counter-acquire, form alliances, or accelerate product development. The deal's value depends not just on the two parties but on the entire market's reaction.
- At either stage, the founder must resist the gravitational pull of deal momentum. The best acquisition discipline is the willingness to walk away at any point in the process when the evidence no longer supports the thesis. Sunk costs in legal fees, management time, and emotional investment are real -- and irrelevant to the forward-looking decision.
