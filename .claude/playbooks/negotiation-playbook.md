# Negotiation Playbook

Most founders enter their most consequential negotiations having prepared the spreadsheet but not themselves. They know their numbers cold and their strategy not at all. The term sheet negotiation that determines ownership dilution, the partnership deal that shapes distribution for three years, the acquisition conversation that could end or transform the company -- these moments compress years of value creation into hours of value division. Preparation is not optional, and preparation means more than rehearsing your ask. It means understanding the game, the other player, the information landscape, and your own psychological wiring under pressure.

---

## When to Use This Playbook

- Fundraising: negotiating term sheets, SAFEs, or convertible notes with investors
- Acquisition: receiving or making an offer to buy or merge
- Partnerships: structuring a deal with a distribution, technology, or strategic partner
- Enterprise sales: negotiating a contract that represents a significant portion of revenue
- Any negotiation where the outcome materially changes the company's trajectory or the founder's equity position
- Situations where the founder feels outmatched by a more experienced counterparty

## Prerequisites

- A specific, identified negotiation with a known counterparty (this is a preparation playbook, not a general education)
- Basic understanding of the subject matter (term sheet mechanics, deal structure, contract terms)
- Honest willingness to examine personal negotiation weaknesses
- Sufficient time before the negotiation to complete preparation -- rushing this playbook defeats its purpose

---

## The Sequence

### Step 1: Negotiation Mindset and Self -- Know Your Defaults

Before analyzing the other side, analyze yourself. Every negotiator carries default tendencies: some capitulate too quickly to preserve relationships; others become adversarial and destroy value trying to "win." Identify where you fall on the cooperative-competitive spectrum, how you respond to pressure, what triggers emotional reactions, and where your ego is most vulnerable. The founder who understands their own patterns can interrupt them. The one who does not will discover them at the worst possible moment.

**Checkpoint:** Written self-assessment completed. At least two personal default tendencies identified with specific countermeasures planned.

### Step 2: Game Theory Fundamentals -- Map the Strategic Structure

Every negotiation has a structure that exists independently of the personalities involved. Map it: What does each party want? What are the payoffs for each possible outcome? Is this a zero-sum division or does value-creation potential exist? Are there multiple rounds or is this a single interaction? Understanding the game structure prevents you from treating a cooperative opportunity as adversarial, or vice versa. It also reveals whether the counterparty's stated position is strategically rational or a bluff.

**Checkpoint:** Game structure documented. Payoff matrix or decision tree created showing each party's incentives and likely strategies.

### Step 3: Information Asymmetry -- Map the Knowledge Landscape

In nearly every negotiation, one party knows something the other does not. Investors know the state of the funding market; founders know the true state of the product pipeline. Map the information landscape explicitly: what do you know that they do not? What do they know that you do not? What do neither of you know? This analysis determines your disclosure strategy (what to reveal and when), your intelligence-gathering priorities (what to learn before or during the negotiation), and where the counterparty's leverage is real versus manufactured.

**Checkpoint:** Information asymmetry map completed. Disclosure strategy drafted. Key questions for the negotiation identified.

---

### Decision Point: One-Shot or Ongoing Relationship?

The balance between value creation and value claiming depends fundamentally on this question.

**If this is a one-shot negotiation** (unlikely to interact with this counterparty again, or the relationship's value is captured entirely in this deal): Weight Step 5 (competitive negotiation) more heavily. Claiming your fair share matters more than leaving the counterparty delighted. This does not mean being adversarial -- it means being precise about your interests and firm about your reservation points.

**If this is a long-term relationship** (an investor who will sit on your board, a partner you will work with for years, a customer you need to renew): Weight Step 4 (cooperative negotiation) more heavily. The surplus generated by creative value-creation will compound over the relationship's duration and far outweigh marginal gains from aggressive claiming. Reputation effects matter enormously -- the negotiation community in any industry is smaller than founders assume.

Most founder negotiations are long-term relationships. Act accordingly.

---

### Step 4: Cooperative Negotiation -- Create Value Before Claiming It

Identify the interests behind each party's stated positions. Positions are what people say they want; interests are why they want it. An investor demanding a board seat may be seeking information access, not governance control. A partner demanding exclusivity may be seeking commitment assurance, not market restriction. Once interests are exposed, creative options emerge that satisfy both parties better than either's original position. Design at least three value-creating proposals that expand the total value available.

**Checkpoint:** Interest analysis completed for both sides. At least three integrative options developed and ranked by mutual benefit.

### Step 5: Competitive Negotiation -- Protect Your Position

Value creation has limits. At some point, the pie must be divided, and this phase requires different skills. Define your BATNA (best alternative to negotiated agreement) with ruthless honesty -- not what you hope your alternative is, but what it actually is. Set your reservation point (the worst deal you will accept). Plan your concession strategy: which issues will you concede, in what order, and what will you expect in return? Prepare for common competitive tactics the counterparty may employ: anchoring, artificial deadlines, good cop/bad cop, and silence as pressure.

**Checkpoint:** BATNA clearly defined. Reservation point set. Concession strategy documented with trade-offs mapped.

### Step 6: Delivering the Deal -- Plan for the Close

Negotiations fail in the final stretch more often than founders expect. Last-minute deal fatigue, new stakeholders appearing, legal review inserting unexpected terms, buyer's remorse on either side -- anticipate all of it. Plan the closing sequence: who signs what, in what order, with what contingencies? Define what "satisfaction" looks like for both parties beyond the contract terms. A deal that leaves the counterparty resentful is a deal that creates problems in execution.

**Checkpoint:** Closing plan documented. Obstacle scenarios identified with contingency responses. Post-signature relationship management planned.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Preparing the numbers but not the psychology.** Founders who know their valuation model but not their emotional triggers get outmaneuvered by experienced negotiators.
- **Falling in love with the deal.** The moment you need this specific deal to close, your BATNA effectively becomes zero and the counterparty owns the negotiation. Maintain genuine alternatives.
- **Treating all negotiations as zero-sum.** Most founder negotiations involve long-term relationships where value creation dominates value claiming. Winning the battle and losing the relationship is a net negative.
- **Negotiating against yourself.** Making concessions before the counterparty asks for them is a surprisingly common founder behavior, driven by anxiety about the relationship. Wait for the ask.
- **Ignoring the decision journal.** The preparation sheet is useful before the negotiation. The decision journal -- recording what happened, what worked, what you would change -- is what makes you better at the next one.

## Adaptation Notes

- **First-time fundraisers** should invest extra time in Step 3 (information asymmetry). The knowledge gap between a seasoned VC and a first-time founder is substantial, and investors are not obligated to close it.
- **Acquisition negotiations** require particular attention to Step 6 (delivering the deal). Acquisition processes involve more stakeholders, longer timelines, and more failure points than any other negotiation type founders typically encounter.
- **Technical founders** negotiating with business-oriented counterparties should be aware that different communication styles can create false impressions of agreement or disagreement. Step 1's self-assessment should include communication style analysis.
- **Solo founders** without a negotiation team should consider engaging an advisor or mentor for Steps 4 and 5 specifically -- having a second perspective on value-creation options and competitive tactics is disproportionately valuable when negotiating alone.
