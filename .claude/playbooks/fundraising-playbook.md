# The Fundraising Playbook

## When to Use This Playbook

The founder has decided to raise external capital. The decision itself is already made -- this playbook does not address whether to raise. It addresses how to raise well: with a model that survives scrutiny, a narrative that compels, data that validates, and a negotiation posture that protects founder interests.

Most fundraising fails not in the pitch meeting but in the preparation. Founders build decks before they build arguments. They rehearse slides before they understand their own unit economics. This playbook reverses that sequence: substance first, presentation last.

## Prerequisites

- A clear funding target (amount and use of proceeds)
- At least 3 months of financial data (revenue, burn, margins) or a detailed financial model
- A functioning product with some form of customer traction (revenue, users, LOIs, or pilots)
- An honest assessment of current runway
- Willingness to hear that the company may not be ready to raise

## The Sequence

### Step 1: Business Model Canvas
- **Purpose:** Investors fund models, not products. This step forces the founder to articulate the complete business model with the precision that due diligence demands. Every canvas block must be defensible.
- **Key questions:** What are the unit economics? Which assumptions remain unvalidated? Where does the model break at scale? What is the LTV:CAC ratio?
- **Input:** Financial data, customer acquisition data, pricing model, channel performance
- **Output:** Investor-grade Business Model Canvas with unit economics overlay
- **Time:** 1.5-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** If unit economics are negative with no credible path to positive, stop. Fix the fundamentals before raising.

## Decision Point

After completing Step 1, a critical gate:

**Is the business actually ready to raise?**

- If unit economics are broken (negative gross margins, CAC exceeding LTV with no improvement trajectory, or a business model that requires magical thinking to reach profitability) -- pause the playbook. Fix the model first. Raising on broken economics means raising at a bad valuation with desperate terms.
- If the model is sound but early, or if the economics work at modest scale -- proceed. Investors at pre-seed and seed expect incomplete data, but they do not forgive incoherent models.

Raising money on a broken model does not fix the model. It postpones the reckoning while adding dilution.

### Step 2: SWOT-PEST Analysis
- **Purpose:** Every competent investor will probe weaknesses. Founders who discover their own vulnerabilities first can frame them as risks being actively managed rather than blind spots. PEST analysis surfaces macro trends that strengthen or undermine the thesis.
- **Key questions:** Which weaknesses will investors find in diligence? Which macro trends support the timing argument? What regulatory or market shifts could invalidate the thesis?
- **Input:** Business Model Canvas from Step 1, market research, regulatory landscape
- **Output:** SWOT-PEST matrix with pre-written responses to likely investor objections
- **Time:** 1-1.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** Every weakness must have a mitigation plan. Every threat must have a monitoring trigger. Investors respect founders who know where the bodies are buried.

### Step 3: Porter's Five Forces
- **Purpose:** "What's your competitive moat?" is the question investors ask most and founders answer worst. This step builds a rigorous competitive analysis that goes beyond a logo slide of competitors.
- **Key questions:** How strong is buyer power? What is the threat of substitution? Where are the barriers to entry? Who are the real competitors -- not just the obvious ones?
- **Input:** Market data, competitor intelligence, SWOT output from Step 2
- **Output:** Five forces map with defensibility narrative and specific moat evidence
- **Time:** 1-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** If the five forces analysis reveals a market with low barriers, high substitution threat, and powerful buyers -- the fundraising narrative must address this head-on. Ignoring it is worse than owning it.

### Step 4: Net Promoter Score
- **Purpose:** Revenue tells investors what happened. NPS tells them what will happen. A high NPS with qualitative depth proves that customers are not just buying but advocating -- the leading indicator of sustainable growth.
- **Key questions:** What is the NPS by segment? What do promoters say in their own words? What drives detractors? How does NPS correlate with retention?
- **Input:** Customer survey data, support tickets, churn data, qualitative interviews
- **Output:** NPS analysis with segment breakdown, trend data, and verbatim quotes suitable for the deck
- **Time:** 1-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** If NPS is below 30, the product story in the deck must lean on other proof points. Low NPS does not prevent fundraising, but it changes the narrative from "customers love us" to "we have a clear path to product excellence."

### Step 5: Story Structure and Arc
- **Purpose:** A fundraising pitch is a story. The best decks follow a narrative arc: the world has a problem, the problem is getting worse, we discovered something others missed, here is the proof, here is what happens next. This step constructs that arc with the rigor of professional storytelling.
- **Key questions:** What is the founding insight -- the moment of discovery? Where are the stakes? What is the "But & Therefore" structure of the narrative?
- **Input:** All outputs from Steps 1-4, founder's personal story, market timing data
- **Output:** Complete narrative arc with opening hook, escalating stakes, traction proof, and vision close
- **Time:** 1-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** Test the narrative on someone outside the company. If they cannot retell the core thesis in two sentences after hearing it once, the story is not sharp enough.

### Step 6: Competitive Negotiation
- **Purpose:** Term sheets arrive fast and expire faster. Founders who have not prepared a negotiation strategy accept terms they later regret. This step builds the tactical framework before the pressure begins.
- **Key questions:** What is the BATNA? What terms are non-negotiable? Where can you concede without structural damage? How do you create competitive tension between investors?
- **Input:** Funding target, current cap table, market comparables, investor pipeline
- **Output:** Negotiation preparation sheet with BATNA, red lines, concession hierarchy, and anchoring strategy
- **Time:** 1-1.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** If the BATNA is "run out of money," the negotiating position is weak by definition. Acknowledge this internally and plan accordingly -- but never signal it externally.

### Step 7: Writing for Navigation
- **Purpose:** Investors skim decks in under three minutes during first pass. If the deck is not scannable, the story from Step 5 never gets heard. This step applies cognitive science to deck structure: headlines that carry the argument, visual hierarchy that guides the eye, and information architecture that survives rapid scanning.
- **Key questions:** Can an investor extract the core thesis from headlines alone? Is every slide earning its place? Does the deck flow as a narrative when read top-to-bottom?
- **Input:** Story arc from Step 5, all analytical outputs from Steps 1-4
- **Output:** Deck structure with slide-by-slide headlines, hierarchy notes, and navigation cues
- **Time:** 1-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** Print the deck. Read only the headlines. If the headlines alone tell a coherent, compelling story, the structure is sound.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Building the deck before the model.** A beautiful presentation of broken economics is still broken economics. The canvas and unit economics come first -- always.
2. **Hiding weaknesses.** Sophisticated investors discover every weakness in diligence. Founders who surface vulnerabilities proactively and pair them with mitigation plans build trust. Founders who hide them destroy it.
3. **Optimizing for the first meeting.** The first meeting gets the second meeting. The due diligence survives or kills the deal. This playbook weights preparation for diligence, not just the pitch.
4. **Neglecting negotiation preparation.** Most founders spend 95% of fundraising energy on getting to "yes" and 5% on terms. The terms matter more. A bad term sheet on a good valuation is worse than a fair term sheet on a lower one.
5. **Raising too early.** The decision point after Step 1 exists for a reason. Capital raised on a broken model creates a ticking clock with investor expectations attached. Sometimes the best fundraising decision is to wait.

## Adaptation Notes

- **Validation stage:** Investors expect a thesis, not a spreadsheet. Lean harder on Steps 5 (narrative) and 3 (market analysis). Unit economics will be modeled, not proven -- acknowledge the assumptions explicitly.
- **Early-traction stage:** The balance shifts toward data. NPS (Step 4) and unit economics (Step 1) carry disproportionate weight. The narrative should be "here is what we have proven" rather than "here is what we believe."
- **Growth stage:** Investors at this stage are buying a machine, not a story. The canvas must show scalable economics. The five forces analysis must demonstrate durable competitive advantages. Negotiation preparation (Step 6) becomes critical as deal sizes increase and terms grow more complex.
