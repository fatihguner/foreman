# The Launch Playbook

## When to Use This Playbook

A product is ready to meet its market -- or a proven product is entering a new one. The technology works. The team is assembled. What remains is the strategic question most founders answer with intuition rather than analysis: how exactly do we enter, and what does success look like?

Launches fail for predictable reasons. The founder overestimates market readiness, underestimates competitive response, prices without data, distributes without strategy, and communicates without compelling the audience to act. This playbook addresses each failure mode in sequence before the launch window opens.

## Prerequisites

- A product or service that is functionally ready (MVP minimum)
- Basic market research: target customer profile, market size estimate, known competitors
- A defined launch timeline with hard constraints (funding milestones, seasonal windows, partnership deadlines)
- Budget parameters for go-to-market spend
- At least one person accountable for launch execution

## The Sequence

### Step 1: Porter's Five Forces
- **Purpose:** Before entering a market, understand its structure. The forces that govern profitability -- buyer power, supplier power, rivalry intensity, substitution threat, and entry barriers -- determine whether the market is worth entering at all and where the points of leverage exist.
- **Key questions:** Who holds the power in this market? What barriers will slow your entry? How intense is existing rivalry? What substitutes compete for the same budget?
- **Input:** Market research, competitor data, customer interviews, industry reports
- **Output:** Five forces analysis with a clear assessment of market attractiveness and entry strategy implications
- **Time:** 1-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** If all five forces are unfavorable -- high rivalry, powerful buyers, low barriers inviting more entrants, strong substitutes, and concentrated suppliers -- reconsider the entry or narrow the beachhead dramatically.

### Step 2: Blue Ocean Strategy
- **Purpose:** Competing on the same dimensions as incumbents is expensive and usually fatal for startups. The strategy canvas reveals which factors the industry competes on and where the opportunity exists to diverge -- to eliminate, reduce, raise, or create factors that define a new value curve.
- **Key questions:** On which factors does the industry over-deliver relative to what customers actually value? Where is the market under-serving customers? What can you eliminate entirely to fund investment elsewhere?
- **Input:** Five forces output from Step 1, customer feedback, competitor feature matrices
- **Output:** Strategy canvas with existing industry curve and your proposed divergent curve, plus an Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create grid
- **Time:** 1-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** The proposed value curve must diverge visibly from the industry standard on at least two dimensions. If it tracks the industry curve closely, you are entering a red ocean with a different logo.

## Decision Point

After completing Steps 1 and 2, assess the strategic landscape:

**Is there a genuine blue ocean, or are you entering a red ocean?**

- **Blue ocean identified:** Proceed with confidence. The differentiation is real, defensible, and valued by customers. Steps 3-6 will translate this advantage into execution.
- **Red ocean confirmed:** Do not abandon the launch, but adjust expectations and strategy. A red ocean entry requires superior execution, not superior strategy. Tighten the beachhead, reduce the initial scope, increase the marketing budget, and compress the timeline to profitability. Consider whether a niche within the red ocean offers blue ocean characteristics.

Entering a red ocean knowingly is not failure. Entering one unknowingly is.

### Step 3: Four Ps Marketing
- **Purpose:** Translate strategic positioning into tactical go-to-market decisions. Product-market fit is necessary but insufficient -- the product must also be priced correctly, distributed through the right channels, and promoted to the right audience with the right message.
- **Key questions:** Does the pricing reflect the value curve from Step 2? Which channels reach the beachhead customers most efficiently? What is the promotion strategy for the first 90 days? Where does the budget concentrate?
- **Input:** Blue ocean output, customer segments, channel cost data, competitive pricing
- **Output:** Four Ps plan with channel priorities, pricing rationale, and 90-day promotional calendar
- **Time:** 1-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** The Four Ps must be internally consistent. Premium pricing with bottom-tier distribution channels signals confusion. A high-touch product sold through self-serve channels signals mismatch. Each P must reinforce the others.

### Step 4: Building Story Stakes
- **Purpose:** A launch without narrative tension is a press release. Stakes create urgency -- the reason this product matters now, the cost of the status quo, the risk of inaction. This step constructs the emotional architecture of the launch story.
- **Key questions:** What is at stake for the customer if they do nothing? What tension does the product resolve? What is the "before and after" transformation?
- **Input:** Value curve from Step 2, customer pain points, market timing factors
- **Output:** Launch narrative with defined stakes, a clear antagonist (the status quo or incumbent approach), and a resolution arc
- **Time:** 0.5-1 hour
- **Checkpoint:** The stakes must be real and felt by the customer, not invented by marketing. If the stakes require extensive explanation, they are not stakes -- they are features.

### Step 5: Writing for Response
- **Purpose:** Launch communications exist to produce action: signups, purchases, demo requests, press coverage. Every word must reduce friction between the reader and the desired response. This step applies behavioral writing principles to all launch materials.
- **Key questions:** What is the single desired action for each piece of communication? What friction stands between the reader and that action? How do you make the default action the desired action?
- **Input:** Story stakes from Step 4, Four Ps promotional plan, audience segmentation
- **Output:** Launch communication templates (landing page copy, launch email, social media posts, press pitch) with embedded response triggers and friction reduction
- **Time:** 1-2 hours
- **Checkpoint:** Each communication must pass the "so what" test. Read the first sentence. If a busy person would not continue reading, rewrite the opening.

### Step 6: SMART Goals
- **Purpose:** A launch without defined success criteria becomes an indefinite campaign that slowly loses energy and budget. SMART goals create the accountability structure: specific targets, measurable indicators, achievable but ambitious thresholds, relevant metrics, and time-bound checkpoints.
- **Key questions:** What does success look like at 30, 60, and 90 days? Which metric is the primary indicator? What triggers a strategy revision? What constitutes failure?
- **Input:** Four Ps plan, financial model, competitive benchmarks, runway constraints
- **Output:** SMART goals matrix with 30/60/90-day targets, leading and lagging indicators, and defined revision triggers
- **Time:** 0.5-1 hour
- **Checkpoint:** Goals must be falsifiable. "Grow awareness" is not a goal. "Reach 500 signups with a 15% activation rate within 30 days at under $40 CAC" is a goal.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Launching broadly instead of narrowly.** The most common launch failure is targeting a market rather than a segment. A beachhead of 1,000 passionate customers beats 100,000 indifferent ones. Define the beachhead before spending a dollar on promotion.
2. **Skipping the competitive analysis.** Founders often believe their product is unique enough to ignore competitors. The five forces analysis almost always reveals competitive pressure the founder underestimated -- better to discover it before launch than after.
3. **Pricing by gut.** Pricing is a strategic decision that signals positioning, determines unit economics, and constrains distribution. The Four Ps analysis must produce a pricing rationale rooted in customer willingness-to-pay and competitive context, not founder intuition.
4. **Measuring everything, tracking nothing.** Dozens of metrics create the illusion of rigor. The SMART goals step should produce 3-5 metrics that genuinely indicate whether the launch is working. Everything else is noise.
5. **Writing communications that describe rather than compel.** Feature lists are not launch copy. The writing-for-response step exists because most launch materials tell the reader what the product does rather than making the reader want to act.

## Adaptation Notes

- **Idea stage:** The "launch" may be an MVP or landing page test. Compress the playbook: skip Step 4 (stakes) and focus on Steps 1-3 (market understanding) and Step 6 (success criteria). The goal is learning, not revenue.
- **Validation stage:** The launch tests product-market fit in a defined segment. All six steps apply, but the SMART goals should emphasize activation and retention over revenue.
- **Early-traction stage:** The launch extends into adjacent segments or geographies. Step 1 (five forces) must be re-run for the new market. The blue ocean analysis from Step 2 may reveal that differentiation that worked in the first market does not transfer.
- **Growth stage:** Launches at scale carry brand risk. Step 4 (stakes) and Step 5 (communications) receive disproportionate weight. The narrative must be consistent with established brand positioning while introducing something genuinely new.
