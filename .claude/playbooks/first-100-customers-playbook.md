# The First 100 Customers Playbook

## When to Use This Playbook

The product exists. Customers do not. Or worse -- a handful of users signed up, poked around, and vanished. The founder has built something and now confronts the disorienting reality that building and selling require entirely different muscles.

This playbook applies when the problem is not product quality but customer acquisition at the earliest stage. The founder cannot yet afford paid channels, does not have inbound traffic, and needs to find, reach, and convert customers one at a time. The first 100 customers are hand-to-hand combat, not air warfare. Every skill in this sequence is designed for that reality.

If the product has already achieved moderate traction (hundreds of active users, consistent organic growth), this playbook is too elementary. Route instead to `scaling-playbook` or `four-ps-marketing` directly.

## Prerequisites

- A functional product or credible MVP (not a slide deck)
- Clarity on what the product does, even if clarity on who wants it is lacking
- Willingness to do unscalable things: manual outreach, personal onboarding, individual conversations
- Enough runway to sustain 4-8 weeks of customer development without revenue pressure
- An email account and a willingness to hear "no" repeatedly

## The Sequence

### Step 1: Business Model Canvas
- **Purpose:** Most founders at this stage describe their customer as "small businesses" or "millennials who care about health." These are not customers -- they are census categories. The canvas forces precision: a specific segment, a specific pain, a specific channel to reach them, and a specific reason they would pay.
- **Key questions:** Can you name ten individuals who match the customer profile? Where do they congregate (online or offline)? What are they currently using to solve the problem you address?
- **Input:** Product description, founder assumptions about the market, any early usage data
- **Output:** Refined canvas with a customer segment specific enough to reach individually
- **Time:** 1-1.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** The customer segment must pass the "can you email ten of them by Friday?" test. If the answer is no, the segment is too broad.

## Decision Point

After completing Step 1, a critical gate:

**Is the customer segment specific enough to reach individually?**

- If the segment remains broad ("SMBs" or "health-conscious consumers"), narrow it further before proceeding. A broad segment makes every subsequent step -- outreach, messaging, pricing -- vague and ineffective. Return to the canvas and pick a niche within the niche.
- If the founder can name specific companies, specific communities, or specific individuals who match the profile -- proceed. Precision here compounds through every remaining step.

Reaching 100 customers begins with being able to name the first ten.

### Step 2: Net Promoter Score
- **Purpose:** At zero customers, NPS seems premature. It is not. Designing the feedback mechanism before the first customer arrives ensures that every interaction produces actionable data, not just revenue. The founders who learn fastest win the early-traction race.
- **Key questions:** How will you survey your first users? At what moment in the experience? What qualitative follow-up question will you ask promoters and detractors?
- **Input:** Product experience map, customer touchpoints, onboarding flow
- **Output:** Pre-launch NPS framework with survey timing, question design, and escalation triggers
- **Time:** 0.5-1 hour
- **Checkpoint:** The NPS framework must be lightweight enough that a single founder can run it manually. If it requires tooling that does not exist yet, simplify.

### Step 3: Four Ps Marketing
- **Purpose:** Positioning, pricing, place, and promotion -- the four decisions that determine whether the right customers find the product and find the price acceptable. At this stage, the emphasis falls on channel selection (where to reach early adopters) and pricing psychology (how to price for learning, not revenue maximisation).
- **Key questions:** Which single channel gives you the highest probability of reaching your specific segment? Should you price low to reduce friction, price high to signal value, or offer free trials to accelerate learning? What positioning separates you from the alternative the customer uses today?
- **Input:** Customer segment from Step 1, competitive landscape, product capabilities
- **Output:** Marketing mix with one primary channel, initial pricing rationale, and positioning statement
- **Time:** 1-1.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** If the plan includes more than two acquisition channels, it is too ambitious for a founder without a marketing team. Pick one channel. Dominate it. Add the second channel at customer 50.

### Step 4: Finding Your Stories
- **Purpose:** Early customers buy from founders, not from companies. The founding story -- why this problem, why this founder, why now -- is the most powerful acquisition tool at pre-traction stage. This step surfaces the authentic narrative that turns a cold email into a human connection.
- **Key questions:** What personal experience led to building this product? What did you see that others missed? What is at stake for you personally?
- **Input:** Founder biography, origin of the idea, personal connection to the problem
- **Output:** Founding narrative arc: origin moment, personal stakes, mission, and the invitation to join early
- **Time:** 0.5-1 hour
- **Checkpoint:** Read the story to someone who knows nothing about the product. If they ask a follow-up question, the story works. If they nod politely, rewrite.

### Step 5: Writing for Response
- **Purpose:** The gap between a founder who sends 100 cold emails and gets 2 replies versus 15 replies is not luck -- it is craft. This step applies the science of response-oriented writing: reducing friction, making the ask specific, respecting the reader's cognitive load, and earning the click.
- **Key questions:** Is the subject line specific enough to avoid the spam filter and generic enough to avoid the trash? Does the first sentence establish relevance? Is the call to action a single, low-friction step?
- **Input:** Customer segment from Step 1, founding story from Step 4, positioning from Step 3
- **Output:** Three outreach templates: cold email, LinkedIn message, and landing page CTA block
- **Time:** 1-1.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** Test the cold email on five people outside the target segment. If they understand the ask within ten seconds, the writing is clear enough for a busy prospect.

### Step 6: Building Story Stakes
- **Purpose:** A pitch without urgency is a suggestion. Early-stage founders often present their product as "useful" without explaining why the prospect should act now rather than next quarter. Stakes create the temporal pressure that converts interest into action.
- **Key questions:** What does the prospect lose by waiting? Is there a limited beta, a founding-member price, or a competitive window that creates genuine scarcity? How do you communicate urgency without resorting to manufactured pressure?
- **Input:** Outreach templates from Step 5, product roadmap, early-adopter offer structure
- **Output:** Stakes-enhanced versions of all outreach materials with urgency drivers
- **Time:** 0.5-1 hour
- **Checkpoint:** The urgency must be genuine. Manufactured scarcity ("only 5 spots left!" when the cap is fictitious) destroys trust at the stage where trust is everything. If no authentic urgency exists, create it through an early-adopter programme with genuine exclusivity.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Going broad to go fast.** The temptation to cast a wide net feels productive but dilutes every message. The first 100 customers come from a niche, not a market.
2. **Building before selling.** Founders who respond to slow customer acquisition by returning to the product are hiding in their comfort zone. The product is not the bottleneck -- distribution is.
3. **Ignoring the feedback loop.** Acquiring 100 customers who never speak to you again is worse than acquiring 30 who tell you exactly what they think. The NPS step exists for this reason.
4. **Pricing too low out of insecurity.** Free users and drastically underpriced customers behave differently from real customers. Price at a level that attracts people willing to pay, even if the number is small. Willingness to pay is a signal; free usage is noise.
5. **Writing outreach that talks about the product instead of the prospect.** Every cold email should be about the reader's problem, not the founder's solution. The product is the answer; the email is the question.

## Adaptation Notes

- **Idea stage:** The product may be a prototype or concierge MVP. The first 100 "customers" may be beta testers or design partners rather than paying users. Adapt the pricing step accordingly -- the currency at this stage is feedback, not revenue.
- **Validation stage:** Some early traction exists but is inconsistent. The canvas refinement (Step 1) should build on existing data: who converted and why? Who churned and why? The segment becomes sharper with evidence.
- **Early-traction stage:** The playbook shifts from finding customers to systematising the acquisition channel that works. If one channel produced the first 30 customers, the remaining 70 should come from scaling that channel before experimenting with new ones.
