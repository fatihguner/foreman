# Scaling Playbook

Every founder who survives the early chaos eventually hits the same wall: the things that got you to ten people will destroy you at fifty. The improvisation, the flat hierarchy, the founder-as-bottleneck operating model -- these are assets at five employees and liabilities at twenty-five. Scaling is not about doing more of the same. It is about becoming a fundamentally different organization while preserving what made the original one worth building.

---

## When to Use This Playbook

- Revenue is growing but operations are fraying at the seams
- The founder is the answer to every question and the bottleneck for every decision
- New hires are not performing because onboarding is "figure it out"
- The culture that once felt organic now feels inconsistent or diluted
- Team leads are managing but not leading, and no one is developing them
- Processes exist in people's heads rather than in documented systems

## Prerequisites

- Product-market fit is established (this playbook assumes traction, not search)
- Revenue or funding supports the planned headcount growth
- The founder is genuinely willing to relinquish control -- not merely claiming to be
- At least 10 people on the team, with plans to grow beyond 30 within 12-18 months

---

## The Sequence

### Step 1: Balanced Scorecard -- Measure What Matters Beyond Revenue

Revenue growth masks structural decay. Before scaling anything, establish a measurement framework that captures the full picture: financial health, customer satisfaction, internal process efficiency, and organizational learning capacity. This becomes the dashboard against which every subsequent step is evaluated.

**Checkpoint:** A functioning scorecard with at least three leading indicators per perspective, reviewed weekly.

### Step 2: Hackman Enabling Conditions -- Design Teams That Scale

Scaling means teams, not headcount. Apply Hackman's five conditions (real team, compelling direction, enabling structure, supportive context, expert coaching) to design each functional group. The output determines how many teams you need, how they are composed, and what authority they carry.

**Checkpoint:** Team architecture documented. Each team has a defined boundary, a compelling purpose, and the structural conditions to self-manage.

### Step 3: Organisational Culture -- Codify Before It Dilutes

Culture at ten people is implicit. Culture at fifty must be explicit. This step forces articulation of the values, norms, and behavioral expectations that define the organization. The work is harder than it sounds -- it requires distinguishing between aspirational platitudes and the actual lived culture, then deciding what to keep, what to change, and what to enshrine.

**Checkpoint:** Culture document drafted, reviewed with the existing team, and pressure-tested against real hiring and firing decisions.

---

### Decision Point: Is the Founder the Bottleneck?

Pause here. Evaluate honestly: does every significant decision still route through the founder? Do people wait for the founder's input before acting? If the answer is yes, **prioritize Steps 4 and 7 immediately** before continuing with Steps 5 and 6. A scaling organization cannot be built on top of a single point of failure. The delegation and role evolution work must happen first, or the team structures and process improvements designed in subsequent steps will remain theoretical.

If the founder has already begun distributing authority effectively, proceed sequentially through Steps 4-7.

---

### Step 4: Delegation and Empowerment -- Let Go to Grow

Map every recurring decision the founder currently makes. Categorize by reversibility and impact. Build an explicit delegation matrix that specifies who owns what, at what authority level, and with what accountability mechanism. The founder retains only the truly irreversible, high-stakes decisions.

**Checkpoint:** Delegation matrix published. At least 60% of recurring decisions moved to team leads with clear authority.

### Step 5: Leadership Pipeline -- Build the Next Layer

Identify the individuals who will carry the organization through its next phase. Assess current leaders against future requirements -- the skills needed to manage five people differ substantially from those needed to manage twenty. Design development paths that close the gaps, and accept that some current leaders may not scale with the organization.

**Checkpoint:** Leadership pipeline document with identified candidates, development plans, and a realistic timeline for readiness.

### Step 6: Kaizen and BPR -- Systematize the Chaos

Audit every critical process: hiring, onboarding, customer delivery, product development, financial operations. Identify which processes are documented, which exist only as tribal knowledge, and which do not exist at all. Apply continuous improvement principles to the functional processes and business process re-engineering to the broken or absent ones.

**Checkpoint:** Top ten processes documented, owners assigned, improvement cadence established.

### Step 7: Senior Leadership Transition -- Evolve the Founder's Role

The founder who built the company is not necessarily the operator who should run it at scale. This step confronts the founder's evolving role directly: what should the CEO be doing at fifty people that differs from what they did at ten? The output is not a succession plan (unless needed) but a role evolution plan -- shifting from tactical execution to strategic leadership, external representation, and culture stewardship.

**Checkpoint:** Written CEO role definition for the next phase, with explicit activities to stop, start, and continue.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Scaling headcount before scaling systems.** Hiring ahead of process maturity creates expensive chaos. Every new hire amplifies existing dysfunction.
- **Codifying culture as aspirational fiction.** Writing values that describe who you wish you were rather than who you are produces cynicism, not alignment.
- **Delegating without authority.** Assigning responsibility without decision-making power is delegation theatre. It frustrates capable people and changes nothing.
- **Treating this as a one-time project.** Scaling is continuous. The measurement system from Step 1 exists precisely to make this an ongoing discipline rather than a quarterly panic.

## Adaptation Notes

- **Hardware or manufacturing startups** should weight Step 6 (process systematization) more heavily, as physical operations tolerate improvisation far less than software.
- **Remote-first organizations** need to invest disproportionately in Step 3 (culture codification) since implicit cultural transmission through physical proximity is unavailable.
- **Technical founders** often find Step 4 (delegation) the most difficult. Pair it with executive coaching if the founder struggles to release technical decision-making authority.
- **Venture-backed companies** facing board pressure to scale quickly should still resist the urge to skip Steps 1-3. Measurement, structure, and culture are the foundation -- not the luxury.
