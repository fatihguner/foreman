# The Pricing Strategy Playbook

## When to Use This Playbook

Pricing is the most underleveraged variable in most startups. Founders agonise over product features, marketing channels, and hiring plans, then set their price by glancing at a competitor's website and picking a number that "feels right." The result is predictable: margins that cannot sustain the business, conversion rates that puzzle the team, and a nagging suspicion that money is being left on the table or -- worse -- that the price itself is repelling the customers who matter most.

This playbook applies when pricing needs to be set for the first time, when existing pricing is not working (low conversion, high churn at renewal, or persistent discounting), or when the business model is shifting in ways that demand a new monetisation structure entirely. It does not apply when the product itself lacks value -- no pricing strategy rescues a product nobody wants.

## Prerequisites

- A product that delivers measurable value to at least some customers
- Basic financial data: cost structure, current margins (or modelled margins), customer acquisition cost
- Access to competitive pricing data (public pricing pages, industry benchmarks, or customer feedback on alternatives)
- Willingness to experiment -- pricing is a hypothesis, not a decree
- Clarity on whether the objective is revenue maximisation, market penetration, or margin optimisation

## The Sequence

### Step 1: Four Ps Marketing
- **Purpose:** Price is one variable in a four-variable equation. A premium price with commodity positioning fails. A low price with a high-touch sales channel destroys margins. This step maps the full marketing mix to ensure pricing decisions are coherent with product positioning, distribution, and promotional strategy.
- **Key questions:** What does the current positioning promise? Does the price reinforce or contradict that promise? Which channel delivers the customer, and what does that channel cost? Is the promotion strategy aligned with the price point?
- **Input:** Current pricing, product positioning, channel economics, competitive context
- **Output:** Marketing mix assessment revealing alignment gaps between price and the other three Ps
- **Time:** 1-1.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** If the positioning says "premium" but the price says "commodity," everything downstream will be confused. Resolve positioning-price alignment before continuing.

### Step 2: Porter's Five Forces
- **Purpose:** The competitive landscape sets the boundaries of pricing power. Strong buyer power compresses margins. Abundant substitutes cap the price ceiling. High barriers protect premium positions. This step maps the forces that constrain and enable pricing decisions.
- **Key questions:** How much pricing power do buyers have? Are there substitutes that set an implicit price ceiling? Do competitors compete on price, features, or service? What would a new entrant price at?
- **Input:** Marketing mix from Step 1, competitive intelligence, market structure data
- **Output:** Five forces pricing map showing where pricing power exists and where it does not
- **Time:** 1-1.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** The forces map must be honest about pricing constraints. Founders who believe they have pricing power they do not have will set prices the market rejects.

## Decision Point

After completing Steps 1 and 2, a strategic fork:

**Is the market price-sensitive or value-sensitive?**

- If the five forces analysis reveals commoditised competition, powerful buyers, and abundant substitutes -- the market is price-sensitive. Proceed to Step 3 (information asymmetry) with priority on signalling strategies that differentiate on perceived value within a constrained price range. Step 4 (blue ocean) becomes about finding the value dimensions competitors ignore, enabling escape from price competition.
- If the analysis shows differentiated competitors, loyal customer segments, and high switching costs -- the market is value-sensitive. Step 4 (blue ocean) takes priority, focusing on value innovation that justifies premium or novel pricing models. Step 3 (information asymmetry) then ensures customers perceive the premium value accurately.

The sequence of Steps 3 and 4 remains the same, but the emphasis and time allocation shift based on this diagnosis.

### Step 3: Information Asymmetry
- **Purpose:** Customers rarely understand the full value of what they are buying. The gap between delivered value and perceived value is where pricing power is won or lost. This step maps the information asymmetries -- where customers underestimate value, where they overestimate risk, and what signals would close the gap.
- **Key questions:** What value does the product deliver that customers cannot easily observe? How do customers evaluate quality before purchase? What signals (credentials, social proof, guarantees) would reduce perceived risk? Where does the customer overvalue a competitor's offering?
- **Input:** Five forces output, customer feedback, churn reasons, sales objection data
- **Output:** Information asymmetry map with signalling strategies for each gap
- **Time:** 0.5-1 hour
- **Checkpoint:** If the primary value is invisible to the customer pre-purchase, the pricing strategy must include mechanisms to make value visible -- trials, guarantees, case studies, or usage-based pricing that links payment to demonstrated outcomes.

### Step 4: Blue Ocean Strategy
- **Purpose:** Competing on the same dimensions as everyone else means competing on price. Blue ocean strategy identifies where to eliminate costs, reduce features customers do not value, raise attributes competitors underinvest in, and create entirely new value dimensions. Each move has direct pricing implications.
- **Key questions:** Which features or costs can be eliminated without losing core value? Where can value be created that competitors do not offer? Does the eliminate-reduce-raise-create grid suggest a fundamentally different price point?
- **Input:** Five forces map, information asymmetry analysis, competitive pricing data
- **Output:** Strategy canvas with pricing implications: where the new value curve justifies premium, enables lower cost, or supports a novel pricing model
- **Time:** 1-1.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** The strategy canvas must show at least one dimension where the company diverges significantly from competitors. If the canvas mirrors the industry, the pricing will too.

### Step 5: Evaluating and Deciding
- **Purpose:** Armed with competitive data, value perception insights, and differentiation opportunities, this step systematically evaluates pricing model options. Subscription, usage-based, freemium, tiered, per-seat, one-time -- each model carries different revenue dynamics, customer psychology, and operational requirements.
- **Key questions:** Which pricing model aligns with how customers experience value? Does the customer receive value gradually (subscription) or in discrete events (usage)? What is the competitive norm, and is there advantage in breaking it? What does each model require operationally?
- **Input:** All outputs from Steps 1-4, current unit economics, customer usage patterns
- **Output:** Decision matrix evaluating 3-5 pricing models against weighted criteria
- **Time:** 1-1.5 hours
- **Checkpoint:** The chosen model must be implementable within 30 days. If it requires infrastructure that does not exist, either simplify the model or plan the build before announcing the price.

### Step 6: SMART Goals
- **Purpose:** A new pricing strategy is a hypothesis until the market validates it. This step defines the experiment: what metrics will confirm the pricing works, what thresholds trigger a revision, and over what time period the test runs.
- **Key questions:** What conversion rate validates the price point? What churn rate at renewal invalidates it? How many customers constitute a statistically meaningful sample? What is the fallback if the experiment fails?
- **Input:** Chosen pricing model from Step 5, current baseline metrics, revenue targets
- **Output:** SMART goals for the pricing rollout with specific metrics, measurement cadence, and decision triggers
- **Time:** 0.5-1 hour
- **Checkpoint:** Every pricing goal must have a time-bound evaluation date. Pricing experiments that run indefinitely produce ambiguous results and organisational paralysis.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Pricing by imitation.** Copying a competitor's price copies their cost structure, positioning, and margin assumptions -- none of which may apply. Competitive pricing is an input, not a formula.
2. **Underpricing from fear.** Founders afraid of losing customers set prices too low, attract price-sensitive buyers who churn at any increase, and build a business that cannot afford to serve them. Price for the customer you want, not the customer you fear losing.
3. **Changing price without changing signal.** Raising the price without improving perceived value creates sticker shock. The information asymmetry step exists to prevent this.
4. **Overcomplicating the model.** Tiered pricing with seven tiers, usage-based pricing with four metered dimensions, and add-on bundles create cognitive load that kills conversion. The best pricing is simple enough to explain in one sentence.
5. **Skipping the experiment.** Launching a new price to the entire customer base without testing it on a subset is a one-way decision made with two-way-door uncertainty. Always test first.

## Adaptation Notes

- **Validation stage:** Pricing is a discovery tool, not a revenue tool. Set a price to test willingness to pay, not to optimise margins. Founder conversations with early customers about pricing generate more insight than any spreadsheet model. The SMART goals at this stage should measure learning velocity, not revenue.
- **Early-traction stage:** Pricing begins to matter financially. The emphasis shifts to unit economics and the relationship between price, CAC, and LTV. Grandfathering early customers while introducing new pricing requires careful communication -- the trust equation matters here.
- **Growth stage:** Pricing becomes a strategic lever. The blue ocean and five forces steps carry disproportionate weight because pricing decisions at scale are difficult to reverse. Consider price segmentation, enterprise tiers, and international pricing. The experiment step becomes essential: test pricing changes on cohorts before rolling them out broadly.
