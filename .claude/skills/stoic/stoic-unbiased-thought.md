# Stoic Unbiased Thought

Every event is neutral -- what makes it good or bad is the colour of the lens through which you perceive it. Marcus Aurelius, who had more reason than most to see the world as either magnificent or terrible, spent years training himself to do neither. Instead, he practised a form of radical descriptive simplicity: wine is "old grape juice," imperial purple robes are "sheep's wool dyed in shellfish blood," sex is "rubbing and a spasmic discharge." The exercise sounds almost comically reductive, and that is precisely the point. By stripping objects and events of the value judgments we habitually attach to them, the Stoics revealed a startling truth: most of what we believe about the world is not observation but interpretation -- and interpretation, unlike observation, can be trained, corrected, and improved.

---

## The Framework

### The Perceiving Eye and the Observing Eye

The Japanese swordsman Miyamoto Musashi distinguished between the "perceiving eye" and the "observing eye." The observing eye sees what is. The perceiving eye sees what things supposedly mean. Marcus Aurelius, writing centuries earlier in a different civilisation, arrived at the same insight: "It isn't events themselves that disturb people, but only their judgments about them."

This distinction has direct operational consequences for entrepreneurs:

| Observing Eye | Perceiving Eye |
|---|---|
| Revenue declined 12% this quarter | We're failing |
| A key engineer gave notice | He's betraying us |
| The board asked for a revised forecast | They've lost confidence in me |
| A competitor raised a large round | We're going to be crushed |

The left column is data. The right column is narrative -- and narrative, once it takes hold, shapes every subsequent decision. The Stoic practice of unbiased thought is the systematic effort to operate from the left column before permitting the right one any influence.

### Contemptuous Expressions: Radical Simplification

Marcus Aurelius developed a technique that Stoic scholars call *contemptuous expressions* -- deliberately describing prized or feared things in their most basic, physical terms. This is not cynicism; it is a cognitive counterweight to the mind's tendency to inflate or catastrophise.

Applied to entrepreneurial contexts, the practice yields clarity:

- A funding round is "other people agreeing to exchange their money for a percentage of future uncertain value"
- A product launch is "making a thing available and seeing if people want it"
- A keynote speech is "one person talking to many people sitting in chairs"
- An acquisition offer is "someone proposing to buy the output of your work for a specific price"

None of these reductions are false. They are merely the same events, seen without the distortion of status, fear, desire, or self-importance. And from that vantage point, decision-making improves markedly -- because the decisions are about the thing itself, not about the story wrapped around it.

### The View from Above

Marcus Aurelius repeatedly practised what scholars call the "view from above" -- a mental exercise in which you imagine seeing your current situation from an increasingly elevated perspective. First, see the room you're in. Then the building. The city. The country. The Earth, spinning in space. At each level, the matters that consumed you a moment ago diminish in proportion to the vastness you're now perceiving.

"Watch the stars in their courses," Marcus writes, "and imagine yourself running alongside them. Think constantly on the changes of the elements into each other, for such thoughts wash away the dust of earthly life."

This is not nihilism. The view from above does not negate the importance of your work. It recalibrates it. The lost deal, the difficult board meeting, the competitive threat -- all remain real and worth addressing. But they are no longer cosmically significant events that justify panic, despair, or the abandonment of principle. They are dust, and dust can be dealt with calmly.

### Testing Impressions: The Stoic Verification Protocol

Epictetus provides the most explicit protocol for challenging one's own perceptions: "From the very beginning, make it your practice to say to every harsh impression, 'you are an impression and not at all what you appear to be.' Next, examine and test it."

The testing process involves three questions:

**1. Is this impression about something within my control?** If not, the emotional charge attached to it is, by definition, misplaced.

**2. Is the judgment I've attached to this event the only possible interpretation?** The Stoics recognised that the same event can support radically different narratives. A customer churning can be "evidence of product failure" or "useful data about market fit." An investor passing can be "rejection" or "misalignment that saves both parties time."

**3. Would a wise person -- my Cato, my impartial spectator -- share this judgment?** If the answer is uncertain, the judgment warrants revision before action.

### Opinions as Voluntary Additions

"We have the power to hold no opinion about a thing and to not let it upset our state of mind," Marcus observes, "for things have no natural power to shape our judgments." This is among the most counterintuitive claims in Stoic philosophy. Surely some things are objectively bad? The Stoics would answer: things are objectively inconvenient, painful, or unpleasant. But "bad" is a layer we add -- and we have the power not to add it.

For entrepreneurs, this practice manifests as the discipline of delayed interpretation. When information arrives -- a metric, an email, a market signal -- the unbiased thinker receives the data and resists the immediate impulse to assign it a value. Is this good news or bad news? Perhaps it is simply news. The interpretive layer can be added later, after analysis, after consultation, after the emotional charge of the moment has dissipated. The Stoics did not claim this was easy. They claimed it was possible, and that the difference between practising it and not practising it was the difference between clarity and confusion.

### The War Within: Conflicting Judgments

Seneca warns that "circumstances are what deceive us" because "we embrace evil before good" and "our prayers are at war with our prayers." The entrepreneur who wants sustainable growth but chases vanity metrics, who values work-life balance but never leaves the office, who prizes culture but tolerates toxic high performers -- these are all examples of what Seneca calls internal warfare. The biases are not external. They are contradictions in the entrepreneur's own system of judgments.

Unbiased thought, in the Stoic sense, begins with recognising that these contradictions exist and tracing them to their source: usually a judgment accepted without examination, an opinion inherited from culture or convention, or a desire masquerading as a principle.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Contemptuous Expressions Exercise:**
> Take the following list of things I'm currently preoccupied with as founder of [company]: [list concerns]. For each, apply Marcus Aurelius's contemptuous expressions technique -- describe it in the simplest, most physically literal terms possible, stripped of all status, emotion, and narrative. Then assess: does the simplified description change how urgent or important this concern actually is?

**Prompt 2 -- Judgment Stripping:**
> I've just received this news about my business: [describe event]. Before I react, help me separate observation from interpretation. State the raw facts with zero value judgment. Then list three to five different interpretations of the same facts, ranging from catastrophic to neutral to opportunistic. For each interpretation, identify the assumption that makes it seem plausible. Which interpretation is best supported by the available evidence?

**Prompt 3 -- View from Above:**
> I'm deeply stressed about [situation] at [company]. Walk me through Marcus Aurelius's "view from above" exercise. Start with the immediate situation, then progressively zoom out -- to the industry, to the economy, to the arc of business history, to the timescale of human civilisation. At each level, reassess the situation's actual significance. Then zoom back in and recommend what a proportionate, clear-headed response would look like.

**Prompt 4 -- Internal Contradiction Audit:**
> Review my stated values and priorities for [company]: [list values]. Now review my actual recent decisions and time allocation: [list decisions/activities]. Apply Seneca's warning about "prayers at war with prayers" -- where do my stated values and revealed behaviour contradict each other? For each contradiction, identify the unexamined judgment or hidden desire driving the gap.

## Use Cases

**Idea-stage founder catastrophising about competition**

A first-time founder discovers that two well-funded startups are building in a similar space. Her immediate perception: "The market is already saturated. I'm too late. These companies will crush me." The contemptuous expressions exercise reduces the situation to its fundamentals: "Two other groups of people are also trying to solve this problem. They have raised capital. I have not yet." Stripped of narrative, the situation is informational, not terminal. The judgment-stripping exercise yields five alternative interpretations, including "the existence of competitors validates the market" and "well-funded competitors often pursue different segments." The founder proceeds with better data and less dread.

**Early-traction startup misreading a vanity metric**

A consumer marketplace at early traction celebrates a spike in user registrations after a press mention. The perceiving eye sees "we're taking off." The observing eye sees "registrations increased 300% this week; we do not yet know whether these users will transact." The view from above reveals that press-driven spikes are among the most common and most misleading signals in consumer businesses -- a pattern repeated across thousands of startups over decades. The unbiased assessment preserves the team's morale (the spike is a preferred indifferent) while preventing the premature scaling decisions that frequently follow misinterpreted traction signals.

**Growth-stage CEO receiving an acquisition offer**

A Series B CEO receives an unsolicited acquisition offer that is "too good to ignore" -- or so it seems. Marcus Aurelius's exercise reduces the offer to its components: "A larger company proposes to pay $X for the business. The price exceeds current revenue by [multiple]. The acquirer's stated intentions are [list]. The transaction would result in [concrete personal and organisational outcomes]." With the glamour stripped away, the CEO can evaluate the offer against actual criteria -- strategic fit, team outcomes, personal goals -- rather than against the narrative of validation and success that acquisition offers inevitably carry.

## Anti-Patterns

**1. Using objectivity as emotional avoidance.** Stripping judgments is a cognitive practice, not an emotional bypass. The founder who describes a team member's departure as "one person choosing to work elsewhere" and then refuses to examine whether the departure reveals a leadership problem is not practising Stoic objectivity -- they are hiding behind it. The practice requires engaging with the stripped-down facts, not dismissing them.

**2. Descending into nihilism through the view from above.** Marcus's cosmic perspective is a recalibration tool, not an argument that nothing matters. The founder who concludes "none of this matters in the grand scheme of things" and stops trying has misapplied the technique. The correct conclusion is closer to: "This matters, but not as much as my emotional reaction is telling me. I can address it with composure."

**3. Applying contemptuous expressions to people.** Reducing objects, events, and institutions to their simplest terms is a useful cognitive tool. Reducing human beings to their simplest terms is dehumanising. Marcus Aurelius explicitly counselled compassion and kinship toward others, even difficult ones. The technique is for examining things, not diminishing people.

**4. Demanding that others adopt your objectivity.** The Stoic practice of unbiased thought is a personal discipline. Telling a distressed colleague that "it's just an impression, not reality" is philosophically accurate and interpersonally disastrous. Apply the framework to your own perceptions. Extend empathy to others who have not yet done the same work.

**5. Mistaking detachment for wisdom.** The Stoics were not detached. Marcus Aurelius governed an empire. Seneca navigated lethal court politics. Epictetus taught with visible passion. Unbiased thought enables better engagement with reality, not withdrawal from it. The entrepreneur who uses the framework to disengage from problems rather than see them more clearly has inverted its purpose.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Seeing the market as it is, not as you wish it to be | The greatest perceptual risk here is confirmation bias -- interpreting ambiguous signals as validation because you want them to be |
| Validation | Receiving experimental data without distortion | Every data point must pass through the observing eye before the perceiving eye; the discipline of delayed interpretation is most critical here |
| Early Traction | Distinguishing signal from noise in early metrics | The flood of new data (users, revenue, churn, NPS) creates endless opportunities for biased interpretation; the Stoic protocol filters them systematically |
| Growth | Resisting narrative capture by success stories | At growth stage, the company's own story becomes a source of bias; "we always succeed" is as dangerous a judgment as "we're going to fail" |
| Scale | Maintaining institutional objectivity | At scale, organisational biases compound individual ones; unbiased thought must be embedded in decision processes, not just individual practice |

At the validation stage, the Stoic discipline of unbiased thought intersects most powerfully with the scientific method. Both demand that the practitioner observe before interpreting, test before concluding, and remain willing to be proved wrong. The founder who defines falsification criteria before running an experiment -- "what specific result would cause me to abandon this hypothesis?" -- is practising exactly what Epictetus prescribes: testing impressions before assenting to them.

At growth and scale, the primary threat to unbiased thought shifts from individual cognitive distortion to organisational narrative capture. Success creates a meta-narrative -- "we're the company that always finds a way" or "our market position is unassailable" -- and that narrative filters all subsequent information. The Stoic view from above provides the institutional corrective: periodically lifting the organisation's perspective above its own story to see the broader competitive, economic, and historical context in which it operates.
