# Writing with Purpose

Same facts. Same recipient. Three entirely different letters -- and three entirely different outcomes. A company discovers its landlord has violated the Americans with Disabilities Act during a building renovation. The general counsel flags the issue. Someone must write to the landlord. But *why* are they writing? To gather information, because the landlord is a friend? To demand compliance, because a disabled employee needs access? To terminate the lease, because the relationship has already soured? Each purpose produces a different document in tone, structure, and diction -- even though the underlying facts have not changed by a single comma.

This is the foundational insight that separates competent business writers from everyone else: purpose is not a detail to consider after the writing begins. Purpose is the architecture that determines everything else.

---

## The Framework

### Purpose Determines Form

Before typing a single word, a writer must answer one question with precision: *What do I want the reader to do after reading this?* The answer governs every subsequent decision -- word choice, sentence length, level of formality, degree of directness, and the balance between warmth and firmness.

Consider the ADA violation scenario across three different purposes:

| Purpose | Tone | Salutation | Key Move |
|---------|------|------------|----------|
| Gather information (friendly inquiry) | Warm, non-confrontational | "Dear Ann" | Compliments the renovation, raises the issue as a question, closes with a lunch invitation |
| Correct the oversight (firm request) | Professional, measured urgency | "Dear Ann" | Acknowledges the renovation positively, states the legal concern directly, requests assurance of a 60-day fix, mentions regulatory reporting as a consequence |
| Terminate the lease (decisive exit) | Firm, civil, bridge-preserving | "Dear Ms. Reynolds" | States the violation, invokes legal entitlement, serves 30 days' notice, expresses hope for continued goodwill |

The shift from "Dear Ann" to "Dear Ms. Reynolds" alone signals a tectonic change in intent. Every element -- from the opening pleasantry to the closing line -- serves the purpose. No sentence is neutral.

### Understanding Your Readers

Purpose without audience awareness is a monologue. Communication is a two-way exercise, and without understanding reader psychology, even a well-intentioned message lands flat. The critical audience realities are:

- **Readers are busy.** Ascend the organisational hierarchy and this tendency intensifies. A CEO scanning an inbox has no duty to finish a rambling message.
- **They will not struggle.** At the slightest friction, readers stop trying -- and think less of the writer.
- **They must see the benefit.** The ever-important "what's in it for them" must be visible early, or the message will be ignored.

### The Buffett Principle

Warren Buffett, in his preface to the SEC's *Plain English Handbook*, offered a deceptively simple technique for writing to large audiences: "I pretend that I'm talking to my sisters. Though highly intelligent, they are not experts in accounting or finance. They will understand plain English, but jargon may puzzle them."

The principle: choose a specific, intelligent non-specialist in the audience and write for that person. The resulting prose strikes a balance between sophistication and accessibility that serves everyone.

### The Goal Discipline: What Do You Want Readers to DO?

Most writers default to informational writing -- they tell readers what they know. Far fewer practise action-oriented writing -- telling readers what to do with what they know. *Writing for Busy Readers* draws a sharp line between these two modes and argues that the distinction is the single most consequential decision a writer makes before drafting.

The discipline is simple in principle and difficult in practice: before writing, complete two sentences. First: "The most important thing I want the reader to *understand* is ___." Second: "The most important thing I want the reader to *do* is ___." If the second sentence remains blank, the communication is informational. That is sometimes appropriate -- a market research summary, a background briefing. But if the writer *intended* the reader to act and failed to specify the action, the message will produce understanding without movement. The reader nods and moves on.

*Writing for Busy Readers* frames this as a design problem, invoking Don Norman's *The Design of Everyday Things*: if a person cannot figure out how to use a door handle, that is the designer's fault, not the user's. If a reader overlooks the writer's intended action, that is the writer's fault. The burden of clarity falls entirely on the author. Writers who do not know their own goals cannot expect readers to infer them -- and when readers are left to interpret what matters, they will frequently interpret wrong.

The practical implication is that goal-setting is not a one-time exercise but a revision loop. An initial draft often reveals that the writer's stated purpose and actual purpose diverge. The writing process itself clarifies thinking -- as organisational psychologist Adam Grant puts it: "Turning thoughts into words sharpens reasoning. What's fuzzy in your head is clear on the page." Effective writers revise not only for style and structure but for goal alignment, asking on each pass: does every sentence advance the reader toward the intended action?

| Writing Mode | Writer's Question | Example |
|--------------|-------------------|---------|
| Informational | "What should the reader know?" | "Q3 revenue declined 12% due to customer churn in the enterprise segment." |
| Action-oriented | "What should the reader do?" | "Q3 revenue declined 12% due to enterprise churn. We need board approval by Friday to fund the retention programme outlined in Appendix B." |

The second version contains the same information as the first. It also contains a verb, a deadline, and a specific request. The difference between a document that informs and a document that moves people is almost always one sentence -- the sentence that states what the writer actually wants.

### Tailoring the Message

Audience tailoring goes beyond tone. Consider where readers sit in the organisation, what they are expected to contribute, and what pressures they face. A memo to engineers about a product pivot emphasises technical implications. The same pivot, communicated to investors, emphasises market rationale and unit economics. The facts are identical; the framing is not.

The writer's job is to answer five questions before writing:

1. **Who** am I writing for? (Their concerns, motivations, background)
2. **Why** am I writing? (Every sentence should advance this purpose)
3. **What** needs saying? (Only the main points that deliver the message)
4. **When** are actions expected? (State the time frame)
5. **How** does this benefit the reader? (Make the value explicit)

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Purpose Clarification:**
> I need to write a [type of document] to [recipient/audience]. The situation is: [describe the facts]. I have not yet decided what I want this communication to achieve. Help me identify three possible purposes I could have, and for each, show me how the tone, structure, and key message would differ. Then help me choose the purpose that best serves my strategic objectives.

**Prompt 2 -- Audience Analysis:**
> My company is at the [stage] stage. I need to communicate [topic/news] to [specific audience: investors / team / customers / board / partners]. Profile this audience: what are their likely concerns, priorities, and time constraints? What do they need to hear first? What framing will resonate? Then draft an outline tailored to this audience.

**Prompt 3 -- Purpose-Tone Calibration:**
> Here is a draft I have written: [paste draft]. My intended purpose is [state purpose] and my audience is [describe audience]. Evaluate whether my tone, structure, and word choices actually serve this purpose, or whether they inadvertently signal a different intent. Identify specific passages where purpose and execution are misaligned and suggest revisions.

**Prompt 4 -- The Buffett Test:**
> I am writing [document type] that will be read by a diverse audience ranging from [technical specialists] to [non-specialists]. Apply the Buffett "writing to my sisters" test: rewrite my draft so that an intelligent non-specialist can follow every point without jargon, while preserving the substance and precision that specialists expect. Here is my draft: [paste draft].

**Prompt 5 -- Goal Extraction and Action Alignment:**
> Here is a draft I have written: [paste draft]. Apply the goal discipline: (1) State in one sentence what the reader is meant to *understand* after reading this. (2) State in one sentence what the reader is meant to *do* after reading this. (3) If the action is missing, unclear, or buried, identify exactly where the draft fails to convert understanding into action. (4) Rewrite the draft so that the intended action is unmistakable -- with a specific verb, a deadline where applicable, and a clear next step. Preserve the informational content but ensure the document moves readers, not merely informs them.

## Use Cases

**Validation-Stage Founder Writing a Rejection to an Investor's Term Sheet**

A founder has received a term sheet with unfavourable liquidation preferences. The investor is well-connected and may be valuable later. The purpose is not to burn the bridge -- it is to decline this specific offer while keeping the relationship warm. Applying purpose-driven writing, the founder opens with genuine appreciation for the investor's time and interest, acknowledges specific aspects of the term sheet that were attractive, explains concisely why the terms do not align with the company's current needs, and closes with an explicit door-opener for future conversations. The tone is warm, the message is clear, and the strategic relationship is preserved.

**Growth-Stage CEO Communicating a Pivot to Three Audiences Simultaneously**

A SaaS company is pivoting from horizontal to vertical. The CEO must write to three audiences: investors (who care about market size and burn rate), the engineering team (who care about technical debt and roadmap), and enterprise customers (who care about continuity and support). Same pivot, three documents. For investors: market opportunity framing, competitive moat, revised projections. For engineers: technical rationale, new architecture direction, timeline. For customers: reassurance, migration plan, dedicated support commitment. The Buffett principle applies to each -- but the "sister" is different every time.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Writing Before Knowing the Purpose.** The most common failure. Writers open a blank document and begin typing, hoping the purpose will emerge from the prose. It rarely does. The result is a meandering message that the reader cannot act on because the writer could not articulate what action they wanted.

2. **Confusing Self-Expression with Communication.** The purpose of business writing is to produce a reaction in the reader -- not to document the writer's thought process. A memo that catalogues everything the writer knows about a topic, rather than what the reader needs to know, serves the writer's ego, not the reader's time.

3. **One Tone for All Audiences.** Sending the same message to investors, employees, and customers is not efficiency -- it is laziness. Each audience has different concerns, different knowledge levels, and different reactions to the same words. "Restructuring" means opportunity to an investor and terror to an employee.

4. **Burying the Purpose.** Some writers treat the purpose as a conclusion to be earned, placing it at the end of a long preamble. Business readers skim. If the purpose is not visible within the first two sentences, many readers will never find it.

5. **Letting Tone Undermine Purpose.** A letter meant to preserve a relationship that opens with legal threats. A fundraising email that sounds apologetic. When tone contradicts purpose, the reader receives the tone, not the purpose.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Vision communication | Purpose is almost always persuasion -- convincing cofounders, early advisors, or pre-seed investors to believe in something that does not yet exist. Tone must balance confidence with intellectual honesty. |
| Validation | Evidence presentation | Purpose shifts toward demonstrating traction. The audience (investors, pilot customers) wants data, not vision. The writer's job is to let evidence speak while framing it strategically. |
| Early Traction | Multi-audience management | The founder now writes to investors, customers, employees, and partners -- often about the same events. Purpose diverges by audience. A single "update" template no longer suffices. |
| Growth | Institutional voice | Communications begin representing the company, not the founder. Purpose must be calibrated for scale -- one email may reach thousands of customers. The Buffett principle becomes essential. |
| Scale | Precision and legal awareness | At scale, every written communication carries reputational and legal weight. Purpose must be defined with precision because ambiguity can trigger regulatory, contractual, or public-relations consequences. |

## Output Template

```markdown
# Communication Purpose Brief

## Document Type
[Email / Memo / Letter / Report / Announcement]

## Purpose Statement
[One sentence: "The purpose of this communication is to [verb] so that the reader [desired action/reaction]."]

## Audience Profile
- **Primary reader(s):** [Who]
- **Their key concern:** [What they care about most]
- **Their knowledge level:** [Expert / Informed / Non-specialist]
- **Their time constraints:** [Seconds / Minutes / Will read in full]

## The Buffett Test
- **My "sister" for this audience:** [Specific person or archetype]
- **Jargon to eliminate:** [List]
- **"What's in it for them" statement:** [One sentence]

## Tone Calibration
- **Warmth level:** [Warm / Professional / Formal / Firm]
- **Directness level:** [Indirect / Measured / Direct / Blunt]
- **Formality markers:** [First name / Title / Salutation style]

## Structure
1. **Opening** (purpose visible in first two sentences): [Draft]
2. **Body** (evidence, context, or rationale): [Key points]
3. **Close** (desired action, next steps, relationship signal): [Draft]

## Pre-Send Checklist
- [ ] Purpose is stated or unmistakable within the first paragraph
- [ ] Every sentence advances the purpose
- [ ] Tone matches the intent (no contradictions)
- [ ] "What's in it for the reader" is visible
- [ ] No jargon the audience would not understand
- [ ] Appropriate level of formality for the relationship
```
