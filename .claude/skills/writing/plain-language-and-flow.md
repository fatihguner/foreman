# Plain Language and Flow

Consider this paragraph from an actual corporate communication: "It's mission-critical to be plain-spoken, whether you're trying to be best-of-breed at outside-the-box thinking or simply incentivizing colleagues to achieve a paradigm shift in core-performance value-adds. Leading-edge leveraging of your plain-English skill set will ensure that your actionable items synergize future-proof assets with your global-knowledge repository."

That passage says absolutely nothing. Strip away the jargon and what remains is a void -- no idea, no argument, no information. The words exist to *sound* like communication while avoiding the labour of actual thought. This is bizspeak: the corporate dialect that substitutes cliches for meaning, boilerplate for analysis, and verbal padding for substance. It is the lingua franca of organisations that have confused formality with competence.

The antidote is not simplification. It is *translation* -- converting corporate noise into human language, imposing chronological order on jumbled narratives, and using transitional phrases to guide readers through the argument. Plain language is not dumbed-down language. It is language that has been thought through.

## The Framework

### Part 1: The Bizspeak Autopsy

Corporate jargon persists for three reasons. Some writers use it to signal belonging -- to sound "in the know." Others have been taught that good writing is formal writing, so they stiffen when they pick up a keyboard. Still others use jargon deliberately to obscure what they are actually doing, or to disguise the fact that they are not sure what they are doing.

Whatever the motivation, the effect on the reader is consistent: bizspeak suggests that the writer is on autopilot, thoughtlessly deploying boilerplate rather than engaging with the material.

**The Bizspeak Translation Table:**

| NOT THIS | BUT THIS |
|----------|----------|
| at your earliest convenience | as soon as you can |
| in light of the fact that | because |
| we are in receipt of | we've received |
| as per our telephone conversation on today's date | as we discussed this morning |
| Pursuant to your instructions, I met with Roger Smith today regarding the above-mentioned. | As you asked, I met with Roger Smith today. |
| Please be advised that the deadline for the abovementioned competition is Monday, April 2, 2012. | The deadline is April 2, 2012. |
| Thank you for your courtesy and cooperation regarding this matter. | Thank you. |

Each translation removes verbal scaffolding while preserving -- and often improving -- the meaning. The plain versions take less time to write, less time to read, and less time to act on.

**The Bizspeak Blacklist (a selection):**

Actionable (apart from legal contexts), as per, bandwidth (outside electronics), bring our A game, core competency, drill down, going forward, hit the ground running, impact (as verb), incentivize, leverage (as verb), mission-critical, net-net, on the same page, operationalize, paradigm shift, push the envelope, repurpose, rightsized, scalable, seamless integration, synergize, think outside the box, utilize, value-added, win-win.

These terms have become voguish through overuse. Each one once carried meaning; repetition has drained it away. When a writer reaches for "synergize," the question to ask is: what specifically are these elements doing together, and can that action be described in plain English?

### The Buffett Translation

Warren Buffett rewrote a passage from a financial-services prospectus to demonstrate the transformation. The original: 136 words, all passive voice, average sentence length 27.2 words, Flesch Reading Ease score 8.2 (virtually unreadable). Buffett's revision: 74 words, no passive voice, average sentence length 14.8 words, Flesch Reading Ease score 60.1.

The revision did not lose information. It gained clarity. Increasing readability is not "dumbing it down" -- it is cleaning it up.

### The Readability Science

Buffett's instinct is backed by a century of measurement. Readability -- the quantifiable ease or difficulty of reading a text -- was first formalised by the United States military in 1917, when the country needed to assess whether soldiers could read well enough to perform their duties. The metrics have grown more sophisticated, but the core insight remains: shorter words, more common words, and shorter sentences produce text that more people read, understand, and act upon.

*Writing for Busy Readers* marshals the evidence. Readability scores are typically expressed as either a numerical index or the school grade level a reader would need to have completed to comprehend the text. The *New York Times* requires an eleventh-grade reading level. Most nursery rhymes sit at fourth grade. Around half of American adults read at an eighth-grade level or below, and roughly 20% read English as a second language. Writing above eighth-grade level does not signal sophistication -- it signals exclusion.

**Cognitive load is the mechanism.** Humans evolved to speak and listen long before developing the capacity to read. Spoken language uses short, incomplete sentences; our brains process these structures with minimal effort. Written prose that departs from this pattern -- long sentences, subordinate clauses, unfamiliar vocabulary -- taxes working memory. Eye-tracking studies show that readers pause at end-of-sentence periods to integrate meaning. Longer sentences force readers to hold more information in memory before that integration can occur, increasing the probability of distraction, re-reading, or abandonment.

**The data on sentence length is unambiguous.** Average sentence length in published novels has dropped from 27 words per sentence in 1800 to 10 words per sentence in 2000. Presidential inaugural addresses show the same trajectory: George Washington's opening five sentences in 1789 averaged 64 words each; Joe Biden's in 2021 averaged 7. The trend reflects not declining intelligence but increasing respect for the reader's cognitive constraints.

**Familiar words outperform sophisticated vocabulary.** When two words are synonyms, the shorter and more common one is almost always more readable. Google's Ngram Viewer confirms that "next" is used more frequently than "subsequent," "get" more than "acquire," "show" more than "demonstrate." Research published under the title "Consequences of Erudite Vernacular Utilized Irrespective of Necessity" found that college students rated writers who used complex language as *less* intelligent than those who used simpler language. The paper won an Ig Nobel Prize -- and its finding has held up. In a separate study, corporations with less readable ethics codes were rated by readers as less moral and less trustworthy.

The implication for business writers is straightforward: readability is not a matter of preference but of reach. A document written at a college reading level excludes a measurable portion of its audience. The same document, revised to eighth-grade level using shorter sentences and more common words, excludes almost no one -- and is read more quickly even by those fully capable of parsing the original.

### Part 2: Chronological Ordering

Stories are inherently chronological. One thing happens, then another, then another. This structure works not only in fiction but in any factual business account -- project updates, status reports, incident descriptions, performance evaluations.

Writers who skip chronological ordering produce stream-of-consciousness messages that confuse readers. The facts are present, but the reader cannot reconstruct the sequence. The fix: before drafting any factual account, create a chronology of relevant events.

**Before chronological ordering:**
> "It was hard making headway with Jim Martinez, but finally we're looking at a demonstration of what our software can do by mid-May, as I established in my first telephone conference with Jim last Monday at 9:00 a.m. He was out Wednesday and Thursday, but on Friday he told me that we'd need a sample app. But prior to that, Magnabilify requires an NDA. Tuesday's meeting should clarify things."

**After chronological ordering:**
> "Last week you asked me to approach Magnabilify Corporation to see whether they might have any interest in our customising some security applications for their computer systems. I finally got through to Jim Martinez, corporate vice president in charge of software, and we have planned a face-to-face meeting at his office next Tuesday. The next steps will be to enter into a nondisclosure agreement, to develop a sample application in less than two weeks, and to schedule a demonstration shortly after."

The first version forces the reader to untangle the timeline. The second version tells a story that any reader can follow on the first pass.

### Part 3: Continuity and Transitions

Smooth writing consists of a sequence of well-joined sentences and paragraphs -- not a mere collection of them. The connections between ideas, executed through transitional words and phrases, are the infrastructure of readable prose. When they work, readers do not even notice them. When they are absent, readers stumble.

**The Taxonomy of Transitions:**

| Function | Transitional Words |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Time sequence** | then, at that point, afterward, as soon as, before, after, first, initially, meanwhile, later, next, now, finally |
| **Add a point** | and, or, further, also, in fact, moreover, not only... but also |
| **Underscore a point** | above all, after all, and so, chiefly, equally important, more so, indeed |
| **Concede a point** | although, and yet, admittedly, at the same time, certainly, even though, granted, no doubt, still, whereas, yet, while |
| **Return to a point** | even so, nevertheless, nonetheless, still |
| **Give an example** | for example, for instance, in particular |
| **Provide a reason** | because, hence, thus, for, it follows, since, so, then, therefore |
| **Set up a contrast** | but, yet, and yet, conversely, despite, by contrast, instead, on the other hand, still, while |
| **Set up a conclusion** | so, as a result, finally, in conclusion, in short, in sum, therefore, thus, to sum up |

Transitions are not decorative. They are navigational. Each one tells the reader the relationship between the current idea and the one that preceded it -- whether the writer is adding, contrasting, conceding, concluding, or illustrating.

### Signposting with Subheads

No matter how smooth the transitions between sentences and paragraphs, time-pressed readers will zone out when confronted with a solid wall of text. Subheadings serve as signposts -- they break the document into navigable sections and allow readers to find the information they need.

Effective subheads are:
- **Informative, not generic.** "Background" tells the reader nothing. "Why Our Current Process Takes Four Weeks" tells them exactly what they will learn.
- **Consistent in syntax.** If one subhead is a directive ("Use LinkedIn to Get Feedback"), all subheads should be directives. Parallel structure reinforces logical and rhetorical cohesion.
- **Scannable.** A reader who reads only the subheads should get the gist of the entire document.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Bizspeak Translation:**
> Here is a business document: [paste text]. Identify every instance of corporate jargon, bizspeak, and boilerplate phrasing. For each, provide a plain-English translation. Then rewrite the entire document in plain language, preserving all substantive content. Show a side-by-side comparison of the original and revised versions with word counts.

**Prompt 2 -- Chronological Reordering:**
> Here is a factual account I need to communicate: [paste text or describe events]. The events are currently described out of order. Create a chronology of relevant events (a simple table with dates/timeframes and what happened). Then rewrite the account in chronological order so that a reader with no prior context can follow the narrative on the first read.

**Prompt 3 -- Transition Audit:**
> Here is a document: [paste text]. Analyse the transitions between paragraphs. For each paragraph boundary, identify: (1) whether a transition exists, (2) what function it serves (adding, contrasting, concluding, etc.), (3) whether it is effective. Where transitions are missing or weak, suggest specific transitional words or phrases that would improve flow. Then provide the revised document with improved transitions.

**Prompt 4 -- Subhead Architecture:**
> Here is a document of [X] words: [paste text]. It currently has [no subheads / weak subheads]. Design a subhead architecture that: (1) breaks the document into logical sections, (2) uses informative rather than generic labels, (3) maintains parallel syntax across all subheads, (4) allows a reader to grasp the document's argument by reading only the subheads. Provide the revised document with subheads inserted.

## Use Cases

**Validation-Stage Startup Translating a Technical White Paper for Investors**

A deep-tech startup's white paper is dense with domain jargon that resonates with engineers but alienates investors. The original opening: "Our platform operationalizes a novel approach to leveraging graph-based neural architectures for scalable, mission-critical anomaly detection in enterprise-grade distributed systems." After the bizspeak translation: "Our software detects problems in large computer networks before they cause outages. It uses a type of artificial intelligence called graph neural networks -- the same technology behind social media recommendation engines, adapted for IT infrastructure monitoring." The second version communicates the same technology to a broader audience. The investor can now decide whether to keep reading.

**Growth-Stage Company Fixing Disorganised Status Reports**

A project manager's weekly status reports read like stream-of-consciousness: events from different weeks are jumbled, outcomes are mentioned before their causes, and the reader cannot tell what happened this week versus last month. Applying the chronological ordering framework: the PM creates a simple timeline before drafting each report -- last week's events, this week's events, next week's planned actions. The report structure becomes: "Where we were / What happened / Where we're going." Reader comprehension (measured by the number of follow-up questions from the VP) drops from an average of five per report to one.

**Scale-Stage Company Establishing a Plain-Language Style Guide**

A 400-person financial services firm discovers that customer communications score below 30 on the Flesch Reading Ease scale -- roughly equivalent to the *Harvard Law Review*, and thoroughly inappropriate for retail clients. The firm creates a plain-language standard: all customer-facing documents must score above 50 (roughly *Time* magazine level), all subheads must be informative rather than generic, and the bizspeak blacklist is circulated to every department. Within six months, customer complaint calls related to "confusing correspondence" drop by 35%.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Replacing Jargon with Different Jargon.** Translating "synergize core competencies" into "align strategic capabilities" is not plain language -- it is a lateral move within the same dialect. Plain language means describing what is actually happening in words a non-specialist would use.

2. **Eliminating All Technical Terms.** Plain language does not mean banning technical vocabulary. When writing for specialists, precise technical terms are more efficient than lengthy plain-language circumlocutions. The test is audience: will the reader know this term? If yes, use it. If no, define it or replace it.

3. **Over-Transitioning.** Cramming a transitional word into every sentence makes prose feel mechanical and patronising. The best transitions are almost subliminal -- readers follow the logic without noticing the connective tissue. Use transitions where the relationship between ideas might be ambiguous; omit them where the connection is self-evident.

4. **Using Subheads as Decoration.** Subheads that do not accurately describe the content below them are worse than no subheads at all. "Miscellaneous" is not a subhead -- it is an admission that the writer could not be bothered to organise the section.

5. **Chronological Ordering When It Does Not Apply.** Not all business documents benefit from chronological structure. Recommendations, analyses, and persuasive arguments are often better organised by importance or logical dependency. Chronology serves factual accounts -- status reports, incident descriptions, project updates. Misapplying it to analytical documents produces a confusing "and then... and then..." narrative.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Jargon-free pitching | Early-stage founders often mimic the jargon of their target industry to seem credible. The opposite is more effective: plain language signals clarity of thought, which investors value more than vocabulary. |
| Validation | Evidence narration | Customer discovery stories, pilot results, and market validation are factual accounts that benefit from chronological ordering. Plain language ensures that non-technical stakeholders can evaluate the evidence. |
| Early Traction | Scaling communication clarity | As the team grows, internal communications must be accessible to new hires who lack the company's institutional jargon. Plain language accelerates onboarding. |
| Growth | Cross-functional readability | Marketing writes for customers, engineering writes for product, finance writes for investors -- and sometimes they must read each other's documents. Plain language is the common dialect that makes cross-functional communication possible. |
| Scale | Institutional plain-language standards | The bizspeak blacklist becomes an organisational style guide. Readability scores become acceptance criteria for external communications. New employees are trained on plain-language principles during onboarding. |
