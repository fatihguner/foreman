# Writing for Response

ONE PERCENT. When DC public schools asked parents to enrol in a text-message update programme via an online portal, 1% did. When the schools simplified the process to a single text reply -- respond "START" -- 11% did. When they made enrolment the default and let parents opt out by replying "STOP," 95% stayed. The lesson is not about parental apathy. The parents who ignored the portal were the same parents who stayed enrolled under the default. Motivation was constant across all three groups; friction was the variable. Response rates are not a measure of how much readers care. They are a measure of how much effort the writer demands.

## The Framework

### The Response Gap

Even when readers understand a message perfectly and want to act on it, three barriers can prevent them from doing so:

1. **They do not understand what is being asked.** The request is ambiguous, buried, or tangled in surrounding information. This is a clarity failure.
2. **They do not believe the action is worth the effort.** The writer has not established relevance, urgency, or personal stakes. This is a persuasion failure.
3. **The action is too hard or too time-consuming to perform right now.** The reader intends to comply but defers, and deferral is the precursor to forgetting. This is a friction failure.

Most writers address the first two barriers instinctively. The third is where the opportunity lies -- and where the research is most striking.

### Three Rules of Easy-Response Writing

**Rule 1: Simplify the Steps Required to Act**

The most powerful technique is eliminating steps entirely by changing the default. When an action happens automatically unless the reader opts out, participation rates approach universality. The retirement savings literature makes the case decisively: automatic enrolment with an opt-out option produces dramatically higher participation than opt-in processes requiring deliberate sign-up, even when the financial incentives are identical.

When changing the default is not feasible, reducing the number of steps still yields large effects. The DC schools study quantified this precisely: moving from a multi-step portal (1% enrolment) to a single-step text reply (11% enrolment) produced a tenfold increase. The parents' interest in their children's grades did not change. The number of actions required did.

Consider meeting scheduling, a task most professionals perform weekly. The open-ended approach -- "Want to talk next week?" -- initiates a multi-round volley of availability comparisons. Each round imposes a cognitive and logistical cost. An alternative:

> Want to meet for 30 minutes next week? If so, how about one of these times (all ET):
> - Tuesday (3/13) at 10:30 am
> - Wednesday (3/14) at 12:00 pm
> - Thursday (3/15) at 3:00 pm

This version specifies duration, proposes concrete options, clarifies time zones, and allows the reader to respond by selecting rather than generating. A study at a major US university tested a further simplification: including a Calendly link that let recipients book directly from the sender's calendar. The Calendly version secured meetings with 33% of recipients versus 17% for the standard "let me know your availability" request. The caveat: some recipients find unsolicited calendar links presumptuous, so the technique requires calibration to context.

**Rule 2: Organise Key Information Needed for Action**

Readers who must hunt for information in order to act are readers who will not act. When the US government offered prescription drug benefits to Medicare recipients in 2006, it sent letters directing recipients to a website with an 84-character URL, plus a booklet on how to navigate the site. Only 17% switched to a better plan. Researchers then tested a simplified letter that included the relevant comparison directly: the recipient's current plan, the lowest-cost alternative, the recommended option, and the estimated savings. No website navigation required. The switch rate nearly doubled to 28%, and average savings were $100 per person per year.

The principle generalises to every action-oriented message. Instead of writing, "See the message below, sent on April 3, and let me know what you think," restate the pertinent information from April 3 in the current message. Searching through an email chain is a step. Every step is a chance for the reader to abandon the task.

For communications that direct readers to online tools -- forms, portals, applications -- include the specific credentials needed to log in, when privacy constraints allow. "Did you know you can file your immigration forms online at [website]?" becomes significantly more actionable as: "Did you know you can file your immigration forms online? Your username is [name] and you can log in at [website]." One additional line eliminates the "forgot my username" barrier that causes a substantial portion of intended actions to stall.

**Rule 3: Minimise the Amount of Attention Required**

The brain's attention budget is finite, and complex decisions accelerate its depletion. A study at a large health services company found that offering employees a preselected retirement plan (with preset contribution rate and asset allocation) increased enrolment by 10 to 20 percentage points compared to requiring them to choose among all available options. Fewer decisions meant more participants.

Narrow questions outperform broad ones for the same reason.

| Question Type | Example | Reader Effort |
|---------------|---------|---------------|
| Broad (open-ended) | "How was yesterday's senior staff meeting?" | High. Requires synthesis and composition. |
| Narrow (constrained) | "In yesterday's meeting, did we decide whether we're submitting a bid?" | Low. Answerable with yes or no. |

The broad question may yield richer feedback -- but only if the reader responds at all, which the data suggests is less likely.

Excess choice creates what researchers call an "attention tax." Studies confirm that when people face too many options, they defer the decision -- often permanently. President Obama reportedly limited his wardrobe to grey and blue suits for precisely this reason: "I'm trying to pare down decisions. I don't want to make decisions about what I'm eating or wearing. Because I have too many other decisions to make."

The ultimate test of attention minimisation is the **30-second response test**: can a reader act on this message within 30 seconds of opening it? If the answer is no, the message needs structural revision -- fewer steps, more constrained choices, or information that was missing and must now be supplied directly.

### Multiple Requests: Bundle or Separate?

Action-oriented messages frequently contain more than one request. A healthcare clinic, for instance, may need to (1) collect insurance information, (2) provide check-in instructions, and (3) confirm an appointment time. All three are urgent and related. In a survey of working professionals, 72% said they prefer receiving one message with three pieces of information over three separate messages. When requests relate to the same event and require similar levels of effort, bundling makes sense.

The calculus changes when requests require different amounts of effort. A family email asking what time a relative will arrive (easy), what holiday gifts to buy (moderate), and where to find a lost family recipe (hard) bundles three fundamentally different cognitive tasks. The reader may answer the easy question and forget the rest -- or defer the entire message until they have time for the hardest question, a moment that may never arrive. Separating unequal requests into distinct messages protects the easy ones from being held hostage by the hard.

When bundling is unavoidable, signpost the structure at the top: "Below are three items: (1) an update on the renovation, (2) a request for your approval on light fixtures, and (3) a request to schedule a call." The preview gives the reader a map before they enter the territory.

### The Friction Spectrum in Practice

| Friction Level | Mechanism | Typical Response Rate |
|----------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Zero (default) | Action happens unless reader opts out | 90-95% compliance |
| Minimal (one-step) | Single reply, single click, or single selection | 10-35% compliance |
| Moderate (multi-step) | Navigate to portal, log in, complete form | 1-5% compliance |
| High (complex) | Research options, make multiple decisions, find credentials | Below 1% |

These numbers shift by context, audience, and stakes, but the gradient is remarkably consistent across domains -- from school enrolment to retirement savings to voter registration to meeting scheduling.

### The IRS and the Power of Simple Instructions

A study with the US Internal Revenue Service tested different styles of notification about the Earned Income Tax Credit, a benefit that eligible low-income workers must actively claim each year (roughly 20% of those eligible fail to do so). The simplified version included a clear, step-by-step explanation of what to do: complete the worksheet, determine eligibility, sign and return if eligible. The complex original version listed six bullet points of instructions plus a footnote. The complex notice produced a response rate 27% lower than the simple one. Moreover, a worksheet with seven eligibility criteria generated 17% fewer responses than one with just two criteria. The additional detail was accurate and potentially useful. It was also, in practice, a deterrent.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Friction Audit:**
> Map every step a reader must take to act on the following message. For each step, estimate the time and cognitive effort required. Then propose a revised version that eliminates at least two steps while preserving all essential outcomes. Message: [paste text]

**Prompt 2 -- Default Design:**
> I need [describe audience] to [describe desired action]. The current approach requires them to opt in. Design an alternative that makes the desired action the default, with a clear and easy opt-out mechanism. Include the exact language for the initial communication and the opt-out instructions.

**Prompt 3 -- 30-Second Response Test:**
> Apply the 30-second response test to this message. Assume the reader opens it on a mobile phone while walking between meetings. Can they complete the requested action in 30 seconds? If not, identify the bottleneck and rewrite the message to pass the test. Message: [paste text]

**Prompt 4 -- Constrained Choice Conversion:**
> The following message asks the reader an open-ended question. Rewrite it as a constrained-choice question with no more than three predefined response options. Preserve the writer's intent while reducing the reader's cognitive load. Message: [paste text]

## Use Cases

**Idea-Stage Founder Scheduling Investor Meetings**

A pre-seed founder sends 30 emails per week requesting introductory meetings with potential investors. The standard close reads: "Would love to find a time to chat -- let me know what works for you." This open-ended request requires the investor to check their calendar, compose available times, and manage the resulting back-and-forth. The founder switches to a constrained format: three specific time slots with durations and time zones, plus a Calendly link as a fallback. Meeting conversion from cold email rises from 4% to 11%. The pitch itself did not improve. The path from "interested" to "booked" shortened.

**Validation-Stage Startup Increasing Survey Completion**

A consumer health startup sends a post-onboarding survey to 2,000 beta users. The survey contains 14 open-ended questions and takes approximately eight minutes to complete. Completion rate: 6%. The product team diagnoses the problem using the friction spectrum: the survey sits at "moderate" friction (click link, load page, read 14 questions, compose 14 answers). Eight minutes is an eternity for a user who signed up three days ago.

The team applies the three rules systematically. They reduce the survey to five questions (Rule 3), convert four of them to multiple-choice with a free-text "Other" option (Rule 3), and embed the first question directly in the email body so readers can begin answering before they even click through to the survey platform (Rule 1). The email subject line changes from "Share your feedback" to "One quick question about your first session." Completion rate rises to 23%. The qualitative richness of individual responses decreases modestly, but the sample size nearly quadruples, improving the statistical reliability of every finding.

**Growth-Stage Company Driving Adoption of a New Expense Tool**

A 400-person company rolls out a new expense reporting tool. The finance team sends an all-hands email with a three-paragraph explanation, a link to a 20-minute training video, and instructions to "set up your account by Friday." Adoption after one week: 22%. The team redesigns the rollout communication. The email now opens with a single sentence ("Submit expenses in two clicks -- your account is already active"), includes a direct link to the tool with the user's credentials pre-filled, and replaces the training video with a three-step inline guide (Rule 2). A 15-second GIF shows the complete submission flow. The message passes the 30-second response test: a reader can click, submit a test expense, and confirm their account works in under half a minute. Adoption after one week with the new approach: 71%.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **The Open-Ended Ask.** "Let me know your thoughts" or "When are you free?" These phrases feel polite. They are also friction machines. Each one transfers the cognitive burden of structuring a response from the writer to the reader. A constrained alternative -- "Does Tuesday at 2 pm work?" -- reduces the reader's task from composition to selection.

2. **The Portal Redirect.** Directing readers to log into an external system to complete an action that could have been completed within the message itself. Every redirect -- especially one requiring credentials -- is a cliff that a large percentage of readers will not climb. The DC schools study measured the cliff at 99% attrition for portal-based enrolment.

3. **The Information Scavenger Hunt.** Asking readers to act but making them search through prior emails, attachments, or external documents to assemble the information needed. If acting on a request requires data, include the data. If the data is too long to include, summarise it and link to the source, but never assume the reader will retrieve it independently.

4. **The Choice Overload.** Offering six meeting times, twelve plan options, or eight survey response categories when three would suffice. Research consistently shows that excess choice induces decision paralysis. The IRS study on Earned Income Tax Credit applications found that a worksheet with seven eligibility criteria produced a 17% lower response rate than one with two criteria -- despite containing more useful information.

5. **The Assumed Follow-Through.** Sending a message that requires action "by end of week" without any follow-up mechanism. Present-bias means readers are structurally inclined to defer. A single well-timed reminder -- sent close to the deadline, not at the beginning of the week -- significantly increases completion rates. Research on medical appointment reminders, savings programmes, and child support payments all confirm this effect. A study on voter registration found that the first five reminder mailings each incrementally increased turnout, but the last five of ten had no further effect. In a Kenyan HIV treatment study, weekly reminders improved adherence by 32%, while daily reminders produced no improvement at all -- likely because their frequency caused patients to tune them out. The principle: remind enough to overcome procrastination, but not so much that reminders become noise.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Meeting requests, advisor asks, survey invitations | Every interaction is a cold start. The reader has no relationship-driven reason to tolerate friction. Requests must be answerable in a single action -- one click, one reply, one selection. |
| Validation | Beta user engagement, investor commitment letters, LOI signatures | The stakes of each response rise. Friction audits of high-value requests (signing an LOI, committing capital) reveal surprisingly removable barriers -- a pre-filled signature block, a direct DocuSign link. |
| Early Traction | Customer onboarding, feature adoption, support ticket resolution | Response design shifts from one-to-one to one-to-many. Templates for common action requests (account setup, feedback, renewals) must pass the 30-second test at scale. |
| Growth | Internal process adoption, cross-team coordination, policy compliance | Friction compounds across a growing organisation. A five-step expense process that "only takes three minutes" costs 400 person-hours per year in a 400-person company. Default design and one-step actions become operational priorities. |
| Scale | Automated enrolment flows, renewal campaigns, regulatory filings | Response architecture is embedded in product and operations. A/B testing of default configurations, step counts, and CTA placements becomes a quantitative practice affecting millions of interactions. |

## Output Template

```markdown
# Response Design: [Message / Flow Name]

## Desired Action
[One sentence: what the reader should do after reading.]

## Current Friction Map
| Step | Action Required | Est. Time | Drop-off Risk |
|------|----------------|-----------|---------------|
| 1 | [e.g., Open email] | 2 sec | Low |
| 2 | [e.g., Click link] | 3 sec | Medium |
| 3 | [e.g., Log into portal] | 45 sec | High |
| 4 | [e.g., Find and complete form] | 3 min | Very High |

## 30-Second Response Test
- **Passes?** [Yes / No]
- **Bottleneck:** [The step where the 30-second budget is exceeded]

## Redesign Strategy
- **Default option:** [If applicable -- what happens if the reader does nothing?]
- **Steps eliminated:** [Which steps were removed and how]
- **Information pre-supplied:** [What data was added to the message to prevent searching]
- **Choice constraints applied:** [Open-ended questions converted to selections]

## Revised Message
[Full rewrite, structured for minimum friction, annotated with rule numbers]

## Follow-Up Plan
- **Reminder timing:** [When to send, relative to deadline]
- **Reminder content:** [Abbreviated version restating the ask and the single easiest step]
```
