# Writing Performance Reviews

A well-written performance review builds a career. A poorly written one invites a lawsuit. Between these extremes lies the vast wasteland of vague, unhelpful evaluations that serve neither the employee nor the organisation: "Meets expectations." "Good team player." "Could improve communication skills." These phrases occupy space on a form without communicating anything that the employee can act upon, the organisation can calibrate against, or a court can interpret as evidence of fair process. The performance review is arguably the highest-stakes document a manager writes, yet it receives less training, less editorial oversight, and less structural rigour than almost any other form of business writing.

The cost of this neglect is measurable. Vague positive reviews make it nearly impossible to justify promotion decisions when challenged. Vague negative reviews provide no legal foundation for termination. And the employees themselves -- the ostensible audience for the document -- learn nothing from prose that could apply equally to any person in any role.

## The Framework

### The Seven Evaluation Dimensions

The framework identifies seven aspects of work that performance reviews should address. Not every review covers all seven -- the selection depends on the role and the review period. But the framework provides a comprehensive vocabulary for evaluation.

**1. Attitude**

| Level | Descriptors |
|-------|-------------|
| Superb | Shows unwavering commitment; always gives maximal effort; is always friendly and happy to help; always brings out the best in others |
| Good | Shows strong commitment; usually makes a strong effort; is usually friendly and happy to help |
| Acceptable | Shows adequate commitment; makes an effort; is often friendly and happy to help |
| Needs Improvement | Could show more commitment; doesn't always make an effort; is sometimes quarrelsome |
| Poor | Lacks commitment; rarely makes a real effort; is quarrelsome and sometimes even hostile |

**2. Efficiency**

| Level | Descriptors |
|-------|-------------|
| Superb | Never wastes time or effort; delegates effectively; always completes tasks on time; manages many projects simultaneously |
| Good | Rarely wastes time; usually delegates appropriately; almost always completes tasks on time |
| Needs Improvement | Sometimes wastes time and effort; tries to do too much without delegating; fails to complete tasks on time |
| Poor | Often wastes time and effort; usually fails to delegate; struggles to manage even one project at a time |

**3. Human Relations**

| Level | Descriptors |
|-------|-------------|
| Superb | Demonstrates keen sensitivity to others; participates actively and collegially in meetings; works exceptionally well on teams; relates to customers extremely well |
| Good | Usually demonstrates sensitivity; participates effectively in meetings; works effectively on teams |
| Needs Improvement | Does not always pick up on interpersonal cues; sometimes wastes others' time in meetings; is sometimes motivated more by personal goals than team goals |
| Poor | Rarely pays attention to others' reactions; often wastes others' time; does not work well on teams; often alienates customers |

**4. Judgment**

| Level | Descriptors |
|-------|-------------|
| Superb | Makes excellent choices and informed decisions; remains utterly calm and collected in crisis; knows precisely which problems need immediate attention |
| Good | Makes sound choices; remains relatively calm in crisis; generally knows which problems need immediate attention |
| Needs Improvement | Sometimes makes poor choices; sometimes lacks composure in high-pressure circumstances; often doesn't distinguish between urgent and non-urgent |
| Poor | Often makes poor choices and ill-informed decisions; often lacks the calm required in high-pressure circumstances |

**5. Knowledge**

| Level | Descriptors |
|-------|-------------|
| Superb | Exceptionally well informed about all aspects of the job; demonstrates extraordinarily comprehensive knowledge; handles complex assignments without supervision |
| Good | Well informed about key aspects; demonstrates thorough knowledge; handles complex assignments with some supervision |
| Needs Improvement | Doesn't fully understand the job; demonstrates less than satisfactory knowledge; sometimes mishandles assignments of moderate complexity |
| Poor | Ill-informed about many aspects of the job; demonstrates inadequate knowledge; mishandles basic assignments |

**6. Reliability**

| Level | Descriptors |
|-------|-------------|
| Superb | Always meets deadlines; is unfailingly dependable; achieves excellent results in urgent situations; always delivers on promises |
| Good | Meets deadlines; is highly dependable; achieves good results in urgent situations |
| Needs Improvement | Sometimes fails to meet important deadlines; is sometimes undependable; sometimes fails to deliver on promises |
| Poor | Often fails to meet deadlines; is rarely dependable; can't be counted on to deliver on promises |

**7. Communication Skills**

| Level | Descriptors |
|-------|-------------|
| Superb | Writes and speaks with remarkable clarity; never gets bogged down in unnecessary details; has superior communication skills in person and over the phone |
| Good | Writes and speaks clearly; rarely gets bogged down; has sound communication skills |
| Needs Improvement | Sometimes writes and speaks unclearly and with undue complexity; sometimes gets bogged down in unnecessary details |
| Poor | Writes and speaks unclearly; gets bogged down in unnecessary details; fails to communicate effectively |

### The Cardinal Rule: Pair Every Assessment with Evidence

A performance descriptor is a hypothesis. Evidence is what makes it credible.

**Vague (useless):**
> "Lauren shows unwavering commitment."

**Specific (useful):**
> "When we had several layoffs last June, Lauren remained utterly calm and collected while demonstrating keen sensitivity to those who lost their jobs. She personally conducted exit interviews with each affected team member, ensured their final paycheques were processed within 24 hours, and organised a quiet send-off that preserved the dignity of the departing employees."

The first version could describe anyone. The second version describes exactly one person doing exactly one set of things. This specificity serves three purposes: it tells the employee precisely what behaviour to continue, it provides the organisation with a defensible record, and it demonstrates that the reviewer was actually paying attention.

### The Legal Dimension

Performance reviews are legal documents whether managers intend them as such or not. They are discoverable in litigation, admissible as evidence, and routinely used to support or undermine claims of wrongful termination, discrimination, and retaliation.

Three legal imperatives for review writers:

1. **Consistency.** A trail of "exceeds expectations" reviews followed by a sudden termination invites a wrongful-termination claim. If performance is deteriorating, the reviews must reflect the deterioration -- contemporaneously, not retroactively.

2. **Specificity.** "Poor attitude" is a conclusion. A court will ask: what, specifically, constituted the poor attitude? When did it occur? Was the employee notified? What was the expected standard? If the review cannot answer these questions, the assessment is legally indefensible.

3. **Fairness.** Applying different evaluative standards to different employees performing the same role -- even unconsciously -- creates exposure to discrimination claims. The seven-dimension framework, applied consistently, provides a structured defence against inconsistency.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Review Drafter:**
> I need to write a performance review for [describe employee, role, and tenure]. Over the review period, the key observations are: [list 3-5 specific situations, behaviours, and outcomes]. Draft a review that covers the relevant evaluation dimensions, pairs every assessment with the specific evidence I've provided, and maintains a tone that is honest, constructive, and respectful. Include both strengths and areas for development.

**Prompt 2 -- Vagueness Eliminator:**
> Here is a draft performance review: [paste text]. Identify every instance of vague or unsupported evaluative language. For each, flag the vague phrase, explain why it is insufficient, and suggest a specific revision that pairs the assessment with observable evidence. If evidence is missing, indicate what kind of evidence would be needed.

**Prompt 3 -- Difficult Review Preparation:**
> I need to write a performance review for an underperforming employee. The situation: [describe specific performance issues with dates and examples]. I need the review to be (1) honest about the performance gaps, (2) specific enough to be legally defensible, (3) constructive enough to give the employee a genuine path to improvement, and (4) documented clearly enough to support further action if improvement doesn't occur. Draft the review with these four objectives in mind.

**Prompt 4 -- Review Template Designer:**
> Design a performance review template for a [describe company size and stage] company. The template should cover [specify which of the seven evaluation dimensions are most relevant for the roles in the company]. Include structured fields that force reviewers to provide specific evidence for every rating, a self-assessment section for the employee, and a development-plan section that translates the review into forward-looking commitments.

## Use Cases

**Early-Traction Startup Conducting Its First Reviews**
A 15-person startup runs its first performance review cycle. The CEO, having never written a review, produces evaluations that read like casual character references: "Alex is great, super dependable, and the team loves working with him." This is gratifying for Alex but useless for calibration. After applying the seven-dimension framework, the review becomes: "Alex demonstrates exceptional reliability -- he has met every sprint deadline this quarter and was the sole engineer who volunteered for the holiday on-call rotation. His knowledge of our payment infrastructure is comprehensive, and he handles complex debugging assignments without supervision. His area for development is communication: his technical documentation, while accurate, sometimes assumes reader familiarity with the codebase that new team members do not have." Alex now knows what to keep doing and what to develop.

**Growth-Stage Company Defending a Termination Decision**
A 200-person company terminates an underperforming sales manager. The manager files a wrongful-termination claim, alleging that the termination was discriminatory. The company's defence rests on a trail of performance reviews documenting specific, dated instances of underperformance across three consecutive quarters: missed pipeline targets (with exact numbers), client complaints (with specific dates and descriptions), and failure to implement the agreed-upon improvement plan (with milestone dates and gaps). The reviews, written using the evidence-pairing discipline, provide a documentary foundation that withstands legal scrutiny. A less rigorous review trail -- "needs improvement in sales performance" -- would have been nearly indefensible.

**Scale-Stage Organisation Calibrating Across Managers**
A 500-person company discovers significant inconsistency in how managers rate performance. One department rates 80% of employees as "superb"; another rates only 15% as such for comparable performance. By standardising on the seven-dimension framework with required evidence for each rating, the company enables meaningful cross-department calibration sessions. Managers must justify their ratings against specific behavioural indicators, reducing the influence of personal bias and relationship dynamics.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **The Recency Trap.** Evaluating an entire year based on the last three weeks because the reviewer kept no notes during the intervening months. Performance reviews require continuous documentation -- not a frantic retrospective the night before the form is due.

2. **The Halo Effect.** Allowing exceptional performance in one dimension to inflate ratings across all dimensions. An engineer who writes brilliant code but alienates every colleague deserves a "superb" in knowledge and a "needs improvement" in human relations. Conflating the two produces a review that is inaccurate and unhelpful.

3. **Euphemism as Kindness.** Describing genuine performance problems in language so gentle that the employee does not recognise them as problems. "Could benefit from additional focus on timeliness" does not communicate the same urgency as "Has missed four of the last six project deadlines, resulting in two client escalations." False kindness in reviews is a form of dishonesty that ultimately harms the employee.

4. **The Copy-Paste Review.** Reusing the same language across multiple employees' reviews, changing only the name. This signals -- accurately -- that the reviewer invested no effort in individualised assessment. It also creates legal exposure if employees compare reviews and find identical language applied to different performance levels.

5. **Avoiding Negative Feedback Entirely.** Writing exclusively positive reviews for fear of confrontation, then using a sudden termination to address performance issues that were never documented. This pattern is both legally dangerous and managerially irresponsible. The employee never received a genuine opportunity to improve because the written record suggested nothing needed improving.

6. **Weaponising the Review.** Using the performance review to settle personal grievances, document subjective "attitude" complaints without evidence, or build a paper trail for a predetermined termination. Reviews serve the employee's development and the organisation's record. Using them as a political tool corrodes institutional trust.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Early Traction | First-time managers writing first reviews | Managers who have never written a review need a framework. Without one, they default to either vague praise or uncomfortable avoidance of constructive feedback. The seven-dimension model provides immediate structure. |
| Growth | Consistency across a growing management layer | With ten or twenty managers writing reviews independently, calibration becomes essential. The framework provides a common vocabulary that enables cross-manager comparison and reduces bias. |
| Scale | Institutional review infrastructure | At scale, performance reviews are embedded in HR systems, tied to compensation decisions, and subject to legal review. Templates, training programmes, and calibration sessions ensure quality at volume. |
