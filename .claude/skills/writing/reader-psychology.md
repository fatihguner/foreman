# Reader Psychology

F-PATTERN. Eye-tracking research reveals that readers do not read -- they scan in an F shape, spending most attention on the first two lines and the left margin. Everything else is a gamble. Psychologist George Miller's landmark study established that human working memory holds approximately seven items, plus or minus two, at any given moment. Between that hard cognitive ceiling and the 121 emails the average manager receives daily, the arithmetic is unforgiving: every sentence a writer produces competes against a reader's finite neural budget. Understanding that budget -- its size, its depletion rate, its failure modes -- is the prerequisite for writing that actually gets through.

---

## The Framework

### The Busy Brain: Three Constraints

The limited capacity of the human attention system produces three consequences that shape every act of reading.

**Constraint 1: We Cannot Process Everything in Front of Us**

At any moment, the brain confronts more information than it can absorb. Its attention system responds by acting as a filter -- selecting what to notice and suppressing the rest. A 1999 Harvard study demonstrated this vividly: when participants were asked to count basketball passes in a video, nearly half failed to notice a person in a gorilla costume strolling through the scene. The brain's suppression of irrelevant stimuli is so aggressive that even conspicuous objects vanish when they fall outside the current focus. For writers, the implication is stark: information that is present in a message but not actively foregrounded may as well not exist.

**Constraint 2: Focus Exhausts Quickly**

Directing attention is effortful, and the resource depletes faster than most people assume. In a study by psychologist Brandon Schmeichel, participants who spent six minutes resisting the urge to look at flashing words on screen subsequently scored 20% lower on a graduate-level reading comprehension test. Six minutes of attentional control was enough to measurably impair later cognitive performance. Physical fatigue compounds the effect -- athletes under physical exhaustion show impaired attention and decision-making, and information scientist Gloria Mark found that workers need an average of 23 minutes to fully return to a task after a single interruption. One study estimated that minds wander a full third of the time while people attempt to read, with the rate climbing further when the material is complicated.

**Constraint 3: Multitasking Fails**

Strictly speaking, the brain cannot think about two tasks simultaneously. What people call "multitasking" is rapid task-switching, and it is cognitively expensive. A study at Butler University and Eli Lilly found that pharmacists asked to answer questions while filling prescriptions took longer and made substantially more errors. US government statistics attribute thousands of road deaths each year to texting while driving -- yet by some estimates, 22% of drivers report doing it daily. The Latin writer Publilius Syrus captured the principle in the first century BCE: "To do two things at once is to do neither." Modern survey data confirms that 63% of professionals report regularly working on two or three tasks simultaneously -- and modern research confirms they are worse at all of them when they do.

The Stroop effect offers a vivid demonstration. When participants are asked to name the formatting applied to a word (e.g., say "bold" when they see the word "italicise" printed in bold), performance collapses. The brain cannot suppress the automatic reading of the word while simultaneously identifying the formatting -- two trivially simple tasks that become agonising in combination. The relevance for writers: any message that requires the reader to hold one piece of information in mind while processing a second is exploiting a cognitive capacity that barely exists.

### How Readers Triage: The Four-Stage Filter

Every time a reader encounters a written communication -- even a text message or a Slack notification -- they pass through four rapid, often unconscious decisions.

| Stage | Question the Reader Asks | What Determines the Answer |
|-------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| 1. Whether to engage | "Is this worth my time?" | Sender recognition, subject line, apparent length. In surveys, working professionals estimate they delete roughly half their emails without reading them. |
| 2. When to engage | "Now or later?" | Perceived effort. In one survey, 99% of professionals said they would respond first to the message that appeared easier. People prefer easy tasks now and defer hard tasks to "later" -- and "later" frequently means "never." |
| 3. How to engage | "Read closely, skim, or scan?" | Expected utility per second. Nearly 40% of emails and 20% of text messages are skimmed. Readers maximise information extraction while minimising time spent. |
| 4. Whether to respond | "Is the requested action worth the cost?" | Clarity of the request, ease of compliance, and perceived importance. Even willing readers fail to act when the steps are unclear or burdensome. |

The preference for easy, short messages is not laziness -- it is a hardwired bias toward present-moment gratification. MRI research shows that limbic reward regions activate when the brain considers desirable outcomes available now, but remain largely dormant for outcomes deferred to the future. A classic experiment found that most people prefer $100 today over $101 in a week, despite the latter representing a 68% annualised return. A survey of the book's own participants revealed that respondents would rather sit in traffic for 31 minutes a week from now than 30 minutes right now -- paying an extra minute of misery for the privilege of deferring. The same bias applies to reading: if a message looks like it will be an unpleasant slog, the reader will push it off until "later," which, for practical purposes, means never.

This temporal myopia shapes not just whether people read but what they read first. When presented with a mix of easy and hard tasks, people consistently tackle the easy ones first -- even when offered financial incentives to prioritise the hard ones. The implication for writers: your message is not competing solely against other messages. It is competing against every easier, more pleasant alternative in the reader's immediate environment.

### How Readers Actually Read: Skimming, Scanning, and the F-Pattern

Eye-tracking studies reveal a consistent scanning pattern across web pages, emails, and documents. Readers fixate most heavily on the first two lines, then scan down the left margin, occasionally darting rightward -- forming the shape of the letter F. Anchor points that attract fixation include headings, first sentences of paragraphs, visually contrasting elements (bold text, bullet points), and images. Text that falls outside these anchor points is routinely skipped.

Psychologists Keith Rayner and Monica Castelhano mapped the difference between close reading and skimming: during close reading, the eyes move word by word in sequence; during skimming, fixations drop sharply and the eyes jump across entire lines. Since busy readers default to skimming, any information not positioned at a natural anchor point is statistically unlikely to be absorbed.

Scanning is the companion strategy to skimming. Where skimming moves linearly but fast, scanning involves jumping between sections of a text, guided by selective attention and rules of thumb about where valuable information is most likely to reside. Readers often expect the first sentence of a paragraph to orient them to the rest of it. They spend more time on those opening sentences and use them to decide whether to read, skim, or skip what follows. Headings serve the same function at a higher structural level: readers use them as a table of contents for the document, zooming into sections that seem relevant and bypassing the rest entirely.

The combined effect is sobering. In surveys conducted by the researchers, participants reported skimming nearly 40% of their emails and 20% of their text messages. These are not long-form documents -- they are the shortest, most urgent forms of written communication available. If readers skim a two-paragraph email, the probability that they will closely read a five-page memo is negligible without deliberate navigational architecture.

A Carnegie Mellon study found that being interrupted by a phone call while taking a reading test decreased performance by 20%. The modern workplace is essentially a continuous interruption environment. The effective writer designs for this reality rather than against it -- placing critical information at the anchor points where even a scanning, distracted, cognitively depleted reader will land.

### The Distracted Driver Analogy

The book's authors draw a useful parallel between busy reading and distracted driving. Most adults are experienced drivers and experienced phone-talkers -- both tasks are individually routine. Yet psychologists have suggested that talking on the phone while driving has as bad an effect on performance as being legally drunk. The explanation returns to the attention system: even two well-practised tasks compete for the same finite cognitive bandwidth when performed simultaneously. Reading a work email while mentally preparing for a meeting, while a Slack notification pulses in the peripheral vision, creates an analogous collision. The reader is not stupid or careless. The reader is a cognitive system operating at capacity, and the writer who fails to account for that capacity is the one whose message gets lost.

### The Writer's Blind Spot

Writers systematically overestimate how much of their message readers will consume. This is a variant of the "curse of knowledge" -- having written every word, the writer assumes the reader will encounter every word. The data says otherwise. Readers skip paragraphs, jump backward to find missed details, and abandon messages mid-sentence when the expected value of the next second of reading drops below the value of doing something else.

A Pew Research Center survey found that 60% of US adults -- and 74% of parents -- report feeling too busy to enjoy life at least some of the time. The writers' own surveys corroborate the figure: 60% of respondents said they often do not have enough time to get everything done in a typical month. Against that backdrop of chronic time scarcity, the writer's job is not to produce comprehensive text but to survive a brutal triage process. The writing that gets through is the writing that respects the reader's constraints -- not the writing that tests them.

Identify: (a) where the reader's F-pattern scan would likely fixate, (b) what information would be missed during a five-second skim, and (c) which elements create unnecessary cognitive load. Then suggest specific revisions. Message: [paste text]

**Prompt 2 -- Triage Simulation:**
> Imagine you are a [describe recipient: e.g., Series A investor reviewing 40 pitch emails per day]. Walk through the four-stage reader triage for the following message: (1) Would you engage? (2) Would you engage now or later? (3) Would you skim or read closely? (4) Would you respond? At each stage, explain what would tip the decision, and flag where the message fails. Message: [paste text]

**Prompt 3 -- Attention Budget Calculator:**
> I need to communicate [describe content] to [describe audience]. Estimate the reader's likely attention budget in seconds, based on the medium ([email/Slack/memo]), the sender-recipient relationship, and the perceived urgency. Then help me restructure the message so that the highest-priority information lands within the first 30% of that budget.

**Prompt 4 -- Distracted Reader Stress Test:**
> Rewrite the following message so it survives the "distracted driver" test: a reader who is simultaneously managing three other tasks, has been interrupted twice in the last ten minutes, and will give this message no more than fifteen seconds. Preserve all essential content but restructure for maximum absorption under minimal attention. Message: [paste text]

## Use Cases

**Idea-Stage Founder Writing a Cold Investor Email**

A fintech founder sends a two-paragraph email to a target angel investor. The first paragraph opens with three sentences of personal background. The second contains the ask -- a 20-minute call to discuss a payments API. Eye-tracking logic predicts the investor's F-pattern scan will fixate on the first line (personal background, no hook), glance left-margin down the second paragraph, and likely disengage before reaching the ask buried in the final sentence. The investor, receiving 40 such emails daily, applies the Stage 1 triage almost instantaneously: sender unknown, subject line generic ("Quick intro"), opening sentence unremarkable. The email is closed within three seconds.

Restructuring the email to lead with a single data point ("Card-present fraud costs US merchants $8.6B annually -- we reduce it by 40%") followed immediately by the ask ("Would you have 20 minutes this week?") places both elements squarely within the F-pattern's high-attention zone. The data point serves as visual contrast -- a number amid text -- which the selective attention system is primed to notice. The ask, arriving in the second sentence, lands before Stage 3 triage (how closely to read) can default to skimming.

**Growth-Stage Company Redesigning Internal Announcements**

A 200-person SaaS company notices that its weekly all-hands email, averaging 800 words, generates fewer than 15% click-throughs on embedded action items. An internal audit applies the four-stage triage model and discovers the problem at Stage 3: readers skim the dense paragraphs, and action items are buried in the middle of blocks of text with no visual differentiation. The company redesigns the email with a three-line summary at the top, bold headings for each section, and action items isolated in a single bulleted block at the end. Click-through rates rise to 38% within one month. The words did not change materially; their cognitive accessibility did.

**Validation-Stage Startup Crafting a Product Onboarding Sequence**

A health-tech startup discovers that 60% of new users abandon its onboarding flow at step three of five. Applying the working-memory constraint, the team realises that step three requires users to hold four distinct pieces of information simultaneously (account type, billing preference, notification settings, and data-sharing consent) -- exceeding Miller's 7 plus-or-minus 2 threshold when combined with the ambient cognitive load of navigating a new interface. Splitting step three into two screens, each requiring only two decisions, reduces abandonment by 35%. The lesson extends beyond product design: any written communication that asks the reader to hold multiple unrelated concepts simultaneously -- a board update that mixes financial results, hiring plans, and legal matters in a single paragraph -- is making the same architectural mistake.

**Scale-Stage Company Testing Subject Lines at Volume**

An e-commerce platform with 2 million email subscribers runs an A/B test on its weekly promotional newsletter. Version A uses a consistent subject line ("This Week's Deals"). Version B varies the subject line each week with specifics ("40% Off Kitchen Essentials -- Ends Thursday"). Open rates for Version B average 28% higher over a 12-week period. The F-pattern predicts this: the subject line is the first -- and often only -- element the reader's attention system evaluates during Stage 1 triage. A static subject line becomes background noise; a variable one forces the attention system to process it anew each time.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **The Completeness Fallacy.** Writing a message that contains every relevant detail, on the assumption that thoroughness equals effectiveness. The reader's attention budget does not expand to match the writer's ambition. A 1,200-word email that covers eight topics will, in practice, communicate fewer of them than a 300-word email that covers three -- because the latter will actually be read.

2. **Burying the Lead in Paragraph Three.** Opening with context, pleasantries, or chronological backstory before stating the purpose. The F-pattern concentrates attention on the first two lines. Anything placed after an unremarkable opening is competing with the reader's impulse to move on, and the impulse usually wins.

3. **Symmetric Emphasis.** Bolding, underlining, or highlighting five different items in a single message under the theory that all five are important. Research shows that formatting five sentences in a passage reduces the noticeability of any single formatted sentence compared to formatting just one. When everything is emphasised, nothing is.

4. **Assuming the Reader Will Return.** Sending a dense message with the expectation that the reader will skim now and read closely later. The data on present-bias suggests otherwise: messages deferred to "later" are rarely revisited. If the reader does not absorb the key information on first contact, the probability of a second contact approaches zero.

5. **Ignoring Medium-Specific Attention Norms.** Writing a 500-word Slack message or a three-sentence board memo. Each medium carries implicit expectations about length and density. Violating those expectations triggers an immediate cost-benefit recalculation in the reader's triage process -- usually unfavourably. Text messages are expected to be immediate and disposable. Emails are expected to be scannable within one screen. Memos are expected to carry structure. Matching the medium's norms is not a stylistic preference -- it is a prerequisite for surviving Stage 1 triage.

6. **The Multitasking-Friendly Illusion.** Writing a message that requires the reader to cross-reference an attachment, a prior email, and an external link to fully understand the request. This design assumes the reader will perform three concurrent cognitive tasks. The Stroop effect, the gorilla experiment, and the pharmacist study all suggest otherwise. If the reader must assemble information from multiple sources, they will assemble none of it.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Cold outreach to strangers (investors, advisors, potential co-founders) | The reader has no prior relationship with the sender. Stage 1 triage ("Should I engage at all?") is the primary hurdle. Subject line and first sentence carry almost the entire burden of persuasion. |
| Validation | Investor updates, early customer communications, survey requests | Readers have some familiarity with the sender but limited patience. Stage 2 ("Now or later?") is the critical gate. Messages must signal low effort and high relevance to avoid being deferred indefinitely. |
| Early Traction | Client-facing emails, onboarding copy, support documentation | The reader base diversifies. Writers must account for varying attention budgets across personas -- a technical user may read closely, while an executive sponsor skims. Designing for the skimmer protects both audiences. |
| Growth | Internal communications at scale (all-hands, policy updates, process changes) | Message volume multiplies. Employees develop aggressive triage habits for internal email. Cognitive load audits of recurring communications (weekly updates, change notices) yield outsized returns. |
| Scale | Templated communications, automated sequences, brand-level messaging | Thousands or millions of readers, each with unique attention constraints. A/B testing of structural elements (subject lines, opening sentences, visual hierarchy) becomes a quantitative discipline grounded in the same cognitive science. |

```markdown
# Cognitive Load Audit: [Message / Communication Name]

## Reader Profile
- **Audience:** [Who is the reader?]
- **Estimated attention budget:** [X seconds, based on medium and context]
- **Primary triage risk:** [Stage 1 / 2 / 3 / 4 -- where the message is most likely to fail]

## F-Pattern Analysis
- **First-line content:** [What the reader sees first -- is it the right thing?]
- **Left-margin anchors:** [Headings, paragraph openers, bullet starts]
- **Dead zones:** [Content that falls outside fixation points]

## Working Memory Load
- **Concurrent concepts required:** [Count of distinct ideas the reader must hold]
- **Exceeds 7 +/- 2 threshold?** [Yes / No]
- **Simplification options:** [How to reduce simultaneous cognitive demands]

## Triage Survival Checklist
- [ ] Purpose is clear within the first two lines
- [ ] Key ask or takeaway survives a five-second skim
- [ ] No critical information is buried below the third paragraph
- [ ] Formatting highlights one (maximum two) focal points
- [ ] Message length matches the medium's attention norms
- [ ] A reader who stops at any point has still absorbed the most important content

## Revised Message
[Restructured version of the communication, annotated with cognitive rationale]
```
