# Clarity and Concision

A 500-word email sent to 200 people that could have been 250 words wastes two hours of collective reading time. Corporate verbosity is not a style problem. It is a cost centre.

---

## Workflow

Follow this sequence when editing any document for clarity and concision:

1. **Structure review** -- Verify the document's macro structure is sound. Cutting words from a structurally flawed document produces a shorter structurally flawed document. Confirm the three main points are identifiable on a single read-through.

2. **Clarity stress test** -- Read the draft as someone with no context. Can you identify the main points and the requested action after one pass? Flag every ambiguous passage, abstract assertion, and moment of re-reading. (Use Prompt 2 below.)

3. **Word economy audit** -- Apply the three surgical techniques: delete prepositions, replace -ion nouns with verbs, replace be-verbs with stronger alternatives. Identify and eliminate all padding phrases. (Use Prompt 1 below.)

4. **Subtraction edit** -- Delete words, sentences, and entire paragraphs. Do not add, rephrase, or restructure -- only cut. Produce the shortest version that preserves every essential idea and action request. (Use Prompt 5 below.)

5. **Metrics check** -- Calculate final word count, average sentence length (target: 20 or fewer), and percentage reduction. Confirm the Flesch Reading Ease score meets the target (50+). If metrics miss targets, repeat steps 3-4.

## The Framework

### The 20-Word Rule

The optimal average for readable sentences is no more than 20 words. This does not mean every sentence should be exactly 20 words -- variety in sentence length creates rhythm and holds interest. Some sentences should be very short. Others can be longer, building layered arguments. But the average, across a document, should hover around 20.

**NOT THIS:**
> Efficiency measures that have been implemented by the company with strong involvement of senior management have generated cost savings while at the very same time assisting in the building of a culture that is centered around the value of efficiency. [40 words]

**BUT THIS:**
> Our senior management team has cut costs and made the company more efficient. We expect to be more profitable for the next four quarters. [24 words]

The first version says less with more. The second says more with less.

### Show, Don't Tell

Concrete business writing is persuasive because it is evidence-based, clear, and memorable. Abstract assertions invite scepticism; specific details earn credibility.

| NOT THIS | BUT THIS |
|----------|----------|
| He was a bad boss. | He got a promotion based on his assistant's detailed reports, but then -- despite the company's record profits -- denied that assistant even routine cost-of-living raises. |
| The company lost its focus and floundered. | The CEO acquired five unrelated subsidiaries -- as far afield as a paper company and a retailer of children's toys -- and then couldn't service the $26 million in debt. |

The vague versions are opinions. The specific versions are evidence that leads readers to draw the same conclusions -- independently, and therefore more convincingly.

### Word Economy: Three Surgical Techniques

Three techniques eliminate the majority of excess:

**Technique 1: Delete prepositions (especially "of").**
- *April of 2013* becomes *April 2013*
- *point of view* becomes *viewpoint*
- *the purpose of this evaluation* becomes *this evaluation's purpose*

**Technique 2: Replace -ion nouns with verbs.**
- *was in violation of* becomes *violated*
- *provided protection to* becomes *protected*
- *made a determination* becomes *determined*

**Technique 3: Replace be-verbs with stronger verbs.**
- *was hanging* becomes *hung*
- *is indicative of* becomes *indicates*
- *are in agreement* becomes *agree*

Applied mechanically, these three rules typically reduce word count by 20-40%.

### Before/After: The Compound Effect

| NOT THIS | Words | BUT THIS | Words |
|----------|-------|----------|-------|
| The trend in the industry is toward self-generation by some companies of their own websites, and Internet technology is changing the nature of training necessary to acquire the skill of website development at an acceptable level of sophistication, so that this activity can more and more be handled in-house. | 49 | Since Internet technology makes it easier than ever to develop sophisticated websites, some companies now develop their own in-house. | 19 |
| We are unable to fill your order at this point in time because there is an ongoing dock strike that affects our operations. | 23 | We cannot fill your order right now because of the dock strike. | 12 |
| I am writing in response to a number of issues that have arisen with regard to the recent announcement that there will be an increase in the charge for the use of our lobby computers. | 35 | You may have heard that we're raising the fees for using our lobby computers. | 14 |
| The greater number of these problems can readily be dealt with in such a way as to bring about satisfactory solutions. | 21 | Most of these problems can be readily solved. | 8 |

In the most extreme case, 49 words become 19 -- a 61% reduction with no loss of meaning. The shorter versions are not merely more efficient; they are more persuasive. Readers trust writers who respect their time.

### Eliminate Padding

Certain phrases contribute nothing but the illusion of seriousness:

- *in this connection it might be observed that* -- delete entirely
- *it is important to bear in mind that* -- delete entirely
- *it is interesting that* -- delete entirely
- *it is notable that* -- delete entirely
- *it is worthwhile to note that* -- delete entirely
- *it should be pointed out that* -- delete entirely

These phrases are throat-clearing. They signal that the writer is about to say something -- rather than simply saying it.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Word Economy Audit:**
> Here is a business document: [paste text]. Apply the three word-economy techniques: (1) delete unnecessary prepositions, (2) replace -ion nouns with verbs, (3) replace be-verbs with stronger alternatives. Also identify and eliminate all padding phrases. Show the original word count, the revised text, and the new word count. Calculate the percentage reduction.

**Prompt 2 -- Clarity Stress Test:**
> Pretend you are an intelligent reader with no prior context about my company or project. Read this document: [paste text]. After one read-through: (1) What are the three main points? (2) What action is the writer requesting? (3) Where did you get confused? (4) Which passages could be misinterpreted? (5) Where did you have to re-read a sentence? Identify every clarity failure and suggest specific revisions.

**Prompt 3 -- Show Don't Tell Conversion:**
> Here is a draft containing abstract assertions: [paste text]. Identify every sentence that "tells" rather than "shows" -- every opinion, generalisation, or claim that lacks supporting evidence. For each, either suggest a concrete, specific replacement or flag it as needing data I should gather. The goal is a document where every claim is supported by evidence.

**Prompt 4 -- Sentence Length Analysis:**
> Analyse the sentence lengths in this document: [paste text]. Calculate the average. Identify the five longest sentences and suggest how to break them up. Identify any passages where all sentences are similar in length and suggest how to vary the rhythm. Target an average of 20 words or fewer.

**Prompt 5 -- The Subtraction Edit:**
> Most editors instinctively add words when revising. Reverse that default. Take this draft: [paste text]. Your only permitted operation is deletion -- remove words, sentences, and entire paragraphs. Do not add a single word. Do not rephrase; only cut. Produce the shortest version that preserves every essential idea and action request. Show the original word count, the revised word count, and the percentage reduction. Then list, in a table, every deleted passage with a one-line justification for why it was expendable.

<details>
<summary>Use Cases</summary>

**Idea-Stage Founder Rewriting a Landing Page**

A founder's landing page opens with 46 words of impenetrable corporate fog: "We aspire to be a partner primarily concerned with providing our clients the maximal acquisition of future profits..." After applying word economy techniques, the revision drops to 30 words a customer can actually understand -- and that a search engine can parse.

**Growth-Stage Company Tightening Investor Updates**

A SaaS company's monthly investor update runs 2,500 words. Applying word economy techniques across three consecutive updates: Update 1 drops to 1,800 words (28% reduction), Update 2 to 1,400, Update 3 to 1,100 -- 56% less space, same information. Investor reply rates increase 40%.

**Early-Traction Nonprofit Doubling Newsletter Engagement**

A nonprofit's 566-word newsletter links to six curated resources with extensive context and background detail. The subtraction edit produces a 275-word version preserving all six links. The concise version generates twice as many link clicks. The lost detail represented supplementary material that writers value and readers skip.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Anti-Patterns</summary>

1. **Confusing Brevity with Concision.** Brevity means being short. Concision means being short without losing substance. Include everything necessary and nothing else.

2. **Using Complexity as a Status Signal.** Long sentences and Latinate vocabulary do not signal sophistication -- they signal that the writer is either hiding something or has not thought clearly.

3. **Cutting Too Aggressively.** Concision taken to the extreme produces telegraphic prose that sounds curt and loses the writer's voice.

4. **Editing for Concision Before Revising for Structure.** Cutting 30% of the words from a structurally flawed document produces a shorter structurally flawed document. Revise macro structure first.

5. **Telling Instead of Showing.** "The company performed poorly" is brief but not persuasive. "The CEO acquired five unrelated subsidiaries and couldn't service the $26 million in debt" is longer but incomparably more effective. Concision serves clarity; it does not replace evidence.

</details>

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Pitch compression | Every word on a pitch deck slide or one-pager must justify its existence. At the idea stage, the entrepreneur has less content and more temptation to pad. Concision here means saying less but meaning more. |
| Validation | Data-driven concision | Replace assertions with numbers. "Strong traction" becomes "47 paying customers in 3 months with 12% week-over-week growth." Specific data is inherently concise because it replaces paragraphs of qualitative hedging. |
| Early Traction | Communication volume management | The sheer volume of writing at this stage makes concision a time-management tool. Cutting 30% of words across all documents saves hours per week for both writer and readers. |
| Growth | Readability standards | Establish organisational standards: maximum average sentence length, maximum word count for recurring document types, required use of headings for documents over 500 words. Concision becomes policy. |
| Scale | Brand voice consistency | At scale, every external communication reflects the brand. A house style that mandates concision prevents the verbal inflation that accompanies corporate growth and bureaucratic layering. |
