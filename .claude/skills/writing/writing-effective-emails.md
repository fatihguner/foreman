# Writing Effective Emails

The average manager receives 121 emails per day. At a generous reading rate of thirty seconds per email, that is an hour simply triaging -- deciding which messages deserve attention and which deserve the archive. The arithmetic is ruthless: each email competes with 120 others for a finite allocation of cognitive bandwidth. Subject line, first sentence, length, formatting, and tone collectively determine whether a message lands in the "read and act" category or the "skim and forget" graveyard. Most business emails -- painstakingly drafted, carefully considered -- end up in the graveyard. Not because they lacked substance, but because they failed the thirty-second audition.

---

## The Framework

### The Six Principles of Effective Email

**1. Get to the Point in the First Two Sentences**

State your purpose immediately. Politely, but immediately. Do not build up to the request through three paragraphs of context that the reader will skip anyway.

- *Before:* "It's been ages, I know, but I've been meaning to tell you just how effective I think you've been as the editor of the Daily Metropolitan these past seven years. Although I cancelled my subscription a few years back (LOL) -- the papers kept cluttering the driveway -- I buy a copy at the coffee shop almost every day. Anyhoo, I have a mentee I'd like you to meet..."
- *After:* "May I ask a favour of you? Glenda Jones, a really sharp mentee in the township's Young Leaders programme, wants to pursue a career in journalism. Would you spend 15 minutes chatting with her at your office sometime this month?"

The second version is longer in total but arrives at its point faster. Everything it includes serves the request.

**2. Copy People Judiciously**

Include only those who will immediately understand why they are on the thread. Reply All is the nuclear option of email etiquette -- use it only when every recipient genuinely needs to see your response. The instinct to "keep people in the loop" produces loops that strangle productivity.

**3. Keep the Message to One Screen**

People find long emails irksome. The more they scroll, the less receptive they become. Many recipients close long emails immediately, planning to return to them later -- a plan that rarely materialises. Compose more than a single screen of reading only when the content genuinely demands it, and in those cases, use subheadings and bullet points to enable scanning.

**4. Write a Short but Informative Subject Line**

The subject line is the email's headline. A generic or blank subject line guarantees burial.

| Weak Subject Line | Strong Subject Line |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| "Program" | "The Nov. 15 Leadership Programme" |
| "Quick question" | "Budget Approval Needed by Friday" |
| "Update" | "Q3 Revenue Update: +14% vs. Plan" |
| "Hello there!" | "Request for an Interview" |

If the email requires action, signal it in the subject line. "Action Required:" or "Decision Needed:" are not rude -- they are respectful of the reader's time.

**5. Maintain Standard Capitalisation and Punctuation**

Rushed emails that violate basic writing conventions -- no capitals, no punctuation, cryptic abbreviations -- communicate carelessness. Standing out as someone who writes properly is a low-effort competitive advantage. Clarity written once costs less time than the follow-up email explaining what the first one meant.

**6. Use a Professional Signature**

Include your title and contact information. Keep it clean -- not too long, not ornate. The signature makes it convenient for recipients to choose how to reach you.

### The Tone Spectrum for Email

Email tone must match the relationship and the content. The framework identifies three registers:

**Collegial and Direct** (for peers, regular collaborators):
> "Can you review the deck by Thursday? I need your input on slides 6-8 before the client meeting."

**Professional and Warm** (for clients, senior stakeholders):
> "Thank you for the thoughtful feedback on the proposal. I've incorporated your suggestions into the revised version -- attached. Would Tuesday work for a brief call to walk through the changes?"

**Firm but Respectful** (for difficult messages):
> "Several people have complained about the email you sent yesterday. Our company's policy does not make an exception for offensive language, even when used in jest. Please think about how future emails will affect your coworkers. If I receive complaints again, HR will have to get involved. But I trust that won't be necessary."

Note the progression: the firm version states facts, cites policy, explains consequences, and closes with a statement of trust. It never attacks the person's character, never uses sarcasm, and never makes threats it cannot back up.

### When Not to Use Email

Email is the wrong medium when:

- **The topic is emotionally charged.** A phone call or face-to-face conversation allows for real-time calibration of tone.
- **The issue requires rapid back-and-forth.** Five rounds of email take five hours. One conversation takes ten minutes.
- **The content is confidential.** Email is a permanent record that can be forwarded, subpoenaed, or leaked.
- **You are angry.** Write the email if you must -- then delete it. Wait 24 hours. Write a new one.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Email Rewrite:**
> Rewrite this email to be shorter, more direct, and more likely to receive a prompt response. Keep the same informational content but restructure it so the purpose is clear in the first two sentences, the body is scannable, and the call to action is unmistakable. Original email: [paste text]

**Prompt 2 -- Subject Line Generator:**
> I need to send an email about [describe topic and purpose]. The recipient is [describe]. Generate five subject line options, ranging from informational to action-oriented. For each, explain why it would work and when it would be most appropriate.

**Prompt 3 -- Difficult Email Drafter:**
> I need to send an email to [describe person and relationship] about [describe sensitive topic]. I want to be direct without being aggressive, and I need to [describe desired outcome]. Draft the email using a firm-but-respectful tone. Then explain the tonal choices -- what you included, what you avoided, and why.

**Prompt 4 -- Email Audit:**
> Analyse my last five sent emails [paste them] against the six principles of effective email: directness, judicious copying, brevity, informative subject lines, standard conventions, and professional signature. Score each email on a 1-5 scale for each principle and provide specific recommendations for improvement.

## Use Cases

**Idea-Stage Founder Sending Cold Outreach**
A founder sends 50 cold emails to potential advisors with the subject line "Hello!" and an opening paragraph of self-introduction that runs eight sentences before mentioning what she wants. Response rate: 2%. After restructuring with a specific subject line ("Request: 15-min Call on EdTech Distribution Strategy"), a two-sentence opening that states the ask, and a total length of six sentences, response rate climbs to 18%. The content of the request did not change. The packaging did.

**Early-Traction Startup Managing a Client Escalation**
A client emails the CEO directly, furious about a missed deliverable. The CEO's instinct is to respond immediately, defensively, and at length. Instead, she waits two hours, drafts a response using the firm-but-respectful framework: acknowledges the problem (first sentence), takes ownership (second sentence), provides a concrete remediation plan (third paragraph), and closes with a specific follow-up commitment. The client de-escalates within one reply. The email thread, later reviewed by the board, demonstrates maturity under pressure.

**Growth-Stage Company Reducing Email Overload**
A 150-person company discovers that the average employee sends or receives 94 internal emails per day. A review of a sample reveals that 40% of internal emails could be replaced by Slack messages, 15% are unnecessary Reply Alls, and 20% are emails that should have been meetings. The company institutes an email protocol based on the six principles, including a "no Reply All without justification" policy. Internal email volume drops 35% in the first quarter.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **The Wall of Text.** An email that runs twelve paragraphs with no subheadings, no bullet points, and no visual breaks. The reader's eye has nothing to anchor on, so it skims, misses the key request buried in paragraph nine, and the sender wonders why nothing happened.

2. **The Passive-Aggressive CC.** Copying someone's manager on an email to apply pressure. This tactic is transparent, breeds resentment, and signals that the sender cannot resolve issues directly. If escalation is genuinely necessary, do it explicitly and openly.

3. **The Emotional Send.** Writing and sending an email while angry. Email is a permanent, forwardable record. The catharsis of an angry send is always outweighed by the regret of re-reading it the next morning. Write it, delete it, wait, then write a new one.

4. **The Subject-Line Hijack.** Replying to an old email thread about a completely different topic because it is easier than starting a new one. The original subject line becomes a lie, the new content is unfindable in future searches, and every recipient on the old thread receives irrelevant noise.

5. **The Disclaimer Novel.** Appending a 200-word legal disclaimer to every email, including internal messages. If legal counsel requires a disclaimer, keep it minimal. A disclaimer longer than the email itself is a parody of professionalism.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Cold outreach to investors and advisors | Every email is an audition. Subject lines and opening sentences determine whether the founder gets a meeting or gets ignored. Volume is high; conversion rate depends entirely on craft. |
| Validation | Investor updates and early customer communication | Regular investor updates establish cadence and trust. Clarity and brevity signal competence. Customers evaluate the company partly through the quality of its email communication. |
| Early Traction | Client management and internal coordination | Email becomes a primary coordination tool. The volume increases, making brevity and clear subject lines essential for operational efficiency. |
| Growth | Email culture and protocols | Bad email habits, multiplied across a growing team, create organisational drag. Establishing email norms -- when to email vs. message, CC etiquette, response-time expectations -- prevents communication debt. |
| Scale | Automated and templated communications | At scale, many emails are templated or automated. Tone, clarity, and personalisation in templates determine whether they feel human or robotic. Each template represents thousands of reader impressions. |

## Output Template

```markdown
# Email Draft: [Purpose]

## Pre-Send Checklist
- [ ] Subject line is specific and action-oriented
- [ ] Purpose is clear in the first two sentences
- [ ] Total length fits within one screen
- [ ] Only necessary recipients are included (To/CC)
- [ ] Tone matches the relationship and content
- [ ] Call to action is explicit and includes a deadline
- [ ] Standard capitalisation and punctuation used throughout
- [ ] Signature includes title and contact information
- [ ] Read aloud for tone -- would I say this in person?
- [ ] If emotionally charged: waited at least 2 hours before sending

## Draft Structure
**Subject:** [specific, informative subject line]

**Opening (1-2 sentences):** [purpose and context]

**Body (3-5 sentences max):** [supporting details, formatted for scanning]

**Close (1-2 sentences):** [specific call to action with deadline]

**Signature:** [name, title, contact]
```
