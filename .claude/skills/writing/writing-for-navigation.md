# Writing for Navigation

MAP. Stop thinking of your writing as a path readers walk along -- think of it as a map they zoom into. The question is: can they find what matters in three seconds? When someone opens Google Maps, the default view is a bird's-eye orientation -- country, state, city -- before any street-level detail appears. Readers approach documents the same way: they scan the landscape, identify regions of interest, and zoom in selectively. If the landscape offers no landmarks, they leave. The US Army codified this principle decades ago as BLUF -- Bottom Line Up Front -- an official policy requiring writers to place key information at the beginning of every communication. Military efficiency aside, BLUF reflects a cognitive reality that applies to every email, memo, and Slack message an entrepreneur will ever send: readers who cannot orient themselves within seconds will not orient themselves at all.

---

## The Framework

### Six Rules of Navigable Writing

**Rule 1: Make Key Information Immediately Visible**

The first question a reader asks is, "What is this about?" The easier the writer makes it to answer that question, the more likely the reader is to stay. In journalism, failing to answer it early is called "burying the lede." Literary magazines bury ledes on purpose, building mood before revealing the central conflict. Practical communications are not literary magazines.

A useful rule of thumb: place the most important information where busy readers expect to find it. BLUF positions the bottom line at the top. Abstracts, executive summaries, and TL;DR headers serve the same function. The specific convention varies by culture -- anecdotal evidence suggests that in some post-Soviet republics, the most important information traditionally appears in the final paragraph, and readers skim bottom-up accordingly. The principle is not "always put it first" but rather "always put it where your specific readers will look first."

There is a tension worth acknowledging. In warm or relationship-driven contexts, a message that opens with a blunt request can feel cold or presumptuous. Consider two versions of the same sales email:

- *Courteous order:* "I've thought more about our conversation. Your organisation is not alone in facing the challenges we discussed. I think the services my firm offers could be of use to you. Can we set up a time to discuss you hiring my firm?"
- *BLUF order:* "Can we set up a time to discuss you hiring my firm? I've thought more about our conversation. Your organisation is not alone in facing the challenges we discussed."

The BLUF version ensures the ask is seen. The courteous version risks the ask being missed if the reader stops early. The practical compromise: one sentence of courteous framing, then the ask. Not three paragraphs of preamble followed by a request the reader never reaches.

**Rule 2: Separate Distinct Ideas**

Placing visual space between unrelated topics allows readers to skim without conflating separate issues. The simplest technique is giving each distinct topic its own paragraph. A Danish study of 888 adults tested this: participants read a description of unemployment benefit requirements, half in a single dense paragraph and half with each requirement on its own line. Comprehension was equivalent, but the separated version was read 15% faster. The readers did not need to spend cognitive effort deciding where one idea ended and the next began.

Bullet points are the strongest visual signal of separation. Numbered lists impose additional sequential logic. Blank space works when neither is appropriate.

**Rule 3: Place Related Ideas Together**

Grouping related items reduces search cost. When a project update assigns tasks to two people -- say, Jessie and Marion -- interleaving their items forces both readers to scan the entire list. Clustering Jessie's tasks together and Marion's tasks together allows each person to find their responsibilities immediately. The clustering also enables consolidation: if both of Jessie's tasks involve writing and both of Marion's involve research, the task descriptions can be simplified, reducing total word count from 46 to 39 in the original example tested in the research.

For messages with multiple requests, the grouping strategy depends on whether the requests relate to the same topic. If they do, cluster them. If each request belongs to a different section, embed it in its section but foreshadow all requests at the top: "Below I cover three items and ask for your input on each: (1) ... (2) ... (3) ..."

**Rule 4: Order Ideas by Priority**

The first item in any list receives disproportionate attention. Research on Texas primary elections showed that moving a candidate from last to first on a ballot increased their vote share by up to 10 percentage points in some races. The multibillion-dollar search advertising industry operates on the same principle: the top position in search results captures the most clicks. Amazon exploits this when promoting specific products over others. A study of link positioning on web pages confirmed the pattern quantitatively: the first link received the most clicks, with a steady decline through each subsequent position, and only a minor uptick at the very last position compared to second-to-last.

When no inherent logic dictates order (chronological, procedural, or causal), default to priority. Place the most important item first. There is modest evidence that the final position in a list also receives slightly elevated attention -- studies of jury trials found that the last evidence presented can be heavily weighted -- but jurors are obligated to sit through the entire trial. Busy readers are under no such obligation. The first position dominates, and the writer who buries a critical item in position four of six is making a bet against the reader's patience.

**Rule 5: Include Headings**

Headings function as section labels on a map. Eye-tracking data shows that readers scanning a page fixate on headings before anything else, using them to decide which sections to zoom into and which to bypass entirely.

A study with the Journalist's Resource tested this directly. A nine-paragraph email newsletter covering three topics was sent to 46,648 subscribers. Half received the newsletter without headings; the other half received the same content with descriptive headings above the second and third topics. Subscribers who received headings were more than twice as likely to click links in those sections. Crucially, engagement with the first section (which had no heading in either version) was unaffected -- the headings did not divert attention, they unlocked it for content that would otherwise have been skipped.

Headings also prove useful when prefacing bulleted lists. A bullet point does not automatically make its content clear. Messages with full paragraphs attached to each bullet, or lists with so many items that their unifying logic is obscure, benefit from a short heading at the start of each entry:

- **Revised contract:** The firm's lawyers have agreed to all proposed edits but have requested two additional clauses. Can you review these additions?
- **Conference planning:** We are three months from the conference date. Could we set up a call to finalise the participant list and programme?

Each heading lets the reader decide in a fraction of a second whether to read the detail that follows. Without it, the reader must parse the entire bullet to determine relevance.

Headings should describe what follows with enough specificity to be useful but not so much length that they become a reading burden of their own. "What is the school doing?" is better than "Next steps." "School is monitoring all close contacts, and closing and disinfecting the building" is unnecessarily verbose.

**Rule 6: Use Enough Formatting but No More**

Formatting -- bold, italic, underline, highlighting, ALL CAPS -- serves two purposes: it conveys meaning beyond the words themselves, and it captures attention through visual contrast. Both purposes degrade with overuse.

In a study of over 1,600 participants reading a five-paragraph passage, a single highlighted sentence was noticed and acted upon by 89% of readers (versus 65% with no formatting). But when five sentences were highlighted, compliance on the key sentence dropped to 65% -- formatting multiple items diluted the benefit by spreading attention across all of them. A separate study found that highlighting an irrelevant sentence actually decreased reading of the important unhighlighted sentence, from 48% to 39%. Readers inferred that the writer had already marked the important parts and paid less attention to everything else.

The Goldilocks principle: format the one thing that matters most. If two or three things genuinely matter equally, format them -- but imagine a reader who reads only the formatted text. Will they have grasped the essentials? If not, reconsider.

Italics and font colour present a different problem: ambiguity. Some readers interpret them as markers of importance; others interpret them as markers of emphasis within a sentence -- a meaningful distinction. "I am very excited" uses italics for emphasis on degree, not for importance. Without explicit guidance ("Key terms are italicised" or "Action items are in blue"), readers must guess. In professional settings where norms have been codified -- legal writing uses italics for case citations, for instance -- the ambiguity resolves. Elsewhere, it persists.

ALL CAPS deserves special caution. While 75% of survey respondents interpret capitalised text as signalling importance, 25% interpret it as anger. More than three-quarters of the terms-and-conditions contracts for Amazon, Uber, and Facebook contain at least one all-caps paragraph, ostensibly to satisfy "clear and conspicuous" disclosure requirements. Courts have recently questioned whether all-caps text actually achieves this, and research shows capitalised passages take longer to read and can decrease understanding, particularly among older adults.

Bullets carry their own risk. Readers interpret bulleted items as the most important content in a message. If low-priority items are bulleted while a high-priority request sits in an unformatted paragraph, the reader may attend to the bullets and skip the paragraph. One board chairperson discovered this after bulleting a thirteen-item list of client welcome gifts while embedding the actual request -- scheduling the next board meeting -- in an unformatted paragraph at the bottom. The meeting proved difficult to schedule.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- BLUF Conversion:**
> Rewrite the following message using the BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) principle. Place the single most important piece of information or request in the first sentence. Follow it with essential supporting context only. Remove or relocate everything else. Original message: [paste text]

**Prompt 2 -- Navigation Audit:**
> Analyse this document as a map. For each section, identify: (a) whether it has a clear heading, (b) whether distinct ideas are visually separated, (c) whether related ideas are grouped, (d) whether items are ordered by priority, and (e) whether formatting highlights the right elements. Score each rule from 1-5 and provide a redesigned version. Document: [paste text]

**Prompt 3 -- Formatting Discipline Check:**
> Review this message for formatting overuse or misuse. Count the number of bold, italic, underlined, highlighted, and capitalised elements. For each, assess whether it marks genuinely critical content or dilutes the reader's attention. Then produce a version that formats no more than two elements and achieves greater clarity. Message: [paste text]

**Prompt 4 -- Cultural BLUF Adaptation:**
> I am writing to [describe audience in a specific cultural or organisational context]. Where would this audience expect to find the key information -- top, bottom, or elsewhere? Restructure the following message to match their expectations while preserving all essential content. Message: [paste text]

## Use Cases

**Idea-Stage Founder Structuring a One-Page Investor Brief**

A first-time founder drafts a one-page summary for angel investors. The draft opens with two paragraphs of market context, moves to the team's background, and places the funding ask ($500K pre-seed round) in the final paragraph. An investor who spends eight seconds on the page -- the median for cold inbound -- never reaches the ask.

Applying the BLUF principle, the founder restructures: the opening line states the ask and the core value proposition ("Raising $500K to eliminate the 72-hour settlement delay in cross-border B2B payments"). Market context moves to a supporting paragraph with a descriptive heading ("The Problem: $47B in annual settlement float"). Team credentials become a two-line sidebar under "Team." The funding terms land under "The Ask" -- bolded, as the single formatted element in the document. The same information, radically different navigability. The first version assumed the investor would walk the path. The second version gave them a map.

**Early-Traction Startup Redesigning a Covid-Era School Notification**

A school district sends parents a letter about a confirmed Covid case. The original version opens with three paragraphs of administrative language before revealing the critical fact: the school is closed for 48 hours. A busy parent -- cooking dinner, managing homework -- skims the first lines, sees no clear signal, and defers reading to "later." The redesigned version applies four navigation rules at once: the key fact ("[SCHOOL NAME] will be closed for at least the next 48 hours") appears as the first line after the salutation (Rule 1 and Rule 4), subsequent sections receive descriptive headings -- "What is [School Name] doing?", "How will you know if your child is a close contact?", "What to do if you or your child is sick?" (Rule 5) -- and each topic is visually separated into its own block (Rule 2).

**Growth-Stage Company Reducing Failure-to-Appear Rates**

The New York Police Department worked with researchers to redesign its court summons notification. The original form, titled "Complaint/Information," placed the court appearance date and time at the bottom of a dense form, below personal details and charge descriptions. The redesigned version applied all six navigation rules: the title changed to "Criminal Court Appearance Ticket" (Rule 1), court date and location moved to the top (Rule 4), distinct sections were separated (Rule 2), related items -- court address, date, time -- were grouped (Rule 3), clear headings flagged each section (Rule 5), and courthouse selection was converted from handwriting to a bubble-fill format (Rule 6). Over 17 months and nearly 324,000 recipients, failure-to-appear rates fell 13%, resulting in an estimated 23,000 fewer arrest warrants.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **The Chronological Trap.** Telling the story of how a decision unfolded rather than stating the decision and its implications first. Chronological order is the writer's natural instinct; it is almost never the reader's preference. The reader wants the conclusion, not the journey.

2. **The Bullet Dump.** Listing twelve or more items as bullet points without headings, grouping, or priority ordering. A long, undifferentiated bulleted list is no easier to navigate than a dense paragraph -- it merely looks different. Each bullet should earn its place, and the list should have a clear heading that tells the reader what connects the items.

3. **The Formatting Carnival.** Combining bold, italic, underline, highlighting, colour changes, and ALL CAPS in a single message. A taxi company email documented by researchers used every available formatting tool simultaneously. When shown to readers, no two agreed on which piece of information the writer considered most important. The effect of formatting many items was worse than formatting none.

4. **Headings as Decoration.** Using vague headings ("Overview," "Details," "Next Steps") that describe structural positions rather than content. A heading should tell the reader what the section is about, not merely that it exists. "Q3 Revenue: +14% vs. Plan" is a heading. "Update" is a placeholder.

5. **BLUF Without Warmth.** Applying Bottom Line Up Front so aggressively that the message reads as curt or demanding, especially in contexts where the reader expects a relational overture. The solution is not to abandon BLUF but to insert exactly one sentence of warmth before the ask -- not three paragraphs of it.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Pitch emails, one-pagers, advisor requests | Every document is competing with dozens of others for a distracted reader's attention. Navigation is the difference between "funded" and "filed." BLUF and ruthless brevity dominate. |
| Validation | Investor updates, customer feedback surveys, partnership proposals | Documents grow slightly longer but readers remain impatient. Headings and priority ordering become essential tools for keeping multi-topic updates scannable. |
| Early Traction | Client onboarding materials, internal process docs, support articles | The writer now serves multiple reader personas with different expertise levels. Headings allow each reader to jump directly to their section without wading through irrelevant content. |
| Growth | All-hands emails, policy memos, cross-functional announcements | Document volume and audience size both increase. Navigation standards must be codified into team style guides. The navigation audit becomes a recurring exercise, not a one-time fix. |
| Scale | Templated communications, automated notifications, regulatory filings | Navigation decisions are embedded in templates that serve thousands of readers. Small structural improvements -- moving a key date from paragraph four to the subject line -- compound across enormous volumes. |
