# Revising and Editing

Watch a mediocre memo become an excellent one. The first draft reads: "In order to facilitate the customers' placement of orders, a new order-processing procedure has been designed. The process will require a customer to enter the product and/or service code into our order-entry system, which will then generate a quote for the job and return it to the customer for approval." By the third draft, it reads: "Our current work-order processing takes a lot of paperwork and phone calls, so it's hard for our customers to make changes to the work before it begins. The procedure is inefficient and subject to error. And it takes up to four weeks from quote to approval to work order. We have therefore designed a new four-step procedure that has two key benefits: (1) Customers can place orders through our website, and (2) we can start jobs faster."

The difference is not cosmetic. The first draft tells the reader *what* is happening. The third draft tells them *why it matters*. Between those two versions lies the entire discipline of revision and editing -- two related but fundamentally different operations that most writers conflate, to their detriment.

---

## The Framework

### Revising vs. Editing: The Critical Distinction

Revising is rethinking the floor plan. Editing is choosing the paint colours. Confuse the two, and a writer will spend an hour perfecting the grammar of a paragraph that should have been deleted entirely.

**Revising** addresses the macro level: the argument, the structure, the completeness, the truthfulness, the diplomacy. It asks whether the right things have been said.

**Editing** addresses the micro level: the sentences, the word choices, the phrasing, the rhythm. It asks whether the right things have been said *well*.

Both require dedicated time. Neurotic perfectionism that delays important documents is as damaging as rash distribution of unvetted drafts. The solution is a structured process that allocates appropriate time to each operation.

### The Revision Checklist

During revision, the writer steps back from the prose and interrogates the document as a whole:

1. **Have I been utterly truthful?** Does the document accurately represent the facts, or have I unconsciously shaded them to support a preferred conclusion?
2. **Have I said all that I need to say?** Are there gaps that leave the reader unable to act? Missing context? Unanswered questions that the reader will inevitably ask?
3. **Have I been appropriately diplomatic and fair?** Will the tone damage a relationship that should be preserved? Have I been fair to opposing viewpoints?
4. **Do I have three parts -- an opener, a middle, and a closer?** Is the structure apparent to the reader?
5. **In my opener, have I made my points quickly, clearly, and concretely?** Or have I buried the lead under a slow wind-up?
6. **Have I avoided a slow wind-up that unnecessarily postpones the message?** The first paragraph should deliver the core point, not prepare the reader for a core point that arrives later.
7. **In the middle, have I proved my points with specifics?** Evidence, data, examples -- not assertions.
8. **Is the structure immediately apparent?** Have I used informative headings?
9. **Is my closer consistent with the rest -- yet expressed freshly?** Have I avoided lame repetition?

### The Editing Checklist

Once the structure is sound, the editor's eye narrows to the sentence and word level:

1. **Can I save some words here?** Every word must earn its place.
2. **Is there a better way of phrasing this idea?** Could a stronger verb replace a weak verb-plus-adverb combination?
3. **Is my meaning unmistakable?** Could any sentence be misinterpreted by a reader with a different context?
4. **Can I make it more interesting?** Varied sentence length? A concrete example instead of an abstraction?
5. **Is the expression relaxed but refined?** Natural language, not corporate stiffness -- but not sloppy either.
6. **Does one sentence glide into the next, without discontinuities?** Are the transitions smooth?

### Three Drafts in Practice

The evolution from first to third draft follows a recognisable pattern:

**First Draft (Carpenter output):** The ideas are present but poorly ordered. Important context is missing. The opening buries the point. Passive constructions dominate. The reader is told what is happening but not why.

**Second Draft (Post-Revision):** The structure has been rethought. The opening now states the problem and the solution. Missing information -- the who, what, when, why -- has been filled in. The document can now stand on its own. But the prose is still wordy, and some phrasing is stiff.

**Third Draft (Post-Editing):** Sentences have been tightened. Passive voice has been replaced with active voice where appropriate. Wordiness has been cut. The steps of the process have been reformatted as bullet points for scannability. The document reads as though it was easy to write -- which is precisely the illusion that careful editing creates.

### The Peer Feedback Process

The writer's worst handicap is familiarity. Having lived with a document through multiple drafts, the writer can no longer see it with fresh eyes. Peer feedback solves this problem -- but only if structured properly.

**Effective peer feedback requires:**

- **Specific questions, not open invitations.** "What do you think?" produces vague responses. "Can you identify my three main points after one read-through?" produces actionable data.
- **Fresh eyes.** The reviewer should not have been involved in the drafting. Their confusion is diagnostic: wherever they stumble, other readers will stumble too.
- **Separation of content and expression.** Ask one reviewer to assess whether the argument is complete and logical. Ask another to assess whether the prose is clear and concise. Combining both tasks in one review dilutes attention.
- **Ego management.** Feedback is not personal. A suggestion to restructure paragraph three is not an attack on the writer's intelligence. Writers who cannot receive criticism without defensiveness should consider whether they are in the right profession.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Revision Audit:**
> Here is a draft of my [document type]: [paste draft]. Apply the full revision checklist: (1) Is it truthful and accurate? (2) Is anything missing that the reader needs? (3) Is the tone appropriately diplomatic? (4) Does the structure have a clear opener, middle, and closer? (5) Is the main point visible in the first paragraph? (6) Are points supported with specifics? (7) Is the structure apparent with informative headings? Provide a paragraph-by-paragraph revision assessment with specific recommendations.

**Prompt 2 -- Editing Pass:**
> Here is a revised draft of my [document type]: [paste draft]. Now edit at the sentence level: (1) Cut unnecessary words -- show me a word-count comparison for each paragraph, (2) Replace weak verbs and passive constructions, (3) Ensure each sentence's meaning is unmistakable, (4) Smooth transitions between paragraphs, (5) Vary sentence length for rhythm. Show the edited version with tracked changes explained.

**Prompt 3 -- Three-Draft Transformation:**
> Here is a rough first draft: [paste draft]. Transform it through three clear stages. Show me Draft 2 (revised for structure, completeness, and argument) with an explanation of what changed and why. Then show me Draft 3 (edited for prose quality, concision, and polish) with an explanation of each edit. I want to see the evolution, not just the final product.

**Prompt 4 -- Peer Review Simulation:**
> Pretend you are a colleague reading my [document type] for the first time: [paste draft]. After one read-through, tell me: (1) What are the three main points? (2) What action am I supposed to take? (3) Where did you get confused or lose interest? (4) What questions remain unanswered? (5) What would you cut? Be direct -- I need a reader's honest reaction, not reassurance.

## Use Cases

**Validation-Stage Startup Polishing a Pitch Deck Narrative**

A founder has drafted a 12-slide pitch deck with extensive speaker notes. The Madman and Carpenter phases are complete, but the narrative feels scattered. Applying the revision checklist: the opener (slide 1) describes the product instead of the problem -- revision moves the problem statement forward. Slides 4-6 present features without connecting them to the market need identified in slide 2 -- revision adds the "so what" connector. The closer asks for investment without specifying terms or use of funds -- revision adds a concrete ask. After structural revision, the editing pass tightens every speaker note from an average of 80 words to 40 words, replaces jargon with plain language, and ensures each slide's headline could stand alone as a summary.

**Scale-Stage Company Establishing an Editorial Review Process**

A 500-person company discovers that external communications vary wildly in quality. Customer emails from support read differently from product announcements, which read differently from investor updates. The company implements a structured revision and editing process: all customer-facing documents above 500 words go through a two-stage review. Stage 1 (revision): a designated editor checks structure, completeness, and tone alignment with brand guidelines. Stage 2 (editing): a copy editor checks prose quality, grammar, and consistency. The peer feedback process uses specific checklists rather than open-ended review requests. Writing quality becomes consistent across the organisation within one quarter.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Editing Before Revising.** Polishing sentences in a paragraph that should be deleted or moved is wasted effort. Always assess the macro structure before descending to the micro level.

2. **Declaring the First Draft Final.** The most costly anti-pattern in business writing. A first draft, by definition, captures what the writer wanted to say -- not necessarily what the reader needs to hear. Skipping revision means skipping the transition from writer-centred to reader-centred prose.

3. **Revising in Perpetuity.** Some writers revise indefinitely, unable to declare a document "done." The antidote: define the number of revision rounds in advance. Two rounds of revision and two rounds of editing is sufficient for most business documents.

4. **Soliciting Feedback from the Wrong People.** Asking a subject-matter expert to assess prose quality, or asking a copy editor to assess strategic content, produces useless feedback. Match the reviewer to the review task.

5. **Taking Peer Feedback Personally.** A colleague who identifies a structural flaw in a draft is saving the writer from presenting that flaw to a less forgiving audience. The revision process requires emotional detachment from the prose.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Self-review discipline | Solo founders have no peer reviewers. The revision checklist serves as a surrogate editor, enforcing structural rigour even when the writer is the only reader before publication. |
| Validation | Evidence verification | Revision at this stage must prioritise truthfulness and completeness. Investors will scrutinise claims. Every data point must survive the "have I been utterly truthful?" test. |
| Early Traction | Speed-quality balance | The volume of writing increases sharply. The temptation is to skip revision entirely. A lightweight checklist -- even a 5-minute structural scan before sending -- prevents the worst errors without creating bottlenecks. |
| Growth | Peer review infrastructure | Dedicated editors or communications staff can own the editing function. Subject-matter experts draft; editors revise and polish. The separation of roles mirrors the separation of revision and editing. |
| Scale | Process and standards | Revision checklists become institutional standards. Style guides codify editing decisions. The three-draft process becomes organisational policy for high-stakes communications. |
