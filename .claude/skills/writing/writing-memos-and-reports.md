# Writing Memos and Reports

Every memo is a decision document. The question is not whether it will influence a decision, but which one -- and whether the writer has been deliberate about the answer. A memo ostensibly about switching paper suppliers is actually a document about cost discipline, vendor relationships, and operational reliability. A report on declining chocolate sales is actually a recommendation to reposition an entire product line. The writers who understand this produce documents that get read, circulated, and acted upon. The writers who do not produce documents that get filed.

The distinction between the two lies not in the quality of the analysis but in the quality of the structure. A brilliant insight buried in paragraph seven of an unsummarised report is, for all practical purposes, an insight that does not exist.

## The Framework

### The Issue-Solution-Reason Summary

Every memo and report should open with a summary that answers three questions, in this order:

1. **Issue:** What is the problem or question, stated so that anyone can understand it?
2. **Proposed Solution:** What do you recommend?
3. **Reason:** Why is this solution preferable to alternatives?

Consider two approaches to the same recommendation:

**Weak Summary:**
> "The cell phone changeover that has been proposed should be rejected. For the reasons stated below, the company would not be well served by accepting the proposal."

**Strong Summary:**
> "Last year, we adopted an officewide policy of issuing cell phones to all executives and sales reps at an annual cost of $58,000. The Persephone company has proposed that we switch to its phones and service at $37,000. The committee recommends rejecting this proposal for four reasons: (1) significantly less coverage in Europe and Asia, (2) our current provider's superior responsiveness, (3) potential costs from dropped calls that dwarf the $21,000 savings, and (4) Persephone's inferior customer service ratings."

The second version can be fully understood by anyone who reads it, at any time. The first assumes familiarity with context that readers may not have.

### The Who-Why-What-When-How Planning Chart

Before writing, answer five questions:

| Question | Key Point |
|----------|-----------|
| **Who** are you writing for? | Consider your audience's concerns, motivations, and background. |
| **Why** are you writing? | Keep your purpose firmly in mind. Every sentence should advance it. |
| **What** needs saying? | Include only the main points and details that get your message across. |
| **When** are you expecting action? | State your time frame explicitly. |
| **How** will your communication benefit readers? | Make it clear how you are meeting their needs. |

This chart is not a writing exercise -- it is a thinking exercise. Most poorly structured documents fail not at the writing stage but at the planning stage. The writer who cannot answer these five questions before drafting will not produce a document that answers them after.

### Writing for Three Audiences

Every memo and report serves three simultaneous readerships:

**Primary audience:** Executives who want conclusions, recommendations, and key data. They will read the summary and skim the rest. Write the summary for them.

**Secondary audience:** Reviewers who may be called in to assess the document's soundness. They will examine your methodology, challenge your assumptions, and check your facts. Write the body for them.

**Future audience:** Readers two years from now who will need to extract information from a document they did not commission and may lack context for. Write with enough specificity and context that the document stands on its own.

### Title Selection

The title or subject line is the document's first promise to the reader. Vague titles break that promise before the reader begins.

| Weak Title | Strong Title |
|-----------|-------------|
| Subject: Siegelson | Subject: Approval of Siegelson Acquisition |
| Subject: Settlement | Subject: Why We Should Reject Frost's Settlement Offer |
| Subject: Print Run | Subject: Ginsburg Autobiography Print Run |

Strong titles tell readers both the topic and what they should do about it.

### Using Graphics and Visual Aids

When writing about complex information, a well-placed chart or diagram conveys at a glance what paragraphs of prose struggle to communicate. Three principles govern effective visuals:

- The graphic must illustrate something discussed in the text -- never use decoration for its own sake.
- Place it near the text it supports, preferably on the same page.
- Use legends and labels that readers can grasp without consulting the text.

Make graphics distinctive and use them sparingly. If every third page contains a chart, the visual emphasis is nullified.

### Business Letter Conventions

Business letters, despite their apparent obsolescence, remain necessary for formal situations: correcting vendor errors, recommending job candidates, announcing services, delivering bad news with appropriate gravity.

**Focus on the reader.** Open with *you*, not *I*. "You might be interested..." rather than "I just thought I'd drop you a note..."

**Say something substantive.** Replace filler ("I trust this finds you prospering in your personal life...") with genuine content ("I hope your family dodged the fires last week -- which sounded devastating").

**Avoid hedging.** Replace "It is with regret that we acknowledge that we do not appear at this time to be in a position to extend an offer of employment" with "We're sorry to say that we aren't now hiring."

**Ease into bad news.** Open on a genuine positive note, then explain the negative and its reasoning. Readers absorb bad news better when they do not encounter it in the first sentence.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Memo Structure Builder:**
> I need to write a memo about [describe topic] for [describe audience]. The recommendation is [describe]. Help me structure it using the Issue-Solution-Reason framework. Draft the summary section, then outline the body sections with recommended content for each. Include three supporting arguments and identify the strongest counterargument I need to address.

**Prompt 2 -- Report Outline Generator:**
> I am preparing a report on [describe topic] for [describe audience]. The report needs to address [describe key questions]. Generate a detailed outline that includes: a summary section written for executives, a body structured for reviewers, and enough context for future readers who lack inside knowledge. Recommend where visual aids would be most effective.

**Prompt 3 -- Business Letter Drafter:**
> Draft a business letter for the following situation: [describe context -- e.g., declining a proposal, responding to a complaint, recommending a candidate]. The tone should be [describe -- e.g., firm but diplomatic, warm and personal, professional and direct]. The recipient is [describe]. Include a before/after comparison if the letter delivers bad news.

**Prompt 4 -- Document Audit:**
> Review this memo/report for structural effectiveness. Assess: (1) whether the summary could stand alone as a complete briefing, (2) whether the body supports the summary with evidence, (3) whether the title accurately signals the content and required action, (4) whether a non-expert reader two years from now would understand the document. Provide specific revision recommendations. Document: [paste text]

## Use Cases

**Validation-Stage Startup Writing Its First Board Memo**
A startup preparing for its first advisory board meeting needs to present a recommendation to pivot from B2C to B2B. The founder drafts a five-page narrative that reads like a journal entry -- chronological, discursive, burying the recommendation on page four. After restructuring with the Issue-Solution-Reason framework, the memo opens with a three-sentence summary: the issue (B2C unit economics are unsustainable at current CAC), the solution (pivot to B2B with existing technology), and the reasoning (three enterprise pilots show 4x better margins with 60% lower acquisition costs). The board reads the summary, nods, and spends the meeting discussing implementation rather than deciphering the problem.

**Growth-Stage Company Producing a Market Analysis Report**
A product team writes a 20-page market analysis for the executive team. The original version opens with four pages of methodology before reaching any findings. The executives, who allocate twenty minutes to each report, never reach the findings. Restructured with the three-audience model: a one-page summary for executives (primary audience), a methodology section for the VP of strategy who will evaluate rigour (secondary audience), and comprehensive data tables with source citations for the analyst who will update the report next quarter (future audience). Every reader gets what they need without wading through content meant for someone else.

**Scale-Stage Organisation Standardising Memo Templates**
A 1,000-person company discovers that its memos follow no consistent structure. Some open with recommendations; others bury them. Some include summaries; others do not. Readers waste time adapting to each writer's idiosyncratic format. The company adopts a standard memo template based on the Issue-Solution-Reason framework, with required sections for summary, body, and recommendations. Document quality, as measured by decision-maker satisfaction surveys, improves 40% in the first quarter -- not because the analysis improved, but because the presentation did.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **The Summary-Free Report.** A report that plunges into analysis without summarising what it found or recommends. Executives who lack time to read the full document -- which is most of them -- never encounter the conclusion. The report produces analysis without influencing decisions.

2. **The Vague Title.** "Subject: Q3" tells the reader nothing about content, purpose, or required action. It guarantees the memo will be opened only by people with nothing better to do, which is precisely the audience least likely to act on it.

3. **Burying the Recommendation.** Presenting all evidence chronologically and revealing the conclusion at the end, as though writing a detective novel. Business readers do not want suspense. They want the answer first and the supporting evidence second.

4. **Writing for Yourself Instead of the Reader.** Structuring the document in the order you discovered the information rather than the order the reader needs it. The research journey and the presentation of findings are different sequences. The reader does not need to retrace your steps.

5. **Overloading with Visuals.** Charts on every page dilute the impact of any single chart. Graphics should be deployed sparingly and only when they communicate something more effectively than prose can. A bar chart showing two numbers is not more informative than a sentence.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Validation | Advisory board memos and investor updates | Documents are short, high-stakes, and read by people who evaluate the founder's thinking as much as the content. Clarity and structure signal analytical competence. |
| Early Traction | Client proposals and internal recommendations | The company begins producing documents that commit resources. Memos that recommend hiring, spending, or changing direction need rigorous structure to withstand scrutiny. |
| Growth | Cross-functional reports and board materials | Multiple teams produce reports for leadership. Without structural standards, the board receives documents in twenty different formats, wasting cognitive energy on navigation rather than evaluation. |
| Scale | Institutional document standards | Memo and report templates become organisational infrastructure. Style guides, training programmes, and editorial review ensure consistency across hundreds of document authors. |

## Output Template

```markdown
# [Document Title: Specific and Action-Oriented]

## Summary
**Issue:** [One to two sentences defining the problem or question]

**Proposed Solution:** [One to two sentences stating the recommendation]

**Reason:** [Two to four sentences explaining why this solution is preferable]

## Background
[Context necessary for readers unfamiliar with the topic -- no more than is needed]

## Analysis
### [Finding/Argument 1]
[Evidence and reasoning]

### [Finding/Argument 2]
[Evidence and reasoning]

### [Finding/Argument 3]
[Evidence and reasoning]

## Counterarguments Considered
[Strongest objection and why the recommendation still holds]

## Recommendations
1. [Specific action, owner, and timeline]
2. [Specific action, owner, and timeline]

## Appendix
[Supporting data, methodology, or detailed tables for secondary and future audiences]
```
