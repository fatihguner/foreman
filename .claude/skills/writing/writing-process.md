# The Writing Process

Good writing is controlled schizophrenia. Four distinct personalities live inside every writer, and the quality of the output depends entirely on whether they are allowed to work in sequence or forced to compete simultaneously. The Madman is the wild-eyed gatherer of raw material -- ideas, facts, hunches, contradictions. The Architect imposes order, distilling chaos into structure. The Carpenter builds sentences and paragraphs, hammering prose into shape. The Judge polishes, tightens, and corrects. Let the Madman and the Judge occupy the same room, and the result is paralysis: one generates, the other censors, and nothing gets written. The secret to productive writing is not talent. It is sequencing.

Betty Sue Flowers, the poet and professor who formalised this framework as MACJ, understood that most writing anxiety stems from attempting all four tasks at once. The moment a writer types a sentence and immediately questions whether it is good enough, the Carpenter and the Judge are fighting for the keyboard. Peter Drucker made the complementary observation that good writing, like good innovation, takes "careful, conscious work, not a flash of genius." The MACJ framework operationalises that insight.

## The Framework

### Phase 1: The Madman (Gathering)

The Madman gathers material without judgement. Ideas from memory, research, observation, conversations, speculation, and imagination -- all are welcome. The goal is volume, not quality.

**How to run the Madman phase:**

- Brainstorm freely. Write down every idea, fact, question, and half-formed thought related to the topic.
- Distinguish facts from opinions. Note sources. Put direct quotes in quotation marks.
- Use a spreadsheet or list with labels indicating the points each piece of evidence supports.
- Do not evaluate. Do not organise. Do not write sentences. The Madman's job is to prevent the problem of "finally thinking of your best point after you've finished and distributed your document."

The Madman phase pays compound interest. Time invested here prevents hours of staring at a blank screen later.

### Phase 2: The Architect (Organising)

The Architect takes the Madman's raw material and imposes structure. The key discipline: **organise in sets of three.**

A mathematician once observed that there are really only four numbers: one, two, three, and many. Four items overload working memory. One or two points feel underdeveloped. Three is the structural sweet spot for business writing -- substantial enough to be persuasive, concise enough to be memorable.

**The Architect's method:**

1. Write down three main points as complete sentences (not topics, not fragments -- full propositions that convey the argument).
2. Arrange them in the most logical order from the reader's perspective.
3. Group supporting material under each point.

Consider an office manager asked to write a memo about acquiring a new building. The Madman brainstorms a list of fifteen topics -- ownership, maintenance, tax consequences, parking, insurance, signage. The Architect groups these into three categories: tasks for the boss (before acquisition), tasks for the manager (before acquisition), and tasks for the manager (after acquisition). What was an overwhelming list of fifteen items becomes three clear sections, each with three supporting points.

The Architect's outline is bare-bones -- three sentences and their subordinate groupings. That is all the structure the Carpenter needs.

### Phase 3: The Carpenter (Drafting)

The Carpenter writes as quickly as possible. Speed is not sloppiness -- it is strategy.

**Rules for the Carpenter phase:**

- **Write against the clock.** Allow 5 to 10 minutes per section (opener, body, closer). Set a timer.
- **Do not edit as you go.** The editorial part of the brain is incompatible with the production part. Letting the Judge kibitz during drafting is multitasking at its worst -- two things done inefficiently rather than one thing done well.
- **Do not wait for inspiration.** After the Madman and Architect have worked, the Carpenter should be primed. Schedule the time, sit down, and begin.
- **Start with whatever section feels most comfortable.** When stuck, skip to another section. If still stuck, say aloud what the passage is trying to convey. Speaking often unlocks the right words.
- **Accept imperfection.** The first draft will be imperfect. That is the point. Perfection is the Judge's job, and the Judge is not invited yet.

Sentences written at speed tend to be shorter and more natural than sentences laboured over. The draft takes shape before the writer has time to second-guess it. Pain decreases as duration decreases.

### Phase 4: The Judge (Polishing)

Once the draft is complete, the Judge takes over. This phase requires as much time as the research and writing phases combined.

**The Judge works in multiple passes, each focusing on one dimension:**

- **Pass 1: Accuracy.** Are the facts correct? Are citations precise?
- **Pass 2: Tone.** Does the tone match the purpose and audience?
- **Pass 3: Transitions.** Does one sentence glide into the next without discontinuities?
- **Pass 4: Economy.** Can any words be cut without losing meaning?
- **Pass 5: Grammar and punctuation.** The final mechanical sweep.

Attempting all five passes simultaneously guarantees that none is done well. Sequential focus produces exponentially better results.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Madman Brainstorm:**
> I need to write a [document type] about [topic] for [audience]. Help me run the Madman phase: generate a comprehensive, unfiltered list of every point, fact, argument, counter-argument, example, and question that could be relevant. Do not organise or evaluate -- just generate. Include ideas I might not have considered.

**Prompt 2 -- Architect Outline:**
> Here is my raw brainstorm for a [document type]: [paste Madman output]. Now be the Architect: group this material into three main points, each expressed as a complete sentence. Arrange them in the most logical order from the reader's perspective, and assign supporting material under each point. Identify any gaps where additional Madman work is needed.

**Prompt 3 -- Carpenter Speed Draft:**
> Here is my three-point outline for a [document type]: [paste Architect output]. Now be the Carpenter: write a rapid, complete first draft. Do not over-polish -- prioritise getting all ideas on paper in a natural, flowing sequence. I will edit later. Include an opener, a body structured around the three points, and a closer.

**Prompt 4 -- Judge Passes:**
> Here is my first draft of a [document type]: [paste Carpenter output]. Now be the Judge. Run five sequential editing passes and show me the result after each: (1) fact-check and flag anything unverified, (2) evaluate tone against my purpose of [state purpose], (3) improve transitions between paragraphs, (4) cut unnecessary words, (5) correct grammar and punctuation. Show the final polished version.

## Use Cases

**Early-Traction Startup Writing a Series A Memo**

A founder needs to write a 10-page investment memo but feels overwhelmed by the scope. Applying MACJ: The **Madman** spends two hours gathering material -- market data, customer quotes, revenue figures, competitive analysis, team bios, product roadmap, risk factors. The **Architect** distils this into three core propositions: (1) the market is large and underserved, (2) the product has demonstrated early traction with strong unit economics, (3) the team has a unique right to win. Each proposition gets a supporting evidence cluster. The **Carpenter** drafts each section in timed 10-minute blocks, producing a rough but complete draft in under two hours. The **Judge** spends a full day running multiple editing passes -- tightening prose, verifying data, calibrating tone for a sophisticated investor audience. The final document is coherent, persuasive, and completed in three days rather than the three weeks of anxious procrastination that preceded the MACJ adoption.

**Growth-Stage Company Producing a Quarterly Board Report**

A VP of Operations dreads the quarterly board report because it requires synthesising input from six departments. The MACJ framework transforms the process. The **Madman** phase involves collecting raw updates from each department head (structured as bullet points, not prose). The **Architect** organises these into three board-level themes: operational performance, strategic initiatives, and risk factors. The **Carpenter** drafts the narrative in a single sitting, using the outline to maintain momentum. The **Judge** reviews over two days, with each pass focused on a single dimension. The result: a board report that takes three days instead of two weeks, with higher quality because each phase received undivided attention.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Letting the Judge into the Carpenter's Workshop.** The most destructive anti-pattern. Editing while drafting feels productive but is actually paralysing. Every sentence gets scrutinised before the next one begins, and the draft never gains momentum. The result is either a polished opening paragraph followed by nothing, or a document that took five times longer than necessary.

2. **Skipping the Architect.** Writers who brainstorm and then draft without organising produce documents that wander. The reader can sense the absence of structure -- ideas repeat, the argument circles back on itself, and the conclusion contradicts the opening. Three sentences of outline prevent hours of structural revision.

3. **Brainstorming in Sentences.** The Madman should generate topics, fragments, and raw ideas -- not polished prose. Writers who brainstorm in full sentences are unconsciously editing (inviting the Judge early) and constraining their creative range.

4. **Single-Pass Editing.** The Judge who tries to check facts, improve tone, tighten prose, and fix grammar in one reading will miss errors in every category. Focused sequential passes -- even if each is quick -- produce dramatically better results than one heroic sweep.

5. **Waiting for Inspiration.** Inspiration is not a prerequisite; it is a byproduct. The Madman and Architect phases generate the raw material that makes the Carpenter productive. Waiting for a "flash of genius" before beginning is waiting for a bus on a train line.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Vision articulation | The Madman phase is dominated by imagination and analogy rather than data. The Architect must structure a narrative around possibility, not proof. The Carpenter's voice should convey conviction. |
| Validation | Evidence synthesis | The Madman phase becomes research-heavy -- customer interviews, pilot data, market sizing. The Architect organises around evidence categories. The Judge must verify every claim. |
| Early Traction | Multi-document management | Founders write constantly -- investor updates, customer proposals, team memos. MACJ prevents each document from consuming disproportionate time. Templated Architect outlines accelerate repetitive formats. |
| Growth | Delegation of phases | At growth stage, different people can own different phases. A subject-matter expert runs the Madman, a communications lead serves as Architect and Carpenter, and leadership plays Judge. The framework scales because it decomposes writing into separable tasks. |
| Scale | Process standardisation | MACJ can be embedded into organisational writing standards -- requiring outlines before drafts, prohibiting single-pass editing, mandating peer review during the Judge phase. |
