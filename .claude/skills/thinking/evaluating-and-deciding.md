# Evaluating and Deciding

MARGIN OF SAFETY. Good decision-makers do not predict more accurately than everyone else -- they build better buffers for being wrong. Warren Buffett sat out the dot-com mania while friends doubled and quadrupled their portfolios, endured years of being called obsolete, and emerged with cash reserves intact when the bubble burst. The hedge fund Long-Term Capital Management, run by two Nobel laureates and some of the highest IQs in finance, delivered 40 percent annual returns until it lost $4.6 billion in under four months and nearly collapsed the global financial system. The difference was not intelligence. It was the presence or absence of a margin of safety -- the deliberate gap between what you expect to happen and what could happen, designed to save you when surprises are expensive. The framework for the full decision arc -- evaluate, safeguard, execute, learn -- rests on this single uncomfortable truth: certainty is a feeling, not a fact, and the moment you are most confident you do not need a buffer is precisely the moment you need one most.

---

## The Framework

The framework spans four stages. Each builds on the preceding one, and skipping any stage degrades the quality of the entire arc.

### Stage 1: Evaluate the Options

Evaluation begins not with data but with criteria. Three features distinguish useful criteria from decorative ones:

- **Clarity.** The criterion can be explained to a twelve-year-old. If it requires jargon, it will be interpreted differently by each person applying it.
- **Goal promotion.** The criterion must favour options that advance the actual objective. NASA's Challenger disaster is the canonical counter-example: launch-date criteria promoted the goal of schedule adherence, not the goal of safety. The result was catastrophic.
- **Decisiveness.** The criteria must narrow the field to one option. Criteria that produce ties are criteria that avoid commitment.

**Finding the Most Important Thing.** The text recounts a lesson from a mentor that teams cannot make decisions independently unless the leader communicates a single overriding priority. Not three priorities. Not a weighted matrix of ten factors. One. The exercise for identifying it is deliberately combative: write each criterion on a separate card, then force them to battle in pairs. "If I could have only one of these, which would it be?" As criteria compete, quantities emerge -- thresholds at which the ranking reverses. The result is an ordered stack with the most important thing at the top and the precise conditions under which the order would change.

**Getting the Right Information.** Two principles govern information quality:

| Principle | Definition | Entrepreneurial Application |
|-----------|------------|----------------------------|
| **HiFi (High-Fidelity)** | Information close to the source, unfiltered by others' biases and incentives. | Talk to customers directly, not to the VP who summarised the survey. General Marshall sent trusted observers to the front lines and discovered that pilots were refusing to fly not because of aircraft defects but because ground crews, ravaged by malaria from lack of mosquito netting, could not be trusted to maintain the planes. Headquarters, in its mosquito-protected offices, had no idea. |
| **HiEx (High-Expertise)** | Information from people with deep, recent experience solving the specific problem at hand. | Seek experts who solved your problem recently, not two decades ago. Pay for their time -- it forces you to prepare precise questions. Ask "how do you think about this?" rather than "what should I do?" The former transfers reasoning; the latter transfers only an answer. |

**Distinguishing Experts from Imitators.** Imitators cannot answer questions at a deeper level, cannot adapt their vocabulary to a non-specialist audience, become frustrated when you say you do not understand, avoid discussing their failures, and cannot identify the boundaries of their own competence. Real experts display the opposite on every count.

### Stage 2: Margin of Safety

A margin of safety is insurance for decisions. In years when nothing goes wrong, it looks like waste. In the year something does, it is the difference between survival and ruin.

The baseline heuristic: a margin of safety sufficient to absorb double the worst-case scenario. If job loss would take six months to recover from, hold twelve months of expenses. If a product launch might miss revenue targets by 30 percent, build a budget that survives a 60 percent miss.

Adjustments to the baseline:

- **High cost of failure, volatile environment:** Increase the margin. The consequences of underprotection are asymmetric -- you can always reduce a margin later, but you cannot retroactively create one.
- **Low cost of failure, established pattern:** Reduce the margin. Not every decision requires a buffer. The discipline is knowing which ones do.
- **Deep expertise and proprietary data:** Reduce the margin further, as Buffett does when buying businesses he understands well enough to pay near full value.

**Bullets Before Cannonballs.** When still gathering information, do not over-invest in a single option. Jim Collins and Morten Hansen's metaphor is precise: fire small, cheap bullets to calibrate aim, then fire the cannonball along the validated line of sight. A founder evaluating two CEO candidates should have both complete a small paid project with the team before committing to either. A founder considering medical school should shadow a resident for a day, not enrol in a programme. Preserving optionality has a cost -- it can look like indecision. But looking short-term stupid while keeping options open is a consistent feature of the most successful long-term decision-makers.

**Inversion: What Would Guarantee Failure?** Rather than asking "what should we do to succeed?" ask "what would guarantee failure?" and then avoid those things. If guaranteed failure for a product launch means shipping without beta testing, launching without a support team, and ignoring the first two weeks of customer feedback -- then the margin of safety must explicitly prevent each of those conditions.

### Stage 3: Execute the Decision

**The Irreversibility Test.** Decisions sit on a spectrum from trivially reversible to permanently irreversible. Two principles calibrate speed to position on this spectrum:

- **ASAP Principle:** If the cost to undo the decision is low, make it as soon as possible. Deliberating over a reversible choice is itself a costly error. Jeff Bezos calls these "two-way doors" -- walk through, and if it is wrong, walk back.
- **ALAP Principle:** If the cost to undo the decision is high, make it as late as possible while continuing to gather information and preserve optionality. These are "one-way doors." Keeping a pocket of space open -- the way a driver maintains following distance at high speed -- is how you preserve the ability to manoeuvre when circumstances shift.

**The Stop, FLOP, Know Principle.** Even for one-way doors, there comes a moment to act. That moment arrives when: (1) you Stop gathering useful information -- confidence is rising but accuracy is not; (2) you experience a First Lost OPportunity, meaning options begin to expire; or (3) you come to Know something -- a piece of evidence or a gut conviction tested against reason -- that makes the right choice evident.

**Live with the Decision Before Announcing It.** Make the decision, then sleep on it. Before bed, write a note to yourself explaining why you decided as you did. Read the note in the morning. More often than expected, the reasoning that seemed airtight at 6 PM looks different at 7 AM. Announcing prematurely starts things moving and makes reversal socially expensive.

**Three Execution Fail-Safes:**

1. **Trip Wires.** Precommit to specific actions triggered by quantifiable conditions. A summit team agrees to turn back if they have not reached Camp 4 by noon. A startup agrees that if month-three retention is below 20 percent, the product thesis is invalidated. No argument at the moment of truth -- the decision was already made.

2. **Commander's Intent.** Communicate the strategy, the rationale, and the operational boundaries so that team members can adapt when circumstances change without waiting for approval. The four components are formulate, communicate, interpret, and implement. If you cannot be away from the office for a week without things falling apart, the failure is not that you are indispensable -- it is that you are an incompetent communicator of intent.

3. **Ulysses Pacts (Tying Your Hands).** Remove the option to deviate from the plan during moments of weakness. Automated investment deposits. Removing junk food from the house. Locking in a product launch date with external commitments that make delay socially or financially costly.

### Stage 4: Learn from Decisions

**The Process Principle.** Evaluate the process you used, not the outcome you got. A good decision can produce a bad outcome (bad luck), and a bad decision can produce a good outcome (good luck). Pete Carroll's decision to throw rather than run on the one-yard line in Super Bowl XLIX was rooted in sound analysis of the opponent's defensive weaknesses. The interception that resulted was, in his words, "the worst result of a call ever" -- not the worst call.

The process-outcome matrix:

| | Good Outcome | Bad Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Good Process** | Deserved success. Stay disciplined. | Bad luck. Trust the process. Learn and continue. |
| **Bad Process** | Undeserved success. You got lucky. Change before luck runs out. | Deserved failure. Let it be a wake-up call. |

**The Transparency Principle.** Make your reasoning visible and open to scrutiny -- primarily to yourself. The ego default rewrites memory after the fact, conflating what you know now with what you knew then. A decision journal defeats this distortion.

What to record at the time of the decision:

- The problem as you understood it
- The options you considered and the criteria you applied
- What you expected to happen and why
- The information you had and the information you wished you had
- Your confidence level

What to review afterward:

- Did the outcome occur for the reasons you anticipated?
- Where did your reasoning hold, and where did it break down?
- Was the result attributable to skill or to luck?

One firm described in the text discovered that its decision-makers were correct only 20 percent of the time -- and that even when anticipated outcomes materialised, they did so for different reasons than predicted. The firm's "success" was indistinguishable from roulette. Without the journal, they would never have known.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Criteria Weighting:**
> "I need to decide between [options] for my [stage] [sector] company. Help me identify the five most relevant evaluation criteria, then run the 'criteria battle' exercise: force each pair to compete ('if I could have only one, which would it be?'), surface the quantities at which rankings reverse, and produce a final ordered stack with the single most important thing at the top."

**Prompt 2 -- Margin of Safety Calculation:**
> "I am planning [initiative/investment/launch] with an expected outcome of [target]. Apply the margin of safety framework: (1) identify what could go wrong, (2) estimate the worst plausible scenario, (3) calculate a buffer that absorbs double that worst case, and (4) recommend adjustments based on the cost of failure, environmental volatility, and my level of expertise. Include a bullets-before-cannonballs plan for low-risk validation before full commitment."

**Prompt 3 -- Irreversibility and Timing Assessment:**
> "I am facing [decision]. Classify it on the consequentiality-reversibility spectrum. Is this a two-way door (ASAP) or a one-way door (ALAP)? Apply the Stop, FLOP, Know principle to determine whether I have enough information to act now, or whether I should continue gathering information and preserving optionality. Identify the specific signals that would trigger action."

**Prompt 4 -- Post-Decision Review:**
> "Six months ago I decided [decision]. The outcome was [outcome]. Help me conduct a process review using the framework: (1) Reconstruct what I knew at the time and the reasoning I used. (2) Determine whether the outcome occurred for the reasons I expected. (3) Classify the result in the process-outcome matrix (good process/good outcome, good process/bad outcome, etc.). (4) Identify what I should change in my process for future decisions of this type."

## Use Cases

**Early-Traction Marketplace Evaluating Expansion Options**

A two-sided marketplace for freelance graphic designers has achieved product-market fit in one vertical (e-commerce brands) and faces three expansion options: geographic expansion, vertical expansion (adding new buyer segments), and service expansion (adding motion design). The founder's instinct is to pursue all three. Applying the criteria battle, the team identifies that the single most important thing is deepening supply-side liquidity in the existing vertical -- without which none of the expansion options will succeed. The margin of safety analysis reveals that pursuing two expansion vectors simultaneously, if either underperforms by 40 percent, would burn through runway before Series A closes. The bullets-before-cannonballs prescription: run a four-week paid pilot in the most promising new vertical with five existing designers and ten new buyers. If conversion rates match the existing vertical within 70 percent, fire the cannonball. If not, recalibrate before committing resources.

**Growth-Stage Fintech Deciding on Regulatory Strategy**

A fintech company approaching $50M ARR must choose between applying for its own banking charter (expensive, slow, high control) and partnering with a sponsor bank (faster, lower cost, less control). Binary framing makes this look like a pure trade-off. The irreversibility test reveals that obtaining a charter is a one-way door (ALAP Principle applies -- delay and gather information), while a sponsor-bank partnership is a two-way door (ASAP Principle -- sign and renegotiate later if needed). Commander's intent for the compliance team: "Our most important thing is maintaining uninterrupted service to existing customers. Any regulatory strategy that risks a service gap is off the table, regardless of long-term efficiency gains." Trip wire: if the charter application has not advanced past preliminary review within nine months, pivot full resources to the sponsor-bank path.

**Solo Founder Learning from a Failed Product Launch**

A solo founder launched a productivity tool for remote teams, priced at $29/month, and acquired 200 users in the first month -- but 90 percent churned within 60 days. The natural instinct is resulting: "the product failed, so the decision to launch was bad." A process review using the decision journal reveals that the launch decision itself was sound (low cost to undo, clear stop-loss criteria, validated demand through a landing page test), but the pricing and onboarding criteria were set without HiFi information -- the founder relied on competitor pricing data aggregated by a third-party report rather than talking to ten target customers about willingness to pay. The process failure was informational, not decisional. The corrective action is not to abandon the product but to invest in direct customer conversations before the next pricing iteration.

## Anti-Patterns

**Treating all decisions as one-way doors.** Founders who deliberate for weeks over reversible choices -- a landing page design, a pricing test, a Slack channel name -- are misallocating their most constrained resource: time. The ASAP Principle exists precisely for these decisions. Walk through the two-way door. If it is wrong, walk back.

**Building no margin of safety because "we know what we're doing."** Buffett's observation applies: "Diversification is protection against ignorance. It makes little sense if you know what you are doing." The corollary is that most of us rarely know what we are doing with the confidence required to go all in. The moment confidence peaks is precisely when the margin of safety is most needed, because overconfidence is the ego default's signature product.

**Resulting -- evaluating decisions by outcomes alone.** A decision that produced a good outcome may have been reckless (bad process, good luck). A decision that produced a bad outcome may have been excellent (good process, bad luck). Without a decision journal that captures reasoning at the time of the decision, there is no way to distinguish skill from luck, and no mechanism for improvement.

**Setting trip wires you are willing to override.** A trip wire that is negotiable at the moment of truth is not a trip wire. It is a suggestion. The entire point of precommitment is that it removes discretion from your future self -- the version of you that is fatigued, emotionally invested, and susceptible to summit fever. If the team agreed to turn back at noon and it is 12:01, the team turns back.

**Confusing commander's intent with abdication.** Empowering others to make decisions requires communicating the strategy, the rationale, and the boundaries. Telling a team "just figure it out" without context is not delegation; it is abandonment. Commander's intent has four components; skipping the first two makes the last two impossible.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Criteria are sparse and personal -- the founder evaluates options against their own goals, risk tolerance, and opportunity costs. | The margin of safety is primarily financial (personal runway). Bullets before cannonballs means validating demand before building product. |
| Validation | Criteria become market-facing -- customer willingness to pay, retention rates, activation metrics. | HiFi information from direct customer contact outweighs any survey or third-party report. The ASAP Principle applies to most validation experiments. |
| Early Traction | The team grows, and the "most important thing" must be communicated to others. Criteria battles happen across functions, not just in the founder's head. | Commander's intent becomes operational. Trip wires attach to leading metrics (activation, retention) not just lagging ones (revenue). |
| Growth | Multiple consequential decisions compete for attention simultaneously. Margins of safety interact -- a thin buffer in one area can cascade into failure in another. | The ALAP Principle governs major strategic bets (new markets, acquisitions, platform changes). Decision journals become institutional, not personal. |
| Scale | The decision-maker is increasingly distant from the source of information. Abstractions multiply. | The HiFi Principle is hardest to maintain at scale and therefore most valuable. Leaders must actively seek unfiltered information from the front lines. The Transparency Principle becomes an organisational practice, not an individual one. |
