# Wanting What Matters

DEATHBED TEST. The clearest decision-making tool is not a matrix or a framework -- it is asking what you would regret not having done from your deathbed. Jeff Bezos used precisely this test when deciding whether to leave a comfortable Wall Street career to start an online bookstore in 1994: "I knew that when I was 80 I was not going to regret having tried. I was not going to regret trying to participate in this thing called the Internet that I thought was going to be a really big deal. I knew that if I failed I wouldn't regret that, but I knew the one thing I might regret is not ever having tried." The regret-minimisation framework cuts through analytical complexity with uncomfortable speed because it does not ask what will maximise returns -- it asks what will minimise the ache of a life unlived. The Clear Thinking framework argues that all the decision-making discipline in the world amounts to very little if the things being pursued are not worth pursuing. Good judgment, at its highest expression, is not knowing how to get what you want. It is knowing which things are worth wanting.

## The Framework

This framework operates at a different altitude from most decision-making tools. Where evaluation frameworks ask "which option is best?", this one asks "best for what?" It proceeds through four lenses, each progressively closer to the core question.

### Lens 1: The Hedonic Treadmill and the Happy-When Trap

Psychologists Philip Brickman and Donald Campbell identified a phenomenon so pervasive it functions almost as a natural law: humans adapt to improvements in circumstances and revert to a baseline level of satisfaction. The car that was supposed to bring lasting happiness provides two weeks of euphoria before becoming the new normal -- and the new basis for comparison with everyone else's car. The promotion that was supposed to validate years of effort produces a brief high before introducing new problems, new peers to compare against, and a fresh version of the same discontent.

The framework labels the sufferers "happy-when" people. They will be happy when they make more money. Happy when they get the credit they deserve. Happy when they find the right partner. The conditional never resolves because each "when" becomes the new floor, and a new "when" materialises immediately above it. It is a one-way door: once through, perspective on where you were is lost, and only the gap to the next level remains visible.

The entrepreneurial version is particularly virulent. The founder who will be happy when they close the seed round discovers, upon closing it, that happiness now depends on the Series A. The Series A produces a brief high before the pressure of growth targets imposes a new conditional. The text quotes an observation that cuts close to the bone: "The next zero in your bank account won't satisfy you any more than you are satisfied now."

**The diagnostic question:** If you achieved the goal you are currently pursuing, and the resulting satisfaction lasted only two weeks before reverting to baseline, would you still pursue it? If not, the goal is a treadmill metric -- it generates motion without destination.

### Lens 2: Dickens's Hidden Lesson

A Christmas Carol is not a holiday story. It is a decision-making parable. Ebenezer Scrooge is visited by three spirits who show him the past, the present, and a future that might be -- a future in which he is dead and no one mourns him. His former associates are relieved, spiteful, or indifferent. The vision provides what no spreadsheet can: the long-term consequences of his current trajectory, compressed into a single night. He gets something almost no one gets -- the chance to see where his current decisions lead and to change course before it is too late.

The text illustrates the real-world version with a retired CEO who had played by society's scoreboard -- wealth, status, power -- with sharp elbows in a hypercompetitive culture. He quoted Tywin Lannister, called himself a lion, and never lacked golfing companions while in office. Upon retiring, most of his "friends" stopped returning calls. His transactional approach to relationships had generated professional compliance, not genuine connection. Unlike Scrooge, he received no second chance.

**The diagnostic question:** If you continue on your current trajectory for ten years and then look back, what will you wish you had done differently? The discomfort this question produces is proportional to the gap between your current direction and your actual values.

### Lens 3: What the Happiness Experts Actually Say

Gerontologist Karl Pillemer spent seven years interviewing Americans in their seventies, eighties, and nineties -- people he describes as "the most credible experts we have on how to live happy and fulfilled lives during hard times." His findings are consistent across demographics and are striking for what they include and what they omit.

**What the elderly say matters:**

- Say things now to people you care about -- gratitude, forgiveness, questions you have always wanted to ask
- Spend the maximum amount of time with your children
- Savour daily pleasures instead of waiting for "big-ticket items" to produce happiness
- Work in a job you love
- Choose your life partner carefully

**What none of them said:**

- None said that to be happy you should work as hard as you can to make money
- None said it was important to be as wealthy as the people around you
- None said to choose your career based on earning potential
- None said they regretted not getting even with someone who wronged them

**Their largest regret:** Worrying about things that never happened. "Worrying wastes your life," one respondent said.

The most powerful finding was attitudinal. An 89-year-old woman, when asked to explain her happiness, answered: "In my 89 years, I've learned that happiness is a choice -- not a condition." Pillemer concluded that the elderly "make the key distinction between events that happen to us, on the one hand, and our internal attitude toward happiness, on the other. Happy in spite of."

This reframes happiness as a decision -- one that sits on the same continuum as every other decision an entrepreneur makes. It can be chosen daily, or it can be deferred to a "when" that never arrives.

### Lens 4: Memento Mori as a Practical Decision Filter

The Stoic practice of memento mori -- remembering that you will die -- is treated philosophically in the [Stoic Mortality and Perspective](../stoic/stoic-mortality-and-perspective.md) skill, where Marcus Aurelius and Seneca use mortality awareness to cultivate urgency, clarity, and the view from above. What follows here is the applied version: mortality as a filter for specific decisions.

**The Deathbed Test.** Imagine yourself at eighty, sitting on a park bench on a beautiful autumn day. Who is in your life? What have you accomplished? What memories do you cherish? What do you regret? Steve Jobs performed this exercise daily: "If today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I am about to do today?" Whenever the answer was no too many days in a row, he knew something had to change.

The deathbed test is not a philosophical exercise. It is a filter with three operational applications:

1. **The regret filter.** We regret the things we did not do more than the things we did. The pain of trying and failing is intense but brief. The pain of failing to try is mild but permanent. When facing a decision between safety and risk, ask which version produces the less tolerable regret at eighty.

2. **The ordinary moments filter.** What we think of as defining moments -- promotions, launches, funding rounds -- matter less to life satisfaction than the accumulation of tiny moments that did not seem significant at the time. The family dinners. The marks on the doorframe measuring how tall the children were. The afternoon you spent on the couch talking about nothing. The phrase is precise: "Tiny delights over big bright lights." The deathbed test consistently reveals that the things remembered are not the things that were optimised for.

3. **The relationship filter.** Across Pillemer's interviews, across hospice research, across every longitudinal study of life satisfaction, the finding is identical: relationships are the variable that matters most and the variable most frequently sacrificed in pursuit of variables that matter less. The deathbed test restores the ranking that day-to-day urgency inverts.

### The Integrating Concept: Phronesis

The ancient Greeks had a word for the wisdom of knowing how to order your life to achieve the best results: phronesis. It is distinct from technical skill (techne) and from theoretical knowledge (episteme). Phronesis is practical wisdom -- the ability to discern which ends are worth pursuing and which means are appropriate for pursuing them.

The framework revolves around this concept: good judgment is not merely knowing how to get what you want. It is knowing what to want. "If you want to develop good judgment, start by asking two questions: 'What do I want in life? And is what I want actually worth wanting?' Until you've answered the second question, all the decision-making advice in the world isn't going to do you much good."

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Hedonic Treadmill Audit:**
> "I am pursuing [goal/metric] for my [stage] [sector] company. Apply the hedonic treadmill test: if I achieve this goal and the satisfaction lasts only two weeks before reverting to baseline, was the pursuit worthwhile? Identify which of my current goals are treadmill metrics (generating motion without destination) and which are genuinely aligned with long-term satisfaction. Suggest replacements for the treadmill metrics."

**Prompt 2 -- Deathbed Test for a Specific Decision:**
> "I am deciding between [Option A] and [Option B]. Project me forward to age 80. From that perspective, which choice produces the more tolerable regret? Apply the three operational filters: (1) regret filter -- which failure of action would haunt me more? (2) ordinary moments filter -- which option generates more of the small daily experiences that matter in retrospect? (3) relationship filter -- which option better preserves or strengthens the relationships that the elderly consistently identify as most important?"

**Prompt 3 -- Trajectory Projection (Dickens Lens):**
> "Describe my current professional and personal trajectory based on these facts: [describe current priorities, time allocation, relationships, and goals]. Now project that trajectory ten years forward, assuming no changes. What does the picture look like? Where is the gap between where this trajectory leads and where I would actually want to end up? Identify the two or three adjustments that would close the gap most effectively."

**Prompt 4 -- Phronesis Audit for the Founder:**
> "I have built or am building a company around [mission/product]. Separate the things I am pursuing into two categories: (1) things that are genuinely worth wanting based on what happiness research and end-of-life wisdom consistently identify, and (2) things I am pursuing because of social comparison, status signalling, or the hedonic treadmill. For each item in category 2, suggest what I might pursue instead that would produce more durable satisfaction."

## Use Cases

**Growth-Stage Founder on the Edge of Burnout**

A founder of a Series B logistics platform works 80-hour weeks, has missed the last three family holidays, and tells herself she will "slow down after the IPO." Applying the hedonic treadmill lens, she recognises a pattern: she said the same thing before the Series A and before the Series B. Each milestone, upon achievement, produced brief satisfaction and immediately generated a new conditional. The deathbed test reveals that she will not remember the Q3 revenue target; she will remember that she missed her daughter's first piano recital. The ordinary moments filter makes visible what the P&L obscures: the daily accumulation of absences that compound as irreversibly as interest. The operational adjustment is not dramatic -- it is scheduling two non-negotiable evenings per week and treating them with the same sanctity as board meetings. The adjustment costs nothing in strategic capability and everything in the metric that actually matters.

**Pre-Seed Founder Choosing Between Safety and Risk**

A 34-year-old product manager at a major tech company has a working prototype for a climate-tech startup but cannot bring himself to leave the security of a $280K salary. Bezos's regret-minimisation framework applies directly: at eighty, will he regret having tried and failed, or will he regret never having tried? The pain of a failed startup is intense but finite -- twelve to eighteen months of reduced income and bruised ego. The pain of "what if I had tried?" is low-grade but permanent. The deathbed test does not guarantee the startup succeeds. It clarifies which failure is easier to live with. He negotiates a six-month unpaid leave of absence -- a margin of safety that preserves the option to return while eliminating the regret of never having started.

**Scaled CEO Redefining Success Metrics**

The CEO of a 500-person company has achieved every goal he set at twenty-five: wealth, recognition, a company that bears his name. He finds himself, at fifty, unable to articulate what he is working toward beyond "more." Pillemer's research provides the mirror: none of the elderly said that happiness came from maximising wealth, and none regretted failing to accumulate more status. The Dickens lens -- projecting the current trajectory forward -- shows a future in which the company thrives and the CEO's closest relationships are transactional. The phronesis audit separates the goals worth wanting (building something that outlasts him, mentoring the next generation of leaders, deepening his marriage) from the goals that are pure treadmill (the next board seat, the next magazine profile). The operational result is a restructured calendar that allocates 30 percent of his time to mentoring and relationship investment, funded by delegating operational decisions he was holding out of habit rather than necessity.

## Anti-Patterns

**Using the deathbed test to justify recklessness.** The test is a filter for values alignment, not a licence to ignore financial reality. "Life is short" is the most frequently abused insight in the self-help canon. The founder who quits a stable job with no savings, no prototype, and no plan is not applying memento mori -- they are applying magical thinking with a philosophical veneer.

**Treating this skill as a replacement for analytical decision-making.** Wanting what matters sits above the evaluation frameworks, not in opposition to them. Once you have clarity on what is worth pursuing, you still need criteria weighting, margin of safety, and execution fail-safes to pursue it effectively. Values without competence produce noble failures.

**Dismissing the hedonic treadmill as obvious.** Everyone nods when told that money does not buy happiness. Almost no one adjusts their behaviour accordingly. The test of whether you have internalised this lens is not whether you agree with it intellectually but whether your calendar and your bank statement reflect the agreement.

**Applying Pillemer's findings selectively.** The elderly do not say "don't work hard." They say "work in a job you love" and "don't choose your career based on earning potential." These are compatible with extreme ambition. What they are incompatible with is pursuing money or status as terminal goals rather than as byproducts of meaningful work.

**Confusing memento mori with pessimism.** Mortality awareness does not produce despair in those who apply it rigorously. It produces clarity. The Stoic skill on mortality and perspective develops the philosophical dimension of this practice. The present skill uses it as a filter: does this specific decision pass the deathbed test? The two applications are complementary, not competing.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | The founder decides what problem to solve and why. The deathbed test filters out ideas pursued for status or trend-following and surfaces ideas aligned with genuine values. | The hedonic treadmill audit is personal: "If this company succeeds and I feel the same way in two weeks, was it the right company to build?" |
| Validation | Testing whether the problem matters to customers, but also whether solving it matters to the founder on a ten-year horizon. | Founders who are not intrinsically motivated by the problem they are solving tend to quit during the hardest phase -- the one between prototype and traction. The Dickens lens exposes this misalignment before capital and time are committed. |
| Early Traction | The hedonic treadmill intensifies as metrics become visible. MRR, user counts, and NPS scores become the new "happy-when" conditionals. | The ordinary moments filter becomes a daily practice: are the rhythms of building this company producing daily satisfaction, or is all satisfaction deferred to a future milestone? |
| Growth | Social comparison accelerates. Other founders raise larger rounds, grow faster, command more press. The treadmill runs faster. | Pillemer's research is the corrective: none of the elderly wished they had been wealthier than their peers. The relationship filter protects against the subtle erosion of personal connections that growth-stage intensity produces. |
| Scale | Legacy questions emerge. The founder asks "what will I be remembered for?" and the answer may not align with current priorities. | The phronesis audit becomes strategic: is the company optimised for the things that will matter in retrospect, or for the things that the market currently rewards? Commander's intent should reflect values, not just targets. |
