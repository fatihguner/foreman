# Doblin's Ten Types of Innovation

Most entrepreneurs, when asked about innovation, picture a product: a new gadget, a novel app feature, a technological breakthrough. This reflex -- equating innovation with invention -- is both pervasive and profoundly limiting. Larry Keeley and his colleagues at Doblin (now part of Deloitte) spent decades analysing thousands of innovations across industries and reached a conclusion that should discomfort any product-obsessed founder: product innovation is the easiest type for competitors to copy and the least likely to deliver sustained competitive advantage. The companies that dominate their markets innovate across multiple types simultaneously, building advantages that are structurally difficult to replicate because they exist not in any single feature but in the configuration of the entire business.

The Ten Types of Innovation framework, published in 2013, provides the most comprehensive taxonomy of innovation available. It is not a theory about creativity. It is a strategic architecture for building businesses that competitors cannot easily decode.

---

## The Framework

The ten types organise into three categories: Configuration (how the business is structured), Offering (what the business delivers), and Experience (how customers interact with the business).

### Configuration (Internal-Facing Innovation)

**1. Profit Model -- How You Make Money**

Innovation in the mechanism that converts value into revenue. Gillette did not invent the razor; it invented razors-and-blades pricing. Salesforce did not invent CRM; it invented SaaS subscription pricing for enterprise software.

*Entrepreneurial application:* Challenge the default pricing model in your industry. Subscription where competitors charge one-time fees. Freemium where competitors charge upfront. Usage-based where competitors charge flat rates. Revenue-share where competitors charge licensing fees.

**2. Network -- How You Connect with Others to Create Value**

Innovation through alliances, partnerships, and ecosystems. Visa created a network of banks that would have been competitors if left to individual action. Apple's App Store turned independent developers into a product-development army.

*Entrepreneurial application:* Identify which external parties could contribute to your value proposition if properly incentivised. Marketplace models, API platforms, franchise structures, and co-branding partnerships all represent network innovation.

**3. Structure -- How You Organise and Align Talent and Assets**

Innovation in organisational design, asset configuration, and human capital management. Toyota's production system. Google's 20% time (when it existed). Valve's flat hierarchy.

*Entrepreneurial application:* How your company is organised affects what it can produce. A remote-first structure enables global talent access. A squad-based model enables parallel product development. Asset-light structures enable rapid scaling.

**4. Process -- How You Use Superior Methods to Do Your Work**

Innovation in the signature processes that create competitive advantage. Amazon's fulfilment logistics. IKEA's flat-pack manufacturing. Zara's fast-fashion supply chain that moves from design to retail in two weeks.

*Entrepreneurial application:* Identify the one or two processes that most directly affect your value proposition. Innovating there -- through automation, proprietary methodology, or radical simplification -- creates defensible advantage because processes are invisible to competitors and difficult to reverse-engineer.

### Offering (Product-Facing Innovation)

**5. Product Performance -- How You Develop Distinguishing Features and Functionality**

The type most people mean when they say "innovation." Superior features, performance, quality, or design. The iPhone's multitouch interface. Dyson's cyclone vacuum technology. Tesla's electric drivetrain.

*Entrepreneurial application:* Product innovation is necessary but insufficient. Without reinforcement from other innovation types, product advantages erode within 12-18 months as competitors catch up.

**6. Product System -- How You Create Complementary Products and Services**

Innovation through creating an ecosystem of connected offerings. Apple's hardware-software-services integration. Microsoft Office as an integrated suite. Peloton's bike-plus-content-plus-community system.

*Entrepreneurial application:* Consider what adjacent products or services would make your core offering more valuable. Integration points, accessories, data layers, and complementary content all represent product system innovation.

### Experience (Customer-Facing Innovation)

**7. Service -- How You Support and Amplify the Value of Your Offering**

Innovation in the services that surround and enhance the core product. Amazon Prime's delivery promise. Zappos's 365-day return policy. AppleCare's service experience.

*Entrepreneurial application:* Service innovation is particularly powerful for startups because it often requires organisational commitment rather than capital investment. A superior onboarding experience, proactive customer success, or unconditional guarantees can differentiate without heavy R&D spend.

**8. Channel -- How You Deliver Your Offering to Customers and Users**

Innovation in how you reach the customer. Warby Parker's home try-on disrupted optical retail. Dollar Shave Club's direct-to-consumer subscription bypassed retail entirely. Tesla's company-owned showrooms eliminated the dealership model.

*Entrepreneurial application:* The default distribution channel in your industry may not be the optimal one. Direct-to-consumer, marketplace-first, partnership-led, or hybrid channel strategies can redefine who captures margin in the value chain.

**9. Brand -- How You Represent Your Offering and Business**

Innovation in how the company communicates its identity and values. Patagonia's "Don't Buy This Jacket" anti-consumerist positioning. Red Bull's content-company-that-sells-energy-drinks identity. Muji's "no-brand brand" philosophy.

*Entrepreneurial application:* Brand innovation is not about a better logo. It is about occupying a distinctive position in the customer's mind that competitors cannot credibly claim. The brand should encode the company's strategic differentiation, not merely describe it.

**10. Customer Engagement -- How You Foster Compelling Interactions**

Innovation in the end-to-end experience of being a customer. IKEA's self-service showroom experience. Nike's running community and app ecosystem. Airbnb's host-guest trust mechanisms.

*Entrepreneurial application:* Map every touchpoint in the customer journey. Engagement innovation often lives in the transitions between touchpoints -- the moments where competitors lose the customer's attention or create friction.

### The Power of Combination

Doblin's research found that the most successful innovations integrate five or more types simultaneously. Innovating on product alone rarely creates lasting advantage. Innovating across profit model, process, product, service, and channel creates a system that competitors cannot replicate by copying any single component.

| Number of Types Combined | Competitive Durability |
|--------------------------|----------------------|
| 1-2 types | Easily copied within 12-18 months |
| 3-4 types | Moderately defensible; requires significant competitor effort |
| 5+ types | Structurally difficult to replicate; creates genuine moats |

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Innovation Audit:**
> "Analyse my business: [describe business model, product, and customer experience]. Map our current innovation across all ten Doblin types. For each type, assess whether we have genuine innovation (differentiation that competitors have not replicated), table-stakes performance (meeting industry norms), or a gap (below industry standard). Identify the three types where investment would create the highest strategic return."

**Prompt 2 -- Competitor Innovation Mapping:**
> "Map the innovation profile of our top three competitors: [names and descriptions]. For each competitor, identify which of the ten types they have innovated in. Overlay their profiles to find: (a) types where no competitor has innovated (white space), (b) types where one competitor dominates (their moat), and (c) types where competition is intense (Red Ocean zones). Recommend where we should focus our innovation investment."

**Prompt 3 -- Innovation Combination Design:**
> "Given our current strengths in [list 2-3 innovation types], design a strategy that integrates at least five types of innovation. For each additional type, explain: (1) what the innovation would look like, (2) how it reinforces the types we already do well, and (3) the investment required to achieve it. Prioritise by impact-to-cost ratio."

## Use Cases

**Idea-Stage Founder Designing a New Business**

A founder planning a meal-kit delivery service recognises that product innovation (better recipes, higher-quality ingredients) is the baseline that every competitor offers. Using the Ten Types framework, she designs across six types: Profit Model (flexible subscription with pay-per-box option), Process (AI-driven demand forecasting to minimise food waste), Product Performance (dietary-restriction personalisation), Service (real-time cooking assistance via chat), Channel (partnerships with apartment complexes for refrigerated locker delivery), and Customer Engagement (a community platform where subscribers share modifications and rate meals). The resulting business model is not a better meal kit; it is a fundamentally different customer relationship that competitors cannot replicate by improving their recipes alone.

**Growth-Stage SaaS Company Breaking Through a Competitive Ceiling**

A project management SaaS with $5M ARR competes in a market with dozens of similar products. Feature-by-feature competition has produced parity; no single product capability provides lasting advantage. A Doblin analysis reveals that the company's innovation is concentrated entirely in Type 5 (Product Performance). The strategic response targets three additional types: Network (building an integration marketplace that makes the product a platform), Service (white-glove onboarding for enterprise accounts, converting a commodity product into a premium service experience), and Profit Model (shifting from per-seat pricing to value-based pricing tied to projects completed). Within twelve months, enterprise churn drops by 40% -- not because the product improved, but because the business model around the product became structurally stickier.

**Early-Traction Fintech Navigating a Regulated Market**

A fintech startup offering small-business lending realises that product innovation (better interest rates, faster approval) faces structural limits set by regulatory capital requirements. The ten-type analysis redirects innovation investment toward Structure (a partnership model where the startup provides the technology platform while banking partners provide the regulated lending licence), Process (automated credit assessment using alternative data sources), Channel (embedded lending offered at point-of-sale through e-commerce partnerships), and Brand (positioning as the "anti-bank" that treats small businesses with transparency). This multi-type approach circumvents the regulatory constraints that limit pure product innovation.

## Anti-Patterns

**Product Tunnel Vision.** Pouring all innovation resources into product features while ignoring the other nine types. This is the most common and most costly error, particularly in technology companies where engineering culture equates innovation with code.

**Innovation Type Confusion.** Mislabelling marketing campaigns as "Brand Innovation" or customer support improvements as "Service Innovation." Innovation in the Doblin sense means creating a differentiated position that competitors have not replicated. If every company in your industry offers a similar service level, improving your service is table-stakes investment, not service innovation.

**Checklist Innovation.** Attempting to innovate across all ten types simultaneously. This dilutes resources and produces shallow innovation everywhere rather than deep innovation in the types that matter most. Five types pursued with depth beats ten types pursued superficially.

**Ignoring the Configuration Layer.** Startups frequently focus on Offering and Experience while neglecting Configuration types (Profit Model, Network, Structure, Process). Yet Configuration innovations are often the most defensible because they are invisible to customers and difficult for competitors to observe and replicate.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Design for multiple types from inception | It is far easier to build multi-type innovation into a business from the start than to retrofit it later |
| Validation | Test the innovation hypotheses | Each innovation type involves assumptions; validate the riskiest ones first |
| Early Traction | Double down on the types driving traction | Identify which innovation types customers actually value versus which you assumed they would |
| Growth | Add innovation types to deepen moats | Use competitive pressure as the signal for which new types to develop |

At the idea stage, the Ten Types framework is most powerful as a design tool. Rather than asking "What product should I build?" the founder asks "What combination of innovations would create a business that competitors cannot easily replicate?" This shifts the conversation from features to strategy.

During growth, competitors begin copying visible innovations (product features, channel strategies). This is the moment to invest in less visible types -- Profit Model, Structure, Process -- that create advantages competitors cannot decode from the outside.
