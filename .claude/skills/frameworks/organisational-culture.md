# Organisational Culture

What you see in an organisation -- the open-plan offices, the Friday beers, the branded hoodies -- is the tip of the iceberg. Roughly ten percent of culture is visible. The remaining ninety percent sits submerged: the unspoken beliefs, the unstated power hierarchies, the assumptions so deeply embedded that no one thinks to question them. Edgar Schein, the MIT professor who spent decades dissecting how organisations actually work, argued that most culture-change initiatives fail because they redecorate the tip while ignoring the mass below the waterline. The iceberg metaphor is not merely illustrative; it is diagnostic. Misread which level of culture you are dealing with, and your intervention will achieve nothing -- or worse, provoke the immune response that organisations mount against superficial change.

Schein's framework identifies three distinct layers, each progressively harder to observe and exponentially harder to alter. Understanding these layers is not an academic exercise. For founders, it is the difference between building a company that scales with coherence and one that fractures under the weight of its own contradictions.

---

## The Framework

### Layer 1: Artifacts (The Visible Surface)

Artifacts are everything you can see, hear, and touch when you walk into an organisation. Office layout. Dress code. Meeting rituals. The language people use in Slack channels. The way decisions get announced. These are tangible, immediately observable -- and profoundly misleading if interpreted without context.

A startup with a foosball table and no dress code might appear egalitarian. The reality could be a cult of personality where the CEO's whims override all process. Artifacts are symptoms, not diagnoses.

**Common artifacts include:**
- Physical environment and workspace design
- Published values and mission statements
- Rituals, ceremonies, and recurring events
- Stories and legends told about the company's origins
- Organisational charts and reporting structures
- Technology choices and communication tools

### Layer 2: Espoused Values (What People Say Matters)

One level below artifacts sit the declared beliefs, strategies, and philosophies. These are the values on the website, the principles recited during onboarding, the strategic priorities invoked in board meetings. Espoused values represent what the organisation *claims* to be.

The critical question: do espoused values match actual behaviour? When a company proclaims "we value work-life balance" while promoting only those who answer email at midnight, a gap has opened between Layer 2 and Layer 3. This gap is where cynicism breeds and trust erodes.

**Diagnostic indicators of alignment (or misalignment):**

| Espoused Value | Aligned Behaviour | Misaligned Behaviour |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| "Innovation" | Budget for experiments, tolerance of failure | Punishing failed projects, rigid approval processes |
| "Transparency" | Open financials, candid all-hands | Information hoarding, surprise layoffs |
| "Customer first" | Support team empowered to act | Support metrics optimised for speed over resolution |
| "Meritocracy" | Promotions track results | Promotions track tenure or political skill |

### Layer 3: Basic Underlying Assumptions (The Invisible Bedrock)

The deepest layer consists of unconscious, taken-for-granted beliefs. These assumptions are so ingrained they feel like objective reality rather than cultural choices. They govern how people perceive problems, define truth, relate to time, and think about human nature.

Schein identified several dimensions of basic assumptions:

- **Relationship to environment:** Does the organisation believe it controls its market, or must adapt to it?
- **Nature of truth:** Is truth discovered through debate, authority, or data?
- **Human nature:** Are people fundamentally trustworthy (Theory Y) or requiring supervision (Theory X)?
- **Nature of relationships:** Is hierarchy natural, or should all voices carry equal weight?
- **Time orientation:** Does the culture privilege the past (tradition), present (agility), or future (vision)?

These assumptions are rarely articulated. They surface only when violated -- when a new hire from a different culture does something that provokes an emotional reaction disproportionate to the action itself. That disproportionate reaction is the clearest signal that a basic assumption has been touched.

### How the Layers Interact

Change flows downward with great difficulty. A founder can redesign the office (Layer 1) overnight. Rewriting the values statement (Layer 2) takes a quarterly offsite. Shifting basic assumptions (Layer 3) requires years of consistent leadership behaviour, critical incidents handled visibly, and patient reinforcement.

Conversely, diagnosis flows upward. To understand Layer 3, observe the patterns in Layer 1 and the contradictions between Layer 1 and Layer 2. The gap between artifacts and espoused values is the most reliable indicator of what the organisation actually believes.

**Prompt 1 -- Culture Audit:**
> Conduct a three-level culture audit for my company using Schein's framework. At the Artifacts level, I will describe our office setup, communication norms, meeting practices, and visible rituals. At the Espoused Values level, analyse our stated mission, values, and strategic principles. Then help me identify the Basic Underlying Assumptions by probing for gaps between what we say and what we do. Company context: [describe company stage, size, industry, and any recent cultural tensions].

**Prompt 2 -- Misalignment Diagnosis:**
> I suspect a gap between our espoused values and our actual organisational behaviour. Our stated values include [list values]. Recent incidents suggesting misalignment include [describe incidents]. Using Schein's model, help me diagnose which basic underlying assumptions are driving the contradictory behaviour, and propose interventions at each of the three levels.

**Prompt 3 -- Culture Integration for M&A:**
> We are acquiring [describe target company]. Using Schein's three levels of organisational culture, help me map the cultural differences between our two organisations. For each level (artifacts, espoused values, basic assumptions), identify potential collision points and recommend an integration strategy that addresses each layer explicitly.

**Prompt 4 -- Founder Culture Imprint:**
> As a founder at [stage], I recognise that my personal behaviours are imprinting onto the company culture. Using Schein's framework, help me audit which of my assumptions, habits, and decisions are becoming embedded as basic underlying assumptions. Identify which imprints are serving the company well and which may become liabilities at scale.

## Use Cases

**Series A SaaS Company Experiencing Growing Pains**
A 45-person B2B SaaS startup has doubled headcount in eight months. The founders notice that new hires "don't get the culture" -- they schedule unnecessary meetings, escalate decisions that should be made autonomously, and default to formal communication. A Schein analysis reveals that the company's basic assumption -- that competent adults make decisions without permission -- was never articulated; it was transmitted osmotically when the team was fifteen people. The fix operates at all three layers: codify the assumption into explicit values (Layer 2), redesign onboarding rituals to demonstrate autonomous decision-making (Layer 1), and have leadership visibly reinforce the assumption by publicly celebrating independent judgment (Layer 3 maintenance).

**Growth-Stage Fintech After a Compliance Crisis**
A payments company with 200 employees suffers a regulatory penalty. Leadership declares a "compliance-first culture" and introduces new processes, reporting lines, and training (Layer 1 and Layer 2 changes). Six months later, the same risk-taking behaviours persist. Schein's framework reveals that the basic assumption -- "move fast, regulators are slow" -- was forged during the company's scrappy early days and reinforced by the promotion of individuals who embodied it. Surface-level changes cannot override Layer 3 assumptions. The intervention requires replacing key role models, restructuring incentives, and having the CEO publicly acknowledge past assumptions that no longer serve the company.

**Pre-IPO Enterprise Company Merging Two Engineering Cultures**
Following an acquisition, two engineering teams with fundamentally different assumptions about quality (one ships fast and iterates; the other ships only when perfect) must integrate. Artifacts conflict immediately: different code review practices, different deployment cadences, different definitions of "done." Mapping each team's three layers reveals that the conflict is not about process -- it is about basic assumptions regarding the nature of professional excellence. Resolution requires surfacing both assumptions explicitly, negotiating a shared assumption appropriate to the combined entity, and rebuilding processes and rituals from that new foundation.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Redecorating the Iceberg Tip.** Installing ping-pong tables, rewriting the mission statement, or launching a "culture committee" without addressing underlying assumptions. These initiatives generate cynicism when employees observe that actual behaviour remains unchanged. Artifacts-only interventions are the most common and most futile form of culture work.

2. **Assuming Culture Is What the Founder Says It Is.** Founders often conflate their *intended* culture with the *actual* culture. Schein's framework demands empirical observation, not aspiration. The culture is what people do when no one is watching, not what the deck says.

3. **Treating Culture as Monolithic.** Large organisations contain subcultures -- engineering, sales, operations -- each with distinct assumptions. Applying a single cultural analysis to the entire company produces a fiction. Map subcultures independently, then identify where they align and where they collide.

4. **Skipping Layer 3 in Due Diligence.** M&A due diligence routinely examines financials, technology, and talent. It almost never examines basic assumptions. The result: post-merger integration failures that no amount of process harmonisation can fix, because the two organisations hold incompatible beliefs about how work should be done.

5. **Expecting Speed.** Layer 3 assumptions take years to form and years to change. Leaders who announce a "culture transformation" with a six-month timeline are confusing artifacts with assumptions. Genuine culture change is measured in leadership generations, not quarters.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Early Traction | Founder imprint audit | Culture is being formed now, largely from the founder's unconscious assumptions. This is the highest-leverage moment to be intentional. |
| Growth | Articulation and codification | As headcount exceeds Dunbar-adjacent numbers (~50-150), osmotic culture transmission fails. Assumptions must become explicit values, and values must become visible artifacts. |
| Scale | Subculture management | Multiple teams, geographies, and functions develop distinct subcultures. The task shifts from building culture to managing cultural coherence across diverse groups. |

At early traction, the primary risk is accidental culture -- the founder's unexamined habits becoming organisational doctrine. At growth, the risk is dilution -- rapid hiring overwhelming the cultural carriers. At scale, the risk is fragmentation -- subcultures diverging until the organisation loses coherence.
