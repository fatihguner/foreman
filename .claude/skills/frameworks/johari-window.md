# Johari Window

What do the people you work with most closely know about you that you do not know about yourself? The question is uncomfortable precisely because the answer, by definition, is invisible to the person it most concerns. Joseph Luft and Harrington Ingham -- whose first names combine to form "Johari" -- devised their four-pane window model in 1955 at the University of California, Los Angeles, as a tool for understanding self-awareness in group settings. Six decades later, it remains one of the most intuitively powerful frameworks for diagnosing trust, communication, and relational effectiveness in teams. Its enduring utility lies in a simple geometric truth: the less a team knows about each other's thinking, motivations, and concerns, the more organisational energy is wasted on misinterpretation, defensive posturing, and avoidable conflict.

---

## The Framework

The Johari Window divides information about a person into four quadrants based on two axes: what is known to the self and what is known to others.

### The Four Quadrants

```
                    Known to Self          Unknown to Self
                ┌─────────────────────┬─────────────────────┐
                │                     │                     │
  Known to      │      OPEN           │      BLIND SPOT     │
  Others        │      (Arena)        │                     │
                │                     │                     │
                ├─────────────────────┼─────────────────────┤
                │                     │                     │
  Unknown to    │      HIDDEN         │      UNKNOWN        │
  Others        │      (Facade)       │                     │
                │                     │                     │
                └─────────────────────┴─────────────────────┘
```

**Open (Arena)** -- Information known to both self and others. This is the quadrant of effective communication: shared understanding, transparent motivations, acknowledged strengths and weaknesses. The larger this quadrant, the more efficiently the relationship functions. Communication requires less guesswork, trust is higher, and collaboration is more fluid.

**Blind Spot** -- Information known to others but not to self. These are the behaviours, habits, communication patterns, and impacts that others observe but the individual does not recognise. A founder who believes she is collaborative but whose team experiences her as directive has a blind spot. The gap between self-perception and others' perception is the territory of this quadrant.

**Hidden (Facade)** -- Information known to self but not shared with others. Fears, insecurities, strategic calculations, past experiences, personal motivations -- the things a person knows about themselves but chooses (consciously or unconsciously) to withhold. A founder who is privately worried about running out of capital but presents confidence to the team is operating with a large Hidden quadrant.

**Unknown** -- Information unknown to both self and others. Latent talents, unconscious motivations, repressed experiences, undiscovered potential. This quadrant shrinks through novel experiences, deep reflection, coaching, and sometimes crisis -- which has a way of revealing capabilities and vulnerabilities that normal conditions do not surface.

### The Two Mechanisms of Change

The Johari Window is dynamic. The quadrant sizes shift through two deliberate actions:

**Self-disclosure** moves the boundary between Hidden and Open. When a person shares information about themselves -- their concerns, motivations, uncertainties, values -- the Open quadrant expands downward at the expense of the Hidden quadrant. For entrepreneurs, strategic self-disclosure builds trust: an investor who understands the founder's genuine concerns makes better-informed decisions than one who only sees the facade.

**Feedback-seeking** moves the boundary between Blind Spot and Open. When a person actively solicits and receives honest feedback, the Open quadrant expands rightward at the expense of the Blind Spot. For founders, feedback is the only mechanism for closing the gap between how they perceive their leadership and how it is actually experienced.

The interaction between these two mechanisms is multiplicative. Self-disclosure invites reciprocal disclosure. Feedback-seeking, when received non-defensively, encourages others to give more feedback. The result is a virtuous cycle that progressively enlarges the Open quadrant -- which is the quadrant where productive work happens.

### The Window at Team Level

While originally designed for interpersonal relationships, the Johari Window applies to teams and organisations. An organisation's "Open" quadrant is its shared understanding: strategy, values, challenges, and information that everyone has access to. Its "Hidden" quadrant is what leadership knows but does not share (strategic plans, financial concerns, personnel issues). Its "Blind Spot" is what employees, customers, or the market perceive but leadership does not recognise. Its "Unknown" quadrant is the unexamined potential and latent risks that no one has yet identified.

Startups tend to have large Open quadrants in their earliest days (when everyone is in one room and information flows freely) and progressively smaller ones as they grow -- unless deliberate mechanisms are built to counteract the natural narrowing.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Personal Johari Audit:**
> Help me conduct a Johari Window self-assessment based on the following inputs. My self-perception: [describe how you see your leadership style, strengths, weaknesses, and impact on others]. Feedback I have received from others: [describe feedback -- formal and informal -- from co-founders, team, investors, mentors]. Identify likely blind spots (gaps between self-perception and feedback), hidden areas (things I know about myself that I am not sharing with my team), and hypothesise about potential unknowns. Recommend specific actions to expand my Open quadrant.

**Prompt 2 -- Team Johari Mapping:**
> Map the Johari Window dynamics of my team based on these observations: [describe each team member's communication openness, receptivity to feedback, tendency to withhold information, and known blind spots]. For the team as a whole, assess the relative size of each quadrant. Identify the highest-leverage intervention: should we focus on increasing self-disclosure (team retreats, structured sharing), feedback (360 reviews, retrospectives), or both? Design a specific exercise we can run in our next team meeting to expand the Open quadrant.

**Prompt 3 -- Investor Relationship Disclosure Strategy:**
> I am managing relationships with [number] investors. Help me design a strategic self-disclosure approach using the Johari framework. Here is what I currently share openly: [list]. Here is what I withhold: [list concerns, challenges, uncertainties you do not share]. For each hidden item, advise whether disclosure would build trust (move to Open) or is appropriately private (remain Hidden). For items I should disclose, recommend how and when to share them for maximum trust-building effect.

## Use Cases

**Early-traction co-founder relationship under strain**

Two co-founders have been working together for 18 months. Surface communication is professional, but both harbour unspoken frustrations. The CEO resents the CTO's resistance to customer feedback (hidden); the CTO feels undervalued because the CEO takes credit in investor meetings (hidden). Neither has shared these concerns. Meanwhile, the CEO has a blind spot: she micromanages technical decisions without realising it. The CTO has a blind spot: his communication style reads as dismissive to non-technical team members.

A facilitated Johari exercise maps the current state: both co-founders have large Hidden and Blind Spot quadrants with each other, despite working together daily. The intervention involves structured self-disclosure (each co-founder shares their three biggest unspoken concerns) followed by structured feedback (each shares one blind spot they have observed in the other). The conversation is uncomfortable but transformative. The CEO acknowledges her micromanagement and commits to defined decision boundaries. The CTO acknowledges his communication style and agrees to a monthly feedback check-in with the team. Eighteen months of accumulated friction dissolves in two hours of structured honesty.

**Growth-stage company with an information silo problem**

A 60-person company has evolved departmental silos where marketing, engineering, and sales each have large Hidden quadrants vis-a-vis the others. Engineering does not share technical constraints until they become crises. Marketing does not share campaign performance data that might reflect poorly on their work. Sales does not share customer complaints that contradict the company narrative. The CEO, aware that something is wrong but unable to pinpoint it, implements a Johari-informed transparency programme: cross-functional standups where each department shares one success, one challenge, and one concern from the week. The format is deliberately structured to normalise disclosure. Within a quarter, the inter-departmental Open quadrant expands measurably, reflected in fewer surprise escalations, faster cross-functional projects, and improved employee satisfaction scores.

**Scale-stage executive discovering personal blind spots through 360 review**

A CEO at a 300-person company commissions a 360-degree feedback review and discovers a jarring blind spot: while she perceives herself as approachable and open to dissent, 70% of direct reports describe her as "intimidating" and report self-censoring in her presence. The CEO's Johari Blind Spot quadrant is large. The corrective is not a single action but a sustained practice: the CEO begins each leadership meeting by explicitly inviting disagreement, publicly changes her position when persuaded, and asks a trusted lieutenant to provide real-time feedback on her nonverbal cues during discussions. Over six months, the 360 scores shift significantly -- not because the CEO changed her personality but because deliberate feedback-seeking and behavioural adjustment expanded her Open quadrant.

## Anti-Patterns

**1. Forced disclosure in a low-trust environment.** Running a Johari exercise before establishing baseline psychological safety produces performative sharing at best and genuine harm at worst. People share what they believe is safe to share, which in a low-trust environment means nothing meaningful -- or, worse, information that is later used against them. Psychological safety is a prerequisite, not a byproduct, of Johari work.

**2. Treating all feedback as equally valid.** The Blind Spot quadrant contains genuine blind spots, but it also contains misperceptions. Not all feedback is accurate. A founder who accepts every piece of feedback uncritically risks distorting their self-model based on others' projections, biases, or limited perspectives. The skill lies in triangulating: feedback that appears from multiple independent sources is more likely to reflect a genuine blind spot.

**3. Asymmetric disclosure.** When leaders demand transparency from their teams without reciprocating, the exercise becomes extractive rather than relational. The Johari Window works through mutual expansion of the Open quadrant. A CEO who asks the team to share concerns while keeping their own concerns hidden creates a surveillance dynamic, not a trust dynamic.

**4. Confusing self-disclosure with oversharing.** Strategic self-disclosure is purposeful: sharing information that helps others work with you more effectively. Oversharing -- unburdening personal anxieties, sharing irrelevant personal details, or processing emotions publicly without regard for the audience -- erodes professional trust rather than building it. The question to ask before disclosing: "Will sharing this help the other person understand me better in a way that improves our working relationship?"

**5. One-time exercise rather than ongoing practice.** A single Johari workshop produces a snapshot. The window's quadrants shift constantly as circumstances change, new people join, and new challenges emerge. Effective application requires regular, lightweight practices (weekly check-ins, quarterly feedback cycles) rather than annual events.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Early Traction | Co-founder transparency, founding team trust | Open quadrant is naturally large due to proximity; the priority is preventing Hidden and Blind Spot growth as the team begins to specialise |
| Growth | Cross-functional transparency, management feedback loops | Information silos form naturally as the organisation departmentalises; deliberate Johari practices counteract this tendency |
| Scale | Executive blind spots, organisational transparency, culture maintenance | The distance between leadership and frontline creates structural blind spots; formal mechanisms (360 reviews, skip-levels, anonymous channels) become essential |

At early traction, the Johari Window is naturally favourable -- small teams in close quarters share information freely, feedback is constant, and little is hidden. The risk at this stage is complacency: assuming that transparency will persist without effort. As the team grows and specialises, Hidden quadrants expand (people withhold concerns to avoid rocking the boat) and Blind Spots calcify (the founder receives less honest feedback as power dynamics solidify).

At growth, the architectural challenge emerges. Departments form, and with them information boundaries. Cross-functional Johari practices -- structured information-sharing rituals that counteract silo formation -- become a deliberate organisational investment. The founder must also reckon with their own growing Blind Spot: as the company grows, fewer people feel comfortable giving the founder candid feedback.

At scale, the Johari Window becomes a governance tool. Executive blind spots at this stage can be catastrophic (recall any corporate scandal where "leadership didn't know" about a systemic problem that was visible to everyone else). Formal feedback mechanisms, anonymous reporting channels, and external advisory relationships are the structural equivalents of feedback-seeking in the original two-person model.
