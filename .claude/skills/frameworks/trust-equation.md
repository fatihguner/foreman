# The Trust Equation

```
         Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy
Trust = ──────────────────────────────────────
                  Self-Orientation
```

Four variables. One fraction. The most elegant decomposition of trust in the management literature. David Maister, Charles Green, and Robert Galford published this formula in *The Trusted Advisor* (2000), and its power lies in making the abstract concrete and the intuitive actionable. Trust is not a mystical quality some people possess and others lack. It is a function of four measurable, improvable variables -- three in the numerator, one in the denominator. Increase any numerator variable and trust rises. Increase the denominator and trust collapses, regardless of how strong the numerator is. The formula explains why brilliant consultants lose clients, why technically competent founders alienate investors, and why likeable managers fail to inspire confidence. It also prescribes, with unusual precision, exactly what to fix.

---

## The Framework

### The Numerator: Three Trust Builders

#### Credibility: "I Can Trust What They Say"

Credibility is the confidence that the other party knows what they are talking about. It operates on two dimensions: expertise (actual knowledge and skill) and communication of expertise (the ability to convey competence without arrogance).

**Credibility markers:**
- Demonstrated domain knowledge
- Relevant credentials and track record
- Accuracy of prior statements and predictions
- Ability to say "I don't know" when appropriate -- paradoxically, admitting ignorance on peripheral topics increases perceived credibility on core topics
- Third-party validation (endorsements, publications, case studies)

**How founders build credibility:**
- Publish original analysis of their market
- Reference specific data points and primary research in pitches
- Acknowledge the limits of their knowledge ("our hypothesis is X; we are testing it by doing Y")
- Build a track record of accurate forecasts in investor updates

**How founders destroy credibility:**
- Overstating traction or market size
- Claiming expertise in domains where they lack it
- Failing to update stakeholders when prior predictions prove wrong
- Using jargon as a substitute for understanding

#### Reliability: "I Can Trust What They Do"

Reliability is the confidence that the other party will do what they say they will do. While credibility is about words, reliability is about actions repeated over time. It is the trust variable most dependent on consistency.

**Reliability markers:**
- Promises kept -- especially small ones
- Deadlines met
- Follow-through on commitments made in meetings
- Predictable behaviour in varied circumstances
- Transparency about what can and cannot be delivered

**How founders build reliability:**
- Under-promise and over-deliver systematically
- Send investor updates on the same day every month without exception
- Respond to communications within a predictable window
- Track and honour every commitment, no matter how minor ("I'll send you that article" matters)

**How founders destroy reliability:**
- Missing self-imposed deadlines
- Changing direction frequently without explanation
- Making commitments in the enthusiasm of a meeting and failing to follow through
- Inconsistency between what is said in different contexts (telling the board one story and the team another)

#### Intimacy: "I Can Trust Them With My Concerns"

Intimacy is the confidence that sharing sensitive information -- concerns, fears, failures, uncertainties -- is safe. This is the most emotionally complex trust variable and the one most often neglected in professional contexts.

**Intimacy markers:**
- Discretion with confidential information
- Emotional safety -- the other party does not judge, mock, or weaponise vulnerability
- Willingness to engage with feelings, not just facts
- Reciprocal vulnerability ("I'm worried about this too")
- Non-transactional concern for the other person's wellbeing

**How founders build intimacy:**
- Share appropriate personal vulnerabilities ("this keeps me up at night")
- Honour confidences absolutely -- never use information shared in trust as leverage
- Acknowledge the emotional dimension of business decisions
- Remember and reference personal details about stakeholders
- Create psychological safety in team settings

**How founders destroy intimacy:**
- Sharing confidential information with third parties
- Responding to vulnerability with judgment or dismissal
- Maintaining a purely transactional demeanour
- Using personal information strategically

### The Denominator: The Trust Destroyer

#### Self-Orientation: "Are They in This for Themselves?"

Self-orientation is the degree to which the other party appears focused on their own interests rather than yours. It is the denominator -- the single variable with the power to nullify an otherwise strong trust position. A person can be highly credible, perfectly reliable, and genuinely intimate, but if they appear self-serving, trust evaporates.

**Self-orientation signals:**
- Talking more than listening
- Redirecting conversations to their own interests
- Offering solutions before fully understanding the problem
- Name-dropping and status-signalling
- Visibly calculating "what's in it for me" in every interaction
- Rushing to close rather than ensuring genuine fit

**How founders reduce self-orientation:**
- Listen before prescribing. In investor meetings, ask about the investor's thesis and portfolio before pitching.
- In sales conversations, spend the first third understanding the customer's situation before mentioning the product
- Refer prospects to competitors when the fit is genuinely poor
- Give useful advice and introductions with no expectation of reciprocation
- In team settings, credit others and share recognition generously

**The denominator trap for founders:** Entrepreneurship selects for high self-orientation. Founders must be driven, ambitious, and focused on their company's success -- traits that can easily read as self-serving to investors, customers, and employees. The discipline is not reducing ambition but ensuring that interactions demonstrate genuine concern for the other party's interests alongside one's own.

### The Mathematics of Trust

The formula's structure reveals several non-obvious insights:

1. **Self-orientation has disproportionate impact.** Because it is in the denominator, doubling self-orientation halves trust, regardless of numerator strength. Conversely, halving self-orientation doubles trust. No numerator improvement can compensate for high self-orientation.

2. **Credibility alone is insufficient.** The brilliant but unreliable founder (high credibility, low reliability) is a common archetype -- visionary but untrustworthy. Investors learn to discount vision when execution track record is poor.

3. **Reliability alone is insufficient.** The dependable but uninspiring operator (high reliability, low credibility, low intimacy) earns respect but not trust. People follow them because they deliver, not because they believe.

4. **Intimacy is the most undervalued variable.** In professional contexts, intimacy is often dismissed as "soft." Yet it explains why people trust their accountant of twenty years over a more credentialed stranger, and why customers stick with a slightly inferior product sold by a vendor who understands their real concerns.

5. **Trust is situation-specific.** A person may be highly trusted in one domain (technical decisions) and not in another (hiring). The equation should be applied per-context, not as a global score.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Trust Diagnostic:**
> Using the Trust Equation (T = (C + R + I) / S), assess my trustworthiness with a specific stakeholder. The stakeholder is [describe -- investor, cofounder, key customer, board member]. Rate myself on each variable (Credibility, Reliability, Intimacy, Self-Orientation) with evidence, not aspiration. Identify which variable is my weakest and design three specific actions to improve it over the next 30 days.

**Prompt 2 -- Trust Repair:**
> I have lost trust with [describe the person and context]. The triggering event was [describe]. Using the Trust Equation, diagnose which variable(s) were damaged. Was it a credibility failure (I said something wrong), a reliability failure (I didn't do what I said), an intimacy failure (I betrayed a confidence or dismissed their concerns), or a self-orientation failure (I appeared to prioritise myself)? Then design a trust repair strategy targeting the specific variable(s) affected.

**Prompt 3 -- Investor Trust Strategy:**
> I am preparing for a fundraising round. Using the Trust Equation, design a comprehensive trust-building strategy for investor relationships. For each variable (Credibility, Reliability, Intimacy, Self-Orientation), identify specific actions I should take before, during, and after investor meetings. My company: [describe stage, traction, prior fundraising history].

**Prompt 4 -- Team Trust Audit:**
> I manage a team of [size]. Using the Trust Equation, assess the current trust level within the team -- both trust in me as a leader and trust between team members. For each variable, identify observable indicators and potential improvement areas. Design a 90-day plan to increase team trust, with specific actions mapped to each variable.

**Prompt 5 -- Customer Trust Analysis:**
> Analyse why we are losing customers to [competitor] despite having a superior product. Apply the Trust Equation to our customer relationships versus the competitor's. Rate both on Credibility, Reliability, Intimacy, and Self-Orientation from the customer's perspective. Identify which trust variable(s) the competitor outperforms us on and recommend specific changes to our customer success approach.

## Use Cases

**Idea-Stage Founder Seeking Pre-Seed Investment**
A first-time founder with no track record pitches angel investors and receives polite passes. Trust Equation diagnosis: Credibility is low (no prior exits, limited domain expertise), Reliability is unproven (no track record of delivering), Intimacy is minimal (investors do not know them), and Self-Orientation appears high (pitch is entirely about the founder's vision, not the investor's thesis). The remediation strategy targets each variable: build credibility through a published market analysis and customer interviews (demonstrating expertise); establish reliability by committing to monthly updates to interested angels even before an investment; develop intimacy by asking investors about their portfolio challenges and offering introductions; and reduce self-orientation by explicitly addressing how the investment fits the investor's thesis, not just the founder's ambition. Second fundraising attempt succeeds.

**Growth-Stage Company with High Churn Despite Strong Product**
A B2B SaaS company with best-in-class technology loses 25% of customers annually. Trust Equation analysis from customer interviews reveals: Credibility is high (customers trust the product), Reliability is low (support response times are inconsistent, and promised features are frequently delayed), Intimacy is absent (customers feel like ticket numbers, not partners), and Self-Orientation is moderate (upsell pressure in every interaction). The intervention focuses on Reliability (SLAs with consequences for missed response times, public roadmap with delivery dates) and Intimacy (dedicated success managers, quarterly business reviews, proactive check-ins not tied to renewals). Churn drops to 12% in two quarters.

**Scale-Stage CEO Navigating a Board Crisis**
A CEO learns that two board members are discussing their replacement after a missed quarterly target. Trust Equation diagnosis reveals the critical variable is Self-Orientation: the CEO has been perceived as optimising for their personal reputation (announcing ambitious targets publicly) rather than for honest company stewardship. Reliability is also damaged (the missed target follows two prior misses). The repair strategy: a board letter acknowledging the pattern, reforecasting conservatively, and committing to a reporting format that highlights risks alongside opportunities. The CEO explicitly addresses their own accountability, reducing perceived self-orientation. Within two board cycles, the replacement discussion dissipates.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Optimising Only the Numerator.** Investing in credibility (more credentials, better pitches), reliability (better processes), and intimacy (more networking) while ignoring self-orientation. The denominator can single-handedly destroy trust. A founder who is brilliant, reliable, and personable but clearly in it only for themselves will not be trusted.

2. **Confusing Reliability with Responsiveness.** Responding to every message within five minutes is not reliability; it is reactivity. Reliability means doing what you said you would do, when you said you would do it. An immediate response that fails to follow through is worse than a delayed response that delivers.

3. **Performing Intimacy.** Sharing vulnerability strategically -- "I'll tell them about my struggles to seem human" -- is detectable and counterproductive. Performative vulnerability increases perceived self-orientation ("they're using vulnerability as a tactic"), which is the opposite of the intended effect.

4. **Assuming Trust Transfers Across Domains.** A founder trusted for technical decisions assumes they are equally trusted for commercial ones. Trust is context-specific. The equation must be evaluated per-domain and per-stakeholder, not as a universal score.

5. **Ignoring the Time Dimension.** Trust is built incrementally and destroyed instantly. A single reliability failure can undo months of consistent delivery. The equation captures a point-in-time assessment; the trajectory matters more than any single snapshot.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Credibility building from zero | With no track record, founders must build credibility through domain expertise, quality of thinking, and borrowed credibility (advisors, prior employers). Self-orientation must be visibly low -- investors are pattern-matching for coachability. |
| Validation | Reliability establishment | Validation is the trust-building phase: every commitment met, every update sent on time, every promise honoured. This is when the founder's reliability track record begins, and early impressions compound. |
| Early Traction | Intimacy with early customers | Early customers are taking a risk on an unproven company. Intimacy -- making them feel heard, safe, and valued -- determines whether they become advocates or cautionary tales. |
| Growth | Scaling trust across relationships | At growth, the founder cannot personally maintain trust with every stakeholder. Trust must be embedded in organisational practices: reliable processes, credible teams, customer intimacy at scale. |
| Scale | Institutional trust | At scale, trust becomes a brand attribute. The trust equation applies to the organisation, not just the founder: corporate credibility, operational reliability, customer intimacy, and perceived self-orientation (profit motive vs. customer mission). |
