# GROW Model

A gardener does not grow a plant. A gardener creates the conditions -- soil, light, water, space -- in which the plant grows itself. John Whitmore, the British racing driver turned executive coach, built the GROW model on precisely this philosophy of human development. Published in his 1992 book *Coaching for Performance*, GROW offers a deceptively simple four-stage questioning framework that has become the most widely used coaching structure in the world. Its power lies not in telling people what to do but in asking them questions that unlock their own clarity. For entrepreneurs, who chronically suffer from both advice overload and decision paralysis, GROW provides a structured pathway from muddled intention to committed action -- and, equally valuable, a repeatable method for coaching the people they lead.

---

## The Framework

GROW is an acronym representing four sequential stages of a coaching conversation. Each stage has a distinct purpose and a characteristic set of questions. The coach (or self-coaching entrepreneur) moves through the stages in order, though real conversations often loop back as new insights emerge.

### The Four Stages

**G -- Goal**

Establish what the person wants to achieve. Not vaguely ("I want to grow the business") but with specificity sufficient to recognise achievement. The goal stage separates wishful thinking from actionable intention.

Essential questions:
- What do you want to achieve?
- What would make this conversation most valuable for you?
- What does success look like? How would you know you had achieved it?
- What is the timeframe?
- How ambitious is this goal on a scale of 1-10?
- Is this a performance goal (within your control) or an end goal (dependent on external factors)?

The distinction between performance goals and end goals is critical for entrepreneurs. "Raise a Series A" is an end goal -- it depends on investor decisions outside the founder's control. "Complete 30 investor conversations and iterate the pitch to a 50% follow-up rate" is a performance goal -- fully within the founder's control and far more useful for coaching.

**R -- Reality**

Explore the current situation with unflinching honesty. Most people skip this stage, jumping from goal to action without understanding their starting position. The reality stage grounds ambition in fact.

Essential questions:
- What is happening now? What is the current state?
- What have you tried so far? What happened?
- What obstacles are you facing?
- Who else is involved, and what is their perspective?
- On a scale of 1-10, where are you currently relative to your goal?
- What is the gap between where you are and where you want to be?
- What resources do you currently have?
- What internal factors (skills, beliefs, habits) are helping or hindering?

The most valuable reality questions are those that surface uncomfortable truths the person has been avoiding. A skilled coach listens for omissions, euphemisms, and assumptions that have not been tested.

**O -- Options**

Generate the widest possible range of options before evaluating any of them. The critical discipline here is separating ideation from judgment. Most people generate two or three options and immediately begin debating their merits. The options stage insists on quantity first, quality second.

Essential questions:
- What could you do? (Repeated multiple times to push past obvious answers)
- What else? (The most powerful coaching question -- it signals that the first answers are rarely the best)
- If you had no constraints, what would you do?
- What would you do if you knew you could not fail?
- What would someone you admire do in this situation?
- What would happen if you did nothing?
- What are the costs and benefits of each option?
- Which option excites you most?

The "What else?" question deserves emphasis. Research on brainstorming shows that the first ideas generated are typically the most conventional. Genuine insight tends to emerge after the obvious options have been exhausted -- usually around the fifth or sixth option.

**W -- Will (or Way Forward)**

Convert the preferred option into a specific, committed action plan. This stage transforms insight into behaviour. Without it, coaching conversations produce understanding but not change.

Essential questions:
- What will you do? (Not "what could you do" -- the shift to "will" is deliberate)
- When will you do it?
- What is the first step?
- On a scale of 1-10, how committed are you to this action?
- What might get in the way?
- How will you overcome those obstacles?
- Who needs to know about your plan?
- How will you hold yourself accountable?
- What support do you need?

If commitment scores below 7, the action plan needs revision. Either the action is wrong, the timeline is unrealistic, or the person is not genuinely ready. Whitmore emphasises that low commitment is information, not failure -- it sends the conversation back to earlier stages.

### GROW as Self-Coaching

GROW is commonly presented as a tool for coaching others, but its highest-frequency use for entrepreneurs is self-coaching. The founder sits with a journal or an AI assistant and works through the four stages on a specific decision. The structure prevents the two most common failure modes of solo decision-making: rumination without progress (endlessly revisiting the problem without generating options) and premature action (jumping to solutions without understanding the reality or goal clearly).

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Full GROW Session:**
> Run a complete GROW coaching session with me on this challenge: [describe challenge]. Start by helping me clarify my Goal with specific, measurable terms. Then explore my current Reality by asking probing questions about what I have tried, what I know, and what I might be avoiding. Generate at least 7 Options, pushing past the obvious ones. Finally, help me select and commit to specific actions with dates, accountability mechanisms, and contingency plans. Push back if my commitment is below 7/10 on any action.

**Prompt 2 -- Reality Check:**
> I believe the current state of [aspect of my business] is: [describe your understanding]. Challenge my reality assessment using GROW Reality questions. Identify assumptions I may be making, perspectives I may be missing, and uncomfortable truths I may be avoiding. Rate my reality assessment on a 1-10 scale for completeness and honesty, and tell me what a 10 would include that my current assessment does not.

**Prompt 3 -- Options Expansion:**
> I am stuck between these options for [decision]: [list 2-3 options you are considering]. Using the GROW Options methodology, generate at least 5 additional options I have not considered. Include at least one option that is radically different from my current thinking, one that involves doing nothing, and one that reframes the problem entirely. For each option, provide a one-sentence summary of its primary advantage and primary risk.

**Prompt 4 -- Coaching Preparation:**
> I need to have a coaching conversation with [team member name/role] about [issue]. Design a GROW-structured conversation plan with specific questions for each stage, tailored to what I know about this person: [describe their situation, personality, likely resistance points]. Include suggested responses for likely pushback and a plan for what to do if commitment remains low after the Will stage.

**Prompt 5 -- Weekly GROW Review:**
> Review my progress on last week's GROW commitments: [list what you committed to and what actually happened]. For any actions not completed, diagnose whether the barrier was in the Goal (wrong target), Reality (unexpected obstacle), Options (wrong approach), or Will (insufficient commitment). Adjust the plan accordingly and set this week's GROW commitments.

## Use Cases

**Idea-stage founder choosing between two venture concepts**

A would-be founder is paralysed between two startup ideas and has spent four months "thinking about it" without making progress on either. A GROW self-coaching session reveals that the stated goal ("decide which idea to pursue") is actually a performance goal disguised as a binary choice. The reality exploration uncovers that the founder's paralysis stems from fear of choosing wrong, not from insufficient information. The options stage generates a third path neither idea had suggested: running a two-week validation sprint on each idea simultaneously, using pre-defined criteria to compare results. The will stage produces a specific calendar with blocked time, measurable milestones, and a hard decision date. The founder launches both sprints and, within two weeks, has enough data to commit with confidence.

**Growth-stage CTO coaching a struggling engineering manager**

A CTO notices that a recently promoted engineering manager is overwhelmed and reverting to individual contributor behaviour -- writing code instead of managing the team. Rather than giving directive advice ("Stop coding and start managing"), the CTO uses GROW. The goal stage reveals the manager wants to "feel effective again" -- which surfaces the real issue (identity transition, not skill deficit). The reality stage uncovers that the manager has never received management training and is using coding as a psychological safety blanket. The options stage generates possibilities ranging from formal training to peer mentoring to a temporary reduction in direct reports. The will stage produces a commitment to complete a management fundamentals course within 60 days and to schedule weekly one-on-ones with each direct report starting immediately. Crucially, the manager arrived at these actions herself -- the CTO asked questions rather than prescribed solutions.

**Scale-stage CEO self-coaching on a potential acquisition**

A CEO is considering whether to acquire a smaller competitor. Using GROW with an AI assistant, the goal stage clarifies that the acquisition's purpose is customer base expansion, not technology acquisition -- a distinction that had been blurred in board discussions. The reality stage reveals that the company's integration track record is poor (two of three past integrations underperformed projections). The options stage generates eight alternatives to acquisition, including partnership, licensing, and organic expansion into the competitor's market segment. The will stage produces a commitment to complete a formal integration readiness assessment before bringing the acquisition proposal to the board, with a hard deadline of six weeks.

## Anti-Patterns

**1. Rushing to the Will stage.** The most common misapplication of GROW is treating it as a goal-setting tool rather than an exploratory process. Coaches (and self-coaching entrepreneurs) who sprint through Goal and Reality to reach Will produce superficially actionable but poorly grounded plans. The value of GROW is proportional to the time spent in Reality and Options.

**2. Asking leading questions disguised as coaching.** "Don't you think you should fire that underperformer?" is not a GROW question -- it is advice with a question mark attached. Genuine GROW coaching requires the coach to suspend their own opinions and trust the process. For founders, who are accustomed to having strong views and sharing them, this discipline is genuinely difficult.

**3. Using GROW for problems that require direction, not coaching.** If a building is on fire, coaching someone through options for extinguishing it is inappropriate. Some situations require direct instruction: compliance issues, safety concerns, ethical violations, and time-critical emergencies. GROW works best when the person being coached has the latent capability to find their own answer and the time to do so.

**4. Treating the model as rigidly sequential.** Real conversations are not linear. A person exploring reality may discover that their goal needs revision. An option may surface a reality they had overlooked. Skilled GROW practitioners move fluidly between stages while maintaining overall forward momentum. Rigid adherence to the sequence produces stilted, unnatural conversations.

**5. Accepting vague goals and weak commitments.** "I want to improve my leadership" is not a GROW-ready goal. "I want my direct reports to rate my feedback quality at 8/10 or above in the next quarterly survey" is. Similarly, accepting "I'll try to do that next week" as a will statement guarantees nothing will happen. The discipline of GROW lies in its insistence on precision.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Founder self-coaching on direction and commitment | GROW helps convert vague entrepreneurial impulse into specific, testable hypotheses |
| Validation | Customer discovery, hypothesis refinement | Reality questions discipline the founder to assess market signals honestly rather than selectively |
| Early Traction | Coaching first hires, decision-making under pressure | GROW becomes a management tool; the founder coaches rather than directs |
| Growth | Management development, strategic choices | GROW scales through the management layer; training managers to coach is a force multiplier |
| Scale | Executive coaching, board-level decision-making | GROW's structured approach brings rigour to complex, multi-stakeholder strategic decisions |

GROW applies at every stage, but its primary function shifts. At the idea and validation stages, it is predominantly a self-coaching tool: the founder uses it to structure their own thinking and avoid the twin traps of analysis paralysis and premature action.

At early traction and growth, GROW becomes an organisational tool. Founders who coach rather than direct build teams that are more autonomous, more engaged, and more capable of solving problems independently. The transition from "I'll tell you what to do" to "What do you think you should do?" is one of the most important leadership shifts a scaling founder must make, and GROW provides the structure for making it.

At scale, GROW's value lies in its ability to bring structured clarity to decisions of genuine complexity. Board-level strategic choices, executive succession planning, and organisational transformation all benefit from the disciplined progression through goal, reality, options, and commitment.
