# Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument

Two cofounders sit across from each other at a board meeting. The CTO wants to rebuild the platform from scratch -- eighteen months of engineering effort that will freeze feature development. The CEO insists the business cannot survive eighteen months without new features; the rebuild must happen incrementally, alongside shipping. Both are right. Both are wrong. Neither can unilaterally win without destroying the partnership. The board members exchange glances, recognising a pattern they have seen dismantle companies more promising than this one.

How this conflict resolves -- not whether it resolves, but *how* -- will determine the company's trajectory more decisively than any product decision. Kenneth Thomas and Ralph Kilmann mapped exactly this terrain in the 1970s, identifying five distinct conflict-handling modes arranged along two dimensions: assertiveness (the degree to which you pursue your own concerns) and cooperativeness (the degree to which you attempt to satisfy the other party's concerns). The genius of their model is not prescribing one mode as superior but revealing that effective leaders command all five and deploy each situationally.

---

## The Framework

### The Two Dimensions

The TKI plots conflict behaviour on two independent axes:

- **Assertiveness** (vertical axis): The extent to which you attempt to satisfy your own concerns. High assertiveness means you push for your position; low assertiveness means you set your concerns aside.
- **Cooperativeness** (horizontal axis): The extent to which you attempt to satisfy the other party's concerns. High cooperativeness means you prioritise the relationship and the other's needs; low cooperativeness means you focus solely on your own outcome.

### The Five Modes

```
                HIGH ASSERTIVENESS
                       |
          Competing    |    Collaborating
          (Win-Lose)   |    (Win-Win)
                       |
    LOW ───────── Compromising ─────── HIGH
    COOPERATIVENESS    |           COOPERATIVENESS
                       |
          Avoiding     |    Accommodating
          (Lose-Lose)  |    (Lose-Win)
                       |
                LOW ASSERTIVENESS
```

**1. Competing (High Assertiveness, Low Cooperativeness)**

You pursue your position at the other party's expense. Power, authority, or force of will determines the outcome.

- **When to use:** Emergencies requiring rapid action. Unpopular but necessary decisions (layoffs, pivots). When you know you are right on matters of principle. Protecting against people who exploit non-competitive behaviour.
- **When to avoid:** Routine disagreements. Situations requiring buy-in from the other party. Relationships you need to preserve long-term.

**2. Collaborating (High Assertiveness, High Cooperativeness)**

Both parties work together to find a solution that fully satisfies everyone's concerns. This is not compromise -- no one gives anything up.

- **When to use:** When both sets of concerns are too important to trade away. When you need to learn (the other party may have information you lack). When building commitment is essential to implementation. Integrating insights from people with different perspectives.
- **When to avoid:** Trivial issues where the time investment is disproportionate. When one party is not acting in good faith. Under genuine time pressure.

**3. Compromising (Moderate Assertiveness, Moderate Cooperativeness)**

Both parties give up something. The solution partially satisfies everyone but fully satisfies no one. Splitting the difference is the archetype.

- **When to use:** When goals are moderately important but not worth the disruption of competing or the time investment of collaborating. As a temporary settlement to complex issues. When two equally powerful parties are equally committed to mutually exclusive goals. As a fallback when collaboration fails.
- **When to avoid:** When the issue genuinely matters and a creative solution exists. When one party can be persuaded by evidence. When "splitting the difference" produces a solution inferior to either original position.

**4. Avoiding (Low Assertiveness, Low Cooperativeness)**

You sidestep the conflict entirely. Neither your concerns nor the other party's concerns are addressed.

- **When to use:** When the issue is trivial. When you have no chance of satisfying your concerns. When the potential damage of confrontation outweighs the benefits. To let people cool down. When gathering more information is essential before engaging.
- **When to avoid:** When issues will fester and escalate. When avoidance signals indifference or weakness. When your silence is being interpreted as agreement.

**5. Accommodating (Low Assertiveness, High Cooperativeness)**

You set aside your own concerns to satisfy the other party. The opposite of competing.

- **When to use:** When you realise you were wrong. When the issue matters far more to the other party than to you. To build social credit for future issues that matter more. When preserving harmony is critical. When continued competition would only damage the relationship.
- **When to avoid:** When your concern is legitimate and important. When accommodating becomes a habitual pattern that breeds resentment. When the other party is not reciprocating over time.

### Mode Selection Matrix

| Situation | Recommended Mode | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Server is down, customers affected | Competing | Speed overrides consensus |
| Cofounder disagrees on company vision | Collaborating | Both perspectives contain essential truth |
| Negotiating office lease terms | Compromising | Moderate stakes, clear trade-offs |
| Team member raises a complaint during a crisis | Avoiding (temporarily) | Address after the immediate crisis resolves |
| Junior team member has a better idea than yours | Accommodating | Their idea wins on merit; your ego is irrelevant |
| Board member pushes strategy you believe is destructive | Competing | Fiduciary principle at stake |
| Product and engineering disagree on sprint priorities | Collaborating | Both functions must buy in for execution |

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Conflict Diagnosis:**
> I am in a conflict with [describe the other party and their role]. The disagreement concerns [describe the issue]. Using the Thomas-Kilmann model, help me identify which conflict mode I am currently defaulting to, which mode the other party appears to be using, and which mode would be most effective given the stakes, the relationship importance, and the time constraints. Explain your recommendation.

**Prompt 2 -- Mode Expansion:**
> Analyse my natural conflict style based on these recent situations: [describe 3-4 recent conflicts and how you handled them]. Using the TKI framework, identify which modes I overuse and which I underuse. Then provide specific behavioural scripts for the modes I need to develop, tailored to my role as [founder/CEO/manager] at a [stage] company.

**Prompt 3 -- Team Conflict Mediation:**
> Two members of my team are in sustained conflict over [describe the issue]. One appears to be operating in [mode] and the other in [mode]. As their manager, help me design an intervention using the TKI framework: What mode should I adopt as the mediator? What questions should I ask each party? What process would move them toward a productive resolution?

**Prompt 4 -- Cofounder Conflict Resolution:**
> My cofounder and I disagree fundamentally about [describe the issue]. Our relationship is critical to the company's survival, but the issue cannot be avoided. Using the Thomas-Kilmann model, walk me through a collaborating-mode approach: How do I structure the conversation? What are the key moves to ensure both concerns are fully surfaced? What happens if collaboration fails -- what is my fallback mode and when do I invoke it?

## Use Cases

**Cofounder Equity Dispute at Validation Stage**
Two cofounders who launched with a 50/50 split now contribute asymmetrically -- one works full-time, the other retains a day job. The full-time founder wants to renegotiate. Default mode: competing ("I deserve more"). Effective mode: collaborating. The conversation reframes from "dividing a fixed pie" to "designing a structure that incentivises both parties to maximise their contribution." The output: a dynamic equity arrangement (using a tool like Slicing Pie) that adjusts ownership based on relative contribution, satisfying both parties' core concerns.

**Product vs. Sales Conflict at Growth Stage**
The VP of Sales demands custom features for a whale client. The VP of Product refuses, citing roadmap integrity. Both escalate to the CEO. Applying TKI, the CEO recognises that competing (choosing a side) will alienate one function. Avoiding (deferring the decision) will cost the deal and the roadmap. The CEO facilitates a collaborating session that reveals an alternative: the whale client's requirements overlap 70% with planned platform features; sequencing those features first satisfies both the client timeline and the roadmap logic. The remaining 30% becomes a scoped professional-services engagement, funded by the client.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Single-Mode Fixation.** Founders who only compete ("my way or the highway") eventually find themselves surrounded by accommodators who have stopped offering honest input. Founders who only accommodate find their authority eroded. Founders who only avoid discover that suppressed conflicts metastasise. Mastery requires range.

2. **Collaborating on Everything.** Collaboration is the most resource-intensive mode. Applying it to every disagreement -- including trivial ones -- produces meeting bloat, decision fatigue, and a reputation for indecisiveness. Reserve collaboration for issues where both parties' concerns are genuinely important and a creative solution is possible.

3. **Confusing Compromising with Collaborating.** Compromise means both parties sacrifice something. Collaboration means neither does -- a genuinely new solution emerges that satisfies all concerns. Settling for compromise on a high-stakes issue often produces a mediocre outcome when a superior one was achievable.

4. **Misreading Power Dynamics.** The TKI assumes roughly equal parties. When power is dramatically asymmetric -- a VC dictating terms to a first-time founder, for instance -- the model's prescriptions need adjustment. Competing against a more powerful party without leverage is not assertiveness; it is self-destruction.

5. **Avoiding Avoidance When Avoidance Is Correct.** Some conflicts should be left alone. Not every disagreement is worth engaging. The anti-pattern is not avoidance itself but reflexive avoidance of issues that demand engagement.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Validation | Cofounder alignment | Conflicts at this stage are existential -- they determine whether the founding team survives. Collaborating mode is essential for vision and equity discussions; competing mode may be necessary to kill ideas that do not survive customer contact. |
| Early Traction | Role clarity conflicts | As the team grows beyond the founders, conflicts arise about ownership, process, and decision rights. The TKI helps founders choose when to assert authority and when to empower team members. |
| Growth | Cross-functional friction | Product vs. sales, engineering vs. design, speed vs. quality. These recurring tensions require sophisticated mode-switching. Growth-stage leaders need all five modes in active rotation. |
| Scale | Organisational conflict management | At scale, the founder cannot mediate every conflict. The task becomes teaching the TKI framework to managers throughout the organisation and building conflict-resolution capacity into the culture. |
