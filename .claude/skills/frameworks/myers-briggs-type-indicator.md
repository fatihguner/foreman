# Myers-Briggs Type Indicator

The most widely administered personality instrument in the world -- taken by an estimated 50 million people and used by 88% of Fortune 500 companies -- is one that a significant portion of academic psychologists consider scientifically dubious. That tension is itself instructive. The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator, developed by Katharine Cook Briggs and her daughter Isabel Briggs Myers based on Carl Jung's theory of psychological types, persists not because it meets the psychometric standards of the Big Five personality model but because it does something the Big Five does not: it gives people a shared, non-judgmental language for talking about differences in how they perceive the world and make decisions. For entrepreneurs building teams under pressure, MBTI's value is practical rather than predictive. It does not tell you who will succeed. It tells you why your co-founder's approach to deadlines drives you to distraction, and why your head of sales and your head of engineering seem to be speaking different languages even when they agree on the goal.

---

## The Framework

MBTI classifies personality along four dichotomies, producing 16 possible type combinations. Each dichotomy represents a preference -- not an ability, not a fixed trait, but a tendency toward one mode of functioning over another. The analogy is handedness: most people can write with either hand, but one feels natural and the other requires effort.

### The Four Dichotomies

**1. Extraversion (E) vs. Introversion (I) -- Energy Direction**

| Extraversion | Introversion |
|-------------|-------------|
| Energised by external interaction | Energised by internal reflection |
| Thinks out loud; processes through conversation | Thinks internally; processes before speaking |
| Breadth of relationships | Depth of relationships |
| Action-oriented; prefers to act, then reflect | Reflection-oriented; prefers to reflect, then act |

Startup relevance: Extraverted founders excel at networking, pitching, and energising teams through visibility. Introverted founders excel at deep strategic thinking, listening to customers, and building products with concentrated focus. Neither orientation is superior; both are necessary at different moments. The danger is assuming everyone on the team shares the founder's energy orientation.

**2. Sensing (S) vs. Intuition (N) -- Information Gathering**

| Sensing | Intuition |
|---------|-----------|
| Focuses on concrete, present facts | Focuses on patterns, possibilities, future implications |
| Detail-oriented; trusts direct experience | Big-picture; trusts conceptual connections |
| Prefers proven methods | Prefers innovation and novelty |
| Asks "What is?" | Asks "What could be?" |

Startup relevance: The S/N dichotomy is the most frequent source of co-founder friction. Sensing-oriented co-founders want data, specifics, and proven models. Intuition-oriented co-founders want vision, possibility, and novel approaches. When the CTO (often S) asks "Show me the data" and the CEO (often N) responds with "Trust the vision," neither is wrong -- they are processing through different information-gathering preferences.

**3. Thinking (T) vs. Feeling (F) -- Decision Making**

| Thinking | Feeling |
|----------|---------|
| Decides based on logical analysis and objective criteria | Decides based on values, impact on people, and harmony |
| Seeks truth | Seeks tact |
| Comfortable with impersonal critique | Sensitive to the human dimension of decisions |
| Asks "Is this logical?" | Asks "What impact will this have on people?" |

Startup relevance: The T/F dichotomy becomes critical during painful decisions -- layoffs, performance management, ending partnerships. Thinking-preference leaders risk making correct decisions that destroy team trust through insensitive execution. Feeling-preference leaders risk delaying necessary decisions to avoid discomfort, allowing problems to compound.

**4. Judging (J) vs. Perceiving (P) -- Orientation to Structure**

| Judging | Perceiving |
|---------|------------|
| Prefers closure and decisiveness | Prefers openness and flexibility |
| Plans ahead; works methodically | Adapts in the moment; works in bursts |
| Comfortable with deadlines and schedules | Energised by last-minute pressure |
| Seeks to resolve ambiguity | Tolerates ambiguity comfortably |

Startup relevance: J/P tension is visible in every sprint planning meeting. Judging types want clear plans, committed timelines, and defined scope. Perceiving types want optionality, room for pivoting, and freedom to follow emerging opportunities. Early-stage startups, which exist in permanent ambiguity, structurally favour P-orientation -- but eventually need J-discipline to scale execution.

### Type Dynamics and Team Composition

The 16 types are not merely four binary choices stacked together; they interact. Each type has a dominant function (the strongest preference), an auxiliary function (the support), a tertiary function (developing), and an inferior function (the blindspot). Understanding type dynamics helps explain why an ENTJ leader (dominant extraverted thinking) and an INFP team member (dominant introverted feeling) can create explosive friction: each person's strength is the other's blindspot.

For team composition, the research-informed principle is cognitive diversity: teams that span the four dichotomies outperform homogeneous teams on complex problems, though they experience more friction during the process. The friction is the feature, not the bug -- it ensures that details are not ignored (S), possibilities are explored (N), logical rigour is applied (T), and human impact is considered (F).

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Team Type Mapping:**
> Based on these behavioural descriptions of my team members, estimate their likely MBTI types and map the team's cognitive diversity: [describe each person's communication style, decision-making approach, energy patterns, and response to ambiguity]. Identify where the team clusters (shared preferences) and where it has gaps. Recommend specific actions to compensate for missing perspectives -- either through hiring, advisory relationships, or structured processes that force the team to consider its blind spots.

**Prompt 2 -- Co-Founder Friction Diagnosis:**
> My co-founder and I are experiencing recurring friction around [describe the pattern -- e.g., decision speed, risk tolerance, communication style, planning approach]. Based on these descriptions of our respective styles, identify the likely MBTI dichotomy driving the tension: my style: [describe], their style: [describe]. Translate the friction from "right vs. wrong" into "different cognitive preferences" and recommend specific protocols we can adopt to leverage both perspectives rather than fight over which is correct.

**Prompt 3 -- Communication Adaptation:**
> I need to present [topic -- e.g., a strategic pivot, a new initiative, a difficult decision] to a group that includes [describe audience members and their likely types]. Design multiple versions of the key message, each adapted to a different MBTI preference pair: one optimised for ST (sensing-thinking), one for SF (sensing-feeling), one for NT (intuition-thinking), and one for NF (intuition-feeling). Highlight what each version emphasises and what it de-emphasises.

**Prompt 4 -- Hiring Complement Analysis:**
> My founding team consists of [describe types/preferences]. We are hiring for [role]. Based on our team's current type composition and the requirements of this role, recommend the personality preferences that would most complement our existing team. Explain which of our current blind spots this hire would address and what new tensions to anticipate (with mitigation strategies).

## Use Cases

**Early-traction startup with co-founder communication breakdown**

Two co-founders of an edtech startup have escalating disagreements that are eroding their working relationship. The CEO (likely ENFP -- extraverted, intuitive, feeling, perceiving) generates ideas rapidly, changes direction frequently, and makes decisions based on passion and user stories. The CTO (likely ISTJ -- introverted, sensing, thinking, judging) wants detailed specifications, stable priorities, and decisions grounded in data. Each interprets the other's style as a character flaw: the CEO sees the CTO as rigid and uninspired; the CTO sees the CEO as chaotic and unreliable. MBTI reframes the dynamic: neither is flawed; they process information and make decisions through fundamentally different cognitive preferences. The intervention establishes a structured protocol: the CEO creates a "vision document" for each initiative (satisfying the N preference), which the CTO then translates into a "specification document" (satisfying the S preference). Disagreements are routed through a shared decision matrix that incorporates both data (T) and impact assessment (F). The relationship stabilises within a month.

**Growth-stage company restructuring teams for product launch**

A 75-person company preparing for a major product launch assembles a cross-functional team from engineering, design, marketing, and sales. Initial meetings are unproductive: engineering wants detailed timelines (J), marketing wants flexibility to respond to competitor moves (P), design insists on user research (S/F), and sales pushes for features that close deals (S/T). The VP of Product administers informal MBTI assessments and maps the team's type distribution, revealing a heavy ST cluster in engineering and an NF cluster in design, with marketing and sales split. Armed with this map, the VP restructures meetings: technical reviews are S/T-optimised (data, specifics, timelines), creative sessions are N/F-optimised (possibilities, user stories, open exploration), and integration meetings deliberately include all preferences with a facilitator who ensures each cognitive style gets airtime. The product launches on schedule -- not because anyone changed their type, but because the process was designed to leverage all types rather than privilege one.

## Anti-Patterns

**1. Using MBTI as a hiring filter.** This is the most prevalent and damaging misapplication. MBTI does not predict job performance, and using it to screen candidates is both scientifically unsupported and ethically questionable. Research consistently shows that MBTI type does not reliably predict success in any role. Use it to understand communication dynamics after hiring, not to make hiring decisions.

**2. Treating type as destiny.** "I'm an introvert, so I can't do sales" is a fixed-mindset application of a tool that was designed to illuminate preferences, not define limits. Every person can operate outside their preferences -- it simply requires more energy. Conflating preference with inability is a distortion of the framework.

**3. Weaponising type in conflict.** "That's just your T talking" or "You're being such a J right now" turns a communication tool into an ad hominem attack. Type language should explain behaviour, not excuse it or dismiss it.

**4. Over-relying on a framework with known psychometric limitations.** MBTI's test-retest reliability is lower than academic psychologists prefer -- studies show that 50% of people receive a different type classification when retested after five weeks. The framework is most useful when treated as a rough map of preferences rather than a precise measurement of personality. Founders who treat MBTI types as rigid categories mistake the map for the territory.

**5. Assembling teams purely by type diversity.** Cognitive diversity matters, but so do domain expertise, cultural fit, work ethic, and interpersonal chemistry. A team with perfect type diversity but poor trust will underperform a less diverse team with strong cohesion. MBTI is one lens among many for team composition.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Early Traction | Co-founder dynamics, first-hire compatibility | MBTI explains interpersonal friction and provides shared language for navigating preference differences |
| Growth | Team composition, management layer communication | MBTI informs team design and helps managers adapt communication to different report types |
| Scale | Organisational culture, cross-functional alignment, M&A integration | MBTI identifies cultural clashes between departments or acquired companies with different cognitive profiles |

At early traction, MBTI's primary value is translating interpersonal friction from personal conflict into cognitive-preference difference. When two co-founders understand that their disagreement about planning rigour reflects a J/P preference difference rather than a competence gap, they can design processes that honour both orientations instead of fighting about who is right.

At growth, MBTI becomes a management development tool. Training managers to recognise and adapt to different preferences in their reports improves one-on-one effectiveness, feedback quality, and delegation accuracy. An ST manager who learns to frame assignments differently for NF reports (leading with purpose and impact rather than specifications and deadlines) will see better outcomes without changing the substance of the assignment.

At scale, MBTI's organisational application emerges. Merging two companies or restructuring departments often produces culture clash that is partly a type clash. Engineering-dominated organisations tend toward ST cultures; marketing-dominated organisations toward NF cultures. Making these preferences visible and creating bridging protocols reduces post-merger friction and cross-functional dysfunction.
