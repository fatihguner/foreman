# Reading People

The claim that words account for only 7% of communication is exaggerated -- but the belief that they account for 100% is far more dangerous.

Albert Mehrabian's 1967 research produced the famous 55/38/7 formula: 55% body language, 38% tone, 7% words. The study's conditions were narrow (single-word emotional statements), and the numbers should not be taken as universal law. What *should* be taken as universal law is the underlying insight: a substantial majority of what people communicate has nothing to do with the sentences they construct. Founders who listen only to words are reading a transcript when they should be watching a film. The investor who says "interesting" while leaning back with crossed arms is not interested. The hire who says "I'm excited" while avoiding eye contact is not excited. The customer who says "we'll think about it" while checking the time has already decided.

Reading people is not mind-reading. It is pattern recognition -- the trained habit of observing behaviour that most professionals ignore because they were never taught to look.

## The Framework

Reading people operates through five interlocking skills, each progressively more difficult to master.

### Skill 1: Stop Waiting to Talk

The precondition for reading people is the ability to stop composing your next sentence while someone else is speaking. Most people do not listen; they queue their response. The internal experience feels like listening -- you are facing the speaker, you are nodding -- but the cognitive bandwidth allocated to comprehension is minimal because most of it is consumed by rehearsal.

Gerald Bell of the Bell Leadership Institute prescribes the **Achiever Listening Position**: sit up straight, lean slightly forward, arms at your sides, eyes fixed on the speaker with the intensity of a child watching television. The physical posture forces cognitive engagement. When the mind wanders -- and it will -- the posture serves as an anchor to pull attention back to the speaker.

The critical reframe: you are not listening with intent to respond. You are listening with intent to *understand*. The response will take care of itself once comprehension is genuine.

### Skill 2: Listen with Your Eyes

Once the mind is fully present, the next skill is to distribute attention beyond words. Seven channels of nonverbal communication carry the information that words omit.

| Channel | What to Observe | Entrepreneurial Signal |
|---------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Facial expressions** | Micro-expressions of surprise, contempt, disgust, or delight that flash before the social mask reasserts itself | An investor's fleeting eyebrow raise when you mention a competitor reveals unexpected concern |
| **Posture and movement** | Open vs. closed stance, comfort vs. tension, leaning in vs. pulling away | A candidate who leans back when discussing team collaboration may be signalling introversion or resistance |
| **Gestures** | Hand movements, self-touching, fidgeting, pointing | A board member who touches her face repeatedly during a financial presentation may be masking discomfort with the numbers |
| **Eye contact** | Sustained vs. averted, blinking rate, direction of gaze | A cofounder who breaks eye contact when discussing equity split is flagging unresolved tension |
| **Touch** | Handshake firmness, duration, and placement; shoulder touch; arm grip | A weak handshake from a prospective partner may indicate low confidence in the deal or in themselves |
| **Proxemics (space)** | Physical distance, territorial behaviour, positioning relative to others | An investor who moves closer during your pitch is engaged; one who subtly increases distance is withdrawing |
| **Vocal qualities** | Pace, volume, pitch, hesitation, filler words | A customer whose speech accelerates when discussing budget is likely under pricing pressure |

The key is to establish a **baseline** for each person before interpreting deviations. Someone who fidgets constantly is not bluffing -- they are a fidgeter. Reading people requires calibrating against their normal behaviour, not against some universal template.

### Skill 3: Accept the Glass of Water

Rapport is built through small, often invisible signals of acceptance and reciprocity. The glass-of-water principle captures one such micro-signal: when offered a beverage at the start of a meeting, always accept.

The reasoning operates on two levels:

- **Self-regulation.** Accepting the drink creates a moment of settling in, reduces physical discomfort (thirst, nerves), and provides a natural pause for observation before the substantive conversation begins.
- **Host validation.** Declining an offered drink -- however politely -- creates a micro-friction. The host's gesture of hospitality has been refused. This is a tiny event, but tiny events compound. Accepting the drink allows the host to feel competent in their role, which primes the interaction toward warmth.

The glass-of-water principle extends beyond beverages. Every interaction contains micro-opportunities for acceptance: accepting a compliment gracefully rather than deflecting it, accepting a small favour rather than insisting on self-sufficiency, accepting an invitation to sit in a preferred chair. Each acceptance reinforces the rapport loop. Each refusal, however minor, introduces static.

### Skill 4: Detect the Bluff

Bluffing -- presenting a false position to extract a concession -- is common in business negotiations. Detection requires comparing the speaker's verbal content against their nonverbal baseline.

**Bluff indicators (deviation from baseline):**

- Face-touching or scratching that appears during the specific claim, absent during prior conversation
- Gaze aversion at the moment of the critical assertion
- Speech-pace acceleration or sudden formality ("To be perfectly honest..." is often a preamble to dishonesty)
- Physical fidgeting that intensifies during the negotiation's pressure point
- Vocal pitch increase -- stress compresses the vocal cords

**The detection protocol:**

1. Establish the person's baseline during low-stakes conversation (greetings, small talk, uncontested topics).
2. Note changes in nonverbal behaviour when the conversation moves to contested claims.
3. Identify whether the change correlates with a specific claim or negotiating position.
4. If indicators cluster around a particular statement, call the bluff -- not aggressively, but by declining to concede. "I understand your position. I'm not able to move on that, but I'm confident the value justifies the terms."

Calling a bluff carries information value regardless of whether the other party was actually bluffing. If they were, you have saved money and learned something about their trustworthiness. If they were not, their genuine conviction will emerge in their response -- and you can adjust accordingly.

### Skill 5: Mirror (The Technique That Sounds Absurd and Works Every Time)

Mirroring -- repeating back someone's exact words, preceded by "I hear you saying" -- sounds like a kindergarten exercise. It is, in practice, one of the most powerful rapport-building techniques available.

**Why it works:** Mirroring provides the speaker with irrefutable evidence that they have been heard. Not paraphrased, not interpreted, not redirected -- *heard*. The psychological experience of being truly heard is so rare in professional settings that when it occurs, it produces an immediate and often dramatic increase in trust and openness.

**Execution rules:**

1. **Repeat their exact words.** "I hear you saying you're frustrated with the timeline." Not "I understand you're upset about scheduling." Close paraphrases feel like interpretation. Exact repetition feels like comprehension.
2. **Emphasise the feeling words.** Mirroring "I'm worried we'll miss the deadline" should place vocal emphasis on *worried* and *miss*. The emotional content is what the speaker most needs reflected.
3. **Never follow with "but."** "I hear you saying you're worried about the timeline, but we're actually ahead of schedule" instantly negates the mirror. The word "but" erases everything that preceded it. If a response is necessary, pause after the mirror. Let the reflection settle. Then respond in a separate sentence.
4. **Care genuinely.** Robotic repetition is detected instantly. Mirroring requires authentic engagement with the speaker's emotional state. If you do not care, the technique will not merely fail -- it will damage trust.

The deeper the emotional content of the original statement, the more powerful the mirror. Mirroring "I took the train to work today" has negligible impact. Mirroring "I'm scared we're going to lose our biggest client" can transform a relationship.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Pre-Meeting Reading Preparation:**
> I have a high-stakes meeting with [describe person, role, context]. Help me prepare a reading plan: (a) what baseline information should I gather in the first two minutes of small talk, (b) what specific nonverbal channels should I prioritise given the meeting format [in-person / video / phone], (c) what are the likely pressure points in the conversation where nonverbal deviations will be most informative, and (d) what mirroring opportunities am I likely to encounter?

**Prompt 2 -- Bluff Detection Scenario:**
> A [counterparty description] has made the following claim during a negotiation: [describe the claim]. I observed the following nonverbal signals: [describe what you noticed]. Analyse whether these signals are consistent with bluffing, genuine conviction, or simple discomfort. Recommend a response strategy that tests the claim without damaging the relationship.

**Prompt 3 -- Listening Audit:**
> Record my performance in a recent important conversation: [describe the conversation]. At what points did I stop listening to understand and start listening to respond? What nonverbal signals from the other person did I miss? Reconstruct the conversation with annotations showing where active listening and mirroring could have changed the outcome.

**Prompt 4 -- Mirroring Script Design:**
> I anticipate a difficult conversation with [describe person and situation]. The other person is likely to express [describe expected emotional content]. Draft three mirroring responses I can use, ensuring each uses the person's likely exact language, emphasises the emotional words, and avoids the "but" trap. Include follow-up questions I can ask after each mirror to deepen understanding.

## Use Cases

**Validation-Stage Founder in Customer Discovery**

A SaaS founder conducts ten customer-discovery interviews in a week. In the first five, she focuses exclusively on the words customers use, recording answers to her structured questions. In the second five, she applies the full reading framework: she establishes baseline behaviour during opening small talk, watches for posture changes when discussing pain points, notes where speech pace accelerates (indicating genuine frustration versus polite agreement), and mirrors customers' emotional language back to them. The second set of interviews yields three insights the first set missed entirely: one customer's claimed "minor annoyance" was accompanied by physical tension that indicated a significant pain point; another customer's enthusiastic verbal response was contradicted by a micro-expression of scepticism that, when explored through mirroring, revealed a pricing concern she had not planned to raise. The founder adjusts both the product roadmap and the pricing model based on information that words alone would have concealed.

**Early-Traction CEO in Investor Negotiation**

A founder receives a term sheet from a venture capital firm. During the negotiation call, the lead partner states that the proposed valuation is "firm" and that the fund "cannot go higher." The founder notes that the partner scratches his face during this statement and increases his speech pace -- deviations from his relaxed baseline during earlier discussion of the product. Rather than conceding, the founder mirrors: "I hear you saying the valuation is firm and the fund cannot go higher." The partner, feeling heard, elaborates -- revealing that the constraint is not the fund's valuation model but a specific partner's concern about the sector. The founder addresses the sector concern directly, and the valuation increases by 15% in the next conversation.

**Growth-Stage CTO Managing a Departing Engineer**

A senior engineer requests a "quick chat" about her career. The CTO reads the signals: the engineer has already accepted the invitation to sit, but her posture is tense, she declines the offered coffee (a rapport-refusal micro-signal), and she avoids eye contact during the opening small talk. Before the engineer speaks her first substantive sentence, the CTO has a hypothesis: this is a resignation conversation. He adjusts his listening posture to full Achiever mode, asks one open question, and mirrors every statement the engineer makes for the next ten minutes without offering a single counterargument. The engineer, feeling genuinely heard for what may be the first time in months, reveals that the issue is not compensation but a sense of isolation on a project. The CTO addresses the structural problem. The engineer stays.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Armchair Body-Language Analysis.** Reading a single gesture in isolation and drawing a sweeping conclusion. Arms crossed can mean hostility, cold temperature, or habitual comfort. Reading people requires *clusters* of signals and *deviations* from baseline, not amateur semiotics applied to one data point.

2. **Weaponising Mirroring.** Using mirroring as a manipulation technique to extract concessions rather than as a genuine listening tool. People can distinguish between being heard and being handled. Mirroring deployed without authentic care produces an uncanny-valley effect that destroys trust faster than not mirroring at all.

3. **Over-Reading Video Calls.** Applying the full nonverbal framework to Zoom calls, where camera angle, lighting, and screen-induced gaze distortion make body-language reading unreliable. On video, prioritise vocal qualities and verbal content. Reserve full nonverbal reading for in-person interactions.

4. **Calling Bluffs Aggressively.** Detecting a bluff and then announcing "I know you're bluffing" is a negotiation-ending move, not a negotiation tactic. The correct response is to decline the bluffer's demand while preserving the relationship. The information value of the detection is strategic; the confrontation is not.

5. **Mirroring Without Stopping.** Applying the technique to every sentence in a conversation, producing a parrot effect that is disorienting rather than validating. Mirroring is a precision tool. Deploy it at moments of emotional significance, not as a conversational default.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Cofounder alignment | At the idea stage, the most critical reading is between cofounders. Misaligned nonverbal signals during early discussions about equity, roles, and vision are the earliest warning signs of partnerships that will fracture under pressure. |
| Validation | Customer truth-detection | Customers in discovery interviews are polite. Politeness masks indifference. Reading nonverbal signals distinguishes genuine pain points from courteous agreement -- the difference between a real market and a phantom one. |
| Early Traction | Hiring signal reading | Candidates optimise their verbal performance. The founder who reads posture, eye contact, and vocal authenticity during interviews makes better hiring decisions than the one who evaluates only what candidates say. |
| Growth | Negotiation and board dynamics | Growth-stage negotiations -- fundraising, partnerships, M&A exploration -- are environments where bluffing is expected and stakes are high. Baseline-deviation detection becomes a measurable financial skill. |
| Scale | Organisational listening systems | At scale, the CEO cannot personally read every stakeholder. The reading skills must be taught to managers and embedded in feedback systems -- 360 reviews that capture nonverbal observations, not just verbal ratings. |
