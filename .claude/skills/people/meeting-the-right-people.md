# Meeting the Right People

The secret to networking is not talking to everyone -- it is doing what nobody else does.

A founder wore bright orange sneakers to a startup event where three hundred people competed for the attention of Dave McClure, founding partner of 500 Startups. McClure ignored all of them. Then he spotted the shoes. "I have got to talk to the man wearing those fucking shoes!" The conversation lasted fifteen minutes. 500 Startups became a key investor in the founder's company. The shoes did not close the deal. But they opened the door -- and in a room of identical pitches, the door is the bottleneck.

This principle generalises beyond footwear. In any saturated environment -- a conference, an inbox, a LinkedIn feed -- the decisive advantage belongs to the person who has solved the attention problem before solving the persuasion problem. Most networking advice focuses on what to say. The prior question is how to get anyone to listen in the first place.

---

## The Framework

Meeting the right people involves five distinct disciplines, each addressing a different failure mode.

### Discipline 1: Stand Out (The Orange Shoes Principle)

In any gathering of professionals, the default is conformity. Dark suits, safe accessories, predictable conversation openers. The person who introduces a single, authentic differentiator -- visual, behavioural, or conversational -- captures disproportionate attention.

The criteria for an effective differentiator:

- **Attention-grabbing.** It must be visible or noticeable without requiring explanation.
- **Authentic.** It must feel natural to you, not costumed. A forced quirk is worse than no quirk.
- **Consistent.** It must appear repeatedly so that it becomes associated with your identity. A one-time stunt is novelty; a persistent signature is brand.

Orange shoes work because they satisfy all three criteria. The differentiator need not be visual -- it could be an unusual opening question (see the three-question method), a distinctive handshake ritual, or a habit of sending handwritten follow-up notes in an era of templated emails. What matters is that it is yours, it is noticeable, and it recurs.

### Discipline 2: Reach Anyone (The Warm Introduction Chain)

Cold outreach has a response rate that approaches zero for high-value targets. The warm introduction chain -- mediated through mutual connections, typically via LinkedIn -- transforms unreachable contacts into coffee meetings.

**The method:**

1. Identify the person you want to meet.
2. Search for mutual connections -- first-degree preferred, second-degree acceptable.
3. Select the mutual connection most likely to respond positively. Two filters: (a) someone for whom you have done a favour in the past (reciprocity capital), and (b) someone with genuine influence over the target.
4. Write a concise introduction request that explains *why* the meeting would be mutually beneficial, not just why *you* want it.
5. Once connected, propose a specific, time-bounded meeting. "Fifteen minutes for coffee" removes friction. "Let's find time to chat sometime" creates it.

The chain works because it transfers trust. The target does not know you, but she knows and trusts the connector. That transferred trust lowers the barrier from "ignore" to "consider." The deeper your network of first-degree connections, the shorter the chain to any target. This is the compound interest of relationship-building -- every genuine connection increases the reachability of everyone else.

### Discipline 3: Build a Personal Advisory Board

Mentorship is not reserved for people who work inside organisations large enough to have formal programmes. Any entrepreneur can construct a personal advisory board -- an informal group of five to twelve people who meet quarterly to advise on specific challenges.

**Construction principles:**

| Element | Guidance |
|---------|----------|
| Purpose | Define the board's focus before recruiting members. "Help me launch a software company" is actionable. "Help me with stuff" is not. |
| Composition | Diverse disciplines and backgrounds. At least half should be people you have met; at least two should be people you have not yet met but can reach through connections. |
| Structure | Scheduled quarterly meetings with a written agenda. Begin with a twenty-minute icebreaker or training exercise. Follow with a business update and one to two specific challenges for discussion. |
| Reciprocity | Provide value to advisers at every meeting -- leadership training, networking opportunities, a small equity stake or stipend. The most powerful compensation, however, is the multiplier effect: smart people in the same room generate connections and insights none of them would have produced alone. |
| Seriousness | The difference between an advisory board and a casual dinner is structure. Agenda, schedule, quiet room, follow-through. The more seriously the founder takes the format, the more seriously advisers will take their role. |

The multiplier effect is the advisory board's core advantage over one-on-one mentorship. When eight experienced people hear the same challenge simultaneously, they produce cross-pollinated solutions -- combinations of expertise that no single adviser would generate alone.

### Discipline 4: Hire Slow, Fire Fast (In All Relationships)

The principle is well-known in management. Its application to *all* professional relationships is less obvious but equally important.

**Hire slow:** Do not grant full trust and access to someone you have just met, no matter how impressive they appear. Early enthusiasm is not evidence of long-term fit. Cofounders, business partners, key hires, and even investor relationships should be entered gradually, with deliberate evaluation at each stage.

**Fire fast:** The moment gut instinct signals that a relationship is not working -- the business partner whose values diverge, the investor whose advice consistently harms, the hire whose culture impact is toxic -- act. Cognitive dissonance is the enemy: the desire to believe you made a good decision causes you to rationalise the bad one, sometimes for years.

The cognitive dissonance trap is particularly dangerous for founders, who are temperamentally biased toward optimism. The script "I'll give them one more chance" or "Maybe they'll change" is almost always the sound of a founder overriding data with hope. Every minute spent with the wrong person is a minute unavailable for the right one.

### Discipline 5: The Productive Blowoff

Time is finite. Not every person who requests a meeting deserves thirty minutes of it. The productive blowoff -- ending an interaction quickly while leaving the other person feeling valued -- is a skill that protects the founder's most scarce resource.

**The three-step blowoff:**

1. **Physical signal.** A handshake or equivalent gesture to mark the beginning *and* the end of the conversation.
2. **Direct, honest communication.** State the situation frankly. "You don't fit our investment criteria, but you might be a partner" conveys respect through candour.
3. **Provide value before exiting.** Make an introduction, offer a specific suggestion, send a useful resource. The person walks away with something concrete rather than a vague rejection.

The founder who masters this discipline converts thirty-minute meetings into eight-minute meetings without creating resentment -- and reclaims the other twenty-two minutes for higher-value activity. Sheryl Sandberg calls it "ruthless prioritisation." The ruthlessness applies to time allocation, not to people.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Networking Differentiation Strategy:**
> I am attending [describe event: type, audience, size, goal]. Help me design a personal differentiation strategy using the Orange Shoes principle. Based on my personality and context [describe], suggest three possible signature differentiators that are attention-grabbing, authentic to me, and repeatable. For each, describe how it would work at this specific event and what opening conversations it would naturally create.

**Prompt 2 -- Warm Introduction Chain:**
> I want to meet [describe target person, their role, and why]. I have the following potential mutual connections: [list 3-5 people]. For each, evaluate (a) the likely strength of their relationship with my target, (b) the reciprocity capital I have with them, and (c) the quality of introduction they would provide. Recommend the optimal connector and draft the introduction request email.

**Prompt 3 -- Advisory Board Design:**
> I am a [stage] founder building [describe company]. Design a personal advisory board for me. Recommend the ideal size, meeting frequency, agenda structure, and compensation model. Define the five to seven competency areas I should recruit for, and for each, suggest the profile of the ideal adviser (not a specific person, but a role/background description). Include a template for the initial recruitment ask.

**Prompt 4 -- Relationship Audit:**
> Audit my current professional relationships against the "hire slow, fire fast" principle. Here are my key relationships: [list 5-8 with brief descriptions of current state]. For each, identify whether I am (a) investing appropriately, (b) over-investing due to cognitive dissonance, or (c) under-investing in a high-potential connection. Recommend specific actions for each, including any "fire fast" decisions I may be avoiding.

## Use Cases

**Idea-Stage Founder at a Demo Day**

A first-time founder attends a demo day with forty startups and two hundred investors. She has no existing investor relationships and no brand recognition. Applying the Orange Shoes principle, she wears a distinctive hand-painted jacket that references her product's design aesthetic -- genuine because she is a trained artist, attention-grabbing because no one else in the room has one. Three investors approach her before she approaches anyone. She uses the warm-introduction chain for the remaining five target investors, reaching three through LinkedIn mutual connections within the following week. Within a month, she has a personal advisory board of four people assembled from connections made at the event: a veteran founder, a domain expert, a marketing executive, and one of the investors who did not invest but offered to advise. The board meets quarterly in a structured format, and within two meetings surfaces a pivot in go-to-market strategy that the founder would not have identified alone.

**Growth-Stage CEO Protecting Executive Time**

A CEO of a sixty-person company receives fifteen meeting requests per week from vendors, potential partners, and aspiring hires. Her calendar is the company's bottleneck. She implements the productive blowoff systematically: every meeting request receives a response within forty-eight hours, but most are converted from thirty-minute meetings to ten-minute calls with a clear three-step structure (signal, directness, value). She creates a "meeting triage" protocol for her executive assistant: requests that align with quarterly priorities get full meetings; all others get the abbreviated format. The CEO recovers seven hours per week -- nearly a full working day -- while maintaining a reputation for responsiveness and generosity.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Collecting Connections Without Purpose.** Treating LinkedIn connections or business cards as a score. Ten thousand connections with no relationship depth is a database, not a network. The warm-introduction chain requires trust, and trust requires genuine relationships -- not a follower count.

2. **Networking as Performance.** Attending every event, working every room, exchanging cards with every person. This produces exhaustion and shallow relationships. Strategic networking means attending fewer events with higher target density and investing more time per interaction.

3. **Advisory Board as Vanity Project.** Recruiting impressive names who never attend meetings or provide substantive input. An advisory board with a former Fortune 500 CEO who shows up once a year is less valuable than one with five engaged operators who attend every quarter and follow up between meetings.

4. **Firing Too Slowly Out of Guilt.** The cognitive dissonance trap is especially potent in founder-investor and founder-cofounder relationships, where the social cost of exit feels prohibitive. The sunk-cost fallacy -- "we've been through so much together" -- is not a strategy. Every week spent in a toxic partnership has a measurable opportunity cost.

5. **Blowing Off the Wrong People.** The productive blowoff is a tool for time management, not for status signalling. The junior employee who requests a meeting today may be the CEO who writes a check tomorrow. Evaluate meeting requests on potential value, not current title.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Network construction | At the idea stage, the founder's network *is* the venture's most valuable asset. Every coffee meeting, warm introduction, and event attendance is an investment in infrastructure that will compound over years. |
| Validation | Customer and partner access | The warm-introduction chain becomes a customer-discovery tool. Reaching ten potential customers through trusted mutual connections yields richer feedback than reaching a hundred through cold outreach. |
| Early Traction | Advisory board formation | With a product in market and early signals of fit, the founder has enough substance to recruit meaningful advisers. This is the optimal window for advisory-board construction -- early enough to benefit from strategic guidance, late enough to articulate a clear purpose. |
| Growth | Relationship pruning | Growth demands "fire fast" discipline. Relationships that served the company at seed stage -- the friendly-but-unstrategic investor, the early hire who cannot grow with the role -- must be evaluated honestly against current needs. |
| Scale | Institutional networking | At scale, the founder's personal network cannot serve the entire organisation. Networking must become a distributed competency -- every executive building their own warm-introduction chains, their own advisory relationships, their own productive-blowoff discipline. |
