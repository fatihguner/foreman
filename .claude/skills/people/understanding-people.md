# Understanding People

Myers-Briggs is a party game. The Enneagram is a real tool. The difference matters.

Myers-Briggs assigns people to one of sixteen types based on binary preferences. Take the test twice and there is a meaningful probability of receiving a different result. The assessment measures states, not traits -- and states shift with mood, context, and Tuesday's weather. The Enneagram, by contrast, identifies nine core motivational patterns that remain stable over time because they measure *what drives behaviour*, not what behaviour looks like on a given afternoon. For entrepreneurs, who must read cofounders across years and investors across minutes, the distinction between a parlour-trick classification and a stable motivational map is the distinction between guesswork and strategy.

Self-awareness is the prerequisite for influence. Until the founder understands her own motivational architecture -- why she says yes to everything (Type 3), why she avoids conflict at the cost of clarity (Type 9), why she hoards information as a form of control (Type 5) -- her attempts to understand others will be filtered through distortions she cannot see.

## The Framework

Understanding people operates on three progressively deeper levels. Each level builds on the one before it, and all three are required for genuine interpersonal effectiveness.

### Level 1: Understand Yourself First (The Enneagram)

The Enneagram identifies nine core types, each defined by a dominant motivation and a characteristic pitfall.

| Type | Core Drive | Common Pitfall | Entrepreneurial Manifestation |
|------|-----------|----------------|-------------------------------|
| 1 | Striving to be perfect | Rigid standards paralysing progress | Founder who cannot ship an MVP because "it's not ready" |
| 2 | Striving to be connected | Over-giving, boundary collapse | Cofounder who cannot say no to any request from team or customers |
| 3 | Striving to be outstanding | Saying yes to everything, burnout | CEO who accepts every speaking invitation while the company drifts |
| 4 | Striving to be unique | Emotional volatility, envy of competitors | Founder who pivots product based on aesthetic vision rather than data |
| 5 | Striving to be detached | Information hoarding, isolation | Technical cofounder who builds in silence and resents interruption |
| 6 | Striving to be secure | Analysis paralysis, worst-case fixation | Operator who cannot commit to a pricing model without twelve spreadsheets |
| 7 | Striving to be excited | Shiny-object syndrome, half-finished projects | Serial entrepreneur who launches five things and finishes none |
| 8 | Striving to be powerful | Bulldozing team, alienating partners | Founder whose "intensity" is a euphemism for intimidation |
| 9 | Striving to be peaceful | Conflict avoidance, passive agreement | CEO who tells every board member what they want to hear |

The Enneagram does not change over time the way Myers-Briggs results do. The founder who is a Type 3 at twenty-five will be a Type 3 at forty-five. What changes is the *relationship* to the type: awareness of the pitfall enables rewriting the internal script. The Type 3 who recognises the "I'll Do It" syndrome can learn that saying no to some commitments produces more outstanding outcomes in the ones that remain.

### Level 2: Get Anyone (The Coffee Experiment)

Understanding people who are naturally likeable requires no technique. The real test is understanding people who provoke frustration, confusion, or outright dislike. Every entrepreneur has a "John" -- the business partner, board member, or key client who seems impervious to rapport. The instinctive response is to label them ("I just don't get him") and route around them. The label, however, is not a diagnosis. It is a resignation that guarantees the relationship will never improve.

The method is deceptively simple:

1. **Refuse the narrative.** Stop saying "I don't get him." The declaration itself becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy -- a closed cognitive loop that prevents new information from entering.
2. **Invest fifteen minutes and five dollars.** Take the difficult person for coffee outside the professional context. The shift in environment reduces defensive posture on both sides.
3. **Ask questions, then shut up and listen.** The objective is not to like the person. It is to understand what drives them -- which Enneagram type they approximate, what their formative experiences were, why they behave as they do.

Understanding does not require agreement or affection. A founder who understands that a difficult board member is a Type 8 driven by fierce independence (often rooted in early-life adversity) can work with that knowledge strategically -- framing proposals as challenges rather than requests, for instance -- even if personal warmth never develops. The coffee meeting is not therapy. It is reconnaissance. Thirty minutes and a five-dollar espresso frequently yield more strategic intelligence than months of frustrated guesswork.

### Level 3: Rapid Understanding (The Three-Question Method)

Standard networking questions -- "Where are you from?" "What do you do?" -- yield standard, forgettable answers. Larry Benet's three-question framework replaces small talk with questions that reveal values, aspirations, and identity in under three minutes:

1. **What is the most exciting thing you are working on right now?** (Reveals current passion and professional direction.)
2. **If you had enough money to retire and then some, what would you be doing?** (Reveals intrinsic motivation stripped of financial necessity.)
3. **What is your favourite charity to support, and why?** (Reveals values and emotional commitments.)

These questions work because they bypass the social scripts people recite on autopilot and engage the deeper structures of identity. A founder who uses these in a first meeting will remember that conversation years later -- and more critically, the other person will remember being understood.

The technique generalises beyond Benet's specific trio. Any question that targets values, aspirations, or emotional commitments will outperform biographical data collection. Ten alternative questions serve the same function:

- What is the most exciting thing in your personal life right now?
- If you were not doing what you do today, what would you be doing and why?
- Other than a family member, who has been your most important influence?
- How would your favourite teacher describe you?
- What would you choose to do for a day if there were no constraints?

The principle is consistent: replace "where" and "what" questions (which elicit facts) with "why" and "how" questions (which elicit identity). The shift takes three seconds of deliberate intention and produces a fundamentally different quality of conversation.

### The Substrate: Loneliness and Connection

Most people are lonely. Even the most outwardly successful, well-adjusted individuals carry stretches of disconnection that shape how they respond to someone who genuinely listens. The founder who approaches people with authentic intent to understand and connect differentiates herself from the vast majority of professionals, who are too preoccupied with their own concerns to focus on others. When the founder fills that void -- even for twenty minutes over a charged phone -- a disproportionate reciprocity often follows. Not because the listener was calculating, but because genuine connection is so rare that people remember and reward it instinctively.

The Carnegie principle -- "be interested, not interesting" -- remains the single most efficient interpersonal technique available. Humans love to talk about themselves. Neuroscience confirms that self-disclosure activates the brain's reward centres. The person who listens attentively, asks follow-up questions, and resists the urge to redirect the conversation to their own story will be remembered as extraordinary -- despite having said almost nothing.

Carnegie articulated this in 1936. The framework updates it for an era of performative self-promotion on social media, where the incentive structure rewards broadcasting over receiving. The modern founder faces a paradox: every platform encourages her to be interesting (post content, build a following, project expertise), while every meaningful relationship rewards her for being interested (ask questions, listen deeply, let the other person feel significant). The resolution is not to abandon the public persona but to ensure that in private interactions -- the ones that actually determine whether investors, partners, and employees say yes -- listening dominates speaking by a ratio no smaller than three to one.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Enneagram Team Mapping:**
> My cofounding team consists of [describe 2-4 people with brief behavioural descriptions]. Based on these descriptions, estimate each person's likely Enneagram type. For each, identify the core motivation, the predictable pitfall in a startup context, and one specific communication adjustment I should make when working with that person. Flag any type combinations that are likely to produce friction and suggest a mitigation strategy.

**Prompt 2 -- Difficult Relationship Decoding:**
> I am struggling to work productively with [describe the person, their role, and specific frustrating behaviours]. Using the Enneagram framework and the "get anyone" method, help me (1) hypothesise what motivates this person's behaviour, (2) design a low-stakes interaction outside our normal context to test that hypothesis, and (3) prepare three questions I can ask during that interaction that will reveal whether my hypothesis is correct.

**Prompt 3 -- First-Meeting Question Design:**
> I have a [15/30/60]-minute first meeting scheduled with [describe the person and context]. Design a sequence of questions that will move quickly past small talk and reveal this person's values, motivations, and potential alignment with my venture. The questions should feel natural, not interrogative, and should be calibrated to the formality of the setting.

**Prompt 4 -- Self-Awareness Audit:**
> Based on the following description of my typical behaviours and tendencies [provide description], identify my likely Enneagram type. Then list three specific situations in my entrepreneurial journey where my type's characteristic pitfall is most likely to cause problems, and provide a concrete rewrite of the internal script for each situation.

## Use Cases

**Idea-Stage Founder Choosing a Cofounder**

A technical founder (likely Type 5 -- detached, analytical, information-hoarding) is evaluating two potential cofounders for a fintech venture. By mapping each candidate against the Enneagram, she identifies that Candidate A is a Type 3 (outstanding, driven, prone to overcommitment) and Candidate B is a Type 8 (powerful, direct, prone to bulldozing). Rather than choosing based on chemistry, she evaluates which pitfall pattern is more manageable alongside her own. A Type 5 paired with a Type 8 will produce direct, uncomfortable conversations about strategy -- which is productive friction. A Type 5 paired with a Type 3 risks two people working furiously in parallel without genuine communication. She chooses Candidate B and structures their working relationship around weekly "coffee experiments" to maintain mutual understanding as pressure increases.

**Validation-Stage Founder in Customer Discovery**

A consumer-health startup founder uses the three-question method during a networking dinner with potential early adopters. Instead of pitching her product, she asks each person at the table: "What is the most frustrating thing about managing your health right now?" and "If you had unlimited resources, what would you change about your daily routine?" Two responses reveal a latent pain point she had not considered -- medication tracking for elderly parents -- which becomes a feature that drives her first thousand sign-ups. She never would have heard it through a survey. The questions created the conditions for people to reveal what they actually care about, not what they think a founder wants to hear.

**Growth-Stage CEO Diagnosing a Dysfunctional Leadership Team**

A CEO notices that leadership meetings have become performative -- people agree in the room and disagree in the corridor. She administers the Enneagram to her six direct reports and discovers the team is heavily skewed toward Types 3, 6, and 9: achievers who fear failure, loyalists who fear uncertainty, and peacemakers who fear conflict. The team's composition creates a structural incentive to avoid hard conversations. She redesigns the meeting format to begin with a two-minute silence followed by the three-question method applied to a business challenge: "What is the most exciting thing about this quarter?" "If resources were unlimited, what would we do differently?" "What is the one thing we are avoiding discussing?" The shift from performative updates to values-revealing questions surfaces the real issues within three meetings.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Using the Enneagram as a Label Gun.** Assigning types to colleagues and then using those types to dismiss behaviour -- "Oh, that's just his Type 8 talking" -- rather than to understand and adapt. The Enneagram is a communication tool, not a sorting hat. The moment it becomes an excuse to stop trying, it has failed.

2. **Listening as Performance.** Sitting in silence while mentally rehearsing what to say next is not listening. Active listening requires genuine cognitive engagement with what the other person is saying. The difference is detectable. People know when they are being heard and when they are being waited out.

3. **Forced Vulnerability in Professional Settings.** Asking the three deep questions at a formal investor pitch or a first board meeting. Context matters. Rapid-understanding techniques work in networking events, coffee meetings, and retreats. They misfire in settings where professional distance is expected and where premature intimacy reads as boundary violation.

4. **Projecting Your Own Motivations.** Assuming that because *you* would respond well to direct feedback, everyone else will too. The entire premise of understanding people is that motivational structures differ. The Type 9 who needs gentle framing will not respond to the bluntness that energises a Type 8.

5. **Treating Understanding as Agreement.** Understanding why a toxic investor behaves as they do does not obligate tolerance. Comprehension is a strategic asset. It does not require submission.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Cofounder selection | At the idea stage, the relationship between cofounders *is* the company. Enneagram mapping and the coffee experiment are cofounder due diligence -- cheaper than a term sheet and more predictive than a resume. |
| Validation | Customer discovery | Rapid-understanding questions applied to potential customers reveal motivations that structured interviews miss. "If money were no object, how would you solve this problem?" surfaces latent needs. |
| Early Traction | Hiring and culture | Administering the Enneagram to the first ten hires establishes a shared vocabulary for communication and conflict. The investment pays compound interest as the team grows. |
| Growth | Leadership development | The CEO's self-awareness becomes the ceiling for the organisation's emotional maturity. Growth-stage founders who have not confronted their own Enneagram pitfalls will reproduce those pitfalls in the culture. |
| Scale | Institutional empathy | At scale, understanding people cannot depend on the founder's personal attention. The techniques must be embedded in management training, onboarding, and meeting design so that the organisation itself listens. |
