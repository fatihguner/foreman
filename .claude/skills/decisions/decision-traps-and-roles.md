# Decision Traps and Roles

TRAP. Every decision is mined with invisible traps -- anchoring, sunk cost, confirmation bias -- and the most dangerous ones are the ones you cannot see operating on you. Researchers have documented these cognitive distortions for half a century, yet a room of experienced executives will fall into them with the same regularity as a room of undergraduates. The traps are hardwired. They do not yield to intelligence, seniority, or good intentions. They yield only to structure: specific counter-routines that force the mind to check itself before it commits. This skill catalogues the traps, provides the counter-routines, and then addresses the organisational question that amplifies every individual bias -- who, exactly, owns the decision?

## The Toolkit

### Tool 1: The Five Hidden Traps

Each trap operates beneath conscious awareness. The counter-measures are not intuitive; they must be deliberately applied.

**The Anchoring Trap.** The mind gives disproportionate weight to the first information it receives. An opening offer in a negotiation, a prior year's revenue figure, a colleague's offhand estimate -- each becomes a gravitational centre around which all subsequent judgments orbit. In one classic experiment, asking whether Turkey's population exceeds 35 million versus 100 million produced wildly different estimates of the actual figure, despite the anchor being arbitrary.

Counter-measures:
- Approach the problem from multiple starting points before consulting others
- Withhold your own preliminary estimates when soliciting input -- otherwise your preconceptions return to you disguised as independent confirmation
- In negotiations, define your position fully before the other party's opening gambit can set the frame

**The Status-Quo Trap.** Defaulting to the current state feels safe because action invites responsibility, and responsibility invites criticism. In one experiment, people randomly given a mug refused to trade it for an equally valued chocolate bar -- the status quo exerted its pull within minutes of being established. In business, sins of commission are punished far more severely than sins of omission, creating a structural incentive to leave things as they are even when change is overdue.

Counter-measures:
- Ask whether you would choose the current state if it were not already the default
- Never treat the status quo as your only alternative -- generate at least one concrete option against which to compare it
- Evaluate alternatives in terms of future desirability, not present comfort

**The Sunk-Cost Trap.** Past investments -- of money, time, reputation -- create psychological pressure to justify those investments by continuing down the same path. A bank that originated a bad loan is far more likely to advance additional funds than a fresh banker who inherits the account, because the originator is unconsciously protecting an earlier, flawed decision.

Counter-measures:
- Seek counsel from people uninvolved with the original decision
- Reassign ownership when the original decision-maker cannot separate past commitment from present merit
- Cultivate a culture that evaluates decision quality at the time of the decision, not solely by subsequent outcomes

**The Confirming-Evidence Trap.** The mind seeks information that supports its existing inclination and discounts information that contradicts it. Two groups -- one favouring and one opposing capital punishment -- each read balanced research evidence and both emerged more entrenched in their original positions. The subconscious decides what it wants before the conscious mind assembles the justification.

Counter-measures:
- Appoint a devil's advocate, or better, construct the strongest counter-argument yourself
- Ask: "Am I gathering information to make a smart choice, or to confirm the choice I have already made?"
- Avoid leading questions when soliciting advice; if an adviser consistently supports your view, find a different adviser

**The Framing Trap.** How the question is posed determines the answer. New Jersey and Pennsylvania introduced identical auto-insurance options but framed the default differently; the framing difference produced a $200 million gap in outcomes. People are risk-averse when a problem is framed as a potential gain and risk-seeking when framed as a potential loss -- even when the underlying options are mathematically identical.

Counter-measures:
- Reframe every problem in at least two ways: as a gain and as a loss, with different reference points
- Challenge others' framings explicitly -- the person who sets the frame controls the decision
- Revisit framing at multiple stages of the decision process, not just at the start

### Tool 2: The RAPID Model for Decision Roles

Ambiguity about decision authority is the organisational accelerant that turns individual bias into institutional paralysis. The RAPID framework, developed by Bain & Company, assigns five discrete roles to every significant decision:

| Role | Letter | Function |
|------|--------|----------|
| **Recommend** | R | Proposes a course of action, marshals data, engages stakeholders, builds the case |
| **Agree** | A | Must formally agree before the recommendation can advance; has explicit veto power |
| **Perform** | P | Executes the decision once made |
| **Input** | I | Provides information and analysis that shapes the recommendation; consulted but not binding |
| **Decide** | D | Brings the decision to closure and commits the organisation to act |

Three structural pitfalls to avoid:

1. **Multiple D's.** If two people believe they own the decision, the result is a tug-of-war. One person holds the D. Always.
2. **Proliferation of A's.** Too many veto-holders paralyse recommenders. If many people must agree, decision authority has not been pushed far enough down the organisation.
3. **Excessive I's.** When everyone provides input, at least some contributions are ceremonial. Each I-role should demonstrably improve the recommendation's quality.

### Tool 3: The Seven-Step Decision Checklist

A lightweight protocol, drawn from behavioural economics research, that consistently improves group decision quality. Managers who follow it save an average of ten hours of discussion, decide ten days faster, and improve outcomes by twenty percent.

1. Write down five pre-existing goals or priorities that the decision will affect
2. Write down at least three -- ideally four or more -- realistic alternatives
3. Write down the most important information you are missing
4. Write down the expected impact one year from now as a brief narrative
5. Involve two to six stakeholders (effectiveness declines by ten percent per person beyond seven)
6. Record what was decided, why, and the team's confidence level
7. Schedule a follow-up review in one to two months

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Trap Diagnosis:**
> "I am about to make this decision: [describe decision]. Before I proceed, audit my reasoning for the five hidden traps. Where might I be anchored by initial information? Am I defaulting to the status quo because it is comfortable rather than optimal? Are sunk costs influencing my judgment? Am I seeking confirming evidence? How is the problem framed, and how would the decision change under an alternative frame? Provide a specific counter-measure for each trap you identify."

**Prompt 2 -- RAPID Role Mapping:**
> "My [stage] company needs to make the following decision: [describe decision]. Map the RAPID roles for this decision. Who should Recommend? Who must Agree (and why do they have veto power)? Who Performs? Whose Input is genuinely necessary versus ceremonial? Who Decides? Flag any structural pitfalls -- multiple D's, excessive A's, or too many I's -- and suggest corrections."

**Prompt 3 -- Checklist Walkthrough:**
> "I am deciding [decision] for my [sector] company. Walk me through the seven-step decision checklist. Help me articulate five affected goals, generate at least four realistic alternatives, identify missing information, write a one-year-out impact narrative, determine the right number of stakeholders, and design a follow-up review protocol."

**Prompt 4 -- Negotiation Anchor Defence:**
> "I am entering a negotiation for [describe -- fundraising round, partnership deal, vendor contract]. The other party is likely to set an anchor with their opening position. Help me prepare: define my own position across all negotiable dimensions before the meeting, identify the anchors I am most susceptible to, and construct alternative frames that prevent their opening gambit from constraining my thinking."

## Use Cases

**Pre-Seed Founder Anchored by a VC's Valuation**

A first-time founder with a B2B SaaS prototype receives a verbal indication from an angel investor: "companies like yours are worth about $2M pre-money." The figure is arbitrary -- the investor has seen two comparable deals in the past quarter, both at distressed valuations. Yet the founder begins using $2M as the reference point for every subsequent conversation, adjusting modestly upward but never escaping the anchor's orbit. A disciplined approach would have the founder construct an independent valuation range from multiple data sources (comparable transactions, revenue multiples for the vertical, the specific value of committed pilot customers) before engaging any investor. The anchor defence is preparation: define your own frame before someone else defines it for you.

**Growth-Stage Company Throwing Good Money After a Failed Product Line**

A consumer hardware startup invested $3M and eighteen months developing a smart home device that has sold 2,000 units in six months -- roughly ten percent of the break-even target. The VP of product argues for an additional $1.5M to "fix the go-to-market strategy," citing the original investment as justification. The sunk-cost trap is operating at full force: the $3M already spent is irrecoverable regardless of the next decision. A fresh evaluator -- someone without emotional investment in the original bet -- would assess the $1.5M purely on its expected return. Reassigning the product review to the head of a different business unit surfaces what the original team could not see: the core product thesis (that consumers would pay $400 for a device that replicates $30 of functionality on their phone) was never validated.

**Scaling Company with Unclear Decision Ownership**

A 200-person e-commerce company finds that pricing decisions take six weeks to implement because marketing, finance, product, and the CEO each believe they own the call. A RAPID mapping reveals that marketing should Recommend (they hold the customer data), finance should provide Input (margin constraints), product should Agree (pricing affects positioning), and the CEO should Decide -- but only for pricing changes above a defined threshold. Below that threshold, marketing holds the D. Pricing decisions now take five days.

## Anti-Patterns

**Treating bias awareness as bias immunity.** Knowing about anchoring does not prevent anchoring. The traps are not eliminated by education; they are mitigated by structure. A team that has read this skill and proceeds to brainstorm without any counter-routines will fall into every trap catalogued here.

**Assigning RAPID roles for trivial decisions.** A five-person startup does not need a formal role-mapping exercise for choosing a project management tool. RAPID is designed for decisions where ambiguity about authority creates measurable delay or conflict. Over-applying it bureaucratises what should be a fast-moving organisation.

**Using the checklist as a post-hoc rationalisation tool.** The seven-step checklist works only if applied before the decision is made. Filling it out after the fact -- to justify a choice already taken -- converts a debiasing tool into a confirming-evidence machine.

**Assuming one person's counter-arguments constitute adequate debiasing.** A single devil's advocate can be dismissed. Structural debiasing requires multiple independent perspectives, ideally from people with no stake in the outcome and no history with the decision.

**Conflating the D with seniority.** The person who Decides should be the person closest to the information and most accountable for the outcome -- not necessarily the most senior person in the room. Pushing the D upward by default is a status-quo trap applied to organisational design.

## By Stage

| Stage | Primary Trap Risk | RAPID Focus |
|-------|-------------------|-------------|
| Idea | Confirming-evidence trap -- the founder seeks validation for a pre-existing belief about the market. Framing trap -- the problem definition constrains the solution space before alternatives are explored. | Roles are informal; the founder often holds all five letters. The discipline at this stage is recognising when to solicit genuine I-roles from people who will challenge, not confirm. |
| Validation | Sunk-cost trap -- early investment in an MVP creates pressure to continue rather than pivot. Anchoring on early customer feedback (first ten users set expectations for the next thousand). | The founding team must explicitly assign the D for pivot-or-persist decisions. If no one owns it, the status quo wins by default. |
| Early Traction | Status-quo trap -- processes that worked for ten customers are preserved unchanged for one hundred. Anchoring on founding-era metrics as benchmarks. | RAPID becomes operational. Decisions about hiring, pricing, and feature prioritisation need clear role assignments as the team grows beyond the founders. |
| Growth | All five traps operate simultaneously at scale. Confirming evidence flows freely in organisations that have been successful -- past success anchors future strategy. | The checklist becomes a team discipline, not an individual exercise. Decision follow-ups at step seven must be enforced by calendar, not memory. |
| Scale | The framing trap is most dangerous at scale because the frame is set by layers of middle management before information reaches the decision-maker. Sunk costs are measured in years and departments, not weeks and features. | RAPID must be documented and published for recurring decisions. Ad hoc role assignment fails when hundreds of people are involved. The D must be explicit, visible, and non-negotiable. |
