# Generating Solutions

BRAINSTORM PARADOX. Traditional brainstorming is the most popular idea-generation method in business -- and research consistently shows it produces fewer and worse ideas than the alternatives. No study has demonstrated that verbal group brainstorming outperforms individuals working alone and then pooling results. The reasons are structural, not motivational: production blocking (only one person talks at a time), evaluation apprehension (people self-censor in front of authority), and social loafing (coasting on others' contributions). The method persists because it feels collaborative. Feeling collaborative and being productive are different things. This skill presents four alternatives -- each addressing a different failure mode -- that a founder can select based on the specific obstacle the team faces.

---

## The Toolkit

### Tool 1: Structured Brainstorming (and Why It Usually Fails)

Traditional brainstorming is not useless; it is misused. The technique works when three conditions hold: the facilitator is scrupulously neutral, ideas are recorded without commentary, and participants feel genuinely safe contributing ideas that contradict the most senior person in the room. In practice, all three conditions are violated within the first five minutes of most sessions.

The failure modes:

| Failure Mode | Mechanism | Symptom |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| **Production blocking** | Only one person speaks at a time; others forget or abandon ideas while waiting | The most vocal participants generate most of the recorded ideas |
| **Evaluation apprehension** | Contributors self-censor to avoid judgment, especially from superiors | Ideas cluster around the safe and conventional; no one proposes anything that could look foolish |
| **Social loafing** | Individuals contribute less effort in a group than they would alone | Five people in a room produce fewer ideas than five people working independently |
| **Anchoring cascade** | The first idea voiced sets the frame; subsequent ideas orbit around it | A marketing manager suggests new packaging, and the entire session becomes a packaging discussion |

Structural fixes that salvage the method:
- Have participants generate ideas independently in writing before any group discussion
- Use anonymous submission (digital or physical) to neutralise hierarchy
- Rotate facilitators so no single authority figure shapes the session
- Separate idea generation from idea evaluation by at least 24 hours

### Tool 2: The Question Burst

Developed by Hal Gregersen at MIT, the question burst inverts the standard brainstorming premise: instead of generating answers, the group generates questions. The shift works because questions bypass the cognitive biases that constrain answer-generation -- they suspend the automatic rush to solutions and reframe the problem from multiple angles simultaneously.

**Protocol (15 minutes total):**

1. **Set the stage (2 minutes).** The problem owner describes the challenge in plain language -- no detailed background, no proposed solutions. State what would improve if the problem were solved, and why it has resisted previous attempts. Include two or three people who have no direct experience with the problem; their ignorance is an asset because they will ask the questions insiders have learned not to ask.

2. **Generate questions (4 minutes).** The group produces as many questions as possible, aiming for at least fifteen. Two rules: only questions are permitted (no answers, no preambles, no justifications), and the problem owner records every question verbatim. Time pressure forces brevity and prevents self-censorship.

3. **Select and deepen (9 minutes).** The problem owner reviews the questions privately, selects three that reframe the challenge in unexpected ways, and applies Sakichi Toyoda's "five whys" to each -- asking why each question matters, then why that reason matters, drilling toward root assumptions. The output is not a solution but a redefined problem that opens new pathways.

**Why it outperforms answer-brainstorming:** Gregersen's field data show that eighty percent of sessions produce at least one question that usefully reframes the problem. The method also reverses the power dynamics that sabotage traditional brainstorming -- a junior employee asking "Why do we assume our customers want this feature?" carries less social risk than the same employee proposing "We should kill this feature."

### Tool 3: BrainSwarming

Developed by Tony McCaffrey, BrainSwarming eliminates talking entirely. It replaces verbal brainstorming with a visual, silent, graph-based process inspired by how ants solve problems -- by leaving signals in the environment for others to build upon.

**Structure:**

A large surface (whiteboard, digital canvas) is prepared with the goal at the top and known resources at the bottom.

- **Top-down thinkers** work downward from the goal, decomposing it into progressively more specific sub-goals
- **Bottom-up thinkers** work upward from the resources, identifying novel combinations and applications
- When the two directions connect on the graph, solutions emerge

**No talking.** Participants add sticky notes and draw connections silently. This eliminates production blocking (everyone contributes simultaneously), evaluation apprehension (no one watches you speak), and social loafing (individual contributions are visible on the graph).

Pilot data show BrainSwarming produces up to 115 ideas in fifteen minutes versus roughly 100 ideas per hour for traditional brainstorming -- a sevenfold increase in throughput. The graph structure also preserves the reasoning behind each idea, making it possible to trace how solutions were assembled from component insights.

### Tool 4: Design Thinking's Four Phases

Warren Berger's distillation of design-thinking practice identifies four behaviours that separate breakthrough innovators from incremental improvers. These are not sequential stages in a rigid process but concurrent habits of mind.

**Question.** Designers begin by asking what Berger calls "stupid questions" -- those that challenge the premises everyone else accepts. "How many designers does it take to change a light bulb? Answer: Does it have to be a light bulb?" In a business context, the equivalent is: "What business are we really in?" and "What do today's customers actually need from us?" -- questions that feel naive but that expose assumptions invisible to insiders.

**Care.** Genuine empathy requires leaving the corporate bubble and immersing in the daily lives of users. Focus groups and surveys are proxies for caring; observation is the real thing. The distinction matters because unarticulated needs -- the ones customers cannot express in a questionnaire -- are where the most valuable solutions live.

**Connect.** Innovation often comes not from invention but from recombination -- what John Thackara calls "smart recombinations." Nike combined a running shoe with an iPod; Apple combined existing technologies into interfaces no one had assembled before. The discipline is lateral search: looking far outside the problem's domain for components that could be combined in new ways.

**Commit.** Ideas become tangible through rapid prototyping -- napkin sketches, foam models, digital mock-ups. Giving form to an idea early means risking early failure, but designers understand that small failures are data. The iterative cycle of prototype, test, learn, and revise is faster and cheaper than the analytical cycle of plan, perfect, and launch.

### Tool 5: Could vs. Should

Research by Francesca Gino at Harvard Business School demonstrates that reframing "What should we do?" as "What could we do?" produces measurably more creative solutions. The "should" frame activates trade-off thinking -- the mind narrows to the most obvious answer. The "could" frame suspends judgment and expands the option set.

In one experiment, participants facing ethical dilemmas with no good choice generated significantly more creative resolutions when asked what they "could" do rather than what they "should" do. A separate experiment found that groups given apparently conflicting goals (build a novel product and keep costs low) produced more original work than groups given a single goal -- the tension itself forced creative synthesis.

The practical application: when a team presents a binary choice ("should we expand into Europe or focus on North America?"), reframe it as "what could we do?" and watch the option set multiply. The maître d' at a Michelin three-star restaurant in Modena, when a child ordered pizza at a restaurant that does not serve pizza, did not ask what he should do. He asked what he could do -- and sent a taxi to the best pizzeria in town.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Method Selection:**
> "My team needs to generate alternatives for [describe challenge] at my [stage] [sector] company. Diagnose which brainstorming failure modes are most likely given my team's composition: [describe team size, seniority mix, introvert/extrovert balance]. Recommend the best idea-generation method from this toolkit (structured brainstorming, question burst, BrainSwarming, design thinking, could-vs-should reframe) and explain why it addresses the specific failure mode."

**Prompt 2 -- Question Burst Facilitation:**
> "I am stuck on this problem: [describe challenge in two sentences]. Act as a question burst facilitator. Generate twenty questions about this problem -- no answers, no preambles. Include at least five questions that challenge my fundamental assumptions, three that reframe the problem from the customer's perspective, and three that come from an outsider who knows nothing about my industry. Then identify the three questions most likely to open new solution pathways."

**Prompt 3 -- BrainSwarming Graph:**
> "My goal is [describe goal]. My available resources are [list resources: team skills, budget, technology, partnerships, customer relationships]. Build a BrainSwarming graph: decompose the goal into sub-goals (top-down), then analyse the resources for novel combinations (bottom-up). Identify at least three points where top-down and bottom-up connect to form potential solutions."

**Prompt 4 -- Could-vs-Should Reframe:**
> "My team has framed this decision as a binary: [Option A] vs. [Option B]. Apply the could-vs-should reframe. Generate at least five additional options we have not considered, including at least one that combines elements of A and B, one that redefines the problem entirely, and one inspired by how a completely different industry solves an analogous challenge."

## Use Cases

**Validation-Stage Healthtech Team Stuck on a Single Feature Concept**

A five-person team building a remote patient monitoring platform holds weekly brainstorming sessions that consistently produce variations on the same theme: adding more clinical metrics to the dashboard. The CEO, a former clinician, speaks first in every session, and the team's ideas orbit her initial framing. Diagnosing the failure modes reveals anchoring cascade (the CEO's clinical lens sets the frame) and evaluation apprehension (junior engineers self-censor around a domain expert). Switching to BrainSwarming -- silent, parallel, graph-based -- eliminates both. The top-down decomposition of "improve patient outcomes" produces sub-goals the CEO had not considered (reduce caregiver burnout, increase medication adherence). The bottom-up analysis of resources surfaces a partnership with a pharmacy chain that the engineering team had discussed informally but never raised in a meeting. The two directions connect in a solution: automated medication reminders sent to both patient and caregiver, fulfilled through the pharmacy partnership. The idea never surfaced in six months of verbal brainstorming.

**Early-Traction Marketplace Reframing Through a Question Burst**

A freelance-talent marketplace is growing supply (freelancers) at three times the rate of demand (clients). The founding team frames this as a marketing problem: "How do we acquire more clients?" A question burst with two outsiders (a logistics founder and a former teacher) produces seventeen questions in four minutes, three of which reframe the challenge entirely: "What if the freelancers are the customers?" "What would happen if you charged freelancers instead of clients?" "Is the problem too few clients or too many of the wrong freelancers?" The third question triggers a five-whys analysis that reveals the real issue: the platform's open registration attracts freelancers whose skills do not match client demand. The supply-demand imbalance is not a marketing problem -- it is a curation problem. The solution (a selective application process for freelancers) reduces supply growth, increases match quality, and improves client acquisition as a second-order effect.

**Growth-Stage Consumer Brand Escaping Binary Framing**

A D2C skincare brand generating $8M in revenue frames its next move as "launch in retail or stay online." The could-vs-should reframe produces five additional options: wholesale to a single prestige retailer as a test (bullets before cannonballs), a pop-up collaboration with a complementary brand, a subscription tier that deepens online revenue without retail risk, licensing the formulations to an established retailer, and a hybrid -- an exclusive online-to-offline experience in one city. The hybrid option, invisible under binary framing, becomes the eventual choice and generates more press coverage than either original option would have alone.

## Anti-Patterns

**Running brainstorming sessions without diagnosing the failure mode first.** The choice of method should depend on the obstacle. If the problem is production blocking, BrainSwarming fixes it. If the problem is a badly framed question, a question burst fixes it. If the problem is binary thinking, the could-vs-should reframe fixes it. Using the wrong tool for the failure mode wastes the session and reinforces scepticism about structured ideation.

**Allowing the most senior person to speak first.** Regardless of which method is chosen, the highest-ranking person in the room sets the anchor the moment they open their mouth. In structured brainstorming, have the most senior person contribute last or in writing. In a question burst, they participate as an equal questioner, not as a framer.

**Generating ideas and evaluating them in the same session.** The entire point of ideation is divergent thinking -- expanding the option set. Evaluation is convergent thinking -- narrowing it. Combining both in a single meeting guarantees that evaluation kills the weaker-sounding ideas before they have time to develop. Separate generation from evaluation by at least a day.

**Treating design thinking as a four-phase waterfall.** Question, care, connect, and commit are concurrent habits, not sequential stages. A team that completes an "empathy phase" and then never returns to observation is performing a ritual, not practising design thinking.

**Stopping at idea generation.** The most creative option list in the world has zero value if no one commits to evaluating and acting on it. Every ideation session should end with a clear next step: who will evaluate which options, by when, and using what criteria. The question burst methodology explicitly requires a three-week action plan. Adopt the same discipline for every method.

## By Stage

| Stage | Best Method | Rationale |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Idea | Question burst + design thinking (Question, Care) | The founder's problem definition is the highest-leverage variable. Reframing the problem through questions and genuine user empathy produces better founding hypotheses than generating solutions to an unvalidated problem. |
| Validation | BrainSwarming + could-vs-should | The team needs divergent alternatives for MVP design, pricing, and positioning. BrainSwarming's speed and inclusiveness suit small teams with limited time. The could-vs-should reframe prevents premature commitment to the first viable option. |
| Early Traction | Structured brainstorming (with fixes) + question burst | The team is large enough for group dynamics to matter but small enough that structural fixes (silent writing, rotating facilitation) are feasible. Periodic question bursts prevent the anchoring cascade that develops as the company's success narrative solidifies. |
| Growth | All methods in rotation | Different challenges demand different tools. Product decisions may benefit from BrainSwarming; strategic decisions from question bursts; customer-experience decisions from design thinking. The discipline is matching method to failure mode, not defaulting to whichever method the team used last. |
| Scale | Question burst + design thinking (Care, Connect) | At scale, the primary failure mode is institutional -- the organisation's own success story frames every problem. Question bursts from outsiders and design-thinking immersion in customer reality counteract the gravitational pull of "how we've always done it." |

The recurring danger across all stages: confusing the volume of ideas with the quality of alternatives. A session that produces fifty variations on the same theme has not generated fifty alternatives. It has generated one alternative and forty-nine echoes. The measure of a productive ideation session is the diversity of the option set, not its size.
