# Mentoring and Coaching

MULTIPLIER. One mentor can alter the trajectory of an entire organisation -- not by running it, but by developing the people who do. The arithmetic is compounding: a leader who mentors three people, each of whom mentors three others, has shaped twelve leaders within a single generation. Yet most mentoring relationships are accidental, unstructured, and ultimately disappointing. The mentor offers vague wisdom. The mentee nods politely. Both parties gradually stop scheduling meetings. The failure is not one of intention but of craft. Vineet Chopra and Sanjay Saint's six-part framework for great mentoring -- choose carefully, establish a team, run a tight ship, head off rifts, don't hold back, prepare successors -- treats mentoring as a discipline with standards, not a favour dispensed on goodwill.

The distinction between mentoring and coaching matters. Mentoring is a long-arc relationship focused on career trajectory, institutional navigation, and wisdom transfer. Coaching is a conversational practice -- often brief, often daily -- focused on helping someone solve their own problems. A leader needs both capabilities. The first builds the next generation. The second builds the next day.

## The Framework

### The Six Disciplines of Great Mentoring

**Discipline 1: Choose Mentees Carefully**

Mentoring is an investment, not a charity. A good mentee demonstrates curiosity, organisation, responsibility, and engagement. The diagnostic is simple: assign a small task -- read a book and discuss it within a month, attend a strategy session and write up observations. What returns tells you everything about their thinking process, communication discipline, and genuine interest. Mentees who expect the mentor to sustain the relationship, or who insist on doing things exclusively their own way, are signalling that the investment will not compound.

**Discipline 2: Build a Mentorship Team**

No single mentor possesses everything a protege needs. The effective mentor identifies what areas the mentee wants to develop, maps those areas to people in their network with relevant expertise, and recruits them as auxiliary advisors. The mentor remains the primary source of career, moral, and institutional support, but the team approach ensures the mentee has breadth of access. Peers who contribute to someone's growth with a lower level of commitment often find the arrangement energising rather than burdensome.

**Discipline 3: Run a Tight Ship**

Ground rules established at the outset prevent the slow erosion that kills most mentoring relationships. Three conversations are required early:

- What does the mentee want from the relationship?
- What is the mentor willing to provide -- and what falls outside scope?
- What cadence, communication norms, and escalation procedures will govern the relationship?

Accountability is non-negotiable. The mentor must make clear that second-rate work reflects on both parties. Standards are not punitive; they are protective.

**Discipline 4: Head Off Rifts**

Disagreements are inevitable. The mentor's responsibility is to address them immediately -- not to let friction accumulate until the relationship quietly dissolves. The repair protocol: initiate the conversation (do not wait for the mentee to raise it), ask for their perspective, resist rushing to judgment, and identify the root cause. Open, frank relationships are the mechanism through which mentoring creates value. Without candour, the relationship is decorative.

**Discipline 5: Don't Hold Back**

Power dynamics in mentoring can corrupt. The mentor must guard against five specific misuses:

| Behaviour | Why It Happens | Why It Destroys |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Taking credit for the mentee's ideas | The mentor's ego conflates guidance with authorship | Erodes the mentee's confidence and public recognition |
| Insisting the mentee advance the mentor's projects | The mentor confuses mentoring with delegation | Converts a developmental relationship into an extractive one |
| Tethering the mentee to the mentor's timeline | Comfort with the status quo overrides the mentee's growth | Slows the protege's career progression unnecessarily |
| Discouraging other mentors | The mentor's ego requires exclusivity | Isolates the mentee from broader learning and recognition |
| Allowing self-destructive mistakes to continue unremarked | Conflict avoidance masquerading as respect for autonomy | Permits damage that honest feedback would have prevented |

**Discipline 6: Prepare Successors**

The mentor's primary job is to transfer wisdom. The secondary job -- equally important -- is to identify mentees who can become mentors themselves. Candidates need true expertise and a generous disposition. The preparation involves transparency about the mentor's own decision-making process: how problems are analysed, solutions weighed, and support calibrated. Co-mentoring a new mentee is an effective bridge until the successor is ready to operate independently. The ultimate measure of a mentor's success is not the protege's achievements but the protege's capacity to develop others.

### Daily Coaching Micro-Moments

Formal mentoring programmes are insufficient. Real development happens in the five-minute check-in, the post-meeting debrief, the hallway observation. Three daily practices fill the gap:

1. **Check-in questions.** "How are things going?" is a start, but "What are your career goals, and how can I help?" signals genuine investment. Ask regularly, not ritualistically.
2. **In-the-moment feedback.** When you observe an employee in action -- a presentation, a negotiation, a difficult conversation -- offer specific feedback: what they did well, what could improve, and why. Timeliness matters; feedback delivered a week later loses 80% of its developmental impact.
3. **Coaching stance over directing stance.** The instinct to tell people what to do is deeply embedded, particularly in leaders who built their reputation on decisive action. Coaching requires the opposite: listen first, ask questions, withhold judgment, and draw wisdom from the person being coached. The goal is helping them learn to solve problems independently, not solving problems for them.

Herminia Ibarra and Anne Scoular's research is direct: leaders who rate themselves as good coaches consistently overestimate their skill. The diagnostic is whether the other person leaves the conversation with their own insight or with the leader's instruction. If it is the latter, it was not coaching.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Mentee Selection Assessment:**
> I am considering mentoring [describe the person -- role, experience, what drew your attention]. Help me design a selection test: a small, revealing task that will surface their curiosity, organisation, communication quality, and genuine interest. The task should take no more than two weeks and produce an artefact I can evaluate. Also suggest three questions I should ask myself after reviewing their work.

**Prompt 2 -- Mentorship Team Design:**
> My mentee wants to develop in these areas: [list areas]. My own expertise covers: [list areas]. Identify the gaps where I cannot provide adequate guidance. Then describe the profile of the auxiliary mentors I should recruit -- what expertise they need, what commitment level is realistic, and how I should approach them.

**Prompt 3 -- Mentoring Relationship Audit:**
> I have been mentoring [person] for [duration]. Here is the current state of the relationship: [describe -- frequency of contact, recent interactions, any tensions, progress observed]. Audit the relationship against the six disciplines: selection quality, team support, ground rules, rift management, power dynamics, and succession preparation. Identify which disciplines are strong and which need intervention. Provide specific actions for the weakest two.

**Prompt 4 -- Daily Coaching Shift:**
> I tend to direct rather than coach. Here is a recent interaction where I told someone what to do instead of helping them figure it out: [describe the situation and what you said]. Rewrite my side of the conversation using a coaching approach -- questions instead of answers, drawing out their thinking instead of imposing mine. Then give me three questions I can use in similar situations going forward.

## Use Cases

**Early-Traction Startup: Founder Mentoring First Managers**
A founder with 25 employees has promoted three individual contributors to management. None has managed before. Rather than sending them to a generic management course, the founder applies the six disciplines: selects carefully (these three demonstrated curiosity and responsibility as ICs), builds a mentorship team (recruits a seasoned COO from their investor's portfolio company as an auxiliary advisor), establishes ground rules (bi-weekly 30-minute check-ins, specific preparation expected from each mentee), and commits to in-the-moment coaching after every difficult management conversation. Within six months, all three managers are conducting their own coaching conversations with direct reports -- the compounding has begun.

**Growth-Stage Company: VP Recovering from a Mentoring Failure**
A VP of Product mentored a promising director for two years. The relationship ended badly: the director felt micromanaged, the VP felt the director was ungrateful. A post-mortem against the six disciplines reveals failures in discipline three (no ground rules established), discipline four (a disagreement about project ownership festered for three months), and discipline five (the VP had been insisting the director advance VP-level projects rather than developing their own work). The VP restructures their approach for a new mentoring relationship, beginning with explicit ground rules, a shared document tracking goals and concerns, and a quarterly check on power dynamics.

**Scale-Stage Organisation: Building a Mentoring Culture Without a Programme**
A 400-person company's HR team wants to launch a formal mentoring programme. The CEO, having seen formal programmes devolve into bureaucratic matching exercises, instead trains all directors in daily coaching micro-moments: check-in questions, in-the-moment feedback, and the coaching-over-directing stance. Each director is asked to identify one person whose development they will actively invest in -- not through programme paperwork, but through the six disciplines. The result: 30 active mentoring relationships, each with ground rules and accountability, none requiring programme administration.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Mentoring as Autobiography.** The mentor who treats every conversation as an opportunity to narrate their own career. The mentee's context is not a prompt for the mentor's reminiscence. Great mentoring is about the mentee's trajectory, informed by -- not replaced by -- the mentor's experience.

2. **The GROW Model as the Entire Coaching Repertoire.** GROW is a powerful conversational structure (covered in detail in the frameworks category). But leaders who apply it rigidly to every interaction -- including five-minute hallway conversations -- turn coaching into a formulaic exercise. Daily coaching micro-moments require fluency, not frameworks. Know when structure helps and when it hinders.

3. **Confusing Sponsorship with Mentoring.** Sponsorship is about using your influence to create opportunities for someone. Mentoring is about developing their capacity to create opportunities for themselves. Both are valuable. Conflating them produces a protege who advances positionally without developing the capability to sustain that advancement.

4. **Avoiding Difficult Feedback to Preserve the Relationship.** Allowing a mentee to repeat self-destructive patterns because correcting them feels uncomfortable. This is not kindness; it is negligence. The mentor who does not hold back -- discipline five -- serves the mentee's long-term interest at the cost of short-term discomfort.

5. **Scaling Mentoring Through Technology.** Believing that a mentoring platform, matching algorithm, or scheduling tool can substitute for the relational investment that makes mentoring work. Technology can support logistics. It cannot manufacture the trust, candour, and accountability that define a productive mentoring relationship.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Early Traction | Founder as primary mentor | The founder mentors the first wave of managers directly. The mentorship team is small or external (advisors, investors). The six disciplines apply in compressed form -- ground rules and accountability are particularly critical because everyone is learning simultaneously. |
| Growth | Mentoring the mentors | The leader's task shifts from direct mentoring to developing others' mentoring capability (discipline six). The mentorship team expands. Daily coaching micro-moments become the primary development mechanism because formal mentoring cannot scale to every employee. |
| Scale | Cultural embedding | Mentoring becomes an organisational expectation, not a personal choice. The leader's role is to model mentoring behaviour, resource it (time, not software), and hold leaders accountable for developing their people. The ultimate diagnostic: does development happen when the CEO is not watching? |
