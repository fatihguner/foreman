# Team Culture and Dynamics

Every team has a temperature. The experienced leader reads it the way a pilot reads instruments -- not by staring at any single gauge but by registering the pattern across many: who speaks first in meetings and who waits; whether laughter is frequent or forced; how quickly Slack messages receive responses; whether disagreements happen in the room or in the corridor afterward; the tone of the last three emails from the same person. None of these signals is diagnostic on its own. Together, they constitute the emotional weather system of the team.

The theoretical architecture of team dynamics is well mapped. Tuckman describes the stages (forming, storming, norming, performing); Belbin catalogues the roles; Hackman identifies the enabling conditions. Those frameworks live in the `frameworks` directory and should be studied for their structural insight. This skill concerns itself with the question those frameworks do not fully answer: what does a leader actually do, day by day, to build and maintain a culture that converts individual talent into collective performance?

## The Practice

### Culture Is Manageable

Boris Groysberg, Jeremiah Lee, Jesse Price, and Yo-Jud Cheng's research contains a sentence that should unsettle every leader who treats culture as atmospheric and uncontrollable: "Culture can, in fact, be managed." The first step is awareness -- understanding how culture works, what values it embodies, and what behaviours it rewards and punishes. The second step is deliberate intervention. Most leaders produce detailed plans for strategy and execution but leave culture unmanaged, relegating it to HR as a secondary concern. The result: culture becomes an emergent property of accumulated defaults, not a designed system.

### Writing the Unwritten Rules

Liz Fosslien and Mollie West Duffy offer the simplest and most underused culture intervention: write down the unwritten rules. Every team operates on implicit norms that insiders absorb over time and newcomers struggle to decode. Making them explicit serves three purposes: it reduces the anxiety of new hires who cannot tell what is acceptable, it surfaces norm conflicts that have been festering below conscious awareness, and it creates a document that can be revisited when circumstances change.

The method: convene the team and brainstorm an "It's OK to ..." list.

Examples:
- "It's OK to say you don't understand a process."
- "It's OK to turn off your camera during a virtual meeting."
- "It's OK to go for a short walk in the middle of the day."
- "It's OK to push back on a deadline if you explain why."
- "It's OK to ask for help before you're stuck, not after."

The list should be living, not laminated. Revisit it quarterly, or whenever the team's composition or circumstances shift significantly.

### When the Vibe Is Off: A Diagnostic Framework

Liane Davey identifies four domains to investigate when a team feels stuck, stale, or subtly wrong:

**1. Mandate and Goals**
- What external trends require adaptation?
- How do shifts in company strategy affect the team?
- How is the team's value within the organisation evolving?
- How can success metrics be improved?

**2. Strategies and Roles**
- Is the team strategy aligned with its mandate?
- What tactics need revision?
- Does anyone need a change in role?
- What assumptions could endanger current plans?

**3. Dynamics and Norms**
- What norms need to be established or revised?
- How can conflicts be resolved more effectively?
- What activities could improve trust and candour?

**4. Communication Habits**
- What channels can be used more effectively?
- What information should be synchronous versus asynchronous?
- How can distractions be reduced?
- What are reasonable expectations for response times?

The diagnostic works best as a group conversation, not a leader's solo analysis. The act of discussing these questions together is itself therapeutic -- it surfaces problems that individual conversations cannot reach.

### The Right Mix of Personalities

Dave Winsborough and Tomas Chamorro-Premuzic's research complements Belbin's role theory with a personality-type lens. Five types matter:

| Type | Characteristic | Value to Team |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| Results-oriented | Organise work, take charge | Drive execution and accountability |
| Relationship-focused | Form genuine connections, read emotions | Build cohesion and trust |
| Process followers | Reliable, detail-oriented, conscientious | Ensure quality and consistency |
| Innovative thinkers | Curious, imaginative, open to novelty | Generate ideas and challenge assumptions |
| Pragmatic | Practical, prudent, level-headed | Ground the team in reality |

A team dominated by one or two types produces predictable dysfunction: all innovators and no process followers create exciting chaos; all process followers and no innovators create reliable mediocrity. The leader's job is to diagnose which types are overrepresented and which are missing, and to hire or reassign accordingly.

### Building Social Connections

Ron Friedman's research, in partnership with learning company ignite80, establishes that social connection and team performance are not merely correlated -- they are causally linked. Two practices matter most:

**Bond over non-work topics.** Discussing hobbies, families, and shared interests yields closer friendships and better teamwork. The leader who restricts all communication to task-related content may be efficient but is building a team that lacks the relational resilience to survive pressure.

**Make appreciation a team norm.** Recognition is more motivating than monetary rewards, and peer-to-peer recognition is often more valued than top-down praise. Build rituals -- a weekly shout-out, a rotating "MVP" acknowledgement, a shared channel for appreciation -- and participate in them without dominating them.

### Cross-Functional Collaboration Traps

Lisa Kwan's research identifies a blind spot in most cross-functional initiatives: leaders focus on logistics, processes, and incentives while ignoring how the request to collaborate makes teams feel. When teams are told to share information, sacrifice autonomy, or cede defining responsibilities, insecurity triggers defensive behaviour. The leader who plans a cross-functional project should explicitly address the emotional dimension: acknowledge what teams are giving up, explain what they gain, and create structures that protect each team's identity while enabling collaboration.

### Staffing for Innovation

Nathan Furr, Kyle Nel, and Thomas Ramsoy identify three capabilities essential for high-profile, uncertain projects:

1. **Comfort with uncertainty** -- People who remain curious when the endpoint is unclear.
2. **Structure in chaos** -- People who create frameworks and drive action when circumstances change.
3. **Connect and execute** -- People who see links between disparate information and produce tangible outputs.

These are not the same as "high performers." A team of individually excellent people who all prefer certainty will stall the moment a project enters ambiguous territory.

### Fighting Toxic Culture with Microculture

Annie McKee addresses the leader who operates within a toxic larger culture. The prescription: create a microculture. Hold meetings -- plural, because one is not enough -- where team members discuss shared values, articulate rules of engagement, and codify norms in a team code of conduct. The microculture will not change the company, but it can protect the team.

### The Learning Curve Mix

Whitney Johnson's model provides a diagnostic for team engagement: at any given time, approximately 15% of the team should be at the bottom of a learning curve (new to their role or responsibilities), 70% should be in the productive middle (building and applying skills), and 15% should be at the top (mastering their current domain and ready for the next challenge). When someone reaches mastery and no new curve is offered, disengagement follows. The leader's job is to keep the curves cycling.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Team Temperature Read:**
> Based on these observations about my team: [describe meeting dynamics, communication patterns, recent interpersonal incidents, energy levels, and any signals that concern you]. Diagnose the team's "temperature" across Davey's four domains (mandate/goals, strategies/roles, dynamics/norms, communication). For each domain, rate it as healthy/strained/critical and recommend one intervention per domain.

**Prompt 2 -- Unwritten Rules Workshop Design:**
> I want to run an "It's OK to ..." session with my team of [size] people. Design a 45-minute workshop: opening framing (why we're doing this), facilitation method (how to generate the list without groupthink), and follow-up plan (how to keep the list alive). Include five example prompts to get the conversation started if the team is hesitant.

**Prompt 3 -- Personality Mix Audit:**
> Here are my team members and brief descriptions of their working styles: [list names and one-sentence descriptions]. Map each to the five personality types (results-oriented, relationship-focused, process follower, innovative thinker, pragmatic). Identify which types are overrepresented and underrepresented. Recommend whether the gap should be addressed through hiring, role adjustment, or development.

**Prompt 4 -- Cross-Functional Launch Plan:**
> I am launching a cross-functional project involving [list teams]. Using Kwan's framework, identify the emotional risks: what is each team being asked to give up? What defensive behaviours might emerge? Design the project kickoff communication to explicitly address these concerns and propose structural safeguards that protect each team's identity while enabling collaboration.

## Use Cases

**Early-traction startup where the culture is the founder's personality**

A 12-person startup has no documented norms. The culture is whatever the founder does on any given day -- which means it is unpredictable. New hires take three months to decode what is acceptable, and two have already left citing "cultural fit" without being able to articulate what the culture actually is. An "It's OK to ..." session produces a list of 20 norms that the founder had assumed were obvious. Documenting them reduces new-hire anxiety, cuts onboarding confusion, and -- unexpectedly -- forces the founder to confront two norms they had been enforcing unconsciously that were actively harmful.

**Growth-stage company where the vibe is off but nobody can say why**

A 75-person company has been growing quickly, and the leadership team senses malaise without being able to diagnose it. A structured Davey diagnostic reveals that the mandate domain is healthy (everyone understands the mission), but the communication domain is critical: the team has outgrown its communication norms. What worked for 30 people (all-Slack, instant responses expected) is suffocating 75 people. New norms are established: asynchronous by default, synchronous by exception, expected response times explicitly stated, and two meeting-free mornings per week.

**Scale-stage company staffing an innovation initiative**

A 500-person company assigns its highest-performing engineers to a new product exploration team. Six months later, the team has produced nothing. A Furr/Nel/Ramsoy analysis reveals that every team member is results-oriented and process-following -- excellent at execution, paralysed by ambiguity. Restructuring the team to include people comfortable with uncertainty and capable of connecting disparate ideas produces a prototype within eight weeks.

## Anti-Patterns

**1. Confusing culture with perks.** Free lunch, ping-pong tables, and unlimited PTO are not culture. Culture is how people treat each other when the project is late, the client is angry, and the budget is halved. Perks are decorative; norms are structural.

**2. Managing individuals, not the team.** Linda Hill's research identifies this as one of the most common new-manager mistakes. Optimising each person's satisfaction independently can produce a collection of happy individuals who do not function as a unit. The leader must attend to group dynamics, shared norms, and collective performance, not just individual KPIs.

**3. Ignoring the emotional dimension of collaboration.** Cross-functional initiatives that treat teams as interchangeable resource pools, without acknowledging the identity and autonomy being surrendered, produce defensive behaviour that manifests as passive resistance, hoarding, or sabotage.

**4. Leaving unwritten rules unwritten permanently.** Every team has implicit norms. Refusing to surface and document them does not make them less powerful; it makes them less equitable, since insiders benefit from tacit knowledge that outsiders must guess.

**5. Assuming culture is set at founding and persists automatically.** Culture degrades without maintenance. Every new hire, every reorganisation, every market shift applies pressure to existing norms. Leaders who assume the culture will self-perpetuate discover, usually too late, that it has mutated into something they would not have chosen.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Early Traction | Founding norms, "It's OK to ..." documentation | Culture is set now, consciously or not; deliberate is better than default |
| Growth | Communication norms, personality mix, microculture vs. company culture | Norms that worked at 20 people break at 80; active redesign is required |
| Scale | Organisational culture architecture, cross-functional dynamics, learning-curve management | Culture becomes a systems problem requiring policy, measurement, and structural intervention |

At early traction, the team is small enough that culture can be shaped through daily behaviour. The founder's habits -- how they handle disagreement, whether they celebrate effort or only results, how they respond to mistakes -- are the culture. Documentation at this stage feels premature but is precisely the intervention that prevents cultural drift as the team grows.

At growth, the critical transition is from founder-driven culture to manager-mediated culture. The founder can no longer personally model norms for every employee. Middle managers become the primary culture-carriers, which means they must be explicitly equipped to understand and transmit the culture -- not just execute tasks.

At scale, culture requires the same rigour as strategy: measurement, feedback loops, and structural reinforcement. Engagement surveys, norm audits, cross-functional trust metrics, and learning-curve analyses become the instruments through which culture is maintained -- not as bureaucratic overhead but as the operating system on which performance runs.
