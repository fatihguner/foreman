# Leading Through Change

Map the emotional terrain before you map the project plan. People cycling through significant organisational change -- a restructuring, a pivot, a merger, a leadership transition -- move through five feelings, not always in order, and often revisiting earlier ones:

1. **Denial** -- "This will not actually affect my work." The comforting fiction that the announcement is theoretical.
2. **Anxiety** -- "What does this mean for me?" The self-preservation instinct that no amount of corporate messaging can bypass.
3. **Anger** -- "Why wasn't I consulted?" The feeling of agency being stripped away, which may be directed at the change itself or at the people leading it.
4. **Grief** -- "I miss how things were." The mourning of routines, relationships, and identities attached to the old order.
5. **Adaptation** -- "I can see my place in this." The tentative engagement with a new reality, contingent on feeling heard and included.

Most change-leadership failures occur because the leader, having already processed these feelings privately over weeks or months of planning, expects the organisation to arrive at adaptation within days. Erika Andersen's research is direct: you have had weeks to think about this change; your employees are hearing about it for the first time. Of course they ask questions. Of course they worry. That is not resistance. That is the normal human response to losing certainty.

Kotter's eight-step model provides the structural architecture for change management (see `kotter-change-model.md`). This skill operates in the spaces between those steps -- the daily emotional labour that determines whether a structurally sound change process succeeds or stalls.

## The Practice

### Ask Before You Tell

Patti Sanchez's research identifies empathetic communication as the single most important leadership behaviour during change. The practice is counterintuitive for leaders accustomed to providing answers: before telling people what is happening, ask them what they are worried about.

The sequence:
1. Explain what is changing and why, with as much transparency as the situation permits.
2. Ask: "What concerns do you have?" and "What improvements would you like to see?"
3. Listen without defending, correcting, or minimising.
4. Address what you heard, being honest about what you can and cannot control.
5. Repeat at each phase of the change, because concerns evolve as reality unfolds.

The goal is not to eliminate worry. It is to ensure that everyone feels included and heard -- which is the necessary precondition for the buy-in that makes change executable.

### Give People Time

Andersen's most practical insight: stop trying to talk employees out of their feelings. Doubt and scepticism are not obstacles to be removed; they are stages to be traversed. The leader who responds to worry with "Don't worry, this will be great" has communicated two things: that the leader does not understand the employee's experience, and that expressing concern is unwelcome. Neither message builds buy-in.

Instead, give people time to process. Acknowledge that the emotions they are feeling are reasonable. Ask about their concerns without being dismissive. Talk through what the change means for them specifically, not just for the organisation abstractly. The time invested in this emotional processing is not a delay in the change process -- it is the change process. Without it, compliance replaces commitment, and compliance does not survive the first setback.

### Give People Choices

Andersen's second principle addresses the mechanism behind much change resistance: loss of agency. Organisational change often makes employees feel they are at the mercy of forces they cannot control. The antidote is to return as many choices as possible:

- Let managers co-create the communication plan for their teams.
- Invite teams to propose timelines for implementing new processes.
- Identify elements that will not change and highlight them explicitly.
- Where the outcome is fixed but the path is flexible, let teams choose the path.

The choices need not be large. Even small decisions -- when to hold the transition meeting, which communication channel to use, which team members will take which new responsibilities -- restore a sense of control that makes the larger change tolerable.

### Caring Transparency

Rasmus Hougaard, Jacqueline Carter, and Rob Stembridge describe "caring transparency" as the practice of being honest about what is on the leader's mind and in the leader's heart, especially when making unpopular decisions. This is not vulnerability for its own sake. It is the recognition that withholding important information -- even with good intentions -- communicates distrust.

The key distinction: caring transparency is not unbounded disclosure. It is the willingness to tell people where they stand, clearly and compassionately, rather than allowing ambiguity to breed worse anxieties than the truth would.

### Courage over Comfort

Hougaard, Carter, and Stembridge also address the leader's own emotional challenge during change: the temptation to choose safety over conviction. Choosing courage -- making decisions based on values rather than quick profits, standing for what you believe even when criticism follows -- can feel vulnerable. But courage, once demonstrated, invites others to do the same. It transforms a change initiative from a top-down mandate into a shared endeavour.

### Patience as a Change Skill

David Sluss's research positions patience not as a personality trait but as a learnable leadership skill. During change, patience serves three functions:

1. **Steadying the team.** A leader who cannot stay calm cannot keep others calm.
2. **Allowing strategy to work.** New approaches take time to produce results; premature abandonment of a sound plan because of early friction is one of the most common change-leadership errors.
3. **Resisting the always-on culture.** The pressure for immediate replies and constant connectivity works against the careful, methodical decision-making that change requires.

### Navigating Personal Identity Transitions

Toubiana, Ruebottom, and Hakak study what happens when a major change -- personal or professional -- upends the leader's own sense of self. Their research identifies four practices for navigating identity disruption:

**1. Create a clean break.** Choose an event -- a date, a milestone, a symbolic moment -- to serve as the dividing line between "before" and "after." Mentally imbue it with significance. Consider telling a colleague or friend to add weight to the transition.

**2. Work through hard emotions.** Acknowledge negative feelings without letting them become permanent residents. If being laid off produces anger and shame, sit with those feelings -- but also identify the positive outcomes the change has produced. The goal is not to suppress negative emotions but to make peace with them.

**3. Focus on your many facets.** Your identity is not reducible to your job title. When professional identity is disrupted, invest in the other elements: family, community, hobbies, friendships. The reminder that you are more than your role can stabilise the ground when the role is shifting.

**4. Craft a linking narrative.** Connect your past and present with a story that highlights continuity of values even as circumstances change. "I used to support my family through salary; now I support them through time" is a narrative that bridges two different chapters without denying the loss involved in the transition.

### Seven-Year Cycles

Avivah Wittenberg-Cox offers a structural perspective: dividing one's life into seven-year periods and mapping the highlights, learnings, and identity shifts of each era. The practice imposes a rhythm -- beginning, middle, end -- that normalises transition. When a significant change arrives, the mental model provides context: "I am nearing the end of one era and entering the beginning of another. I have done this before."

### Chaos as Growth

Rebecca Knight's reframe for organisational upheaval: choose to see chaos as a chance to grow, not a barrier to progress. When leadership changes, restructurings loom, or strategy pivots, the leader's mindset determines whether they -- and their team -- navigate the turbulence or succumb to it. The questions to ask: How do the changes affect my role? What do they mean for the company's future? How could they advance my career or enhance my skills? Staying "heads up" -- scanning the environment for opportunity -- beats "heads down" -- burying oneself in routine until the storm passes.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Emotional Map of Change:**
> I am leading (or about to announce) this change: [describe the change, its scope, and its rationale]. Map the likely emotional journey for three different stakeholder groups: [group 1], [group 2], [group 3]. For each group, predict which of the five feelings (denial, anxiety, anger, grief, adaptation) will be strongest and in what order. Design a communication cadence that addresses each group's emotional arc over the first 90 days.

**Prompt 2 -- Empathetic Communication Script:**
> I need to announce [describe the change] to my team of [size]. Draft the announcement using Sanchez's framework: (a) what is changing and why, (b) what I want to ask them (concerns, hopes, suggestions), (c) what I can commit to transparently, and (d) what I genuinely do not know yet. Include follow-up questions for one-on-one conversations and a timeline for when I will provide updates.

**Prompt 3 -- Choice Architecture for Change:**
> This change is non-negotiable: [describe what must happen]. Using Andersen's framework, identify every element of the change where people can be given a choice about how, when, or by whom the change is implemented. Design a "choices menu" I can present alongside the announcement to restore agency without compromising the initiative's objectives.

**Prompt 4 -- Personal Transition Navigation:**
> I am going through a significant professional change: [describe it -- new role, company restructuring, departure, pivot]. Using Toubiana, Ruebottom, and Hakak's four practices, help me: (a) identify a clean "before/after" marker, (b) name the emotions I need to work through, (c) identify the non-work facets of my identity I should invest in, and (d) craft a linking narrative that connects my past and future through shared values.

## Use Cases

**Early-traction startup pivoting its product**

A 15-person startup is abandoning its original product to pursue a new market. The founder, who has been processing this decision for two months, announces the pivot on Monday and expects the team to be executing by Wednesday. Instead, three engineers who joined specifically because of the original product feel betrayed. The founder implements the ask-before-tell protocol: individual conversations about concerns, explicit acknowledgement that the emotional response is legitimate, and a week of structured transition time before any new-product work begins. Two of the three engineers choose to stay, having been given time and voice.

**Growth-stage company restructuring teams**

A 150-person company reorganises from functional teams to cross-functional squads. The announcement produces widespread anxiety about reporting lines, role identity, and career paths. The leadership team creates a "choices menu": each squad can design its own communication norms, choose its meeting cadence, and propose which responsibilities transfer and which stay. The elements of the change that are non-negotiable (squad structure, key assignments) are clearly stated. The elements that are flexible (how, when, and by whom) are handed to the teams. Resistance decreases measurably within two weeks.

**Scale-stage leader navigating personal identity disruption**

A long-tenured division president is moved to a newly created "strategic initiatives" role as part of a CEO succession plan. The leader interprets the move as a demotion and experiences anger, grief, and a profound loss of identity. Using the seven-year-cycle framework, they map their career into eras and recognise that previous transitions -- from individual contributor to manager, from manager to director -- also felt like losses before they became gains. A linking narrative emerges: "My value has always been in building things that outlast me. The division was one such thing. Strategic initiatives is the next."

## Anti-Patterns

**1. Leading with logic when people need empathy.** The leader who responds to emotional resistance with a business case for change has correctly identified the destination and entirely misjudged the starting point. Logic is necessary but not sufficient; empathy must come first.

**2. Labelling all pushback as "resistance."** Andersen is explicit: questions and concerns during change are not resistance. They are the normal, healthy, and predictable response of human beings who have just lost certainty. The leader who frames all pushback as opposition creates a self-fulfilling prophecy: people stop voicing concerns, not because they have been convinced, but because they have been silenced.

**3. Expecting adaptation on the leader's timeline.** The leader has had weeks or months to process the change. Expecting the team to process it in days is unrealistic and, when enforced through impatience, destructive. Genuine buy-in takes the time it takes.

**4. Eliminating all choice in the name of speed.** Speed and agency are not always in conflict. Giving people choices about implementation details rarely slows the initiative and frequently accelerates it, because people execute plans they helped design more willingly than plans imposed upon them.

**5. Ignoring the leader's own emotional journey.** The leader going through change is also a human being experiencing uncertainty, loss, and identity disruption. Pretending otherwise -- performing certainty while feeling doubt -- eventually produces either burnout or a credibility collapse when the mask slips.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Early Traction | Pivot communication, founder's emotional honesty | The team is small enough for individual conversations; use that advantage |
| Growth | Restructuring, scaling norms, manager enablement | Changes affect people the leader does not personally know; communication must cascade through managers |
| Scale | Enterprise transformation, culture preservation during M&A | Change becomes a programme, not a conversation; structural supports (choice architecture, feedback loops, transition teams) replace personal touch |

At early traction, the leader can speak to every affected person individually. This is an enormous advantage that founders routinely squander by defaulting to all-hands announcements. In a 15-person company, 15 individual conversations -- each tailored to that person's concerns, each asking "What worries you?" -- take a few hours and produce buy-in that a single group presentation cannot.

At scale, the leader cannot reach everyone personally. The critical infrastructure becomes the manager layer: equipping middle managers to have empathetic conversations, providing them with honest information, and giving them the same emotional processing time before expecting them to transmit confidence to their teams. The change process becomes recursive: leadership processes the change, then equips managers, who then support their teams, each layer given its own time and space.
