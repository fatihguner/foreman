# Cooperative Negotiation

POSITIONS TO INTERESTS -- two people fighting over an orange. One wants the peel, the other wants the juice. Same position ("I need the orange"), different interests. The negotiation fails because nobody asks why. This parable, worn smooth by repetition in every negotiation textbook, persists because its lesson remains almost universally ignored. Most business negotiations collapse not from bad faith or insufficient leverage but from a far simpler failure: both sides argue about what they want without ever surfacing why they want it.

Fisher and Ury's principled negotiation framework (covered in the `win-win-negotiation` skill) provides the theoretical architecture -- separate people from problems, focus on interests, generate options, use objective criteria. That skill teaches the blueprint. This one teaches what to say when you sit down across the table.

---

## The Framework

### The Anatomy of a Deal

Every negotiation begins with an issue -- a problem or opportunity that requires agreement. Each party arrives with a position: a concrete statement of how the issue should be resolved. When positions collide, the instinct is to argue harder, concede reluctantly, or walk away. This is the positional death spiral. It escalates tension, damages relationships, and wastes resources.

The cooperative alternative requires a single, decisive move: transition from positions to interests.

**Positions** are concrete, divisible, and visible. They are the stated demands -- price, terms, equity split, delivery date.

**Interests** are intangible, often unarticulated, and frequently invisible even to the person holding them. They include:

- **Ego** -- the need to look competent, respected, successful
- **Fear** -- of loss, exposure, failure, humiliation
- **Relationships** -- preserving a partnership, impressing a boss, honouring a commitment
- **Values** -- fairness, loyalty, autonomy, recognition
- **Circumstances** -- budget cycles, reporting deadlines, personal pressures

The hospital case illustrates the principle with surgical precision. A woman with compression fractures from a botched bed transfer demanded $150,000. Her lawyer set that position. But the woman herself wanted three things: a guarantee of safer protocols, an apology, and continued access to her physician. The litigation manager addressed all three interests directly -- promising procedural review, delivering a genuine apology, and welcoming her back. The case settled for far less than the demand because the demand was never the point.

### The 70/30 Rule

Research consistently finds that roughly 70% of business decisions are driven by emotion, not logic. People overpay for products that make them feel good. They reject objectively superior deals that make them feel diminished. The implication for negotiators is stark: the other party's decision will depend less on what they think about your offer and more on how they feel about themselves when considering it. Your task is to construct a process in which saying yes feels like a victory for them.

### The Roadmap to Resolution

Moving from positions to interests does not happen organically. People resist it because sharing interests feels like surrendering control. A structured process restores that sense of control. Six steps, in strict sequence.

**Step 1: Steer the conversation.** Actively redirect the discussion away from positional haggling. The phrase "Before we get into numbers, can we spend a few minutes understanding what each of us is trying to accomplish here?" is worth more than most opening offers.

**Step 2: Ask questions.** Open-ended questions are the cooperative negotiator's primary instrument. Not cross-examination -- exploration.

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---------------|--------|
| "Why do you need that?" | "Help me understand what's driving that priority." |
| "That's too high." | "Tell me more about how you arrived at that figure." |
| "Can you do better?" | "I'm curious about what a good outcome looks like from your side." |
| "Is that your final offer?" | "What is it about this particular term that matters most?" |

The objective is to get the other side talking at length. People who feel heard tend to share information they did not intend to disclose. That information is your material for crafting a deal.

**Step 3: Listen.** The human brain processes speech at 400-800 words per minute but people speak at 125-175. The excess capacity breeds distraction. Disciplined listening means watching for the unstated -- the ego that cannot be acknowledged, the fear that cannot be articulated. When you detect an unspoken interest, name it gently: "It sounds like making sure your team sees this as a win is important here. Am I reading that right?"

**Step 4: Create options.** Brainstorm freely. Whiteboard it. Invite the other party into the process. Place both sides' interest lists on the board and look for non-competing elements -- the surprising number of interests that do not actually conflict. Generate volume before applying judgement.

**Step 5: Develop options.** Filter for feasibility. Which ideas are logistically achievable? Maintain optimism during this narrowing -- the temptation to abandon the cooperative approach peaks here.

**Step 6: Evaluate options.** The criterion is not "what is best for me" or "what is best for them" but "what doable option best satisfies both sets of interests?" Evaluation comes last. Premature critique kills creative solutions.

### Empathy as Strategy

Abraham Lincoln's observation -- "If you would win a man to your cause, first convince him that you are his sincere friend" -- is not sentimentality. It is a negotiation protocol.

Empathy in this context is not sympathy. The distinction matters. Sympathy says "I feel terrible for you." Empathy says "I understand how you feel, and I understand why you feel that way." The empathetic negotiator comprehends the adversary's position without experiencing it or agreeing with it.

**The Lead-with-Need Model (OURS):**

1. **Observe** -- Watch body language, listen to tone, note what provokes energy or withdrawal. Stop interrupting.
2. **Understand** -- Get the counterpart to explain as much as possible. The goal is comprehension of both the problem and the emotion behind it.
3. **Respect** -- Acknowledge their perspective without conceding your position. "Thank you for sharing that with me. That is very helpful" has never once produced a negative reaction in a negotiation.
4. **Solve** -- Use what you have gathered to build toward common ground. The conversation pivots from adversaries across a table to colleagues facing a shared problem.

**Environmental engineering:** Physical setup matters. Lounge seating rather than a boardroom table. A whiteboard for collaborative ideation. Sitting adjacent rather than across. The law of conformity means people mirror the environment -- a relaxed setting produces relaxed disclosure.

### The Lockheed Martin Playbook

Marillyn Hewson's negotiation with President Trump over the F-35 programme remains a masterclass in cooperative execution against a formidable counterparty. Trump held the most powerful office on earth and was publicly attacking Lockheed's pricing. Hewson's competitive options were limited.

Her response: hire a former Trump staffer to understand his interests. Map those interests -- public "wins," job creation, the image of a skilled dealmaker. Then satisfy every one of them. She discounted planes whose costs were already declining due to economies of scale, announced workforce expansion at the Fort Worth plant, and publicly supported regulatory reform. Trump received his negotiation victory. Lockheed secured current contracts and additional orders worth billions over the following years.

The lesson is not about flattery. It is about diagnosis. Hewson identified that the obstacle was not price -- it was image. Once she addressed the actual interest, the positional conflict dissolved.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Interest Mapping:**
> I am preparing for a negotiation about [describe: partnership, contract, equity, vendor deal]. The other party's stated position is [describe]. Using the positions-to-interests framework, help me: (1) identify at least five possible underlying interests behind their position, including ego, fear, and relationship factors, (2) map my own interests separately from my position, (3) identify where our interests overlap or are non-competing, and (4) suggest three creative options that address both sets of interests. Context: [company stage, relationship history, power dynamics].

**Prompt 2 -- Empathy Deployment Plan:**
> I need to negotiate with [describe counterparty] who is emotionally invested in [describe their concern]. Using the Lead-with-Need model (Observe, Understand, Respect, Solve), help me draft: (1) three open-ended questions to surface their real interests, (2) two empathetic acknowledgement statements that do not concede my position, (3) a proposed physical setup for the meeting, and (4) a bridging statement that transitions from empathy to problem-solving. My situation: [describe].

**Prompt 3 -- Roadmap to Resolution Session Design:**
> I have reached an impasse in a negotiation about [describe]. Both sides are locked in positional bargaining. Design a cooperative session using the six-step Roadmap to Resolution: (1) script my opening statement to redirect the conversation, (2) list ten open-ended questions to surface interests, (3) propose a whiteboard exercise for joint brainstorming, and (4) define evaluation criteria for the options we generate. Current positions: [ours] vs. [theirs]. Relationship importance: [high/medium/low].

**Prompt 4 -- Formidable Counterparty Strategy:**
> I am negotiating with a counterparty who holds significantly more power than I do -- [describe: large client, dominant investor, regulatory body, acquirer]. A competitive approach will fail. Using the cooperative negotiation framework, help me: (1) hypothesise their ego, image, and relationship interests, (2) identify low-cost concessions I can offer that satisfy their intangible needs, (3) design a strategy to give them a visible "win" while protecting my core interests, and (4) prepare fallback positions if cooperation is not reciprocated.

## Use Cases

**Early-Traction SaaS Company Negotiating a Channel Partnership**

A 15-person B2B SaaS company needs a distribution partnership with a large systems integrator. The integrator's position: exclusive rights for the enterprise segment, 40% revenue share, and co-branding that subordinates the startup's identity. The founder's position: non-exclusive, 20% share, and independent branding.

Positional bargaining produces a stalemate. The cooperative approach begins with interest mapping. The integrator's interests: their sales team needs a differentiated product to pitch against competitors; their VP of partnerships needs a visible win for the quarterly business review; they fear investing in training only to see the startup sign a rival integrator. The founder's interests: reach enterprise buyers without building a direct sales team; maintain brand identity for future fundraising; avoid dependency on a single channel.

The resolution: a 12-month "preferred partner" designation (not exclusive, but the integrator is the only partner in their specific vertical), 30% revenue share on deals they originate (splitting the difference on economics but tying it to origination), co-branded landing pages with equal logo treatment, and a commitment that the startup will not sign a competing integrator in the same vertical for 12 months. The integrator's VP gets a headline for the QBR. The founder retains optionality. Neither party's stated position survives, but both parties' interests are fully served.

**Growth-Stage Founder Resolving a Co-Founder Dispute**

Two co-founders of a Series A company disagree on strategic direction. One wants to double down on the current market; the other wants to expand into an adjacent vertical. The positional argument has lasted months and is paralysing the executive team.

A cooperative intervention starts with separate interest conversations. Founder A's interests: fears diluting focus and repeating the mistake of a previous startup that expanded too early; wants validation that the current strategy is working. Founder B's interests: sees the current market commoditising and fears being caught without diversification; wants to be seen as the visionary partner, not the operational one.

Neither interest conflicts with the other. The resolution: a 90-day experiment in the adjacent vertical with a capped budget and pre-defined success metrics, run by Founder B, while Founder A leads an intensified push in the core market. If the experiment hits metrics, expansion proceeds. If not, full resources return to the core. Both founders' fears are addressed, both egos are served, and the company gets data instead of arguments.

## Anti-Patterns

**Cooperation as conflict avoidance.** The cooperative approach is not permission to avoid hard conversations. Founders who use "let's find a win-win" as a euphemism for "I'm uncomfortable saying no" will consistently leave value on the table. Cooperation requires the courage to surface difficult interests -- including your own -- and to hold firm on those interests even when the process feels tense.

**Skipping the sequence.** The Roadmap to Resolution has a strict order for a reason. Jumping to option evaluation before interests are fully surfaced produces solutions that address the wrong problem. The most common violation: brainstorming solutions before the other party has finished explaining their perspective. Patience in steps one through three pays compound returns in steps four through six.

**Projecting your interests onto the other party.** Assuming you know what they want without asking is positional bargaining with a cooperative veneer. The hospital litigation manager did not assume the patient wanted money -- he asked, and discovered she wanted an apology. The discipline of genuine inquiry cannot be faked.

**Deploying empathy as a tactic.** When empathy is performed rather than practised, the counterparty detects it. The result is worse than no empathy at all -- it registers as manipulation. Empathy requires genuine curiosity about the other person's experience, even if you plan to negotiate aggressively on substance.

**Abandoning cooperation at the first impasse.** Cooperative negotiation is not a fallback for when competition fails; it is a different operating system. Switching back to competitive bargaining at the first sign of difficulty signals that the cooperative posture was insincere. Commit to the process or do not begin it.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Co-founder alignment and early advisor relationships | Interests are personal and existential -- identity, vision, fear of failure. The Roadmap to Resolution prevents founding teams from entrenching positions before the company even exists. |
| Validation | First customer deals and pilot negotiations | Weak BATNA makes cooperation essential, not optional. Surface the pilot customer's underlying fears (risk, internal credibility) and build the deal around removing those fears rather than arguing price. |
| Early Traction | Vendor contracts, partnership terms, early hires | Negotiations multiply rapidly. Establish cooperative norms now -- they compound. An early hire negotiation that surfaces the candidate's real interests (autonomy, learning, equity upside) costs less and retains longer than one that only haggles salary. |
| Growth | Fundraising, enterprise sales, strategic alliances | Multi-stakeholder negotiations demand interest mapping at scale. Each investor, board member, and procurement officer has distinct interests. Map them individually, not as a monolith. |
| Scale | M&A, cross-border deals, regulatory negotiation | Cultural adaptation of empathy becomes critical. The Lead-with-Need model must account for indirect communication norms, hierarchical decision-making, and relationship-first cultures. |
