# Finding Your Stories

INVISIBLE GOLD -- You live through story-worthy moments every day but record none of them. Homework for Life is the mining operation.

The framework behind this skill draws on a list of more than five hundred untold story ideas. Most professional storytellers have a handful. The gap is not talent or life experience. It is a system. Three techniques for extracting stories from the raw material of ordinary existence were discovered and codified. The techniques are disarmingly simple. Their cumulative effect is not.

The core insight is counterintuitive enough to bear stating plainly: the most compelling stories are almost never the dramatic ones. They are small moments at a dining-room table, a two-in-the-morning walk with an ageing dog, a child's whispered sentence in the morning light. These moments happen constantly. They pass unnoticed because no one has trained themselves to see them. The dramatic events -- the near-death experiences, the arrests, the crises -- make serviceable stories, but audiences connect most deeply to the small and recognisable. Everyone has felt alone at two in the morning. Almost no one has been robbed at gunpoint.

For entrepreneurs, this reframes the perennial complaint: "I don't have any good stories for my pitch." The stories are there. The problem is an untrained eye.

## The Framework

### Technique 1: Homework for Life

Homework for Life is the signature practice. It is also, by the framework's own assessment, the single most important technique in the craft. The method:

**At the end of every day, ask one question:** If I had to tell a five-minute story from today -- as benign, boring, or inconsequential as it might seem -- what would it be?

**Write the answer in a spreadsheet.** Two columns: date and story. No more than a sentence or two. Not the full story -- just enough to recall the moment later.

| Date | Story |
|------|-------|
| 11/5/15 | Taught Clara about the Rolling Stones while lying in bed with her. |
| 11/9/15 | Man gets in line at Southwest Airlines, has moment with me, now my best friend, now trying to skip line, now a jerk, but still, my only friend. |
| 11/14/15 | Clara makes snowman almost without my help. So proud. So afraid that she won't need me much longer. |

The constraints are deliberate. A spreadsheet forces brevity. Brevity ensures the habit survives a busy day. Five minutes is the maximum investment. Miss one day and you permit yourself to miss two; miss two and you abandon the practice entirely.

**What happens over time:**

1. **A storytelling lens develops.** Within weeks, practitioners begin noticing story-worthy moments as they happen rather than retrospectively. Experienced practitioners report recognising most moments in real time.

2. **Forgotten memories resurface.** Recording a present moment frequently triggers associations with the past. An entry about walking a dog in the rain at two in the morning triggered a forgotten childhood memory of a father disappearing underwater, and then a separate memory from a golf course at midnight. One entry. Three recovered stories.

3. **Patterns emerge.** Seeing recurring themes across entries reveals truths about yourself that isolated incidents conceal. One practitioner discovered he fights with his wife not through words but through angry chore completion -- a pattern only visible across multiple entries.

4. **Time slows.** Multiple practitioners report that the habit of daily reflection makes life feel longer and more significant. Throwaway days disappear. A woman who had never felt like "an important person in this world" called six months after starting the practice, crying, to say it had changed her life. She never gave her name.

**The entrepreneurial application is direct.** A founder who has been doing Homework for Life for six months before a fundraise will have a reservoir of personal moments from which to draw. The moment a customer said something unexpected. The evening a co-founder's offhand remark reframed the entire product thesis. The morning the founder realised the team had been solving the wrong problem. These moments are the raw material of compelling pitches, and they are invisible without a system.

### Technique 2: Crash and Burn (Freewriting)

Crash and Burn is an adaptation of stream-of-consciousness writing, tuned specifically for story generation. Where Homework for Life captures the present, Crash and Burn excavates the past.

**The method:** Set a timer for ten minutes. Write without stopping. Follow three rules:

**Rule 1: Do not get attached to any idea.** When a new thought crashes in -- however unrelated or inferior it seems -- abandon the current thread immediately. The intersections between disparate ideas are where recovered memories and new stories live.

**Rule 2: Do not judge.** Grammar, punctuation, penmanship, and quality are irrelevant. Everything lands on the page. The inner editor is locked outside the room.

**Rule 3: The pen never stops.** When the mind empties, list colours (red, green, blue, black, brown...) or numbers in word form until a new thought triggers. The physical act of continuous writing keeps the associative engine running.

A single ten-minute session produced six potential stories, three anecdotes, three possible articles, and one addition to his personal bucket list (committing a petty crime at sea to be technically classified as a pirate). The session began with the word "grape" and ended with an unopened letter from his father.

**For entrepreneurs,** Crash and Burn is the pre-meeting preparation tool. Fifteen minutes of freewriting before a board meeting, investor conversation, or keynote produces not polished material but raw connections -- the unexpected link between a childhood memory and the company's origin, the forgotten conversation that crystallised the product vision. These connections, refined later, become the moments that separate a memorable pitch from a forgettable one.

### Technique 3: First Last Best Worst

Adapted from an idea by The Moth's artistic director Catherine Burns, First Last Best Worst is a structured prompt exercise. It works as a solo brainstorming tool, an improv game, and -- the text notes with practical enthusiasm -- an excellent activity for long car rides and first dates.

**The method:** Create a grid. The columns are labelled First, Last, Best, and Worst. The rows are prompts -- any category of human experience.

| Prompt | First | Last | Best | Worst |
|--------|-------|------|------|-------|
| Job | [Your answer] | [Your answer] | [Your answer] | [Your answer] |
| Failure | [Your answer] | [Your answer] | [Your answer] | [Your answer] |
| Customer | [Your answer] | [Your answer] | [Your answer] | [Your answer] |
| Partnership | [Your answer] | [Your answer] | [Your answer] | [Your answer] |
| Risk | [Your answer] | [Your answer] | [Your answer] | [Your answer] |

After completing the grid, analyse it with three questions:

1. Do any entries appear more than once? (Recurring entries signal likely stories.)
2. Could any entry become a useful anecdote?
3. Could any entry become a fully realised story with a transformation?

Testing showed that nineteen of twenty-eight entries from standard prompts yielded potential stories. Even when students deliberately chose uninteresting prompts (tree, toaster, socks, flag), eight stories emerged from twenty possibilities.

**The entrepreneurial variant** replaces personal prompts with business ones: first customer, worst hire, best pivot, last rejection, worst meeting, first investor conversation. The grid fills in and fills out the founder's narrative landscape, connecting moments that seemed isolated into a coherent personal history of the venture.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Homework for Life Setup:**
> I want to start a daily story-finding practice. I am a [role] at a [type of company] at [stage]. Design a Homework for Life spreadsheet template for me with the two-column format (date, story) and provide ten example prompts I can use on days when nothing seems story-worthy. Also suggest three recurring themes I should watch for based on my role that are likely to produce material useful for [pitches / team talks / board updates / customer conversations].

**Prompt 2 -- Crash and Burn Session Guide:**
> I have a [pitch / keynote / team address] in [timeframe] on the topic of [topic]. Guide me through a Crash and Burn freewriting session. Give me a starting word related to my topic, then after I share my raw output, identify the threads worth saving: potential stories, anecdotes, recovered memories, and unexpected connections that could strengthen my [deliverable].

**Prompt 3 -- First Last Best Worst for Founders:**
> Generate a First Last Best Worst grid with these business-relevant prompts: first customer, worst failure, best decision, last rejection, first hire, worst meeting, best feedback, worst assumption. After I fill in my answers, analyse the grid for recurring entries, potential stories with transformation arcs, and useful anecdotes. Flag any entry that could serve as an opening for an investor pitch.

**Prompt 4 -- Story Reservoir Audit:**
> I have been collecting moments for [duration]. Here are my ten most recent Homework for Life entries: [paste entries]. Analyse them for: (a) which entries contain a genuine five-second moment of transformation, (b) which are anecdotes that could support a larger story, (c) what patterns emerge across entries, and (d) which entry has the highest potential for a [pitch / keynote / team narrative]. For the top candidate, suggest the transformation arc (beginning state to ending state).

## Use Cases

**Idea-stage founder preparing for an accelerator interview**

A first-time founder has a technically sound product concept but struggles to articulate why she personally cares about the problem. She has been doing Homework for Life for three weeks. Reviewing her entries, she notices a cluster of moments related to her mother's struggle with a broken healthcare system -- a pattern she had not consciously recognised. One entry, just eight words long, captures the afternoon her mother gave up trying to schedule a specialist appointment. That entry becomes the opening of her accelerator pitch. The judges remember her application not for the TAM slide but for the eight-word moment that made the problem human.

**Validation-stage team running a First Last Best Worst session before a customer-discovery sprint**

A three-person team preparing for fifty customer interviews runs First Last Best Worst with prompts tailored to their market: first time you encountered [problem], worst workaround you have seen, best customer reaction, last assumption that proved wrong. Each team member fills the grid independently, then they compare. Overlapping entries reveal shared assumptions; divergent entries reveal blind spots. Two entries from the worst-workaround column become opening anecdotes for customer conversations, replacing the clinical problem statements they had been using. Interview engagement improves measurably.

**Scale-stage executive building a personal speaking practice**

A COO who speaks at three to four industry events per year has been recycling the same company origin story for two years. Audiences have heard it. She begins Homework for Life and, within two months, accumulates forty-seven entries. Nine contain transformation moments. She now has enough material for three years of distinct keynote openings, each rooted in a genuine personal moment rather than the corporate narrative. Her speaking invitations increase because event organisers value speakers who bring new material.

## Anti-Patterns

**1. Writing too much.** The spreadsheet format exists to enforce brevity. A sentence or two per entry. Writing full paragraphs converts a five-minute habit into a thirty-minute obligation, and obligations die quickly. The goal is capturing a trigger for memory, not composing a draft.

**2. Waiting for the dramatic moment.** The most common failure mode is recording only "big" events and ignoring the small ones. The most popular story in the framework involves a wife revealing she knew a childhood secret -- a moment at a dining-room table. Stories about near-death experiences, while exciting, connect less deeply. Founders who only record their biggest wins and losses miss the quiet moments that audiences find most compelling.

**3. Skipping days and abandoning the practice.** Miss one day and you will miss two. Miss two and the week is gone. The discipline is non-negotiable. Only a "tragically small percentage" of the thousands taught this method actually sustain the practice. The ones who do report life-changing results. The ones who do not have no stories to show for it.

**4. Using Crash and Burn as outlining.** Crash and Burn is not a planning exercise. Attempting to steer the freewriting toward a predetermined topic defeats the associative mechanism that produces unexpected connections. The entrepreneur who sits down to "freewrite about my pitch" will produce pitch notes, not stories. Start with an unrelated object in the room and let the mind wander.

**5. Treating First Last Best Worst as trivia.** The exercise is not about identifying facts (my first car was a Honda). It is about locating moments of emotional significance attached to those facts. The answer "Honda Civic" is useless. The answer "Honda Civic -- the morning I drove away from my parents' house for the last time and realised I was terrified" is a story seed.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | Personal moments that reveal why you care about this problem | The founder's story inventory IS the company's narrative capital |
| Validation | Customer-interaction moments that shift your understanding | Homework for Life entries increasingly feature other people's words and reactions |
| Early Traction | Team moments, pivot moments, failure moments | Story material expands beyond the founder; the practice should spread to co-founders |
| Growth | Encouraging managers to maintain their own story practices | Narrative capacity must scale with the organisation; one storyteller is not enough |
| Scale | Institutional story collection across functions | Systematised story-gathering (customer stories, team stories, field stories) mirrors Homework for Life at organisational level |

At the idea stage, a founder with sixty days of Homework for Life entries possesses something no slide deck can replicate: a reservoir of genuine, personal, transformation-bearing moments. Investors who have heard ten pitches that day will remember the one that opened with a real moment, not a market statistic.

At scale, the challenge is distributing story-finding capability. A single executive with a rich story practice cannot supply narrative material for an organisation of hundreds. The techniques taught here -- particularly Homework for Life and First Last Best Worst -- transfer well to team workshops, offsites, and onboarding programmes. They are inexpensive, require no special tools, and produce results within weeks.
