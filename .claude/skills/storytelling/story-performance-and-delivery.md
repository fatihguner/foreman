# Story Performance and Delivery

The best storytellers do not perform on stages -- they perform at dinner tables, in meeting rooms, and in elevator pitches. The principles are identical. The record holder for Moth StorySLAM victories insists that the gap between a great written story and a great told story is bridged by a handful of concrete, trainable techniques: the strategic use of present tense, scene-based memorisation instead of word-for-word recitation, deliberate word selection, controlled vulnerability, and the discipline to keep talking no matter what. None of these require natural charisma. All of them require practice and intention.

---

## The Framework

### Present Tense as the Default

The present tense creates immediacy. It functions as a temporal magnet, pulling the audience out of whatever room they occupy and into whatever room the story occupies. "I'm standing in the parking lot" places the listener in that parking lot more forcefully than "I stood in the parking lot." The past tense describes. The present tense transports.

The technique is demonstrated by writing an entire chapter in real time, on an Amtrak train, while his five-year-old son refuses to use the restroom. The reader is on the train. The reader feels the rocking. The reader worries about the boy's bladder. That is the present tense at work.

**When to shift to past tense:**

- **Backstory.** When slipping into events that precede the main narrative, the past tense signals temporal distance. A story cannot have two present-tense timelines without diluting both. The backstory is in the past; present it as such.
- **Moments best told from a distance.** Not every scene benefits from the audience being pulled inside it. Some moments -- particularly those involving discomfort, vulnerability, or physical specifics -- are more effective with a layer of temporal remove.
- **Returning to present tense after backstory** functions as a reset. It pulls the audience back into the primary timeline. The effect is like pressing a button on an arcade racing game: shifting the camera from an overhead view back to the driver's seat.

**Tense shifts should be instinctual, not mechanical.** In live storytelling, speakers shift tenses constantly -- often mid-sentence -- and audiences never notice. The shifts become visible only on the written page. The principle: when the emotional intensity of a moment demands closeness, shift to present. When distance serves the narrative, shift to past. When in doubt, stay present.

Not every speaker can tell stories in the present tense. Some have spent years in the past tense, and the cognitive load of switching overwhelms their delivery. There is nothing wrong with telling stories exclusively in the past tense. A well-told past-tense story beats a poorly told present-tense story without exception.

### The Two Ways of Telling a Hero Story

No one likes a braggart. Proverbs, da Vinci, and Mark Twain all agree on this point, and audiences confirm it nightly. Failure is more engaging than success. The story of a strikeout with the bases loaded is more compelling than the story of a walk-off home run. Getting fired from a dream job produces better narrative than getting hired.

Yet entrepreneurs must frequently tell success stories: to investors, to recruits, to customers, to the press. The challenge is doing so without becoming insufferable. The framework offers two strategies:

**1. Malign yourself.**

Before telling the success, share a highly imperfect moment. Cast yourself as the underdog. In one example, a story about saving a student's life opens with a scene in which the teller accidentally strikes a child in the head with a thrown shoe. The audience knows he is not pretending to be the world's greatest teacher. By the time the success arrives, it feels earned rather than boasted.

This is why Bruce Willis is barefoot and outnumbered in *Die Hard*. Why the rebels fly a single tiny ship against a star destroyer in the opening of *Star Wars*. Underdog status invites the audience to root for the protagonist. It converts the success story from self-congratulation into something the audience feels they participated in.

**For founders:** Before the fundraising win, describe the seventeen rejections. Before the product breakthrough, describe the version that crashed during the demo. Before the hiring coup, describe the candidate who turned you down. The vulnerability precedes the victory, and the victory is sweetened by it.

**2. Marginalize the accomplishment.**

Rather than telling the complete success, tell a small part of it. Allude to the larger outcome but focus on one step. Human beings prefer stories of small steps over large leaps. Overnight successes are rare and alienating. The step-by-step nature of achievement is what audiences understand and connect with.

In another example, a story about saving one student immediately catalogues the students the teller failed to save. The success is real but circumscribed. The audience connects with the partial, imperfect version more deeply than they would with an unblemished triumph.

**For founders:** The story of achieving product-market fit is best told as the story of one customer's experience. The story of scaling to a hundred employees is best told as the story of one hire who changed the culture. Small steps. Never the panoramic victory lap.

### Scene-Based Memorisation

Do not memorise a story word for word. Actors memorise lines because they have fellow actors and stage directions to anchor them. A storyteller has nothing but the microphone and the audience. When a memorised word goes missing, the storyteller freezes. Speakers have been observed standing in silence for a minute or more, trapped in the gap between a forgotten word and the next remembered one.

Instead, memorise three things:

1. **The first few sentences.** Always start strong. The opening must be rehearsed to the point of automatic delivery.
2. **The last few sentences.** Always end strong. The landing is the part the audience carries with them.
3. **The scenes.** If every moment of the story is anchored in a physical location (per the Cinema of the Mind principle), then the story is a sequence of places. Remember the places, and you remember what happens in each one.

The technique involves remembering scenes as circles in the mind -- varying in size (reflecting the scene's duration) and colour (reflecting its emotional tone). Seven scenes is the maximum. The telephone company settled on seven digits for a reason: it is the upper limit of what most people can retain at once.

If every prepared word flees the speaker's mind, the scenes remain. The storyteller may lose a laugh line or a golden sentence, but the story still gets told. As The Moth's artistic director once observed: "You could certainly mess up your story, but you never stop talking, so I know you'll eventually find your way back."

The principle for entrepreneurs is identical. A pitch is not a script. It is a sequence of scenes. Know the opening, know the close, know the scenes in between. The rest is navigation.

### Words to Use, Words to Avoid

**Profanity.** Use sparingly. Swearing is lazy in most cases; a better word almost always exists. The exceptions: repeated dialogue (someone else's words, reported faithfully), moments of extreme emotion, and the rare occasion when a profane word is genuinely the most precise one available. Avoiding profanity also broadens the contexts in which a story can be told -- from religious institutions to school classrooms to corporate retreats.

**Vulgarity.** Distinct from profanity. Vulgarity is the description of events that are profane in nature. The rule: if the topic would be awkward to discuss with a grandparent, tread lightly. Use euphemism, allusion, and self-deprecation. The audience can fill in the reality without needing it spelled out. Graphic specificity repels; suggestive restraint engages.

**Celebrity and pop-culture references.** Avoid. They alienate the half of the audience that does not recognise the reference. They insert a famous person into the audience's mental movie, breaking the story's internal reality. And they are lazy -- a shorthand that reveals little about character. Instead of "she was like Zooey Deschanel," describe the qualities directly: "She never seemed to have a job but always seemed to have just enough money. She was carefree. The girl who would never grow up."

**Accents.** The rule is simple: do not attempt them. Imitating the accent of someone from another culture risks insensitivity at best, offence at worst. The sole exception is the accents of one's own parents or grandparents, where parental love provides cover. When in doubt, abstain.

**Oddly specific words.** Prefer them. Specificity is inherently engaging and often funny. "Raisin Bran" is better than "cereal." "Pop-Tarts" is better than "snack." Precision of language is not decoration; it is the mechanism by which the audience's mental movie achieves resolution.

### Performing Across Contexts

The techniques above apply identically whether the venue is a thousand-seat theatre or a Thanksgiving dinner table. Context modifies scale, not substance.

**Eye contact.** When the audience is visible (boardroom, dinner table, small stage), find three people who like you -- left, centre, right. These are the smilers, the nodders, the laughers. Use them as guideposts. The people near each guidepost will feel attended to as well. Choosing friendly faces also bolsters the speaker's confidence.

**Microphone use.** If offered a microphone, accept it. Hearing impairment is far more prevalent than most people realise, and few hearing-impaired individuals wish to announce their condition. Three universal principles: (1) the microphone is not magic -- still project to the back of the room, (2) adjust the stand before speaking, not during, and (3) learn to cut through laughter and applause to regain control of pacing.

**Emotional control.** Some stories provoke emotion in the teller. This is natural and, within limits, endearing. The technique for managing excessive emotion: shift perspective from first-person to overhead. Instead of reliving the moment through one's own eyes, watch it from above -- a wounded person, not oneself, seeing friends appear in a doorway. This "B button" (a term drawn from arcade racing games) provides enough distance to continue speaking. Alternatively, inoculate against the power of specific sentences by repeating them dozens of times before performing. Repetition strips some emotion; the audience's presence restores just enough.

**Nervousness.** A moderate amount of visible nervousness is an asset. It signals vulnerability, authenticity, and that the speaker cares about the outcome. Audiences root for speakers who appear human. The exception: contexts where authority is expected (professional conferences, board presentations, State of the Union addresses). In those settings, confidence is required. But in pitches, toasts, first dates, and storytelling stages, nervousness bridges the gap between speaker and audience.

**Never rehearse in front of a mirror.** The only person the speaker will never see during a performance is themselves. Practising before a mirror encourages attention to physical appearance, which is irrelevant. Practise before other people, or before pictures of other people, or before a wall. Anything but a reflection.

## Prompts

**Prompt 1 -- Tense Mapping:**
> Here is a story I plan to deliver live: [paste narrative]. Map it for tense usage. Mark which sections should be told in present tense (high emotional intensity, primary timeline, key reveals) and which in past tense (backstory, moments best told from a distance). Show where tense shifts should occur and explain why.

**Prompt 2 -- Hero Story Redesign:**
> I need to tell a success story about [achievement]. Help me apply the two hero-story strategies: (1) identify a moment of imperfection, failure, or vulnerability I can share before the success to cast myself as an underdog, and (2) identify the smallest step within the larger success that I can focus on, marginalising the accomplishment without diminishing it. Rewrite the story using both techniques.

**Prompt 3 -- Scene-Based Memorisation Plan:**
> Here is my story: [paste]. Break it into no more than seven scenes, each anchored in a physical location. For each scene, provide: the location, the key action or dialogue that occurs there, and the emotional tone (which I will remember as a colour). Then write the first three sentences and the last three sentences, which I will memorise word-for-word.

**Prompt 4 -- Word Audit:**
> Review this narrative for word-choice issues: [paste]. Flag any profanity that could be replaced by a more precise word, any vulgarity that should be softened with euphemism, any celebrity or pop-culture references that should be replaced with direct description, and any generic words that would benefit from odd specificity. Provide suggested replacements.

## Use Cases

**Demo Day Pitch -- Accelerator Cohort**

A founder prepares a three-minute pitch that currently reads as a memorised script. She freezes in rehearsal when she forgets a sentence. Applying scene-based memorisation: the pitch is restructured into four scenes (the customer's office where the problem was observed, the garage where the prototype was built, the Zoom call where the first paying customer signed, and the stage where the founder is standing now). The opening and closing sentences are memorised precisely. Everything in between is navigated by scene. The founder also applies the hero-story framework: before describing the first customer win, she describes the demo that crashed in front of a prospect's entire leadership team. The vulnerability earns the audience's investment in the success that follows.

**Board Presentation -- Growth-Stage Company**

A CEO delivers a quarterly board update that sounds like a recitation of a pre-read document. Board members check their phones. Applying the framework: the CEO shifts to present tense for the narrative sections ("It's the second week of October, and our head of product walks into my office with a chart I've never seen before"). Backstory is delivered in past tense to create temporal contrast. The success metric is introduced using the marginalization technique: rather than presenting the full dashboard, the CEO tells the story of one customer whose usage pattern revealed the insight. The board leans in. The metrics, when they appear, land in a narrative context that makes them memorable.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Memorising word for word.** The single most common cause of catastrophic performance failure in live storytelling and pitching. When a word goes missing, the speaker's mind goes blank. Scene-based memorisation provides a recovery path that script memorisation does not.

- **Telling the entire success story.** Recounting every milestone, every win, every compliment received. The audience disconnects halfway through. Small steps are relatable. Panoramic victory laps are not. The most effective success story is the one that includes the failure that preceded it and the doubt that accompanied it.

- **Defaulting to past tense out of habit.** The present tense is available and powerful, but many speakers never consider it. Past tense is safe and intuitive; present tense is transporting and immediate. The best speakers toggle between the two. The worst speakers do not know they have a choice.

- **Performing for the mirror.** Rehearsing alone while staring at one's own reflection cultivates self-consciousness about appearance, which is the last thing a storyteller should be thinking about during delivery. The words, the pacing, the emotional arc -- these are what matter. The tilt of a head and the position of hands are irrelevant.

- **Avoiding all vulnerability in professional contexts.** The instinct to project flawless competence in pitches, board meetings, and sales calls leads to stories that no audience connects with. Vulnerability -- the admission of a mistake, a fear, a moment of doubt -- is the bridge between speaker and listener. The observation is blunt: failure is more engaging than success. The professional who shares only success shares nothing memorable.

- **Imitating accents.** However well-intentioned, accent imitation in storytelling carries asymmetric risk: modest upside (a moment of flavour) and catastrophic downside (the audience perceives insensitivity). The return-on-risk calculation is unambiguous. Describe the person's qualities. Leave their accent alone.

## By Stage

| Stage | Focus | Key Difference |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Idea | The hero-story problem is acute: founders must sell themselves without sounding like braggarts | Malign yourself early; the idea stage is where vulnerability is most credible and most needed |
| Validation | Present tense shines in customer-discovery storytelling | "I'm sitting across from a nurse practitioner who tells me..." is more visceral than a past-tense summary |
| Early Traction | Scene-based memorisation becomes essential as pitches multiply | Founders tell the same story dozens of times; scene-based structure prevents staleness and enables adaptation per audience |
| Growth | Word choice becomes a brand concern | As the company's stories reach wider audiences, profanity and vulgarity boundaries tighten; oddly specific language becomes a differentiator |
| Scale | Performance delegation -- others now tell the company's story | The CEO must train executives, salespeople, and marketers in these delivery techniques, not merely use them personally |

At the idea stage, the founder is the company's only storyteller. Performance quality and company credibility are one and the same. At scale, the challenge inverts: the founding story is now told by dozens of people who were not there. Scene-based memorisation scales better than scripts because it allows each teller to navigate the story in their own voice while hitting the same emotional beats.

```markdown
## Tense Plan
- **Primary tense:** [Present / Past]
- **Tense shifts:**
  - Shift to [past/present] at [moment] because [reason]
  - Shift to [past/present] at [moment] because [reason]

## Scene Map (max 7)
| # | Location | Key Action / Dialogue | Emotional Tone |
|---|----------|-----------------------|----------------|
| 1 | [Place] | [What happens] | [e.g., light, tense, vulnerable] |
| 2 | [Place] | [What happens] | [Tone] |
| ... | ... | ... | ... |

## Memorised Opening (first 2-3 sentences)
[Write them here, word for word]

## Memorised Close (last 2-3 sentences)
[Write them here, word for word]

## Hero-Story Check
- **Vulnerability shared before success:** [The imperfect moment]
- **Accomplishment marginalised to:** [The small step told, not the full victory]

## Word-Choice Notes
- **Replace:** [generic word] with [specific word]
- **Remove:** [unnecessary profanity / pop-culture reference]
- **Add specificity:** [where]

## Context Adaptation
- **If boardroom:** [Adjust formality, reduce nervousness display, increase authority signals]
- **If pitch stage:** [Allow vulnerability, open with laugh, end on heart]
- **If dinner table:** [Shorten, increase conversational pacing, make eye contact with all]
```
