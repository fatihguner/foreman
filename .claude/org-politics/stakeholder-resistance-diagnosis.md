# Stakeholder Resistance Diagnosis

## Entry Symptoms

The entrepreneur arrives frustrated. "My board won't approve this." "The team is pushing back on everything." "One person is blocking the entire initiative and I don't know why." "I thought we had alignment, but nothing is moving." Resistance takes many forms -- some loud and direct, others quiet and corrosive. The founder who cannot distinguish a valid objection from a territorial reflex will either capitulate when they should push or bulldoze when they should listen.

## Triage Questions

### Q1: Is the resistance coming from individuals or the organization's structure?

- **Specific individuals** -- one or a few named people are the source of opposition. Their removal or conversion would resolve the issue. Proceed to Q2.
- **Structural** -- the resistance is diffuse, emerging from processes, culture, or organizational design rather than specific actors. Multiple people resist for similar reasons. Likely diagnosis: **fear-of-change** or **organizational inertia**. Proceed to Q3 to determine whether the structural resistance has rational underpinnings.

### Q2: Is the resistance public (stated objections) or covert (passive-aggressive, delays, silence)?

- **Public** -- the resister states objections openly, raises concerns in meetings, argues against the initiative visibly. This is the healthier form. Proceed to Q3 to assess the substance of their objection.
- **Covert** -- the resister agrees in meetings but fails to act, delays deliverables, withholds information, or lobbies against the initiative in private conversations. Likely diagnosis: **territorial-defense** or **loss-of-power**. Proceed to Q4 to determine whether the covert resistance stems from genuine concern they are afraid to voice or from political positioning.
- **Mixed** -- some concerns are stated publicly; the real objection remains hidden. The public objection serves as a socially acceptable proxy for the actual resistance. Likely diagnosis: **proxy-resistance**. Proceed to Q5.

### Q3: Is the resistance based on genuine concerns (rational) or political positioning (territorial)?

- **Genuine concerns** -- the resister has data, experience, or logic supporting their objection. If you set aside ego, their argument has merit. Likely diagnosis: **rational-objection**. This is not a political problem; it is an intellectual disagreement that deserves engagement on substance.
- **Political positioning** -- the resister's objection does not withstand scrutiny, but they maintain it because the initiative threatens their territory, budget, headcount, or influence. Likely diagnosis: **territorial-defense** or **loss-of-power**.
- **Information gap** -- the resister's objection is based on incomplete or outdated information. They are drawing reasonable conclusions from an incomplete picture. Likely diagnosis: **information-gap**.

### Q4: Does the resister have veto power or just influence?

- **Veto power** -- they can unilaterally block the initiative through formal authority (board vote, budget control, contract approval). Their resistance is existential to the initiative. Engagement is mandatory; workarounds are unlikely.
- **Influence without veto** -- they cannot block alone but can slow progress, degrade enthusiasm, or recruit other resisters. Their resistance is damaging but not fatal. The question becomes whether to engage directly or build a coalition that makes their resistance irrelevant.
- **Neither** -- they have neither veto power nor significant influence. Their resistance is noise. The risk is overreacting and elevating a minor objection into a political event.

### Q5: Have you engaged the resister directly or are you working around them?

- **Engaged directly** -- you have had a candid conversation and understand their stated objection. Proceed to the Diagnosis Map to match the root cause.
- **Working around them** -- you have avoided direct engagement, preferring to build support elsewhere or escalate above them. This strategy sometimes works but often backfires -- the resister interprets the workaround as confirmation that their concerns were dismissed, entrenching their opposition.
- **Attempted engagement, met with deflection** -- the resister avoids substantive conversation, responds with vague agreement that does not translate to action, or refuses to articulate their objection. Likely diagnosis: **proxy-resistance** or **loss-of-power** -- they are protecting something they cannot publicly admit to protecting.

## Diagnosis Map

| Diagnosis | Key Signals | Confirmation Path |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Rational objection** | Specific, evidence-based concerns; objection survives when you steelman it; resister is open to dialogue | Q3 confirms genuine concerns + Q5 shows direct engagement produces substantive debate |
| **Territorial defense** | Resistance maps to the resister's budget, headcount, or domain; objection is strongest where the initiative overlaps with their territory | Q3 shows political positioning + Q2 reveals covert behaviour in areas of territorial overlap |
| **Fear of change** | Resistance is diffuse, non-specific ("this isn't the right time"), correlated with the magnitude of change rather than its content | Q1 shows structural resistance + Q3 reveals concerns dissolve under scrutiny but reform as new objections |
| **Loss of power** | The initiative would reduce the resister's authority, visibility, or control; resistance intensifies as the initiative gains momentum | Q3 shows political positioning + Q4 reveals the resister is using whatever power they have to slow or block |
| **Information gap** | Resistance based on misunderstanding of the initiative's scope, impact, or rationale; new information genuinely shifts the resister's position | Q3 shows apparent concerns + information correction produces measurable attitude change |
| **Values conflict** | Deep disagreement on direction, priorities, or principles that cannot be resolved by information or compromise; both sides are internally consistent | Q3 shows genuine concerns + Q5 reveals engaged debate that reaches impasse on fundamental premises |
| **Proxy resistance** | Stated objection does not match the intensity of resistance; the resister appears to represent someone else's interests; objection shifts when addressed | Q2 shows mixed signals + Q5 reveals deflection or the stated objection changes shape repeatedly |

## Routing

**Rational objection:**
1. Apply `thomas-kilmann-conflict` -- use Collaborating mode to integrate the resister's valid concerns into a stronger initiative
2. Apply `win-win-negotiation` -- find the solution that addresses both your initiative's goals and their legitimate concerns
3. Output with `stakeholder-influence-map` -- update the resister's position from blocker to potential contributor

**Territorial defense:**
1. Apply `cialdini-influence` -- Reciprocity (offer something that acknowledges their domain) and Unity (frame the initiative as expanding, not encroaching on, their territory)
2. Apply `coalition-and-partnership-games` -- assess whether including the resister in the initiative's governance structure stabilizes the coalition
3. Output with `resistance-management-plan` -- document the territorial concern and the engagement strategy

**Fear of change:**
1. Apply `persuasion-and-influence` -- build the narrative that makes the change feel manageable and desirable
2. Apply `cialdini-influence` -- Social Proof (show that similar changes have succeeded elsewhere) and Commitment (secure small initial agreements that build momentum)
3. Output with `resistance-management-plan` -- design a phased engagement that reduces perceived risk

**Loss of power:**
1. Apply `trust-equation` -- assess the relationship foundation before attempting influence
2. Apply `coalition-and-partnership-games` -- determine whether the resister can be given a meaningful role in the new structure that preserves their influence
3. Output with `stakeholder-influence-map` -- map the power shift and design an approach that addresses the real concern

**Information gap:**
1. Apply `persuasion-and-influence` -- design the communication that closes the gap
2. Apply `trust-equation` -- ensure the information source is credible to the resister
3. Output with `stakeholder-influence-map` -- update and track position change after information delivery

**Values conflict:**
1. Apply `thomas-kilmann-conflict` -- assess whether Collaborating mode can find a synthesis, or whether Compromising or Competing is necessary
2. Apply `resolving-people-conflict` -- facilitate the conversation that surfaces the values difference explicitly
3. Decision point: if the values conflict is irreconcilable, the initiative may need to be redesigned, or the organizational relationship may need to be restructured

**Proxy resistance:**
1. Apply `trust-equation` -- build enough trust with the proxy to surface the real principal
2. Apply `coalition-and-partnership-games` -- map the hidden coalition structure to identify who is actually driving the resistance
3. Output with `stakeholder-influence-map` -- map both the proxy and the principal, then design engagement for the principal

## Red Flags

- **The resister's objection changes every time you address it.** This is a hallmark of proxy resistance or loss-of-power masquerading as rational concern. The real objection is not being stated.
- **Resistance intensifies after a private meeting with another stakeholder.** Someone is coordinating opposition. Map the network before responding.
- **You are told "everyone feels this way" but cannot identify specific people.** Manufactured consensus is a political tactic. Verify independently.
- **The resistance is framed as concern for the company but maps precisely to the resister's personal interests.** This is territorial defense wrapped in organizational language. Name it -- privately, carefully, but clearly.
