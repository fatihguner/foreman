# Power Dynamics Diagnosis

## Entry Symptoms

The entrepreneur senses a disconnect between the visible and the real. "I pitched to the VP but the decision was made by someone I never met." "Decisions we made in the meeting were reversed the next day." "I keep being told to talk to different people and nothing moves." "Someone is pulling strings and I cannot see who." Power in organizations flows through channels that the org chart does not depict. Founders who navigate by title alone will miss the informal networks, personal loyalties, and information asymmetries that determine outcomes.

## Triage Questions

### Q1: Is this about FORMAL power (authority, titles, budget control) or INFORMAL power (influence, relationships, information access)?

- **Formal** -- the question is about who holds decision authority, budget sign-off, or veto rights. The confusion is structural: reporting lines are unclear, authority is ambiguous, or multiple people claim the same decision rights. Proceed to Q4.
- **Informal** -- the question is about who influences decisions behind the scenes, regardless of title. The org chart is clear; the power flow is not. Proceed to Q2.
- **Both** -- formal authority exists but is overridden by informal influence. The person with the title defers to someone without it. Proceed to Q2 and Q4 simultaneously.

### Q2: Are decisions made in meetings or outside them?

- **In meetings** -- decisions are made where you can observe them. The power structure is visible even if complex. Focus on who speaks, who is deferred to, and whose objections carry weight. Likely diagnosis: **formal-authority-mismatch** or **competing-power-centers**.
- **Outside meetings** -- meetings ratify decisions already made in hallway conversations, private dinners, or pre-meetings. The real power operates in spaces you may not be invited to. Likely diagnosis: **shadow-influence-network**.
- **Decisions reverse after meetings** -- agreement is reached in the room, then overturned afterward. Someone with power was either absent or silent during the meeting and exercised influence later. Likely diagnosis: **shadow-influence-network** or **gatekeeper-bottleneck**.

### Q3: Who does the decision-maker listen to most?

- **A specific trusted advisor or confidant** -- the decision-maker delegates cognitive authority to a single person. That person's opinion effectively determines the outcome. Likely diagnosis: **shadow-influence-network** with a single node of concentrated informal power.
- **The last person who spoke to them** -- the decision-maker is susceptible to recency, and multiple influencers compete for the final word. Likely diagnosis: **competing-power-centers** where influence is unstable.
- **Data and evidence** -- the decision-maker is genuinely analytical. Influence depends not on relationships but on the quality and control of information. Likely diagnosis: **information-as-power** -- whoever controls the data controls the decision.
- **No one -- they avoid deciding** -- the decision-maker does not listen because they do not decide. Authority exists on paper but is not exercised. Likely diagnosis: **power-vacuum**.

### Q4: Is there a gatekeeper controlling access to the decision-maker?

- **Yes -- a specific person filters access, information, or requests** -- the gatekeeper shapes decisions by controlling what the decision-maker sees, hears, and from whom. Likely diagnosis: **gatekeeper-bottleneck**. The gatekeeper's interests may or may not align with yours.
- **No -- access is open but influence is unequal** -- anyone can reach the decision-maker, but some voices carry more weight. Proceed to Q3 to determine why.
- **The entrepreneur themselves is being gatekept** -- information or access that should flow to the entrepreneur is being filtered by a direct report, partner, or intermediary. The power dynamic is operating against the entrepreneur, not just around them.

### Q5: Has the power structure recently shifted (new person, departure, reorg)?

- **Yes -- recent change** -- power structures are most visible and most volatile during transitions. A new executive changes the influence map. A departure creates a vacuum. A reorg reshuffles formal authority without immediately resettling informal networks. Likely diagnosis: **power-vacuum** if authority has not settled, or **competing-power-centers** if multiple people are vying for the vacated influence.
- **No -- stable for months or years** -- the power structure is entrenched. Mapping it requires observation over time rather than analysis of recent events. Entrenched structures are harder to change but easier to predict.
- **Gradual erosion** -- no single event, but a slow shift in who holds influence. A board member's engagement has waned. A department head's political capital has been spent. Gradual erosion is the hardest to detect and the most dangerous to ignore.

## Diagnosis Map

| Diagnosis | Key Signals | Confirmation Path |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Formal authority mismatch** | Decisions attributed to one person but actually made by another; titles do not predict who controls outcomes; reporting lines are misleading | Q1 shows formal confusion + Q2 shows decisions made in unexpected places |
| **Shadow influence network** | Decisions reverse after meetings; a non-obvious person's opinion consistently predicts outcomes; informal pre-meetings shape agendas | Q2 shows outside-meeting decisions + Q3 identifies a specific trusted advisor |
| **Gatekeeper bottleneck** | Access to the decision-maker is controlled; information is filtered before reaching leadership; one person's approval is a prerequisite for progress | Q4 confirms gatekeeper + Q3 shows decision-maker relies on filtered input |
| **Power vacuum** | Decisions are delayed indefinitely; no one claims authority; escalation produces no resolution; competing proposals persist without adjudication | Q3 shows avoidance of decisions + Q5 reveals recent departure or structural ambiguity |
| **Competing power centers** | Two or more people issue conflicting direction; teams receive contradictory instructions; political alignment with one center alienates another | Q1 shows both formal and informal confusion + Q3 shows decision-maker influenced by the last speaker |
| **Information as power** | One person or group controls data access; decisions are shaped by what information is presented; alternative analyses are suppressed or discredited | Q3 shows data-driven decision-maker + Q4 reveals information is gatekept |

## Routing

**Formal authority mismatch:**
1. Apply `managing-up` -- understand the actual authority structure and adapt engagement strategy accordingly
2. Apply `strategic-thinking-and-alignment` -- align your initiative with the interests of the actual decision-maker, not the nominal one
3. Output with `stakeholder-influence-map` -- redraw the power map with formal and actual authority distinguished

**Shadow influence network:**
1. Apply `trust-equation` -- assess and build trust with the shadow influencer, not just the formal decision-maker
2. Apply `cialdini-influence` -- Liking and Unity principles to build rapport with the influencer; Authority principle to establish credibility
3. Output with `stakeholder-influence-map` -- map the shadow network and design dual engagement (formal decision-maker + shadow influencer)

**Gatekeeper bottleneck:**
1. Apply `cialdini-influence` -- Reciprocity (provide value to the gatekeeper) and Liking (build genuine rapport) to gain access
2. Apply `trust-equation` -- the gatekeeper's trust is the prerequisite to reaching the decision-maker
3. Output with `resistance-management-plan` -- if the gatekeeper is blocking, treat them as a stakeholder with their own interests

**Power vacuum:**
1. Apply `strategic-thinking-and-alignment` -- determine whether to fill the vacuum yourself, advocate for someone to fill it, or restructure to eliminate the need
2. Apply `coalition-and-partnership-games` -- in a vacuum, coalitions determine outcomes; build one before someone else does
3. Output with `coalition-building-plan` -- design the coalition that fills the authority gap for your initiative

**Competing power centers:**
1. Apply `thomas-kilmann-conflict` -- determine the appropriate conflict mode for navigating between competing authorities
2. Apply `coalition-and-partnership-games` -- assess which power center to align with based on Shapley Value logic (whose support contributes most to your initiative's success)
3. Output with `stakeholder-influence-map` -- map both power centers, their supporters, and the contested territory

**Information as power:**
1. Apply `systems-thinking` -- map the information flows and identify where data is being controlled or filtered
2. Apply `cialdini-influence` -- Authority principle to establish your own information credibility; Social Proof to introduce independent data sources
3. Output with `stakeholder-influence-map` -- map information flows alongside power flows

## Red Flags

- **You are consistently surprised by decisions.** If outcomes repeatedly differ from what the visible process predicted, the real power structure is invisible to you. Stop executing and start mapping.
- **A single non-executive's opinion predicts every decision.** Shadow influence concentrated in one person creates a single point of failure and a single point of necessary engagement.
- **Your champion says "I'll handle it" but nothing happens.** Your champion may lack the power they claim, or they may be encountering resistance they are not disclosing. Verify independently.
- **Everyone defers to "the process" but no one owns the process.** Procedural deference without clear ownership is a power vacuum disguised as governance.
