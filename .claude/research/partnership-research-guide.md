# Partnership Research Guide

Partnerships fail more often than marriages, and for similar reasons: insufficient diligence before commitment, incompatible expectations discovered too late, and an asymmetry of investment that breeds resentment. The handshake feels good. Eighteen months later, one side has delivered and the other has not, and the lawyers are expensive.

The antidote is research conducted before the commitment. A prospective partner's financial health, litigation history, employee sentiment, and strategic trajectory are all discoverable — if you know where to look. This guide provides the protocol.

---

## What You'll Learn

- Financial health assessment and reputation profile for each candidate
- Strategic fit evaluation: where interests align and where they might collide
- Culture compatibility signals derived from observable artifacts, not gut feeling
- A risk matrix quantifying dependency, power imbalance, and exit difficulty

## Research Protocol

**Step 1 — Define What You Need from a Partner**
Articulate the gap. What capability, market access, technology, or credibility do you lack? Write a one-sentence need statement. This prevents pursuing a partnership because it feels exciting rather than because it solves a specific problem.

**Step 2 — Identify 3-5 Candidates**
Generate a shortlist through industry mapping, competitor analysis, investor introductions, and LinkedIn searches. Three candidates is the minimum for meaningful comparison.

**Step 3 — Financial Health Screen**
For public companies, pull the most recent 10-K and 10-Q from EDGAR. Examine revenue trends, debt ratios, and cash position. For private companies, use Dun & Bradstreet credit reports as a proxy. Red flags: declining revenue for two consecutive quarters, debt-to-equity above 3:1, or recent layoffs exceeding 10%.

**Step 4 — Reputation Check**
Search PACER for litigation involving the company. Check Glassdoor for recurring themes in employee reviews — isolated complaints are noise; patterns about leadership, ethics, or broken promises are signal. Search news archives for the past three years.

**Step 5 — Strategic Fit Assessment**
Map each candidate's products, customers, and growth trajectory against your own. The ideal partner has complementary strengths, not overlapping ones. Assess whether your roadmaps converge or diverge over 2-3 years.

**Step 6 — Reference Calls (3 Per Candidate)**
Speak with three people who have worked with each candidate: one current partner, one former partner, and one industry observer. Key questions: What did the partnership deliver versus what was promised? How did the partner behave when things went wrong?

**Step 7 — Build the Risk Matrix**
For each candidate, score dependency risk, power imbalance (relative size, revenue dependency, brand leverage), and exit difficulty (contractual lock-in, integration depth). Weight each factor by relevance to your situation.

## Interpretation Guide

**Financial health thresholds:** A partner with strong revenue growth but negative cash flow is betting on continued funding — acceptable for a startup, concerning for an enterprise partner.

**Glassdoor pattern recognition:** Ignore the star rating. Read the text of negative reviews for recurring themes. "Promises not kept" is the single most predictive phrase for future partnership friction. "Constant pivoting" signals strategic instability.

**Reference call calibration:** Current partners will be diplomatic. Former partners will be honest — but may carry grievances. Industry observers provide the most balanced view. If all three categories express the same concern, treat it as confirmed.

**Risk score interpretation:** No partnership scores zero on all risk factors. A high dependency score with a low exit-difficulty score is manageable — you can leave. A high dependency with high exit-difficulty is a trap.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Brand-name infatuation.** Pursuing a partnership with a prestigious company without verifying that the specific division and team share the parent company's reputation. Partnerships happen between teams, not logos.
- **Symmetry assumption.** If you represent 0.1% of their revenue and they represent 30% of yours, the power asymmetry will shape every negotiation and renewal.
- **Skipping the former-partner call.** Current partners are curated references. Former partners discovered the failure modes. If a candidate cannot provide a former partner reference, that absence is informative.
- **Confusing strategic alignment with cultural compatibility.** Compatible products and still-failing partnerships usually trace to process mismatch: one side moves fast while the other requires six levels of approval.
