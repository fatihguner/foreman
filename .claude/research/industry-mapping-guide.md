# Industry Mapping Guide

## Purpose

Every strategic framework assumes you understand the industry you operate in. Porter's Five Forces requires knowing who the suppliers and buyers are. SWOT demands an honest assessment of external threats. Scenario planning needs an inventory of forces that could reshape the landscape. This guide builds that foundational map -- the territory itself, before you start strategising about it.

## What You'll Learn

- How to define industry boundaries without making them too narrow or too broad
- How to trace the full value chain from raw inputs to end customers
- How to identify every category of player in the ecosystem and their relationships
- How to read signals from job postings, patent filings, and VC investments to detect strategic shifts before they become obvious
- How to spot white spaces where no incumbent has staked a defensible position

## Data Sources

| Source | What It Provides | Access Method |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Industry reports (IBISWorld, Gartner, Forrester) | Market size, growth rates, segmentation, competitive concentration | Subscription or library access; summarise key findings |
| Trade associations | Industry standards, lobbying priorities, membership directories | Association websites; annual reports; event calendars |
| Conference speaker lists | Who the ecosystem considers important; emerging topic trends | Review agendas from the 3-5 largest industry events |
| LinkedIn job postings | Company strategy signals (hiring for AI = building AI; hiring for compliance = regulatory pressure) | Search by company + role keywords; track patterns over 6 months |
| Patent filings (USPTO, EPO, Google Patents) | Technology direction of major players; defensive vs. offensive IP strategies | Search by company, inventor, or technology keyword |
| Regulatory bodies | Current regulations, proposed rules, enforcement actions | Government agency websites; Federal Register or equivalent |
| VC investment databases (Crunchbase, PitchBook) | Where money is flowing; which segments attract capital; emerging players | Search by category, stage, and date range |

## Research Protocol

### Step 1: Define Industry Boundaries

Determine what is inside and outside your industry map. Three boundary tests: (a) substitution -- if a customer could use Product B instead of Product A, they are in the same industry; (b) supply chain -- if Company C provides essential inputs to most players, it is part of the ecosystem; (c) regulation -- if a set of companies falls under the same regulatory framework, they share an industry boundary. Document your boundary decisions explicitly. They will be challenged, and you need defensible reasoning.

### Step 2: Map the Value Chain

Trace the flow from raw inputs to end customer. Identify every stage: who produces, who aggregates, who distributes, who sells, who supports, who finances. For each stage, note: (a) number of players, (b) concentration level (fragmented vs. oligopoly), (c) margin profile (where does value accumulate?), (d) switching costs between stages. The stages where margins are thickest and switching costs highest reveal where power concentrates.

### Step 3: Identify Key Players Per Segment

Within each value chain stage, catalogue the major players. For each: (a) estimated revenue or market share, (b) primary competitive advantage, (c) recent strategic moves (acquisitions, product launches, pivots), (d) funding history and investors. Group players into tiers: dominant incumbents, established challengers, and emerging entrants.

### Step 4: Map Relationships and Dependencies

Draw the connections. Who supplies whom? Who partners with whom? Where do exclusive relationships exist? Where are multi-homing patterns (customers using multiple providers simultaneously)? Identify structural dependencies -- if one player disappeared, who would be most affected? These dependencies reveal both vulnerabilities and acquisition logic.

### Step 5: Identify White Spaces

Overlay the value chain map with customer pain points and unmet needs. White spaces appear where: (a) a value chain stage is underserved or fragmented, (b) adjacent industries have solutions that have not crossed over, (c) technology shifts create new possibilities that incumbents have not addressed, (d) regulatory changes open previously inaccessible segments. Rank white spaces by addressable market size and defensibility.

### Step 6: Assess the Regulatory Landscape

Catalogue current regulations affecting the industry. Identify proposed regulations under review. Note recent enforcement actions and their implications. Assess regulatory trajectory: is the industry heading toward more regulation (fintech, healthtech) or deregulation? Regulatory shifts create both risk and opportunity -- new entrants often exploit the gap between regulation change and incumbent adaptation.

## Interpretation Guide

**High-opportunity industry**: Multiple white spaces, fragmented value chain stages, capital flowing into the sector, regulatory environment stable or newly opened. Good conditions for new entrants.

**Mature, consolidated industry**: Few white spaces, dominant incumbents with high switching costs, declining VC investment, established regulatory framework. Entry requires either deep differentiation or disruption from an adjacent technology.

**Transitioning industry**: Technology or regulatory shifts creating turbulence, incumbents visibly restructuring (evidenced by layoffs and pivots in job posting data), patent filing activity spiking in new domains. High risk, high opportunity -- timing matters enormously.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Drawing boundaries too narrowly**: Defining your industry as only direct competitors blinds you to substitutes and adjacent entrants -- the sources of most disruption.
- **Mistaking a list for a map**: Cataloguing players without mapping their relationships misses the structural dynamics that determine competitive outcomes.
- **Relying solely on published reports**: Industry reports describe last year's landscape. Job postings, patent filings, and VC activity signal next year's.
- **Ignoring the regulatory layer**: Founders building in regulated industries who treat compliance as an afterthought discover too late that it shapes every strategic decision.
- **Static mapping**: An industry map is a living document. Revisit quarterly. The players and power dynamics shift faster than most founders assume.
