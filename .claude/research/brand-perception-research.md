# Brand Perception Research

What you intend your brand to mean and what the market believes it means are frequently two different things. Founders operate inside their own positioning narrative -- the vision, the differentiation, the carefully chosen adjectives on the homepage. Customers form perceptions from fragments: a demo call, a support interaction, a competitor's comparison page, a friend's offhand remark. This distinction matters commercially, not aesthetically. Misaligned brand perception distorts conversion rates, increases churn when onboarding reveals a gap between promise and reality, and erodes pricing power when the market positions you as a commodity you believe you have transcended.

---

## What You'll Learn

- Current brand awareness levels: who recognises your name, and in what context
- Brand associations: the words, feelings, and attributes people connect to you
- Competitive perception positioning: how you rank against alternatives on key attributes
- Social sentiment patterns and employer brand perception
- A gap analysis between desired positioning and actual market perception

## Research Protocol

**Step 1 -- Define Desired Positioning**
Before measuring perception, articulate what you want to be known for: (a) primary attribute, (b) emotional tone, (c) competitive distinction. These three statements become the benchmark against which you measure reality. If you cannot state desired positioning in three sentences, the market has no chance of receiving it clearly.

**Step 2 -- Measure Current Awareness**
Survey 100+ respondents from your target market (not existing customers). Test unaided recall and aided recall. The ratio reveals whether your brand has penetrated beyond your customer base.

**Step 3 -- Map Current Associations**
Ask 50+ respondents: "When you hear [company name], what comes to mind?" Code responses into functional, emotional, category, and negative themes. Frequency quantifies your actual brand territory.

**Step 4 -- Run Competitive Perception Comparison**
Select 3-5 positioning attributes that matter in your category. Survey customers and prospects, rating your brand and 2-3 competitors on each attribute (1-7 scale). This reveals where the competitive perception gap is widest.

**Step 5 -- Social Listening Analysis**
Collect 30 days of brand mentions across Twitter/X, LinkedIn, and Reddit. Categorise by sentiment and topic. Calculate share of voice against competitors. Social listening captures unfiltered perception that surveys polish away.

**Step 6 -- Gap Analysis**
Place desired positioning alongside actual perception data. The gap analysis produces three categories: aligned (market sees what you intend), misaligned (market sees something different), and invisible (no one in the category owns it).

**Step 7 -- Establish Tracking Cadence**
Run the full protocol at baseline, then repeat quantitative components quarterly. Meaningful repositioning requires 6-12 months to register in perception data.

## Interpretation Guide

**High aided recall, low unaided recall -- awareness without salience.** People recognise your name when prompted but do not think of you spontaneously. The fix is sharper positioning, not more volume.

**Associations cluster around a single attribute -- narrow brand.** If 70% of mentions cite "easy to use" and nothing else, your brand occupies a thin perception slice. Powerful if that attribute drives purchase decisions; fragile if a competitor matches it.

**Competitors own the attribute you claim -- uphill repositioning.** When a rival rates higher on your intended differentiator, either invest heavily to overtake their association or pivot to an attribute where you can credibly lead.

**Negative sentiment concentrates on one topic -- containable.** If 80% of negative mentions reference a single issue, fixing it addresses most perception drag. Diffuse negativity across many topics is harder to remediate.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Surveying only existing customers.** Your customers already chose you -- their perception skews positive. Include prospects, lapsed users, and people who have never heard of you.
- **Confusing awareness with perception.** A widely known brand can be widely known for the wrong reasons. Measure what people associate with the name, not just whether they recognise it.
- **Ignoring employer brand.** Glassdoor is public. A 2.8-star employer rating undermines premium positioning regardless of product quality.
- **Running perception research once.** Brand perception is a lagging indicator. A single measurement establishes a baseline but cannot detect change. Quarterly tracking is the minimum.
- **Reacting to individual mentions.** One negative tweet is an anecdote; a recurring theme across 30 mentions is data. Aggregate before acting.
