# Market Sizing Worksheet

A startup that cannot articulate the size of its market has not yet articulated its ambition. Market sizing is not a ritual performed for investors — it is the exercise through which a founder discovers whether the opportunity justifies the sacrifice. Get this wrong and everything downstream inherits the error: your business model targets the wrong segment, your pricing leaves money exposed, and your fundraising pitch collapses under the first skeptical question.

Three numbers matter: TAM (Total Addressable Market), SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market), and SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market). Each represents a progressive narrowing from theoretical ceiling to realistic capture. The discipline lies in the narrowing.

---

## What You'll Learn

- The total value of your market category (TAM)
- The segment you can actually reach with your product and distribution (SAM)
- A defensible estimate of what you can capture in 3-5 years (SOM)
- Cross-validated numbers from at least two independent sizing approaches
- A clear articulation of the assumptions behind each figure

---

## Three Approaches

**Top-Down:** Start with a published industry figure and narrow it. Fast, credible when sourced well, but prone to inflated starting points.

**Bottom-Up:** Start with unit economics — price per customer multiplied by the number of reachable customers. Slower, more credible, and forces you to confront real distribution constraints.

**Value Theory:** Estimate the total value your product creates for customers, then apply a capture rate. Best for new categories where no industry report exists.

A defensible market size uses at least two of these approaches and reconciles the results. If top-down and bottom-up diverge by more than 3x, your assumptions need work.

## Research Protocol

**Step 1 — Define the Market Category**
Write a one-sentence market definition. Be specific. Not "the global software market" but "cloud-based inventory management for mid-market e-commerce retailers." The definition should describe a buyer, a problem, and a solution category.

**Step 2 — Top-Down TAM Estimation**
Find 2-3 industry reports that size your market category or the nearest parent category. Record the figure, the source, the year, and the methodology. If you must use a parent category, estimate your sub-segment as a percentage — and justify that percentage.

**Step 3 — Calculate SAM**
From the TAM, remove segments you cannot serve. Filters include: geography (where you can sell), customer size (who your product fits), channel (who you can reach), and regulatory (where you're permitted to operate). Each filter should reduce the number with a stated rationale.

**Step 4 — Bottom-Up Cross-Check**
Count the number of potential customers in your SAM. Multiply by your expected average revenue per customer. This figure should land within the same order of magnitude as your top-down SAM. If not, revisit your assumptions.

**Step 5 — SOM Estimation**
Apply a realistic market share percentage to your SAM. For a startup entering an established market, 1-5% in year 3-5 is defensible. For a new category, use analogous company growth trajectories instead of share percentages.

**Step 6 — Value Theory Validation (Optional)**
Estimate the cost of the problem you solve per customer per year. Multiply by the number of affected customers. Apply a 10-30% value capture rate. This provides an independent ceiling check.

## Interpretation Guide

**Credible TAM range:** For a venture-backable startup, TAM between $1B and $100B is typical. Below $500M signals a lifestyle business (not wrong — just different). Above $100B usually means the market definition is too broad.

**SAM reality check:** SAM should be 10-40% of TAM for a focused product. If your SAM is 80% of TAM, you have not filtered enough. If it is 2% of TAM, you may be in a niche — validate that the niche is large enough.

**SOM defensibility:** SOM is a bet on execution, not a market fact. Defend it with analogies: "Company X captured Y% of a comparable SAM in Z years with a similar go-to-market." Never cite SOM without naming the assumptions.

**Convergence signal:** If top-down and bottom-up estimates fall within 2x of each other, your sizing has internal consistency. If they diverge by 5x or more, one set of assumptions is materially wrong.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **The trillion-dollar TAM.** Citing a massive parent category ("the global healthcare market is $8.3 trillion") without narrowing to your actual segment. Investors read this as analytical laziness.
- **TAM without SAM/SOM.** Presenting only TAM communicates that you have not thought about who you can actually reach and how much you can capture.
- **Confusing TAM with revenue potential.** TAM is the total spend in the category, not what your company will earn. Your revenue ceiling is SOM, not TAM.
- **Single-source dependence.** Using one industry report as gospel. Reports disagree. Use multiple sources and note the range.
- **Static sizing.** Markets grow. A $5B market growing at 25% annually is more attractive than a $20B market growing at 2%. Always include the growth rate alongside the absolute figure.

Package your reconciled market sizing for use in the following Foreman skills and templates:
