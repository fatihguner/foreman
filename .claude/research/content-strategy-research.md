# Content Strategy Research

Publishing without research is journaling. A founder who writes three blog posts a week without understanding search intent, audience distribution, or competitor gaps is manufacturing noise, not signal. Content strategy research answers three questions before a single word is drafted: what does your audience need to hear, where are they listening, and how will you know it worked? The difference between companies that build audiences and those that broadcast into silence is rarely talent or budget. It is the research that precedes the first published piece -- understanding which topics carry demand, which channels reach your buyers, and which formats earn attention in a saturated landscape.

---

## What You'll Learn

- A prioritized list of topics and keywords mapped to search intent and business value
- A competitive content audit revealing gaps you can exploit and formats that outperform
- A channel selection rationale based on audience behaviour data, not assumption
- A content format hypothesis ready for structured testing
- A 90-day content calendar grounded in research rather than editorial whim
- A measurement framework connecting content activity to business outcomes

## Research Protocol

**Step 1 -- Define the Audience Persona**
Before researching topics, define who you are creating for. Identify 1-2 specific personas: their role, seniority, industry, primary challenges, and where they consume professional content. Use SparkToro to validate assumptions with data -- enter a competitor domain or relevant hashtag and examine the audience profile. A persona without behavioural data is a guess wearing a name tag.

**Step 2 -- Keyword and Topic Research**
Generate 20-30 topics from three sources: (a) questions your sales or support team hears repeatedly, (b) keyword tools showing volume and difficulty, (c) Reddit/HN threads where your audience asks questions. Record monthly volume, difficulty, search intent, and business relevance (1-3). Prioritize moderate difficulty with high relevance.

**Step 3 -- Competitor Content Audit**
Select 3-5 competitors or adjacent publications. For each, inventory their last 30 pieces: format, topic, estimated engagement (via BuzzSumo or Ahrefs), publishing frequency, and distribution channels. Identify crowded topics, neglected gaps, and formats earning disproportionate engagement.

**Step 4 -- Channel Selection**
Using SparkToro data, competitor patterns, and resource constraints, select 2-3 channels maximum. For each, define publishing cadence and the metric that signals traction. Depth on two channels outperforms breadth on six.

**Step 5 -- Content Format Testing**
Design a 30-day test comparing 2-3 formats on your primary channel. Hold topic constant, vary format. Measure engagement per format against a pre-defined threshold.

**Step 6 -- Build the 90-Day Calendar**
Anchor each month around one pillar topic (high business relevance, moderate difficulty) supported by 3-4 related pieces. Interleave formats based on Step 5 findings. Leave 20% of slots flexible for reactive content.

**Step 7 -- Set the Measurement Framework**
Define metrics at four levels: reach (traffic, impressions), engagement (time on page, shares), conversion (signups, demo requests), and attribution (which content touched customers who converted). Measure the portfolio, not individual articles.

## Interpretation Guide

**High volume + low difficulty = tactical wins.** These keywords represent quick content opportunities. They build domain authority and traffic while you invest in harder, higher-value topics over time.

**Competitor gap = your opening.** If no competitor covers a topic your audience searches for, you face an editorial vacuum. Fill it with definitive content and own the position before others notice.

**Engagement concentration signals format preference.** When one format consistently outperforms others across competitors, it reflects audience behaviour, not competitor skill. Respect the data even when it contradicts your preference.

**Attribution is imperfect -- accept it.** Content rarely converts on first touch. A prospect may read four blog posts over three months before requesting a demo. Measure assisted conversions and first-touch attribution alongside last-touch. Demanding perfect attribution from content is demanding certainty from a probabilistic channel.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Writing for yourself, not your audience.** Founders gravitate toward topics they find interesting. Your audience's questions, not your intellectual curiosity, should drive the calendar. Keyword data is the corrective.
- **Launching on five channels simultaneously.** Each channel has its own format norms, posting cadence, and feedback loop. Master one before adding another. Two channels done well outperform five done poorly.
- **Ignoring search intent.** A keyword with 10,000 monthly searches is worthless if the searcher wants a Wikipedia definition and you are selling enterprise software. Match content format to the intent behind the query.
- **Measuring vanity metrics alone.** Page views and social shares feel gratifying but mean nothing without downstream conversion data. Connect content to pipeline from the start, even if early numbers are small.
- **Abandoning the strategy after six weeks.** Content compounds. Most content strategies show meaningful results at 6-9 months. Pivoting every month based on early data guarantees you never reach compounding velocity.
