# Customer Interview Guide

Every failed startup is a monument to assumptions that were never tested. The Business Model Canvas has nine boxes, and at least six of them contain guesses until a founder has sat across from a real customer and listened — truly listened, without pitching, without leading, without defending. Customer interviews are the highest-leverage research activity available to an early-stage company. They cost nothing but time and ego. Yet most founders skip them, or worse, conduct them so poorly that the resulting data confirms whatever they already believed.

The difference between useful and useless interviews is not the questions asked but the discipline with which they are asked. This guide provides that discipline.

---

## What You'll Learn

- Whether your target customers actually experience the problem you are solving
- How they currently handle the problem (and what they spend — in money, time, or frustration)
- Whether your proposed solution generates genuine interest or polite nodding
- Willingness-to-pay signals grounded in behavior, not hypothetical approval
- Patterns across interviews that reveal segment-level truths, not individual opinions

---

## Interview Types

**Problem Interviews** — Conducted before you build. The goal is to validate that the problem exists, that it is painful enough to motivate action, and that your target segment experiences it frequently. You are not selling; you are learning.

**Solution Interviews** — Conducted once you have a concept, prototype, or MVP. The goal is to test whether your specific approach to the problem resonates. Show the solution, observe reactions, and probe for honest assessment.

**Pricing Interviews** — Conducted when you have a working solution. The goal is to discover willingness to pay through behavioral cues and structured pricing exercises, not direct questions ("Would you pay $X?" yields unreliable answers).

**Churn Interviews** — Conducted with customers who left or stopped using your product. The goal is to identify the actual reason for departure, which is rarely the reason stated in a cancellation form.

---

## Recruiting Participants

**How many:** 5-8 interviews per customer segment for pattern detection. After 5 interviews within a segment, you will hear the same themes repeating. After 8, you have statistical confidence in qualitative patterns.

**Where to find them:**
- LinkedIn outreach (direct, honest, brief — "I'm researching [problem area], not selling")
- Industry communities and forums (Reddit, Slack groups, Discord servers, Facebook groups)
- Existing customer base (for solution/pricing/churn interviews)
- Referral chains ("Who else deals with this problem?")
- Conference attendees and meetup members
- Cold email to companies in your target segment (response rate: 5-15% with a good subject line)

**Who NOT to interview:**
- Friends and family (they will tell you what you want to hear)
- People outside your target segment (their pain points are irrelevant to your thesis)
- Fellow entrepreneurs (they will give you advice instead of data)
- Anyone who stands to benefit from flattering you (potential co-founders, investors, employees)

**Incentives:** For B2B, a genuine 30-minute conversation with someone interested in their problems is often sufficient. For B2C, a $25-50 gift card or equivalent is standard. Never incentivize with your product — it biases toward people who already want it.

## Interview Protocol

**Phase 1 — Context Setting (5 minutes)**
Thank them. Explain you are researching, not selling. Ask permission to take notes. Establish that honesty is more valuable than politeness. State there are no wrong answers.

**Phase 2 — Current Behavior (10 minutes)**
Understand their world before introducing your topic. What does their day look like? What tools do they use? What frustrates them? Let them lead — you are mapping their reality.

**Phase 3 — Problem Exploration (10 minutes)**
Narrow to the problem space. Probe for frequency, severity, and current coping mechanisms. Ask for specific recent examples. Quantify the cost of the problem in time, money, or missed opportunity.

**Phase 4 — Solution Concept (10 minutes)**
For solution interviews only. Show or describe your approach. Watch their face before listening to their words. Ask what would need to be true for them to switch from their current approach. Probe for concerns and objections — these are gold.

**Phase 5 — Willingness to Pay (5 minutes)**
Do not ask "Would you pay $X?" Instead, use these approaches:
- "What do you currently spend on handling this problem?" (anchors to real behavior)
- "If this existed, what budget would it come from?" (reveals organizational decision-making)
- "At what price would this be a no-brainer? At what price would you hesitate?" (Van Westendorp range)

## Question Bank

### Problem Interview Questions
1. "Tell me about the last time you dealt with [problem area]."
2. "Walk me through how you handle [process] today, step by step."
3. "What is the most frustrating part of [process/task]?"
4. "How often does [problem] come up? Daily? Weekly? Monthly?"
5. "What have you tried to solve this? What happened?"
6. "How much time do you spend on [problem] each week?"
7. "If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about [process], what would it be?"
8. "What does this problem cost you — in dollars, time, or missed opportunities?"
9. "Who else in your organization deals with this?"
10. "Have you actively looked for a solution? What did you find?"

### Solution Interview Questions
11. "What is your first reaction to this?" (then silence — let them fill it)
12. "What part of this would be most useful to you?"
13. "What is missing?"
14. "What would make you nervous about using this?"
15. "How would this fit into your existing workflow?"
16. "What would have to be true for you to switch from your current approach?"
17. "Who else would need to approve this decision?"
18. "What would you compare this to?"
19. "If this disappeared tomorrow, what would you do instead?"
20. "On a scale of 1-10, how likely are you to try this? What would make it a 10?"

### Pricing Interview Questions
21. "What do you currently pay for tools in this category?"
22. "What budget does this type of purchase come from?"
23. "At what price would this be an obvious yes?"
24. "At what price would you start to question whether it's worth it?"
25. "At what price would it feel too cheap — like something must be wrong?"
26. "Would you prefer to pay per month, per year, or per use?"
27. "What is the most you have ever paid for a tool like this?"

### Churn Interview Questions
28. "What originally made you sign up?"
29. "Walk me through the moment you decided to stop using it."
30. "What are you using instead now?"
31. "Was there a single event that triggered the switch, or was it gradual?"
32. "What would have needed to change for you to stay?"
33. "If we fixed [the issue], would you consider coming back?"
34. "What did we get right?"

## Interpretation Guide

**Pattern threshold:** A pain point mentioned by 4 out of 5 interviewees is a pattern. Mentioned by 2 out of 5 is an observation. Mentioned by 1 is an anecdote. Build on patterns, investigate observations, and set aside anecdotes.

**Behavior over opinion:** "I would definitely use that" is an opinion. "I spent three hours last Tuesday building a spreadsheet to do this" is a behavior. Weight behaviors over opinions by a factor of ten.

**Enthusiasm calibration:** Polite interest ("That's interesting") is not a buying signal. Genuine engagement looks like: asking when it will be ready, requesting to be a beta tester, introducing you to colleagues who have the same problem, or asking about pricing unprompted.

**Negative data is the most valuable data.** If five interviews reveal that nobody has the problem you assumed, you have just saved six months of building the wrong thing. Treat disconfirmation as the prize, not the disappointment.

**Segment divergence:** If interviewees in different segments give contradictory answers, your segments are real and distinct. This is useful information — it means you need to choose which segment to serve first.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Leading questions.** "Don't you think it would be great if..." is not a question. It is a pitch disguised as inquiry. Every question should be answerable with information that surprises you.
- **Confirmation bias.** You will unconsciously weight the interviews that support your thesis and discount those that challenge it. Counter this by writing down your hypothesis before the first interview, then explicitly tracking disconfirming evidence.
- **Talking more than listening.** The ideal ratio is 20% you, 80% them. If you catch yourself explaining your product for more than 60 seconds, stop and ask a question.
- **Interviewing the wrong people.** Talking to ten people outside your target segment produces noise, not signal. Define your target segment precisely before recruiting.
- **Not recording or noting.** Human memory is unreliable. Take notes during the interview (with permission) and write a summary within two hours. Without documentation, interviews collapse into vague impressions.
- **Stopping at positive signal.** Five enthusiastic interviews feel conclusive. They are not. Seek out the skeptics and the disinterested — they reveal the boundaries of your market.

## Output

Package your interview findings for use in the following Foreman skills and templates:

- **business-model-canvas** — Pain points map to "Value Propositions." Current behavior maps to "Customer Relationships" and "Channels." Willingness-to-pay maps to "Revenue Streams." Segment characteristics map to "Customer Segments."
- **net-promoter-score** — Solution interview enthusiasm scores provide a pre-launch proxy for NPS. Churn interview data feeds NPS diagnostic work.
- **product-market-fit-diagnosis** — The synthesis template's pattern matrix is the primary input. Strong patterns across pain points + willingness-to-pay signals = product-market fit evidence.
- **four-ps-marketing** — Pricing signals feed the "Price" dimension directly. Customer language and pain point framing feed "Promotion." Channel preferences feed "Place."

Transfer the synthesis template, key quotes (verbatim customer language is invaluable for marketing copy), and the willingness-to-pay summary. Flag the two or three findings that most challenge your original assumptions — these are the insights with the highest strategic value.
