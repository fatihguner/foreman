# Talent Market Research

Hiring is a market transaction, and like any market transaction, the party with better information captures more value. Most founders approach hiring with a salary figure plucked from a single Glassdoor search, a job description copied from a competitor, and a timeline based on optimism rather than supply data.

The talent market is researchable. Compensation data is increasingly public. Hiring activity signals strategic intent. Talent pool sizes constrain timelines. This guide provides the protocol for gathering this intelligence before you post the role, not after the third failed offer.

---

## What You'll Learn

- Salary benchmarks at the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentile for your target role, adjusted by geography and stage
- The size of the available talent pool for your specific requirements
- Which competitors are hiring for similar roles and what their postings reveal about strategy
- A competitive offer structure balancing cash, equity, and non-monetary factors
- A realistic hiring timeline based on market supply, not aspiration

## Research Protocol

**Step 1 — Define the Role Precisely**
Write a one-paragraph role definition: function, level, key skills, and experience range. "Senior backend engineer with distributed systems experience, 5-8 years" is searchable. "Technical person" is not.

**Step 2 — Benchmark Salary Range**
Gather compensation data from at least three sources. Record the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentile for base salary and total compensation. Filter by geography (San Francisco commands 40-60% more than Austin) and company stage (Series A typically pays 10-20% below public company base but compensates with equity).

**Step 3 — Estimate Talent Pool Size**
Use LinkedIn (role title + key skills + geography) to count matching professionals. Discount by 70-80% for passive candidates. Below 200 addressable, expect a long search or broaden requirements.

**Step 4 — Map Competitor Hiring**
Search job boards for identical roles at talent competitors (similar stage, sector, geography — not necessarily product competitors). Note posted salary ranges, qualifications, and benefits. Patterns reveal strategic priorities.

**Step 5 — Assess Your Employer Brand**
Read your own Glassdoor reviews as a candidate would. Search Blind. Ask recent hires what almost made them say no. Your employer brand is what candidates discover, not what you declare.

**Step 6 — Design Competitive Offer Structure**
Construct an offer at or above the 50th percentile for base. Benchmark equity against Carta or Pave for your stage. Identify non-monetary differentiators that your talent pool values.

## Interpretation Guide

**Salary positioning strategy:** The 50th percentile attracts average candidates at average speed. The 75th percentile accelerates hiring but sets a compensation precedent. For critical hires, the premium often pays for itself.

**Talent pool scarcity signals:** Fewer than 500 matching LinkedIn profiles indicates a tight market. Expect 60-90 day searches and above-median compensation. Consider remote hiring to expand the pool.

**Competitor posting velocity:** A competitor that posted the same role three times in twelve months is either growing fast or cannot retain. Their retention problem may be your recruiting opportunity.

**Employer brand asymmetry:** A 3.2 Glassdoor rating citing "great technology, poor management" deters senior candidates. Address the issue or expect to compensate financially.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Single-source salary anchoring.** Using one data point as your benchmark. Compensation data is noisy — three sources, triangulated, produces a defensible range.
- **Ignoring total compensation.** Comparing base salary while ignoring the competitor's equity and bonus. Candidates compare packages, not line items.
- **Geographic arbitrage without transparency.** Hiring remote workers in lower-cost markets at below-market local rates creates resentment when employees discover the disparity. Transparent compensation bands prevent retention crises.
- **Conflating talent competitors with product competitors.** Your talent competes with Google, the well-funded startup down the street, and the hedge fund that just opened a tech office — not just your product competitors.

## Output

Package your talent market research for use in the following Foreman components:

- **hiring-diagnosis** — Salary benchmarks and talent pool data feed the diagnostic that evaluates whether a hiring challenge stems from compensation, brand, process, or market factors.
- **team-rebuild-playbook** — Competitor hiring signals and talent pool estimates inform sequencing and timeline for rebuilding a team. Market data prevents unrealistic hiring plans.
- **hiring-scorecard** — The role definition and required skills from Step 1 become evaluation criteria. Salary benchmarks set offer parameters.
- **role-description-template** — Competitive analysis of job postings informs how to position your role against alternatives candidates are evaluating.
- **ceo-succession-brief** — For executive searches, salary benchmarks, talent pool size, and competitor data inform the board on search difficulty and compensation requirements.
