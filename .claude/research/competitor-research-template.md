# Competitor Research Template

The most dangerous competitor is the one you have not identified. Most founders monitor the two or three companies that look like them — same features, same pitch, same target customer. They miss the adjacent player approaching from a different angle, the incumbent adding a feature, and the non-consumption alternative where customers simply do nothing. Competitive intelligence is not surveillance. It is the systematic mapping of the landscape your customer navigates when deciding whether to choose you, choose someone else, or choose nothing at all.

Rigorous competitor research precedes every strategic framework that references competition: Porter's Five Forces requires it, Blue Ocean Strategy depends on it, and SWOT without it is fiction.

---

## What You'll Learn

- A comprehensive list of direct, indirect, potential, and substitute competitors
- A structured profile of each competitor's offering, pricing, traction, and positioning
- A feature comparison matrix that reveals gaps and clusters
- A positioning map that visualizes competitive space and opportunity
- Pattern-level insights about market structure and strategic openings

---

## Competitor Identification

**Direct competitors:** Companies solving the same problem for the same customer with a similar approach. Search your product category on G2, Capterra, ProductHunt, and app stores. Query Crunchbase with industry and funding filters.

**Indirect competitors:** Companies solving the same problem but with a different approach or technology. Search for the *problem* rather than the *product category*. Ask potential customers: "How do you handle [problem] today?"

**Potential competitors:** Companies not currently in your market that could enter easily — adjacent products, well-funded startups pivoting, or incumbents with distribution advantages. Monitor funding announcements and product launches in adjacent categories.

**Substitutes:** Non-software alternatives including manual processes, spreadsheets, consultants, or inaction. Interview potential customers to discover these. The most overlooked competitor is "do nothing."

## Research Protocol

**Step 1 — Build the Initial List**
Identify 8-12 companies across all four types (direct, indirect, potential, substitute). Cast a wide net. You will narrow to 5-8 for deep analysis in the next step.

**Step 2 — Profile Each Competitor**
For each company, fill in the competitor matrix (below). Spend 30-60 minutes per competitor. Use the data sources table above — no single source tells the full story.

**Step 3 — Build the Feature Comparison Matrix**
List the 10-15 features or capabilities most relevant to your target customer. Score each competitor (has it / partially has it / does not have it). Include yourself. Gaps in this matrix are either opportunities or warnings.

**Step 4 — Construct a Positioning Map**
Choose two axes that matter most to your target customer — not features, but positioning dimensions. Common pairs: price vs. complexity, horizontal vs. vertical, self-serve vs. enterprise, breadth vs. depth. Plot each competitor as a point. Where do clusters form? Where is empty space?

**Step 5 — Assess Strengths and Weaknesses**
For each competitor in your top 5, identify 2-3 genuine strengths and 2-3 genuine weaknesses. Source these from user reviews (G2, Capterra, app stores), employee reviews (Glassdoor), and observable behavior (pricing changes, feature gaps, slow release cadence).

**Step 6 — Synthesize Patterns**
Step back and look at the landscape as a whole. What patterns emerge? Where is the market heading? What are competitors collectively ignoring?

## Interpretation Guide

**Clustering signals commodity dynamics.** When most competitors occupy the same quadrant on your positioning map, the market has converged on a standard approach. Competing here requires superior execution or lower cost. The strategic alternative is to move to an unoccupied quadrant — but only if customers exist there.

**An empty quadrant is either an opportunity or a graveyard.** Before celebrating an unoccupied position, ask why it is empty. Sometimes nobody serves that quadrant because nobody wants to be there. Validate with customer interviews before building for a white space.

**One dominant player changes everything.** If a single competitor holds 60%+ market share, frontal assault is expensive. Consider a flanking strategy: a different segment, a different distribution model, or a different value proposition that avoids the incumbent's strength.

**Funding patterns reveal market timing.** Heavy recent funding in your space means investors believe the timing is right — but competition for customers will intensify. No recent funding may mean the market is too early, too small, or already captured.

**Job postings decode strategy.** A competitor suddenly hiring machine learning engineers signals an AI pivot. A surge in enterprise sales hires means an upmarket move. Job postings are the most underused competitive intelligence source.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Only tracking direct competitors.** The company that disrupts you is unlikely to look like you. Amazon did not look like a bookstore chain. Track indirect competitors and substitutes with equal diligence.
- **Ignoring non-consumption.** If your potential customer's alternative is a spreadsheet, a consultant, or doing nothing, that is your real competition — and it shapes your go-to-market strategy more than any funded startup.
- **Confusing features with positioning.** A feature matrix tells you what competitors *do*. Positioning analysis tells you how they *compete*. Two products with identical features can occupy completely different positions based on pricing, branding, and target segment.
- **One-time research.** Competitive landscapes shift quarterly. Set a calendar reminder to refresh your competitor matrix every 90 days. Flag new entrants, funding events, and major product changes.
- **Assuming competitor weakness is your opportunity.** A competitor's weakness only matters if their customers care about it. Validate through customer interviews before building features that exploit perceived gaps.
