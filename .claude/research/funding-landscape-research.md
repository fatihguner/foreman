# Funding Landscape Research

Capital is not interchangeable. A dollar from a strategic investor who opens distribution channels differs fundamentally from a dollar borrowed against future revenue, and both differ from a grant that demands no equity but constrains your roadmap. Founders who treat fundraising as a single question -- "who will give us money?" -- end up with the wrong capital on the wrong terms from the wrong partner. The research that precedes a fundraise determines not just whether you raise, but whether the capital you secure accelerates or distorts your trajectory. This guide provides a systematic method for mapping every relevant funding source, filtering to the ones that fit your stage and sector, and researching individual investors with enough depth to make every outreach attempt count.

---

## What You'll Learn

- A categorized inventory of funding sources appropriate to your stage and sector
- A target list of 30-50 investors with thesis fit, check size, and portfolio conflict data
- Partner-level profiles for your top 15 targets, including investment interests and warm intro paths
- An assessment of non-dilutive alternatives (grants, revenue-based financing, venture debt)
- A prioritized outreach sequence balancing fit, accessibility, and strategic value

## Research Protocol

**Step 1 -- Define Funding Need**
Specify three parameters before searching: (a) the amount you need to raise, (b) the timeline within which you need the capital deployed, and (c) your flexibility on terms -- specifically, how much dilution you will accept and whether you are open to debt. These three numbers filter out 80% of irrelevant sources immediately.

**Step 2 -- Assess Stage-Appropriate Sources**
Map your stage to typical capital sources. Pre-seed: angels, pre-seed funds, accelerators, SBIR. Seed: seed funds, micro-VCs, syndicates. Series A: institutional VCs ($100M-$500M funds). Later: growth equity, venture debt, revenue-based financing. Pursuing capital from the wrong stage wastes months.

**Step 3 -- Build the Long List (30-50 Targets)**
Using Crunchbase, AngelList, and Signal, generate investors matching on stage, sector, and check size. Include 5-10 non-dilutive options if applicable. Record fund size, last fund vintage, and deployment pace in the past 12 months. A fund that has not deployed in a year is either fully committed or in transition.

**Step 4 -- Research Portfolio Fit and Conflicts**
For each investor, review portfolio companies on Crunchbase. Flag direct competitors -- most investors will not fund a conflict. Note complementary companies that signal thesis alignment and may provide warm intros.

**Step 5 -- Build Partner-Level Profiles (Top 15)**
For your highest-fit investors, identify the specific partner most likely to lead your deal. Research their background on LinkedIn, read their published writing, and note which portfolio companies they led. Generic outreach to "the fund" converts at a fraction of the rate of targeted outreach to the right partner.

**Step 6 -- Identify Warm Introduction Paths**
For each top-15 target, map intro sources: mutual connections, portfolio founder relationships, accelerator alumni networks. A warm introduction from a portfolio founder converts at roughly 10x the rate of a cold email. Rank each target by warmest available path.

**Step 7 -- Build the Outreach Sequence**
Prioritize by thesis fit, accessibility, and strategic value. Sequence in waves of 5-8, starting with second-tier targets to refine your pitch before approaching top choices.

## Interpretation Guide

**Thesis match is non-negotiable.** An investor who focuses on enterprise SaaS will not lead your consumer marketplace round. Thesis misalignment is not overcome by enthusiasm. Filter ruthlessly.

**Recent deployment pace signals appetite.** Eight investments in 12 months means active deployment. One investment means concentrated or winding down. Check fund vintage -- years 1-3 deploy; years 4-5 follow on.

**Portfolio density is a double signal.** Three portfolio companies in your space signals thesis understanding but may mean the thesis is fully expressed. Ask portfolio founders directly.

**Warm paths have a half-life.** An introduction loses potency with delay. Activate within one week of identifying the path.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Spraying cold emails to 200 investors.** Volume signals desperation. A targeted 30-investor process with warm intros outperforms a 200-investor cold campaign.
- **Ignoring non-dilutive capital.** Grants and revenue-based financing are underutilised. At pre-seed, $150K in SBIR funding preserves meaningful ownership.
- **Researching the fund but not the partner.** Funds do not write checks -- partners do. Your pitch must resonate with the person across the table.
- **Optimising for valuation over partner quality.** The highest valuation often comes from the least helpful investor. Operational value compounds far beyond the term sheet.
- **Starting with your top choice.** Your first three pitches will be your worst. Sequence so top-priority investors hear the refined version.
